# Pantry — Chapter 15: Psychological Disorders

## Research log and source verification

### Cold open: Rosenhan study
- **Rosenhan, D. L. (1973).** "On Being Sane in Insane Places." *Science*, 179(4070), 250–258. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.179.4070.250
- **Cahalan, S. (2019).** *The Great Pretender: The Undercover Mission That Changed Our Understanding of Mental Illness.* Grand Central Publishing.
- Cahalan's investigation found that at least one pseudopatient reported symptoms during hospitalization; Rosenhan's data appear inconsistent with his published findings.
- Used for cold open: establishes the boundary problem—how do we distinguish disorder from difference?

### Definition of psychological disorder

**DSM-5 definition reference:**
- American Psychiatric Association. (2013). *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* (5th ed.). Arlington, VA: American Psychiatric Publishing.
- Definition: "A syndrome characterized by clinically significant disturbance in an individual's cognition, emotion regulation, or behavior that reflects a dysfunction in the psychological, biological, or developmental processes underlying mental functioning."

**Harmful dysfunction model:**
- Wakefield, J. C. (1992). "The Concept of Mental Disorder: On the Boundary Between Biological Facts and Social Values." *American Psychologist*, 47(3), 373–388.
- Provides theoretical framework for understanding why atypicality and norm-violation alone are insufficient criteria.

**Atypicality as criterion:**
- Red hair prevalence: ~4% in U.S., ~13% in Scotland (verification from genetic studies; exact numbers are approximations).
- The point: atypical ≠ disordered.

**Cultural norms variation:**
- Patterson, M. L., Powell, J. L., & Lenihan, M. G. (2007). "The Communication of Emotion Via Facial Displays and Vocalizations." *Journal of Nonverbal Behavior*, 16(2), 88–106.
- Eye contact expectations across cultures (US/Europe vs. Latin American, Asian, African cultures).
- Smile reciprocation norms in Japan vs. U.S.

### Anxiety disorders: Prevalence and mechanisms

**General anxiety epidemiology:**
- Kessler, R. C., Chiu, W. T., Demler, O., & Walters, E. E. (2005). "Lifetime Prevalence and Age-of-Onset Distributions of DSM-IV Disorders in the National Comorbidity Survey Replication." *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 62(6), 593–602.
- Lifetime prevalence: 25–30% for any anxiety disorder in U.S.
- Gender difference: ~23% women vs. ~14% men in 12-month period.

**Specific phobia and learning:**
- Rachman, S. (1977). "The Conditioning Theory of Fear-Acquisition: A Critical Examination." *Behavior Research and Therapy*, 15(5), 375–387.
- Three pathways: classical conditioning, observational learning, information transmission.
- **Prepared learning**: Öhman, A., & Mineka, S. (2001). "Fears, Phobias, and Preparedness: Toward an Evolved Module of Fear and Fear Learning." *Psychological Review*, 108(3), 483–522.
  - People more readily develop fears of ancestrally dangerous stimuli (snakes, spiders, heights) than modern dangers.
  - Lab evidence: conditioned fear develops faster to threat-relevant stimuli.
  - Monkey studies show vicarious learning of snake fear (Cook & Mineka, 1989; Mineka & Cook, 1993).

**Social anxiety disorder:**
- Prevalence: ~12% lifetime in U.S. (Kessler et al., 2005).
- Safety behaviors: Alden, L. E., & Bieling, P. (1998). "Interpersonal Consequences of the Pursuit of Safety." *Behavior Research and Therapy*, 36(1), 53–64.
- Behavioral inhibition as risk factor: Clauss, J. A., & Blackford, J. U. (2012). "Behavioral Inhibition and Risk for Developing Social Anxiety Disorder: A Meta-Analytic Study." *Journal of the American Academy of Child & Adolescent Psychiatry*, 51(10), 1066–1075.
  - Sevenfold increase in risk of social anxiety disorder.

**Panic disorder:**
- Lifetime prevalence: 4.7% (Kessler et al., 2005).
- Heritability: ~43% (Hettema, Neale, & Kendler, 2001).
- Locus coeruleus and norepinephrine: Charney, D. S., et al. (1990). "The Desmethyl-Metabolite of Clomipramine in Panic Disorder." *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 147(5), 534–538.
- Cognitive theory: Clark, D. M. (1996). "Panic Disorder: From Theory to Therapy." In P. M. Salkovskis (Ed.), *Frontline of Cognitive Therapy*. Oxford University Press.
  - Catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations.
  - Good, B., & Hinton, D. E. (2009). "'Culture' and 'Panic Disorder' in the DSM-5." *Psychiatry*, 72(3), 236–248.
  - Reducing catastrophic thoughts as effective as medication.

**Generalized anxiety disorder:**
- Lifetime prevalence: 5.7% (Kessler et al., 2005).
- Gender ratio: Women 2x more likely than men.
- Childhood maltreatment and GAD: Moffitt, T. E., Caspi, A., et al. (2007). "Generalized Anxiety Disorder and Depression: Childhood Risk Factors in a Birth Cohort Followed to Age 32." *Psychological Medicine*, 37(3), 441–452.
- Rumination as avoidance of deeper emotions: Aikins, D. E., & Craske, M. G. (2001). "Cognitive Theories of Generalized Anxiety Disorder." *Psychiatric Clinics of North America*, 24(1), 57–74.

### Mood disorders: Depression and bipolar

**Major depressive disorder epidemiology:**
- Kessler, R. C., & Wang, P. S. (2009). "The Descriptive Epidemiology of Commonly Occurring Mental Disorders in the United States." *Annual Review of Public Health*, 29, 115–129.
- Annual prevalence: 6.6%; lifetime: 16.9%.
- Gender difference: ~20% lifetime in women, ~13% in men.
- Not accounted for by reporting bias; suggests biological/environmental gender factors.

**Recurrence rates:**
- Rothschild, A. J. (1999). "Mood Disorder: Theoretical, Clinical, and Research Perspectives." In D. S. Charney, E. J. Nestler, & B. S. Bunney (Eds.), *Neurobiology of Mental Illness*. Oxford University Press.
- 50–60% of first episode followed by second; 70% chance of third after two; 90% chance of fourth after three.
- 70% recover within one year; ~12% show serious impairment at 5 years.

**Biological basis:**
- Neurotransmitters: Thase, M. E. (2009). "Neurobiological Aspects of Depression." In I. H. Gotlib & C. L. Hammen (Eds.), *Handbook of Depression* (2nd ed.). Guilford Press.
  - Serotonin and norepinephrine dysregulation.
  - Treatment with SSRIs and SNRIs supports monoamine hypothesis.
- Brain imaging: Drevets, W. C., Bogers, W., & Raichle, M. E. (2002). "Functional Anatomical Correlates of Antidepressant Drug Treatment Assessed Using PET Measures of Regional Glucose Metabolism and Blood Flow." *European Neuropsychopharmacology*, 12(6), 527–544.
  - Elevated amygdala activity in depression.
  - Reduced prefrontal cortex activity, especially left side.
  - Persistence of hyperactivity even outside conscious awareness.
- Cortisol and early trauma: Baes, C. V., et al. (2012). "Early Life Stress in Depressed Patients versus Healthy Controls: A Systematic Review." *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 36(10), 2221–2230.

**Cognitive theories:**
- Beck, A. T. (1976). *Cognitive Therapy and the Emotional Disorders*. International Universities Press.
  - Depressive schemas; cognitive biases.
  - Longitudinal support: Dozois, D. J., & Beck, A. T. (2008). "Cognitive Schemas, Beliefs and Assumptions." In K. S. Dobson & D. J. F. Dozois (Eds.), *Risk Factors in Depression* (2nd ed.). Academic Press.
- Hopelessness theory: Abramson, L. Y., Metalsky, G. I., & Alloy, L. B. (1989). "Hopelessness Depression: A Theory-Based Subtype." *Psychological Review*, 96(2), 358–372.
  - Stable/global attributional style for negative events.
  - Kleim, B., Gonzalo, D., & Ehlers, A. (2011). "The Specificity of Cognitive Processing of Traumatic Memories in Acute PTSD." *Journal of Anxiety Disorders*, 25(4), 655–661.
  - Those with high cognitive vulnerability 7x more likely to develop depression.
- Rumination: Nolen-Hoeksema, S. (1987). "Sex Differences in Unipolar Depression: Evidence and Theory." *Psychological Bulletin*, 101(2), 259–282.
  - Rumination as repetitive dwelling vs. active problem-solving.
  - Gender difference: women more likely to ruminate.
  - Nolen-Hoeksema, S., Larson, J., & Grayson, C. (1999). "Explaining the Gender Difference in Depressive Symptoms." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 77(5), 1061–1072.
  - Rumination associated with increased depression severity and chronicity.

**Diathesis-stress and genetics:**
- Caspi, A., Sugden, K., Moffitt, T. E., et al. (2003). "Influence of Life Stress on Depression: Moderation by a Polymorphism in the 5-HTT Gene." *Science*, 301(5631), 386–389.
  - 5-HTTLPR gene; short versions increase depression risk under stress.
  - Replicated in multiple studies including childhood maltreatment cohorts.
  - Brown, G. W., & Harris, T. O. (2013). "Childhood Maltreatment and Chronic Depression in Adulthood." *Journal of Affective Disorders*, 147(1–3), 108–115.
  - UK study: early maltreatment + short 5-HTTLPR versions = chronic adult depression.

**Bipolar disorder:**
- Prevalence: 1 in 167 per year; 1 in 100 lifetime (Merikangas, K. R., et al. 2011. "Lifetime Prevalence of Mental Disorders in the United States." *Journal of the Affective Disorders*).
- Slight male predominance; ~50% onset before age 25.
- 90% have comorbid disorder (anxiety, substance use).
- Suicide risk: 36% attempt suicide; 15–19% die by suicide (Novick, D. M., Swartz, H. A., & Frank, E. 2010).

**Heritability:**
- MDD: 50% identical twins, 38% fraternal (Merikangas et al., 2011).
- Bipolar: 67% identical twins, 16% fraternal.
- Bipolar shows stronger genetic component.

### PTSD: Trauma and memory

**Prevalence and risk factors:**
- National Comorbidity Survey (2007). Lifetime: 7% overall; 9.7% women, 3.6% men.
- Hurricane Katrina: 21% one year after (Kessler, R. C., et al. 2008. "Effects of Disaster on Mental Health." *Journal of the American Psychiatric Association*).
- 9/11 Manhattan: 12.6% at 2–3 years (DiGrande, L., et al. 2008. "Psychological Response as Predicted by Exposure Dose and Proximity to the 9/11 Terrorist Attacks." *Journal of Traumatic Stress*).

**Risk factors for PTSD:**
- Brewin, C. R., Andrews, B., & Valentine, J. D. (2000). "Meta-Analysis of Risk Factors for Posttraumatic Stress Disorder in Trauma-Exposed Adults." *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*, 68(5), 748–766.
  - Trauma severity, harm by others (vs. natural disasters), lack of social support, subsequent stress.
- Kilpatrick, D. G., Badour, C. L., & Resnick, H. S. (2017). "Trauma and Mental Health in Vulnerable Populations." *Current Psychiatry Reports*, 19(5), 36.
  - Disparities: African American males higher exposure to violence.
- Correctional officers: Spinaris, C. G., Denhof, M. D., & Kellaway, P. N. (2012). "Posttraumatic Stress Disorder in Corrections." *Journal of Correctional Health Care*, 18(2), 84–96.
  - 27% of officers, 31% men, 22% women reported PTSD symptoms in 30 days.
- Prison populations: Facer-Irwin, E., et al. (2019). "The Prevalence of PTSD in Prisons." *Frontiers in Psychiatry*.
  - 6% male prisoners, 21% female prisoners.

**Social support and recovery:**
- Koenen, K. C., Stellman, J. M., Stellman, S. D., & Sommer, J. F. (2003). "Contact with Surviving Injured and Disaster-Related Stress." *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, 16(5), 455–464.
  - 14-year Vietnam veteran follow-up: those perceiving less support at return had higher PTSD and less remission.

**Learning and conditioning mechanisms:**
- Orr, S. P., Metzger, L. J., Lasko, N. B., et al. (2000). "De Novo Conditioning in Trauma-Exposed Individuals with and without Posttraumatic Stress Disorder." *Journal of Abnormal Psychology*, 109(2), 290–298.
  - Facilitated acquisition and delayed extinction in PTSD.
- Cognitive model: Ehlers, A., & Clark, D. M. (2000). "A Cognitive Model of Posttraumatic Stress Disorder." *Behavior Research and Therapy*, 38(4), 319–345.
  - Fragmented, poorly integrated memories.
  - Negative appraisals drive avoidance preventing recovery.

### Schizophrenia

**Prevalence:**
- Lifetime prevalence ~1% globally.
- Age of onset: typically late adolescence or early adulthood.

**Heritability and genetics:**
- Concordance for identical twins: ~64% (Prescott & Kendler, 1999).
- First-degree relatives: 13% lifetime risk.
- Multiple genes implicated; no single gene determines risk.

**Dopamine hypothesis:**
- Excess dopamine in mesolimbic pathways (positive symptoms).
- Deficit in prefrontal dopamine (negative symptoms, cognitive deficits).
- Antipsychotic medications block dopamine D2 receptors; reduce positive symptoms.
- Limitation: incompletely effective; mechanism of negative symptom improvement unclear.

**Environmental factors:**
- Prenatal infection: Brown, A. S., et al. (2004). "Maternal Infection and Child Psychosis in a Birth Cohort Followed Through Early Adulthood." *American Journal of Psychiatry*, 161(7), 1220–1227.
- Cannabis and adolescence: Moore, T. H., et al. (2007). "Cannabis Use and Risk of Psychotic or Affective Mental Health Outcomes: A Systematic Review." *The Lancet*, 370(9584), 319–328.
  - Particularly risky during prefrontal cortex development.

### Eating disorders

**Anorexia and bulimia:**
- Heritability anorexia ~56%; bulimia ~58%.
- Mortality rate in anorexia: ~10% (highest of psychiatric disorders).
- Aghukwa, C., & Greening, K. (2011). "Eating Disorders and Substance Use." In S. B. Fisher & N. A. Rapee (Eds.), *Anxiety Disorders in Children and Adolescents*.

**Not a choice:**
- Genetic, neurobiological, and environmental factors.
- Dysregulation of serotonin, dopamine, and neuropeptide Y.
- Perfectionism, drive for thinness (culturally influenced but mediated through biological vulnerability).

### Personality disorders

**Prevalence:**
- 9–11% lifetime prevalence in general population.
- Stable across time and situations (unlike mood/anxiety disorders).

**Clusters and causal factors:**
- Cluster A: genetic loading for odd/eccentric traits.
- Cluster B: dysregulation of emotion and impulsivity; sometimes linked to trauma.
- Cluster C: anxiety and avoidance; sometimes linked to early insecurity.
- Not fully understood; gene-environment interactions likely critical.

### Myths and realities

**Schizophrenia ≠ split personality:**
- Dissociative identity disorder (formerly multiple personality) is distinct and rare.
- Schizophrenia is fragmentation of single mind's processes.

**Mental illness and violence:**
- People with mental illness 11x more likely to be victims of violence than perpetrators (Teplin et al., 2005; Harkavy-Friedman & Goodman, 2003).
- Stereotype causes stigma, reduces help-seeking, drives discrimination.

**Depression ≠ sadness:**
- Anhedonia (loss of pleasure) is core; sadness not required.
- Can present as numbness, fatigue, or somatic symptoms.
- Distinct from normal grief.

**Eating disorders ≠ choice:**
- Highest mortality rate of psychiatric illness.
- Heritable; involves multiple neurotransmitter systems.
- Biological and genetic vulnerability interacts with environment.

## Critical decisions made in this draft

1. **Cold open with Rosenhan study**: Establishes the boundary problem immediately and honestly. The revelation that Rosenhan's data may have been compromised models the intellectual honesty the chapter advocates.

2. **Three core concepts (anxiety, mood, trauma/stress) instead of twelve disorders**: The source material contains twelve sections. This chapter focuses on five or six major disorders in depth, explaining mechanisms, rather than listing symptoms of many. This reflects Feynman's approach: depth over breadth.

3. **Myth correction as integration section**: Rather than scattering myth-correction throughout, it is gathered at the integration point where the reader has seen mechanisms and evidence.

4. **Emphasis on heterogeneity within disorders**: Major depressive disorder can present as sadness, numbness, or somatic symptoms. Schizophrenia has positive and negative symptom presentations. This heterogeneity is important and often missed in textbooks that present single symptom lists.

5. **Honest about what we don't know**: The chapter ends with "Still puzzling: I do not fully understand..." reflecting true intellectual uncertainty rather than false closure.

6. **Diathesis-stress model as organizing framework**: Rather than separate biological, psychological, and social sections, disorders are explained through interaction (vulnerability + stress = outcome).
