# Chapter 14 Pantry — Research Notes and Supplementary Material

## Primary Sources and Key Studies

### Foundational Research

- **Selye, H. (1936).** "A syndrome produced by diverse nocuous agents." *Nature, 138*, 32–33.
  - The original brief note establishing the general adaptation syndrome concept from the McGill laboratory.

- **Cannon, W. B. (1932).** *The Wisdom of the Body.* W. W. Norton.
  - Historical foundational work on the fight-or-flight response and homeostasis.

- **Lazarus, R. S., & Folkman, S. (1984).** *Stress, Appraisal, and Coping.* Springer.
  - The canonical text on stress as a process involving appraisal and coping. Defines the process-based approach used throughout this chapter.

### Immune Function and Stress

- **Cohen, S., Frank, E., Doyle, W. J., Skoner, D. P., Rabin, B. S., & Gwaltney, J. M. J. (1998).** "Types of stressors that increase susceptibility to the common cold in healthy adults." *Health Psychology, 17*, 214–223.
  - The landmark cold-virus study: chronic stressors predicted whether exposure led to infection.

- **Kiecolt-Glaser, J. K., & Glaser, R. (2005).** "Stress-induced immune dysfunction: Implications for health." *Nature Reviews Immunology, 5*, 243–251.
  - Meta-analysis of stress effects on immune function, with emphasis on mechanisms.

- **Segerstrom, S. C., & Miller, G. E. (2004).** "Psychological stress and the human immune system: A meta-analytic study of 30 years of inquiry." *Psychological Bulletin, 130*, 601–630.
  - Comprehensive meta-analysis documenting consistent stress-immune suppression relationship across 30 years of research.

### Cardiovascular Disease and Personality

- **Friedman, M., & Rosenman, R. H. (1959).** "Association of specific overt behavior pattern with blood and cardiovascular findings." *Journal of the American Medical Association, 169*(12), 1286–1296.
  - The original Type A personality and heart disease study.

- **Chida, Y., & Steptoe, A. (2009).** "The association of anger and hostility with future coronary heart disease: A meta-analytic review of prospective evidence." *Journal of the American College of Cardiology, 53*, 936–946.
  - Meta-analysis showing hostility (not Type A broadly) predicts cardiovascular disease.

- **Chang, P. P., Ford, D. E., Meoni, L. A., Wang, N. Y., & Klag, M. J. (2002).** "Anger in young and subsequent premature cardiovascular disease." *Archives of Internal Medicine, 162*, 901–906.
  - Decades-long follow-up of medical students: high anger at age 30s predicted 6x higher heart attack risk by 55.

- **Whang, W., Kubzansky, L. D., Kawachi, I., Rexrod, K. M., Kroenke, C. H., Glynn, R. J., & Albert, C. M. (2009).** "Depression and risk of sudden cardiac death and coronary heart disease in women." *Journal of the American College of Cardiology, 53*, 950–958.
  - Over 63,000 nurses: depressed symptoms predicted 49% higher mortality from cardiac disease.

### Telomeres and Stress

- **Epel, E. S., Blackburn, E. H., Lin, J., Dhabhar, F. S., Adler, N. E., Morrow, J. D., & Cawthon, R. M. (2004).** "Accelerated telomere shortening in response to life stress." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, USA, 101*, 17312–17315.
  - Mothers of chronically ill children showed telomere shortening equivalent to 9–17 years of aging.

- **Shalev, I., Moffitt, T. E., Sugden, K., Williams, B., Houts, R. M., Danese, A., & Caspi, A. (2013).** "Exposure to violence during childhood is associated with telomere erosion from 5 to 10 years of age." *Molecular Psychiatry, 18*, 576–581.
  - Childhood violence exposure predicted telomere erosion years later.

### Social Support and Health

- **Berkman, L. F., & Syme, S. L. (1979).** "Social networks, host resistance, and mortality: A nine-year follow-up study of Alameda County residents." *American Journal of Epidemiology, 109*, 186–204.
  - The landmark epidemiological study showing social isolation predicts mortality (2.3–2.8 times higher risk).

- **Holt-Lunstad, J., Smith, T. B., & Layton, J. B. (2010).** "Social relationships and mortality risk: A meta-analytic review." *PLoS Medicine, 7*(7), e1000316.
  - Meta-analysis of 148 studies, over 300,000 participants: strong social relationships predict 50% greater survival likelihood.

- **Cohen, S., Doyle, W. J., Turner, R., Alper, C. M., & Skoner, D. P. (2003).** "Sociability and susceptibility to the common cold." *Psychological Science, 14*, 389–395.
  - Sociability (number and quality of social contacts) was linearly associated with lower cold risk.

### Perceived Control

- **Marmot, M. G., Bosma, H., Hemingway, H., & Stansfeld, S. (1997).** "Contribution of job control and other risk factors to social variations in coronary heart disease incidence." *The Lancet, 350*, 235–239.
  - British civil servants: low-status, low-control jobs predicted heart disease more than high demands alone.

- **Infurna, F. J., & Gerstorf, D. (2014).** "Perceived control relates to better functional health and lower cardio-metabolic risk: The mediating role of physical activity." *Health Psychology, 33*, 85–94.
  - Perceived control predicts better health through both direct physiological pathways and behavioral pathways (exercise).

### Asthma and Stress

- **Smyth, J. M., Soefer, M. H., Hurewitz, A., Kliment, A., & Stone, A. A. (1999).** "Daily psychosocial factors predict levels and diurnal cycles of asthma symptomatology and peak flow." *Journal of Behavioral Medicine, 22*, 179–193.
  - Real-time monitoring showed daily stress and negative emotions predicted airway obstruction in asthmatics.

- **Kilpeläinen, M., Koskenvuo, M., Helenius, H., & Terho, E. O. (2002).** "Stressful life events promote the manifestation of asthma and atopic diseases." *Clinical and Experimental Allergy, 32*, 256–263.
  - Parental conflict and separation increased asthma onset risk.

### Exercise and Stress Buffering

- **Puterman, E., Lin, J., Blackburn, E., O'Donovan, A., Adler, N., & Epel, E. (2010).** "The power of exercise: Buffering the effect of chronic stress on telomere length." *PLoS ONE, 5*(5), e10837.
  - Exercise prevented telomere shortening in chronically stressed individuals.

- **Head, D., Singh, T., & Bugg, J. M. (2012).** "The moderating role of exercise on stress-related effects on the hippocampus and memory in later adulthood." *Neuropsychology, 26*, 133–143.
  - Exercise in older adults minimized stress-related damage to the hippocampus.

## Etymological Notes

- **Stress** (noun/verb): From Latin *strictus*, "drawn tight; constricted." Originally used in physics to describe forces acting on material. Walter Cannon and Hans Selye borrowed the term for physiology. Now ubiquitous in popular discourse.

- **Cortisol**: From *cortex* (Latin, "rind" or "bark"), referring to the outer layer of the adrenal glands where it is produced. *-ol* is the suffix for steroid hormones.

- **Immune** (adjective): From Latin *immunis*, "exempt; freed from charges." The immune system is named for its function of exempting the body from infection.

- **Sympathetic** (nervous system): From Greek *sympatheia*, "fellow feeling." The naming reflects early (now outdated) theories about how the nervous system "felt" with different body systems.

- **Psychoneuroimmunology**: Coined in 1981 as a term unifying three disciplines: psychology (mind), neurology (nervous system), and immunology (immune function). Reflects the paradigm shift away from mind-body dualism.

## Trade-offs Explored in Depth

### Acute Stress as Protective vs. Harmful

**When useful:** Moderate stress enhances memory, improves focus, increases performance on tasks requiring attention and quick thinking. The alarm stage of Selye's model activates useful systems.

**When harmful:** Sustained alarm stage or repeated cycling through the stages without recovery. The body's tissue and organs experience cumulative wear.

**The research question:** Is there an optimal level of stress for each person and task? Some research suggests a "sweet spot" (the Yerkes-Dodson law) where moderate stress enhances performance, but the details vary by individual, task type, and baseline state.

### Problem-focused vs. Emotion-focused Coping

**Problem-focused works when:** The stressor is controllable, the person has resources to address it, and action is feasible.

**Problem-focused fails when:** The stressor is uncontrollable, action is unavailable, or the person believes they lack capacity.

**Emotion-focused works when:** The stressor is uncontrollable or unchangeable (death, past events, unchangeable circumstances).

**Emotion-focused fails when:** Applied to controllable problems, allowing fixable issues to worsen while only managing the emotional response.

**The practical implication:** Flexibility matters. People who can accurately assess controllability and shift strategies accordingly have better outcomes.

### Social Support as Protective and Resource-Dependent

**The protective mechanism:** Physiological (immune boost, blood pressure reduction), behavioral (encouragement of healthy choices), and psychological (sense of belonging, validation, perspective).

**The access problem:** Not equally available. People in poverty, isolation, abusive relationships, or marginalized groups often lack adequate support. Loneliness is itself a stressor that creates vulnerability.

**The quality issue:** Weak ties can be protective; weak AND negative ties are harmful. Family relationships that involve conflict can increase rather than decrease stress.

## Common Errors in Interpreting the Research

1. **Treating correlation as causation:** The studies showing depression predicts heart disease are correlational. Does depression cause heart disease, or does heart disease cause depression, or do both emerge from common causes? The evidence points to bidirectional effects and common mechanisms, but the causal arrow is not settled. Interventions treating depression do improve cardiovascular outcomes, suggesting some causal effect.

2. **Overgeneralizing from specific populations:** Many stress and immune studies use healthy young adults. Stress effects may differ in older adults, immunocompromised individuals, or people with chronic illness. The generality of findings needs testing.

3. **Assuming individual differences mean stress management works equally for all:** Stress is a risk factor, not a destiny. Some people are more resilient, some have more resources. Individual coping is important but cannot overcome structural inequalities (poverty, discrimination, unsafe environments).

4. **The "just relax" fallacy:** Some stress requires action, not relaxation. Telling someone in an abusive relationship or unsafe situation to "manage their stress" through breathing exercises misses the point. The stressor must be addressed.

## Extensions and Adjacent Topics

- **Burnout (Chapter 13)**: Burnout is chronic job stress that depletes coping resources. It illustrates the exhaustion stage of Selye's model in modern workplace context.

- **PTSD (Chapter 15)**: Post-traumatic stress disorder shows what happens when acute stress exposure is so severe or unpredictable that normal coping and recovery mechanisms fail.

- **Depression and Anxiety (Chapter 15)**: These disorders often emerge from or are exacerbated by chronic stress and inadequate coping resources. The stress-disorder connection is bidirectional.

- **Sleep and Health**: Chronic stress disrupts sleep; sleep deprivation increases stress reactivity. A feedback loop amplifies both.

- **Discrimination and Health Disparities**: Perceived discrimination is a chronic stressor that contributes to cardiovascular disease, hypertension, and mental health problems, particularly in marginalized groups.

## Questions for Deeper Exploration

1. **Mechanism specificity:** Why does stress affect the cardiovascular system more severely than, say, the musculoskeletal system in most people? Is this genetic, developmental, or based on prior disease?

2. **Individual differences in stress response:** What determines stress resilience? Genetics clearly matter, but so do childhood experience, current resources, and specific coping skills. How much of the difference is modifiable?

3. **Cultural variation:** Do cultures that emphasize social interdependence show different stress responses and health outcomes than individualistic cultures? The limited cross-cultural research suggests yes, but the mechanism is unclear.

4. **Telomeres and aging:** Does stress-induced telomere shortening directly cause disease, or is it a marker of general cellular damage from which disease follows? The causality matters for intervention.

5. **Prevention vs. intervention:** Which is more effective — preventing chronic stress through social change, or building individual coping skills to manage stress that cannot be prevented? Likely both are needed, but the balance is unknown.
