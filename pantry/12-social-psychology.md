## Key empirical findings

| Phenomenon | Key finding | Strength of evidence |
|------------|------------|----------------------|
| Fundamental attribution error | 75%+ of observers blame questioner's intelligence, not structural advantage | Strong; replicated cross-culturally |
| Asch conformity | 76% conform at least once; conformity increases up to 7-person majority; one dissenter drops conformity near zero | Very strong; >70 years of replications |
| Milgram obedience | 65% administer maximum shock; varies with proximity and authority gradient | Strong; replicated in multiple countries (US, Poland, France) with similar rates |
| Cognitive dissonance | Low-pay condition ($1) leads to greater attitude shift than high-pay condition ($20) | Strong; consistent across variations |
| Self-fulfilling prophecy | Teacher expectations of student ability predict end-of-year achievement independent of initial IQ | Strong; demonstrated in field and lab |
| Implicit Association Test | Most subjects show automatic associations inconsistent with explicit egalitarian beliefs | Moderate; controversial regarding what IAT actually measures |
| Racial profiling | Black drivers searched 3–4x more frequently than White drivers; hit rates lower | Strong; documented across jurisdictions |

## Conceptual trade-offs explored

1. **Attribution: Speed vs. Accuracy** — We make fast judgments about others (sacrificing accuracy) using dispositional rather than situational explanations.

2. **Dissonance: Comfort vs. Truth** — We reduce discomfort by rationalizing or changing beliefs, sometimes moving away from truth.

3. **Conformity: Cohesion vs. Critical Thinking** — Groups need shared norms to function, but conformity suppresses the dissent that leads to good decisions.

4. **Prejudice: Categorization vs. Individuation** — We must categorize people to navigate social reality, but categorization overlooks individuality.

## Methodological notes

### What Milgram's study does and does not show
- **Does show:** Ordinary people will obey authority to the point of administering what they believe is serious harm.
- **Does not show:** Subjects were unaware the behavior was wrong or evil. Many were distressed and conflicted.
- **Limitation:** Ethical cost was real. Study could not be conducted today under modern IRB standards.

### What the Stanford Prison Experiment was claimed to show vs. what it actually shows
- **Claimed:** Ordinary people become sadistic when placed in a role; situational factors override personality.
- **Actually shows:** In a situation with little structure and some coaching from the experimenter, some participants exhibited abusive behavior.
- **Problems:** Guard behavior was encouraged by Zimbardo's directives; self-selection of participants; demand characteristics; failure to replicate without coaching.

### The Kitty Genovese myth vs. the historical record
- **Myth:** 38 witnesses heard screams; none called police; paradigm case of bystander effect.
- **Fact:** Fewer neighbors actually heard the attack; several intervened (one shouted, another called police, a friend comforted her); killer was apprehended later due to police tip from a neighbor.
- **Lesson:** Myth is pedagogically powerful but historically inaccurate. Revised account teaches something different: help may be insufficient, not absent.

## Etymology & language precision

- **Attribution:** *attribuere* (Latin) = to assign to, allocate. An attribution is an explanation assigned to behavior.
- **Conformity:** *conformare* (Latin) = to shape along with. Conformity = taking the shape of the group.
- **Prejudice:** *praejudicium* (Latin) = judgment before the facts. *Prae* (before) + *judicium* (judgment).
- **Dissonance:** *dissonare* (Latin) = to sound discordant. Cognitive dissonance = mental discord between belief and behavior.

Precise terminology matters because it shapes thought. "Stereotype," "prejudice," and "discrimination" are often used interchangeably in casual speech, but they are distinct: stereotype is belief, prejudice is feeling, discrimination is action. Using them precisely opens up the question of whether you can hold one without the others.

## Open questions & edges of understanding

1. **Why does one dissenter have such power?** The mechanism by which a single voice breaks unanimity remains incompletely understood. Is it signaling of possible alternatives? Loss of consensus illusion? Something else?

2. **What is the actual prevalence of implicit bias?** The Implicit Association Test is controversial. Some argue it measures mere familiarity, not bias. The relationship between IAT scores and discriminatory behavior is weaker than intuition suggests.

3. **How much of Milgram's effect persists because of graduated commitment vs. authority gradient?** The various conditions show that both matter, but the relative weight is unclear.

4. **Why do some people and organizations resist groupthink better than others?** Psychological safety, institutional culture, and leadership matter, but the exact recipe is not yet clear.

5. **Can prejudice be permanently reduced, or does it resurge when contexts change?** Contact-based interventions show promise, but long-term sustainability and generalization to new outgroups are uncertain.
