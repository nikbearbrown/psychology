# Chapter 10 — Emotion and Motivation: Pantry (Definitions & Reference)

## Core Terms — Emotion

**Emotion** — a subjective state of feeling that results from the convergence of physiological arousal, cognitive appraisal, and social context. Emotions are typically brief, intense, and intentional (directed at something).

**Amygdala** — a brain structure in the limbic system that processes emotional information, particularly fear and threat detection. The amygdala can activate emotion responses rapidly, often before conscious awareness.

**Autonomic nervous system** — the branch of the nervous system that controls involuntary functions. The sympathetic division produces arousal (fight-or-flight); the parasympathetic division produces calm (rest-and-digest).

**Limbic system** — a network of brain structures involved in emotion, memory, and motivation, including the amygdala, hippocampus, hypothalamus, and thalamus.

**Appraisal** — the process of evaluating a situation's meaning and relevance to your wellbeing. Appraisal determines emotional response; different appraisals of the same situation produce different emotions.

**Emotion regulation** — the process of managing emotional experiences and expressions. Reappraisal (changing how you think about a situation) is more effective than suppression (hiding the expression).

**Facial feedback hypothesis** — the principle that your facial expression can influence your emotional experience. Smiling can increase happiness; frowning can increase sadness.

**Display rules** — culturally specific norms about which emotions are acceptable to display, when, and to whom. Display rules explain why people from different cultures show different emotional expressions even when feeling the same emotion.

**Mood** — a prolonged, less intense affective state that does not occur in response to a specific trigger. Moods are often not consciously recognized.

## Major Theories of Emotion

**James-Lange theory** — proposes that emotion arises from physiological arousal. You feel afraid *because* your heart is racing, not the other way around. The body's response comes first; the feeling follows.

**Cannon-Bard theory** — proposes that physiological arousal and emotional experience occur simultaneously and independently. You feel fear and your heart races at the same moment, but the two are separate processes.

**Schachter-Singer two-factor theory** — proposes that emotion requires two factors: physiological arousal and a cognitive interpretation of what that arousal means. Identical arousal in different contexts produces different emotions.

**Appraisal theory (Lazarus, Arnold)** — proposes that your cognitive appraisal of a situation precedes and produces the emotional response. How you evaluate a situation determines what you feel.

**Constructed emotion theory (Barrett)** — proposes that emotions are not built into the brain but constructed based on predictions and past experience. The same physiological state can produce different emotions depending on context and prediction.

## Core Terms — Motivation & Homeostasis

**Motivation** — wants or needs that direct behavior toward a goal.

**Homeostasis** — the tendency of a system to maintain balance or return to a set point when pushed away from it.

**Set-point theory** — proposes that each individual has an ideal body weight, genetically determined, that the body defends through changes in hunger and metabolism. Evidence suggests set points are more flexible than the theory proposes.

**Ghrelin** — a hormone produced by the stomach that signals hunger when the stomach is empty. Ghrelin increases appetite.

**Leptin** — a hormone produced by fat cells that signals fullness to the brain. High leptin signals *do not eat*; low leptin signals *eat*.

**Leptin resistance** — a state in which fat cells produce leptin but the brain stops responding to it, usually due to chronic overeating or obesity.

**Hypothalamus** — a brain structure involved in regulating hunger, thirst, body temperature, sex, and other homeostatic functions.

## Eating-Related Terms

**Satiation** — the feeling of fullness and satisfaction that leads to cessation of eating.

**Satiety** — the state of feeling full and satisfied after eating.

**Anorexia nervosa** — an eating disorder characterized by severe restriction of food intake, often to the point of starvation, and distorted body image.

**Bulimia nervosa** — an eating disorder characterized by cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors (vomiting, laxative abuse, excessive exercise).

**Binge eating disorder** — an eating disorder characterized by episodes of eating large quantities followed by distress, without compensatory behaviors.

**Body mass index (BMI)** — a measure of weight relative to height (weight in kg / height in m²). A crude measure that does not distinguish fat from muscle or account for variation across ethnicities.

**Obesity** — a state of being significantly overweight, typically defined as BMI ≥ 30.

**Bariatric surgery** — surgical procedures that modify the gastrointestinal system to reduce food intake or absorption, used for severe obesity.

## Sexual Motivation & Orientation

**Sexual motivation** — the drive to engage in sexual behavior, rooted in the hypothalamus and influenced by hormones (particularly testosterone).

**Testosterone** — a hormone produced in testes and (in smaller amounts) ovaries, involved in sexual motivation and behavior across genders.

**Sexual orientation** — the pattern of emotional, romantic, and sexual attraction to people of specific genders or sexes. Categories include heterosexual, lesbian, gay, bisexual, asexual, and others.

**Conversion therapy** — attempted use of psychological, behavioral, or biomedical interventions to change sexual orientation. Demonstrated to be ineffective and harmful.

**Gender identity** — one's sense of being male, female, neither, both, or another gender. Gender identity may or may not match the sex assigned at birth.

**Transgender** — a person whose gender identity differs from the sex assigned at birth.

**Cisgender** — a person whose gender identity matches the sex assigned at birth.

**Intersex** — a person whose sex traits, reproductive anatomy, hormones, or chromosomes are different from the typical male or female pattern.

## Achievement & Motivation

**Achievement motivation** — the drive to accomplish challenging goals, overcome obstacles, and perform well.

**Intrinsic motivation** — motivation based on internal satisfaction and enjoyment of the activity itself.

**Extrinsic motivation** — motivation based on external rewards (money, praise, grades) or avoidance of punishment.

**Overjustification effect** — when adding external rewards to an activity that was intrinsically motivating reduces intrinsic motivation by changing how you think about why you do the activity.

**Fixed mindset** — the belief that ability is fixed and unchangeable. When facing failure, fixed-mindset individuals often avoid challenges.

**Growth mindset** — the belief that ability is developed through effort. When facing failure, growth-mindset individuals see it as temporary and continue to pursue challenges.

**Self-efficacy** — your belief in your own ability to complete a task or succeed in a domain. High self-efficacy is associated with persistence and achievement.

## Maslow & Self-Determination

**Maslow's hierarchy of needs** — a framework proposing that human needs are organized in a hierarchy from physiological needs (food, water, sleep) through safety, belonging, esteem, and self-actualization (realizing full potential). Evidence does not support strict ordering.

**Self-actualization** — the fulfillment of your potential and the realization of your unique capabilities. Maslow proposed this as the top of the hierarchy.

**Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan)** — proposes that people are motivated by three basic needs: autonomy (feeling you are acting by choice), competence (feeling you can succeed), and relatedness (feeling connected to others).

**Need for affiliation** — the motivation to maintain positive relationships and be part of a group.

**Need for power** — the motivation to influence or control others and situations.

## Research Methods & Concepts

**Thematic apperception test (TAT)** — a projective test in which people write stories about ambiguous images; the themes in the stories are analyzed to infer psychological characteristics like achievement motivation.

**Twin study** — a research method comparing identical twins (100% genetic similarity) to fraternal twins (50% genetic similarity) to estimate heritability.

**Polygenetic** — involving many genes, each with small effects. Sexual orientation, like height and intelligence, appears to be polygenetic rather than determined by a single gene.

**Endocrine system** — the system of glands that produce hormones. The hypothalamus coordinates the endocrine system and thus regulates motivation and emotion.

**Hypogonadism** — reduced function of the testes or ovaries, leading to low hormone production and reduced sexual function.

## Common Errors & Clarifications

**Error:** Emotions are purely biological.  
**Clarity:** Emotions require biology (arousal) + cognition (appraisal) + context (culture). All three are necessary.

**Error:** You can read emotion accurately from facial expression alone.  
**Clarity:** Facial expressions are informative but not diagnostic. Display rules, individual differences, and context all affect what expression means.

**Error:** Arousal determines emotion type.  
**Clarity:** Arousal is necessary but not sufficient. The same arousal in different contexts produces different emotions (Schachter-Singer).

**Error:** Eating disorders are a choice or a sign of vanity.  
**Clarity:** Eating disorders involve brain changes, psychological vulnerabilities, and cultural pressures. They are disorders, not character flaws.

**Error:** Sexual orientation is a choice and can be changed.  
**Clarity:** Sexual orientation is determined early, is stable, and has biological foundations. Conversion therapy is ineffective and harmful.

**Error:** Maslow's hierarchy is law—lower needs must be satisfied before higher ones matter.  
**Clarity:** People pursue meaning and connection even when basic needs are not fully met. The hierarchy is not strictly ordered.

**Error:** Extrinsic rewards always reduce intrinsic motivation.  
**Clarity:** Unexpected rewards, intangible rewards, and rewards for tasks that are not intrinsically motivating may not reduce intrinsic motivation.
