# Pantry: Chapter 6 — Learning

## Research notes and source material

### Key experiments and findings

**Pavlov's dogs (1927)**
- Pavlov (1927). *Conditioned Reflexes: An Investigation of the Physiological Activity of the Cerebral Cortex*. Oxford University Press. (Original publication; translated and edited by G. V. Anrep.)
- The classic experiment: dogs salivated to the sound of a metronome paired with meat powder. After sufficient pairings, the metronome alone produced salivation.
- Core finding: association allows prediction. The organism learns to predict the UCS from the CS.
- Design implications: timing of CS before UCS is critical (usually 0.5–5 seconds); backward conditioning (UCS before CS) does not produce learning.

**Acquisition, extinction, spontaneous recovery (Pavlov)**
- Acquisition phase: progressive strengthening of the CR with repeated CS-UCS pairings.
- Extinction phase: CS presented repeatedly without UCS; CR weakens and eventually disappears.
- Spontaneous recovery: CR reappears after a rest period, indicating the association is suppressed but not erased.
- Mechanism interpretation: extinction is not erasure. New learning (CS no longer predicts UCS) suppresses but doesn't erase old learning.

**Watson and Little Albert (1920)**
- Watson, J. B., & Rayner, R. (1920). "Conditioned emotional reactions." *Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 3, 1–14.
- Albert was a 9-month-old infant without fear of a white rat.
- Procedure: Watson paired the white rat (CS) with a loud noise (UCS) that naturally elicited fear (UCR).
- Result: Albert became conditioned to fear the white rat, and the fear generalized to other furry objects (rabbit, fur coat, Santa mask).
- Ethical note: This experiment would be considered unethical by today's standards. It demonstrated that human emotions could be classically conditioned but raised serious questions about research ethics.
- Later identification: In 2010, DeAngelis revealed that "Little Albert" was Albert B. (later known as William Albert Henry), though details remain disputed. DeAngelis, T. (2010). "Little Albert regains his identity." *Monitor on Psychology*, 41(1), 10.

**Taste aversion and biological constraints (Garcia & Koelling, 1966; Garcia & Rusiniak, 1980)**
- Garcia, J., & Koelling, R. A. (1966). "Relation of cue to consequence in avoidance learning." *Psychonomic Science*, 4, 123–124.
- Garcia, J., & Rusiniak, K. W. (1980). "What the nose learns from the mouth." In D. Müller-Schwarze & R. M. Silverstein (Eds.), *Chemical Signals: Vertebrates and Aquatic Invertebrates* (pp. 141–156). Plenum Press.
- Finding: Rats readily form taste-illness associations but fail to form associations between lights/sounds and illness.
- Implication: Not all associations are equally learnable. Biology constrains what can be classically conditioned. Taste predicts digestive consequences; lights do not. Evolution has prepared the organism to learn certain associations more easily.
- Application: Cancer patients often develop food aversions after chemotherapy. The chemotherapy drugs (UCS) produce nausea (UCR). The food consumed before treatment (CS) becomes associated with illness. This is an example of taste aversion learning in humans.

**Rescorla-Wagner model (1972)**
- Rescorla, R. A., & Wagner, A. R. (1972). "A theory of Pavlovian conditioning: Variations in the effectiveness of reinforcement and nonreinforcement." In *Classical Conditioning II: Current Research and Theory* (Vol. 2, pp. 64–99).
- Mathematical formulation: predicts the strength of learning from the discrepancy between expected and actual outcomes.
- Core insight: Pavlov's dogs learn when the CS reliably predicts the UCS. If the UCS sometimes occurs without the CS, or if the CS sometimes occurs without the UCS, learning is weaker.
- Practical implication: Conditioning is about *prediction*, not just pairing. A stimulus that predicts the UCS reliably (like Ari's dad's 6:00 dinner time) produces stronger conditioned responses than a stimulus that sometimes predicts it (like Soraya's mom's variable dinner time).

**Thorndike's law of effect (1911)**
- Thorndike, E. L. (1911). *Animal Intelligence: An Experimental Study of the Associative Processes in Animals*. Psychological Monographs, 8.
- Method: Thorndike placed hungry cats in "puzzle boxes." Each box had a latch; opening the latch released the cat to food.
- Finding: Initially, cats' behavior was random — scratching, meowing, pacing. Eventually, by chance, a cat would hit the latch, the door would open, and the cat would escape to food. On subsequent trials, the time to escape decreased progressively.
- Insight: Behaviors followed by "satisfying consequences" are more likely to be repeated. This is the foundation of operant conditioning.
- Limitation: "Satisfying" is defined empirically (behavior increases) not a priori. A consequence is satisfying if and only if the behavior it follows increases in frequency.

**Skinner's operant conditioning (1938, 1953)**
- Skinner, B. F. (1938). *The Behavior of Organisms: An Experimental Analysis*. Appleton-Century-Crofts.
- Skinner, B. F. (1953). *Science and Human Behavior*. Macmillan.
- Method: Skinner developed the "Skinner box" — an operant conditioning chamber containing a lever (for rats) or disk (for pigeons) that, when pressed, dispensed food.
- Findings: Rats and pigeons learn to press the lever / peck the disk when the behavior is followed by food. The rate of pressing depends on the reinforcement schedule.
- Terminology precision: Skinner was meticulous about distinguishing reinforcement (behavior increases) from punishment (behavior decreases), and positive (adding) from negative (removing). This terminology is not intuitive, which is why confusion persists.

**Reinforcement schedules (Skinner, 1961)**
- Skinner, B. F. (1961). *Cumulative Record: A Selection of Papers*. Appleton-Century-Crofts.
- Fixed interval: Behavior is reinforced after a set time period. Produces a moderate response rate with pauses after reinforcement (because the organism has learned that reinforcement won't come immediately).
- Variable interval: Behavior is reinforced at unpredictable time intervals. Produces a steady, consistent response rate.
- Fixed ratio: Behavior is reinforced after a set number of responses. Produces a high response rate with brief pauses after reinforcement.
- Variable ratio: Behavior is reinforced after an unpredictable number of responses. Produces the highest and most persistent response rate. Most resistant to extinction.
- Application: Gambling and gambling addiction can be understood as variable ratio reinforcement. The slot machine reinforces pulling the handle on an unpredictable schedule, producing persistent, compulsive behavior.

**Cognitive map and latent learning (Tolman & Honzik, 1930; Tolman, Ritchie, & Kalish, 1946)**
- Tolman, E. C., & Honzik, C. H. (1930). "Degrees of hunger, reward, and non-reward, and maze performance in rats." *University of California Publications in Psychology*, 4, 241–256.
- Tolman, E. C., Ritchie, B. F., & Kalish, D. (1946). "Studies in spatial learning: II. Place learning versus response learning." *Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 36, 221–229.
- Finding: Rats explored a maze without receiving food reward. When food was later introduced at the end of the maze, the previously unrewarded rats performed as well as rats that had been rewarded from the start.
- Interpretation: Learning occurred during the unrewarded exploration — the rats developed a "cognitive map" of the maze — but the learning was not *apparent* in behavior until motivation (hunger) was present. This demonstrated latent learning and suggested that learning involves cognition, not just behavioral change.
- Implication: Operant conditioning alone cannot explain this. Cognition — mental representation — is part of learning.

**Bandura's Bobo doll experiment (1961)**
- Bandura, A., Ross, D., & Ross, S. A. (1961). "Transmission of aggression through imitation of aggressive models." *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, 63, 575–582.
- Method: Children watched an adult (the "model") act aggressively toward a five-foot inflatable doll — hitting, kicking, punching, shouting. In one condition, the adult was then praised and given treats. In another condition, the adult was scolded.
- Finding: Children who saw the model reinforced imitated the aggression. Children who saw the model punished were less likely to imitate immediately. However, when later offered rewards for imitating the aggression, all children could reproduce the aggressive actions.
- Critical insight: Learning and performance are separate. The children learned the aggressive behavior through observation. Whether they *performed* it depended on what they had seen happen to the model (vicarious reinforcement vs. vicarious punishment) and on direct motivation (the reward offer).
- Impact: This experiment demonstrated observational learning and suggested that media violence might increase aggression through modeling, though the relationship is complex and mediated by motivation.

### Real-world applications and extensions

**Classical conditioning in clinical settings**
- Cancer-related nausea: Jacobsen et al. (1993). "Formation of food aversions in cancer patients receiving repeated infusions of chemotherapy." *Behaviour Research and Therapy*, 31(8), 739–748.
- Patients often develop nausea and food aversions conditioned to the hospital or to foods eaten before chemotherapy. This is an unwanted classical conditioned response. Understanding the mechanism allows intervention: eating different foods than usual during treatment, changing the location of treatment when possible.

**Token economies and behavior modification in schools**
- Adibsereshki, N., Abkenar, S. J., Ashoori, M., & Mirzamani, M. (2015). "The effectiveness of using reinforcements in the classroom on the academic achievement of students with intellectual disabilities." *Journal of Intellectual Disabilities*, 19(1), 83–93.
- Cangi, K., & Daly, M. (2013). "The effects of token economies on the occurrence of appropriate and inappropriate behaviors by children with autism in a social skills setting." *West Chester University: Journal of Undergraduate Research*.
- Token economies apply operant conditioning principles in classroom settings. Students earn tokens (secondary reinforcers) for appropriate behavior and can exchange tokens for privileges. Effectiveness is documented for increasing on-task behavior and reducing disruptive behavior.

**Negative effects of physical punishment**
- Gershoff, E. T. (2002). "Corporal punishment by parents and associated child behaviors and experiences: A meta-analytic and theoretical review." *Psychological Bulletin*, 128(4), 539–579.
- Gershoff, E. T., Grogan-Kaylor, A., Lansford, J. E., Chang, L., Zelli, A., Deater-Deckard, K., & Dodge, K. A. (2010). "Parent discipline practices in an international sample: Associations with child behaviors and moderation by perceived normativeness." *Child Development*, 81(2), 487–502.
- Physical punishment (positive punishment in operant terms) is associated with increased aggression, anxiety, and externalizing behavior. It may suppress the target behavior in the moment but produces side effects: fear of the punisher, avoidance of the setting, and modeling of violence as a problem-solving strategy.
- Implication: Positive reinforcement of desired alternative behaviors is more effective and produces fewer side effects.

**Media violence and aggression**
- Anderson, C. A., & Gentile, D. A. (2008). "Media violence, aggression, and public policy." In E. Borgida & S. Fiske (Eds.), *Beyond Common Sense: Psychological Science in the Courtroom* (pp. 307–325). Blackwell.
- Anderson, C. A., Bushman, B. J., Donnerstein, E., Hummer, T. A., & Warburton, W. (2015). "SPSSI research summary on media violence." *Analyses of Social Issues and Public Policy* (ASAP), 15(1), 4–19.
- Ferguson, C. J. (2011). "Video games and youth violence: A prospective analysis in adolescents." *Journal of Youth and Adolescence*, 40(4), 377–391.
- The evidence is mixed. Some longitudinal studies find a small correlation between violent media exposure and aggressive behavior, particularly in younger children. Other studies (Ferguson) find the effect is smaller than often claimed and may be mediated by family factors and personality traits. The mechanism is observational learning, but the effect size and causal direction remain debated.

**Gambling addiction and neural substrates**
- Chase, H. W., & Clark, L. (2010). "Gambling severity predicts midbrain response to near-miss outcomes." *The Journal of Neuroscience*, 30(18), 6180–6187.
- Laskowski, C. S., Dorchak, D. L., Ward, K. M., Christensen, D. R., & Euston, D. R. (2019). "Can slot-machine reward schedules induce gambling addiction in rats?" *Journal of Gambling Studies*, 35(3), 887–914.
- Murch, W. S., & Clark, L. (2016). "Games in the brain: Neural substrates of gambling addiction." *The Neuroscientist*, 22(5), 534–545.
- Potenza, M. N. (2013). "Neurobiology of gambling behaviors." *Current Opinion in Neurobiology*, 23(4), 660–667.
- Gambling activates dopamine reward centers. Near-misses (almost winning) activate these centers almost as much as actual wins. Variable ratio reinforcement schedules produce the most persistent behavior. The dopamine system responds to prediction error, making the uncertainty itself rewarding. This is why slot machines are designed to show many near-misses.
- Recent research suggests that variable ratio schedules alone do not cause pathological gambling in rats (Laskowski et al., 2019), but combined with certain brain chemistry or susceptibilities, they can. The relationship is complex and involves both the schedule and individual differences.

**Modeling in therapy and education**
- Tolman's work on cognitive maps suggests that learning is not purely behavioral. Students benefit from seeing the problem-solving process, not just the answer.
- Bandura's modeling suggests that observational learning is powerful when the model is respected, when the behavior is shown to have positive consequences, and when the observer believes they can reproduce the behavior (self-efficacy).

### Biological constraints on learning

**Preparedness and constraints**
- Garcia and Koelling's taste aversion work shows that some associations are more easily learned than others. Organisms are "prepared" to learn certain associations based on evolutionary history.
- Taste-illness associations are learned very easily, sometimes from a single pairing and even with long delays between taste and illness.
- Sight-sound/taste associations are nearly impossible to learn.
- This suggests that classical conditioning is not a general-purpose learning mechanism but has evolved to serve specific functions.

## Distinctions and clarifications

### Classical conditioning vs. operant conditioning

| Dimension | Classical | Operant |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Reflex involved? | Yes — redirects an existing reflex | No — shapes new voluntary behavior |
| Stimulus timing | CS before UCS (predictive) | Response before consequence (reactive) |
| Type of learning | Anticipation and prediction | Motivation and behavior frequency |
| What changes? | Trigger for the reflex | Likelihood of the behavior |
| Reflex involved | Involuntary response | Voluntary behavior |

### Reinforcement vs. punishment (operant conditioning)

| Term | Definition | Effect | Example |
|------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Positive reinforcement | Add something desirable | Behavior increases | Ice cream after cleaning room → more room cleaning |
| Negative reinforcement | Remove something undesirable | Behavior increases | Seatbelt beeping stops when buckled → more buckling |
| Positive punishment | Add something undesirable | Behavior decreases | Scolding for running into street → less street running |
| Negative punishment | Remove something desirable | Behavior decreases | Phone taken away for disrespect → less disrespect |

**Key insight**: Reinforcement always *increases* behavior. Punishment always *decreases* it. The positive/negative distinction refers to whether something is added (positive) or removed (negative), not to whether the consequence is "good" or "bad."

### Continuous vs. partial reinforcement schedules

| Schedule | Timing | Effect | Durability | Example |
|----------|--------|--------|-----------|---------|
| Continuous | Every response | Fastest learning | Rapid extinction when stopped | Training a new dog behavior |
| Fixed interval | Set time elapsed | Moderate rate, pause after | Moderate durability | Paycheck every 2 weeks |
| Variable interval | Unpredictable time | Steady rate | Good durability | Checking email |
| Fixed ratio | Set number of responses | High rate, pause after | Moderate durability | Piecework commission |
| Variable ratio | Unpredictable number | Very high, steady rate | Most durable | Slot machines |

## Common teaching challenges

### Why students think operant conditioning is "bribery"

Students often object that "using reinforcement is just bribing the kid to do what you want." The confusion is that bribery explicitly offers the reward *before* the behavior to induce it: "I'll give you $5 if you clean your room." Operant reinforcement provides the consequence *after* the behavior. The organism behaves, and the consequence adjusts future frequency. The precision matters because it tells you where the learning is happening — not in the promise but in the experienced relationship between action and outcome.

### Why students confuse negative reinforcement with punishment

"Negative" sounds bad, so students think negative reinforcement is punishment. But negative reinforcement *increases* behavior (the reinforcement part), while punishment *decreases* it. A parent who removes grounding when the teenager starts going to school on time is using negative reinforcement (removing something aversive increases the behavior). This should be taught with clear contrasts and multiple examples.

### Why observational learning seems obvious but isn't

Students see "we learn by watching" and think there's nothing more to it. The power of Bandura's work is showing that watching and imitating are separate. The four steps (attention, retention, reproduction, motivation) reveal why some models are imitated and others aren't. A common misconception is that exposure to a model automatically produces imitation. But if the model is punished, or if the observer lacks the ability to reproduce the behavior, imitation won't follow.

## Worked example: Integrating mechanisms in a real case

**Case: A person trying to quit smoking after quitting for two years**

A person quit smoking two years ago and has been smoke-free. Encounters an old friend who smokes. Suddenly feels an intense urge to smoke. Why?

*Classical conditioning:* The cues associated with smoking — the friend, the setting, the time of day — may trigger a conditioned response (the urge) through pairing with past nicotine use. The UCS was nicotine (produced a "feel-good" response); the CS became the friend/setting. Even though the person hasn't smoked in two years, the association is still encoded.

*Operant conditioning:* Smoking was reinforced by the nicotine's effects. That reinforcement history is still present. If the person gives in and smokes, the behavior is immediately reinforced (nicotine's effects return), which would restart the behavior pattern.

*Observational learning:* Seeing the friend smoke is modeling the behavior. The friend is a model. What happened to the model (presumably the friend is still smoking, presumably without visible negative consequences)? That affects motivation to imitate.

*Interventions based on each mechanism:*
- To target classical conditioning: Use extinction (be around the friend without smoking repeatedly) or substitute a new response (bring gum, exercise).
- To target operant conditioning: Use reinforcement for not smoking (reward milestones), or remove access to cigarettes.
- To target observational learning: Spend less time with modeling figures; observe successful quitters instead.
- Most effective: Use all three, because all three are likely operating.

## Extension questions for deeper exploration

1. **Why does biological preparedness matter?** If an organism can be classically conditioned to almost anything, why do we see constraints like taste aversion? What evolutionary logic does this reveal?

2. **Why is variable ratio so powerful?** At a neural level, what makes unpredictable reinforcement produce more durable behavior than predictable reinforcement? (This involves dopamine and prediction error.)

3. **Can you teach morality through operant conditioning?** If you reinforce kind behavior, are you teaching kindness or teaching the child to perform kindness for reward? Does the distinction matter?

4. **Is observational learning fundamentally different from classical and operant conditioning?** Could it be a combination of classical conditioning (emotional responses to models) and operant conditioning (motivated by vicarious consequences)?

5. **What are the limits of these mechanisms for complex learning?** Can you learn calculus through classical conditioning? Operant conditioning? Observational learning alone? What does this tell you about the boundaries of these mechanisms?
