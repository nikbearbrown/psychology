# Pantry: Chapter 13 — Industrial-Organizational Psychology

## Source extraction and evidence log

### Key claims and their primary sources

**Hawthorne effect — observation changes behavior**
- Source: Elton Mayo et al., 1924–1932, Western Electric Hawthorne Works study
- Data: Roethlisberger & Dickson (1939), *Management and the Worker*
- Finding: Output increased regardless of lighting change (up or down) when workers knew they were being observed; improvement faded when observation stopped
- Caveat: Reanalysis suggests magnitude of effect was smaller than originally reported

**Job analysis — specification of job requirements**
- Source: U.S. Department of Labor, O*Net database (onetonline.org)
- Content: Tasks, knowledge, skills, abilities, work context, education, personality requirements for nearly every occupation

**Predictive validity — measuring whether assessment tools predict job success**
- Source: Meta-analyses in personnel psychology (Barrick, Mount, Schmidt, Hunter)
- Typical ranges: Structured interviews r = 0.37–0.50; cognitive ability r = 0.30–0.50; work simulations r = 0.37–0.50

**Personality testing — Big Five vs. MBTI**
- Big Five: Predictive validity r = 0.25–0.40 depending on job type
- MBTI: No empirical evidence of predictive validity for job performance

**Herzberg's two-factor theory**
- Source: Herzberg, Mausner, & Snyderman (1959), *The Motivation to Work*
- Hygiene factors (absent = dissatisfaction): pay, working conditions, supervision
- Motivational factors (absent = no engagement): variety, challenge, autonomy, growth

**Expectancy theory**
- Source: Vroom (1964), *Work and Motivation*
- Formula: Motivation = Expectancy × Valence × Instrumentality

**Job satisfaction and job performance**
- Source: Judge et al. (2001), meta-analysis
- Finding: r ≈ 0.30 (modest correlation)
- Pay and satisfaction: Weak correlation; people adapt to pay increases

**Lewin's leadership styles research**
- Source: Lewin, Lippitt, & White (1939)
- Democratic leadership: Engaged groups, low aggression regardless of leader presence
- Authoritarian: Low aggression when leader present; high when absent

**Psychological safety**
- Source: Edmondson (1999); Edmondson & Lei (2014)
- Predicts: Team performance, innovation, error detection

**Organizational culture and performance**
- Finding: Culture correlates with organizational performance, innovation, retention
- Bidirectional causality: Unclear whether culture causes performance or vice versa

**Remote work outcomes (Social Security)**
- Source: Documented in official communications
- Measurement: Wait times increased; backlogs increased
- Countervailing factors: Motivation, commitment, ergonomics, trust

## Limitations in evidence base

- Most research on Western, wealthy organizations (U.S., Western Europe)
- Sample bias toward employed
- Cultural context may not generalize
- Publication bias: Successful interventions published more than null results
- Causal uncertainties: satisfaction ↔ performance; culture ↔ performance; leadership ↔ team cohesion
