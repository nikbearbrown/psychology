## Concept specifications

### Concept 1: What development is, and how we measure it

**Key terms to specify:**

- **Development** — change in human behavior, cognition, and physiology that can be documented and explained, not predetermined or narrative-bound. Change + measurement + explanation.
- **Three domains** — physical (growth, motor skills, nervous system maturation), cognitive (thinking, reasoning, learning), social-emotional (relationships, emotion, self-understanding).
- **Normative approach** — charting average ages for milestones (Gesell, Hall early 1900s). Useful for identifying outliers. Dangerous when *normal* is read as moral judgment. Milestones are culturally embedded, not universal except for biological maturation.
- **Continuous vs. discontinuous** — height grows continuously; object permanence appears discontinuously. Most development mixes both.
- **One course or many** — universal patterns (all children language, all attach) vs. culture-specific paths (how independence is fostered, emotional expression norms). Both exist.
- **Nature vs. nurture** — false binary. Better: how genetic predispositions and environmental input *interact* to shape outcomes.

**Primary sources:**

- Moffitt et al. (2011). A gradient of childhood self-control predicts health, wealth, and public safety. *PNAS*, 108(7), 2693–2698. [DOI: 10.1073/pnas.1010076108](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1010076108) — Dunedin Study flagship result.
- Karasik et al. (2010). WEIRD Walking: Cross-cultural research on motor development. *Behavioral & Brain Sciences*, 33(2-3), 95–96. — Aché motor development example.
- Gesell, A. (1933). Maturation and the patterning of behavior. *Handbook of Child Psychology*, 2nd ed. — Normative approach pioneer.
- Hart & Risley (2003). The early catastrophe: The 30 million word gap. *American Educator*, 27(1), 4–9. — language input disparities as environmental mechanism.

**Trade-offs explained in text:**

- Cross-sectional (fast) vs. longitudinal (true tracking, but attrition) vs. adoption (natural experiment, but non-representative sample).

**Debunked myths:**

- Infantile amnesia is not repression; it is hippocampal immaturity.
- "Terrible twos" is culture-specific, not inevitable.
- Personality at age five predicts but does not determine adult personality.

**Deep-dive mechanism:** The Dunedin Study correlation between childhood self-control and adult health/income/crime — three pathways: direct (learning → education → income), social (peer relationships → support), biological (stress regulation → immune/inflammatory health). Show the three pathways operate in parallel.

### Concept 2: Attachment and early relationships

**Key terms to specify:**

- **Attachment** — biological bond between infant and caregiver, serving survival/protection function, measurable by behavioral patterns in reunion and separation.
- **Secure attachment** — infant uses caregiver as secure base; explores freely; distressed at separation; soothed by reunion. Implies sensitive, responsive caregiving.
- **Anxious-resistant** — clingy; not much exploration; very distressed at separation; ambivalent/resistant at reunion. Implies inconsistent caregiving.
- **Avoidant** — explores freely; not distressed at separation; ignores caregiver at reunion. Implies emotionally unavailable caregiving.
- **Disorganized** — contradictory behaviors, fear/approach conflict, freezing. Implies caregiver as both comfort and threat (abuse, neglect, parental mental illness).
- **Strange Situation** — Ainsworth's twenty-minute observational procedure measuring attachment quality through reunion/separation patterns (not isolated behaviors).

**Primary sources:**

- Ainsworth, M. D. S., & Bell, S. M. (1970). Attachment, exploration, and separation: Illustrated by the behavior of one-year-olds in a strange situation. *Child Development*, 41, 49–67. — Gold standard methodology.
- Ainsworth et al. (1978). *Patterns of Attachment: A Psychological Study of the Strange Situation*. Erlbaum. — Full taxonomy.
- Bowlby, J. (1969). *Attachment and Loss: Attachment* (Vol. 1). Basic Books. — Theoretical foundation.
- Main, M., & Solomon, J. (1990). Procedures for identifying infants as disorganized/disoriented during the Ainsworth Strange Situation. In *Attachment in the Preschool Years*. University of Chicago Press. — Disorganized attachment identification.
- Harlow, H. (1958). The nature of love. *American Psychologist*, 13, 673–685. — Contact comfort in monkeys; overturned feeding-as-attachment theory.

**Cultural variation:**

- van Ijzendoorn & Sagi-Schwartz (2008). Cross-cultural patterns of attachment: Universal and contextual dimensions. In *Handbook of Attachment*. — Secure attachment is normative across cultures, but distributions and behavioral norms vary. Japanese infants higher avoidant/anxious because of separation norms, not maternal responsiveness.
- Rothbaum et al. (2000). Attachment and culture: Security in the United States and Japan. *American Psychologist*, 55, 1093–1104. — US emphasizes autonomy; Japan emphasizes interdependence; same attachment, different cultural expression.

**Outcomes predicted:**

- Secure attachment → better peer relationships, lower behavior problems, better emotion regulation, lower anxiety/depression in childhood and adolescence.
- Insecure attachment → behavior problems, peer difficulties, higher internalizing.
- Disorganized → aggression, internalizing, relationship difficulty.
- Effect sizes are modest but consistent across studies. Attachment is probabilistic, not deterministic.

**Deep-dive mechanism:** How caregiving sensitivity leads to secure attachment. Responsive caregiver → infant learns caregiver is predictable → infant can explore knowing safety-net exists → infant develops trust in self and other. Show the behavioral loop. Contrast with anxious (unpredictable caregiver → infant learns to hypervigilant-signal → clingy-resistant at reunion) and avoidant (unresponsive caregiver → infant learns to self-soothe → dismissive at reunion).

**Debunked myths:**

- Insecure attachment in infancy does not doom a child.
- Working mothers do not inherently create insecure attachment.
- Attachment is about responsiveness and quality of interaction, not constant physical presence.

### Concept 3: Cognitive development and the logic of childhood

**Key terms to specify:**

- **Schema** (plural schemata) — mental model of how the world works. Not learned knowledge but the structure in which knowledge is held.
- **Assimilation** — taking in information that fits existing schema.
- **Accommodation** — revising schema when new information doesn't fit.
- **Object permanence** — understanding that objects exist when out of sight. Develops ~5–8 months.
- **Stranger anxiety** — wariness of unfamiliar people. Emerges ~8 months. *Not* a deficit; indicates child can distinguish familiar from novel.
- **Egocentrism** — inability to take another's perspective. Preoperational child assumes others see/think/feel what she does. Not selfishness; cognitive limitation.
- **Conservation** — understanding that quantity remains constant despite change in appearance (water, number, mass). Preoperational children fail; concrete operational children pass.
- **Reversibility** — understanding that actions can be undone (water poured back = same amount).

**Piaget's stages:**

1. **Sensorimotor (0–2 years):** World through senses and action. Object permanence emerges.
2. **Preoperational (2–7 years):** Symbols used (pretend play, language), but no logical operations. No conservation. Egocentric.
3. **Concrete operational (7–11 years):** Mental operations possible. Can conserve, classify, reverse. But limited to concrete, visible things.
4. **Formal operational (12+ years):** Abstract reasoning. Hypotheticals. Metacognition.

**Primary sources:**

- Piaget, J. (1954). *The Construction of Reality in the Child*. Basic Books. — Foundational text.
- Piaget, J. (1930). *The Child's Conception of the World*. Harcourt, Brace & World. — Egocentric thinking.
- Piaget, J. (1932). *The Moral Judgment of the Child*. Harcourt, Brace & World. — Moral development stages.
- Bogartz, R. S., Shinskey, J. L., & Schilling, T. (2000). Object permanence in five-month-old infants. *Infancy*, 1(4), 403–428. — Challenges Piaget's age estimates using nonverbal methods.
- Baillargeon, R. (1987, 2004). Young infants' reasoning about physical and spatial properties; Infants' reasoning about hidden objects. *Cognitive Development* & *Developmental Science*. — Shows infants younger than Piaget claimed have some object understanding.
- Vygotsky, L. (1978 trans.). *Mind in Society: Development of Higher Psychological Processes*. Harvard University Press. — Social/cultural alternative to Piaget. Zone of proximal development.
- John-Steiner & Mahn (1996). Sociocultural approaches to learning and development: A Vygotskian framework. *Educational Psychologist*, 31, 191–206. — Vygotsky's framework.

**Challenges to Piaget:**

- Stages overlap; no sharp transitions.
- Nonverbal methods show competence earlier than Piaget claimed (object permanence in infants younger than 5–8 months).
- Cross-cultural research: Piaget's stages describe schooled Western children better than non-schooled populations. Vygotsky's social-learning framework fits cross-cultural data better.
- Skills are domain-specific: child might conserve number but not liquid.
- The scaffolding and social context matter more than Piaget's individual-child focus allowed.

**Postformal thought (mentioned briefly):**

- Beyond formal operations: problem-solving integrates emotion with logic; context-dependent reasoning; recognizing multiple valid perspectives.
- Labouvie-Vief & Diehl (1999). Self and personality development. In *Gerontology: An Interdisciplinary Perspective*. — Adult cognitive development.

**Deep-dive mechanism:** Walk through the process of assimilation and accommodation using the dog/sheep example from text. Show how a schema starts underdifferentiated, gets challenged by disconfirming example, requires accommodation to integrate new information. Then show how this *process* recurs throughout development: as cognitive structures get more sophisticated, they accommodate to more fine-grained reality. The mechanism is learning through disequilibrium.

**Debunked myths:**

- Stages are not package deals; skills emerge unevenly.
- Pretend play and egocentrism are not deficits; they are developmentally appropriate.
- Earlier stages are not mistakes to overcome.

## Etymology and definitions

- **Development** — Latin *developare*, to unwrap, unfold.
- **Attachment** — Latin *attache*, to fasten to.
- **Schema** — Greek *schēma*, form, shape.
- **Egocentric** — Latin *ego* (self) + Greek *kentron* (center); literally, centered on self.
- **Conservation** — Latin *conservare*, to keep, preserve.

## Common misconceptions flagged in text

1. Infantile amnesia is repression (wrong: hippocampal immaturity).
2. "Terrible twos" is universal (wrong: culture-specific).
3. Personality locked by age five (wrong: developmental trajectories, not fixedness).
4. Insecure attachment = lifetime problems (wrong: patterns can change).
5. Working mothers = insecure attachment (wrong: responsiveness matters, not presence).
6. Piaget stages are sharp transitions (wrong: overlap and messy).
7. All preoperational children lack all logical operations (wrong: domain-specific).
8. Earlier stages are mistakes (wrong: developmentally appropriate).
