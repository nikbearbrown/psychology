### Key Concepts and Trade-Offs

**Sensory Memory:**
- Trade-off: Vast capacity (complete sensory snapshot) vs. extremely brief duration (< 1 second for vision; up to 10 seconds for auditory).
- Function: Initial filter. Prevents information overload by being discarded unless attended to.

**Working Memory (Short-Term Memory):**
- Trade-off: Conscious accessibility vs. minimal capacity (4±1 chunks per Cowan; 7±2 per Miller).
- Function: Active workspace for problem-solving, reading comprehension, mental calculation.
- Rehearsal moves information from working to long-term; decay erases it after ~30 seconds.

**Long-Term Memory:**
- Trade-off: Virtually unlimited capacity and duration vs. reconstructive (error-prone) nature.
- Not a recording. Encoded, compressed, connected to existing knowledge. Retrieval is reassembly.
- Two types: Explicit (facts, events; conscious; hippocampus-dependent) and Implicit (skills, priming, conditioning; unconscious; cerebellum/basal ganglia-dependent).

**Forgetting:**
1. Encoding failure: Never stored (most preventable).
2. Decay: Trace weakens over time (partially preventable through review).
3. Interference: Old information blocks new (proactive) or new blocks old (retroactive). Preventable through spacing and organization.
4. Distortion: Reconstructed inaccurately. Vulnerable to suggestion, misattribution, bias.

### Mechanism: Three-Stage Model vs. Alternatives

**Atkinson-Shiffrin (1968) vs. Baddeley-Hitch (1974) Working Memory:**

- Atkinson-Shiffrin: Linear flow (sensory → STM → LTM).
- Baddeley-Hitch: Working memory has multiple modalities (phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, episodic buffer) supervised by central executive. Allows feedback from LTM.

**Chapter presents Atkinson-Shiffrin as the foundational model** but mentions Baddeley-Hitch as an alternative that better accounts for the structured nature of working memory. Both are useful; Atkinson-Shiffrin provides clearer conceptual boundaries; Baddeley-Hitch is more architecturally accurate.

### Explicit vs. Implicit Memory

**Explicit (Declarative):**
- Episodic: Events (what, when, where); recollection; autonoetic consciousness; depends on medial temporal lobes (hippocampus).
- Semantic: Facts, concepts, knowledge; reportable; more stable in cortex over time.

**Implicit (Non-Declarative):**
- Procedural: Skills, motor learning, habits; demonstrated in behavior, not reportable; cerebellum, basal ganglia.
- Priming: Facilitation of response to a stimulus by prior exposure; semantic networks; spreading activation.
- Emotional conditioning: Associations between neutral stimulus and emotion; amygdala-mediated; unconscious but influence behavior.

**H.M. and Clive Wearing:**
- Both lost explicit memory formation (especially episodic).
- Both retained implicit memory capacity (procedural learning, priming, emotional responses).
- Demonstrates memory is not monolithic; multiple systems can be damaged independently.

### Forgetting: Ebbinghaus Curve

Ebbinghaus tested himself over intervals from 20 minutes to 30 days after memorizing nonsense syllables.

- 20 minutes: 50% retained.
- 24 hours: 30% retained (~70% lost).
- 30 days: ~25% retained.

Decay is rapid initially, then levels off. This pattern holds for intentional memorization; everyday memory may follow different curves depending on elaboration and rehearsal.

### Memory Distortion: The Misinformation Effect

**Classic Study (Loftus & Palmer, 1974):**
- Participants watch film of car accident.
- Asked: "How fast were the cars going when they [smashed/collided/bumped/hit/contacted] each other?"
- "Smashed" → higher speed estimates.
- One week later: Asked if they saw broken glass (there was none).
- "Smashed" group → 32% reported seeing glass; "contacted" group → 14%.
- Language influenced not just the estimate but the memory itself.

**Why it happens:**
- Memory is reconstructive. Retrieval reassembles fragments.
- New information provided after the event can be incorporated into the memory.
- The reconstruction process does not distinguish between original and new information.
- Confidence and vividness are no guarantee of accuracy.

### Flashbulb Memories

**Original Definition (Brown & Kulik, 1977):**
- Exceptionally vivid, detailed, automatic memories of learning about a major public event.
- Characterized by "photographic" clarity and emotional intensity.

**Contemporary Evidence (Hirst & Phelps, 2016):**
- Flashbulb memories are not more accurate than ordinary memories.
- They feel vivid and detailed, but tested against journal entries made at the time, they show distortions.
- Example: President Bush's three accounts of learning about 9/11 were all inaccurate and contradicted each other.
- Vividness is a false marker of accuracy.

### Eyewitness Testimony and False Conviction

**Jennifer Thompson / Ronald Cotton Case:**
- Thompson was raped in 1984; tried to memorize rapist's face.
- Shown photos; said "I think" but wavering (police told her she was right).
- Lineup; said "looks most like him" (police reinforced choice).
- By trial: absolute certainty. Compelling testimony.
- Ronald Cotton convicted, sentenced to life + 50 years.
- After 11 years, DNA exonerated Cotton. Thompson had misidentified.

**Why it happened:**
- Initial memory was partial and brief.
- Police procedures (showing photos, lineups, feedback) were suggestive.
- Each time Thompson remembered (interviews, trial prep, trial itself), the false version was reinforced.
- Confidence increased due to repeated memory and police confirmation, not due to accuracy.

**Policy implications:**
- Blind photo lineups (administrator doesn't know which is suspect).
- Neutral language from police.
- No feedback on witness choices.
- Recording witnesses' confidence at the time they make the identification, not months later when it has been inflated.

### Recovered Memory Controversy

**The claim:**
- Some therapists in the 1980s-90s used hypnosis, guided imagery, and suggestion to help clients "recover" memories of childhood abuse.
- Many people did recover such memories, with complete conviction.

**The evidence:**
- Loftus, Ceci, Bruck, and others showed that:
  - The techniques used to recover memories are exactly the techniques that create false memories in the lab.
  - Hypnosis does not recover lost memories; it increases suggestibility.
  - Leading questions and suggestion can plant false memories.
  - Example: Ceci & Bruck showed that even young children can be led to "remember" being abused with an anatomically correct doll, even when no abuse occurred.

**The problem:**
- Certainty is not evidence. Someone can remember something with absolute conviction that did not happen.
- The feeling of remembering (vividness, emotion, sensory detail) is a byproduct of memory reconstruction, not a marker of accuracy.

**Current understanding:**
- Some people do forget traumatic memories (dissociation is real).
- But recovered memories obtained through suggestion are unreliable.
- Memory of trauma, like other memory, is reconstructive and vulnerable to distortion.

### Etymology and Language

- **Memory** (Latin *memor* = mindful; *memoria* = remembrance).
- **Hippocampus** (Greek *hippokampos* = sea-horse; named for shape).
- **Mnemonic** (Greek *mneme* = memory).
- **Encoding** (to code, to translate into a form suitable for storage).
- **Consolidation** (from Latin *consolidare* = to make solid; here, to stabilize and integrate information).

### Scale Shift

The chapter attempts a scale shift from the intimate (single synapse, single memory trace) to the cosmic (lifetime of memories, cultural memory across generations):

- One synapse strengthening (LTP) happens in the milliseconds after learning.
- Memory consolidation happens over hours and days.
- A lifetime of memories (H.M.'s 55 years of forgetting) reveals the architecture of systems.
- Collective memory (cultural memory, historical records) shows how societies preserve and distort the past.

The shift is meant to show that memory is not just an individual phenomenon. It scales from cellular biology to personal identity to cultural history.

### Common Misconceptions and Corrections

1. **"Memory is like a video camera."** No. It is reconstructive. Each retrieval reassembles fragments, and the reassembly can be altered.

2. **"If I remember it vividly, it is true."** Vividness is a feeling, not evidence. Vivid false memories are common.

3. **"Hypnosis recovers lost memories."** Hypnosis increases suggestibility and is one of the most effective ways to create false memories.

4. **"Flashbulb memories are accurate."** They feel vivid, but they are no more accurate than ordinary memories and are vulnerable to the same distortions.

5. **"Eyewitness testimony is reliable."** Eyewitness identification and testimony are among the least reliable forms of evidence. Confidence does not correlate with accuracy, especially when the witness has been exposed to suggestive procedures.

6. **"Repressed memories can be recovered in therapy."** Memories obtained through suggestion are likely false. The techniques used to recover them in therapy are the same techniques used to implant false memories in the lab.

### Alternatives Not Fully Explored

- **Connectionist models of memory:** Rather than discrete stages, memory as a network of associations with spreading activation. Mentioned in the context of semantic networks but not deeply explored.

- **Embodied memory:** Memory as inherently tied to the body and sensorimotor systems, not just abstract representation. Implicit memory research touches on this; could be developed further.

- **Memory and emotion:** The amygdala's role in consolidation of emotional memories is briefly mentioned but could be developed. Emotion does strengthen memory (through arousal-induced neurotransmitter release), but emotional memories are also more vulnerable to distortion.

### What Would Change This Chapter

- If evidence emerged that false memories implanted through suggestion are neurobiologically indistinguishable from true memories at the neural level, the chapter's confidence in the ability to distinguish true from false would need to revise. Currently, fMRI research shows differences (false memories show different activation patterns than true memories), but this is an active area of research.

- If the three-stage model were definitively overturned in favor of a different architecture of memory, the chapter would need restructuring. Currently, the model holds up well as a pedagogical tool, even if neuroscientific details require refinement.

### Still Puzzling

- The phenomenology of false memory remains puzzling. Why does the brain generate such convincing subjective experiences of remembering things that did not happen? The mechanism of the feeling (why it feels so real) is not fully understood.

- Why some people are more susceptible to false memory suggestion than others is not well understood. Individual differences in memory suggestibility do not strongly correlate with overall memory ability.

- The exact mechanisms by which emotional arousal strengthens memory consolidation (arousal theory) are not fully worked out. The amygdala is involved, but the precise neural steps remain unclear.

### Pedagogical Architecture

**Three core concepts:**
1. The three-stage model (sensory, working, long-term) with capacity/duration trade-offs.
2. Types of long-term memory (explicit vs. implicit) and how they can be dissociated.
3. Forgetting and distortion as inevitable consequences of how memory actually works.

**Each concept section:**
- Cold open in scene.
- Mechanism from first principles.
- Named trade-off.
- One fully worked example.
- Common misconceptions corrected.

**Exercises:**
- Warm-up: Tests working memory capacity directly (number span task) and chunking effect.
- Application: Distinction between explicit learning (facts) and implicit learning (skills).
- Synthesis: Designing a study system based on mechanisms; evaluating eyewitness testimony.
- Challenge: Limits of lab research; designing memory recovery studies ethically.

**Connections forward:**
- Lifespan development (memory changes across age).
- Learning (encoding and consolidation principles).
- Trauma and stress (amygdala, emotional memory, false recovery).
- Disorders (dissociation, OCD, depression as disorders of memory function).
