# Pantry for Chapter 1: Introduction to Psychology

## Scenes and cold opens

**Wundt's laboratory, Leipzig, 1879:** Small rooms with brass instruments, reaction-time apparatus, tachistoscopes. A subject sits isolated, a light appears, they press a button, an apparatus records the time to one-thousandth of a second. The scientific study of mind begins with measurement.

**William James watching an introspectionist fail:** A colleague tries to introspect on the sensation of hearing a bell. But in the moment of attending to the sensation, the sensation changes or disappears. Introspection cannot observe what it aims to observe.

**Emergency room, present day:** A motorcycle accident victim. A neurologist assesses brain damage through behavioral questions. A clinical psychologist assesses trauma and resilience. They work together. One focuses on the brain, one on the person.

**Stanley Milgram's laboratory, 1961:** A participant is told to deliver electric shocks to another person for wrong answers. The shocks get stronger. The participant protests. An experimenter in a white coat says, "You have no choice, you must continue." Nearly two-thirds of participants continue, believing they are delivering lethal shocks. No actual shocks occur; it's a ruse. The discovery: ordinary people will harm others under authority.

**A therapist's office:** A man says, "I can't stop thinking about what I did wrong." The therapist listens, asks questions, neither of them uses brain imaging. This is psychology too.

## Etymologies

- *Psychology:* Greek *psyche* (mind, soul, breath, the animating principle) + *logos* (study, reasoning). Literally, the study of the mind.

- *Introspection:* Latin *intro* (inward) + *specere* (to look). To look inward at one's own mind.

- *Consciousness:* Latin *con* (with) + *scire* (to know). To know with, or aware of. The state of being aware.

- *Behavior:* From Old French *behaviur*, related to "have" and "bear." What one bears or carries oneself as; observable actions.

- *Empirical:* Greek *empeiria* (experience). Based on observation and experience, not theory alone.

- *Cognition:* Latin *cognoscere* (to know). Mental processes involving knowing, thinking, perceiving, remembering.

- *Clinical:* Greek *kline* (bed, couch). Originally "of or at the bedside," now referring to practical, applied work with patients.

- *Stimulus:* Latin *stimulus* (goad, stick for driving cattle). Something that provokes a response.

- *Reinforcement:* From Latin *re* (again) + *force* (strength). In behaviorism, anything that strengthens a behavior by following it.

## Trade-offs across schools

**Introspection's intimacy vs. unreliability:**
- What it gains: Direct access to subjective experience, rich detail, authenticity
- What it loses: Objectivity, reliability, the ability to compare across people
- Why it matters: The mind is private, but privacy comes at the cost of rigor

**Structuralism's detail vs. inability to generalize:**
- What it gains: Precise description of components of experience
- What it loses: Any ability to say whether the components are real or are artifacts of introspection
- Why it matters: Details without generality are not science

**Functionalism's purpose vs. vagueness:**
- What it gains: An explanation for why mental capacities exist (they aid survival)
- What it loses: Precision about how to measure function
- Why it matters: Purpose is explanatory but difficult to test

**Behaviorism's objectivity vs. blindness to the mind:**
- What it gains: Rigor, measurement, experimental control, ability to predict and change behavior
- What it loses: Any study of consciousness, intention, emotion, thinking — the inner life
- Why it matters: Objectivity is powerful but incomplete; it ignores what makes us human

**Cognitive psychology's richness vs. artificiality of laboratory settings:**
- What it gains: Understanding of mental processes, how people think and remember
- What it loses: The texture of real-world thinking, the role of context and emotion
- Why it matters: Laboratory findings may not transfer to how people actually think in life

**Clinical psychology's practical help vs. lack of experimental control:**
- What it gains: Real-world benefit to real people, understanding of complexity
- What it loses: Ability to isolate causes, to know what works for whom and why
- Why it matters: Helping people matters, but understanding why the help works also matters

## Scale shifts

**One neuron's firing to behavior:**
A single neuron fires when you see your mother's face. But 86 billion neurons fire in your brain. From the coordination of billions of neurons emerges memory, emotion, decision-making, behavior — the entire landscape of human experience. How does that scale shift happen?

**One person's reaction time to a theory of mind:**
Wundt measures how long it takes one person to react to a light. Multiply that across thousands of people, across cultures, across ages. From individual measurements, a science of the mind emerges.

**One study to a replication crisis:**
A single study shows that women are worse at math if they're reminded of their gender (stereotype threat). But when researchers try to replicate the finding in different populations, it doesn't always hold. Psychology discovers that individual findings don't always scale to whole populations or cultures.

**One patient's trauma to theories of memory:**
H.M., a patient whose hippocampus was surgically removed in 1953, cannot form new long-term memories. One person's catastrophic loss teaches the field about the structure of memory itself. From one case, a principle emerges.

**Millions of individual choices to social movements:**
Each person decides, individually, whether to help a stranger in distress. Multiply across millions of people and moments, and you see patterns: helping is more likely in small groups, less likely in large crowds (the bystander effect). From individual psychology emerges social psychology.

## Worked examples and analogies

**The 10% brain myth:**
Claim: "We use only 10% of our brains."
Question: How would you test this?
Evidence: Brain imaging shows that even during sleep, the entire brain is active. Different regions specialize (visual cortex for sight, motor cortex for movement), but there is no unused region. Damage to any region typically affects specific functions rather than producing an unused zone.
Lesson: Pop-psychology claims are often based on misunderstandings of real research.

**Why introspection fails:**
Try this: Feel the weight of your shoe on your foot. Now, while focusing on that sensation, try to describe what it feels like. The moment you attend to it, it changes. That's Wundt's problem. You cannot observe your own mind the way a physicist observes a pendulum, because the observation changes the thing observed.

**How behaviorism shaped clinical practice:**
A child is afraid of rabbits. John B. Watson conditions them to fear by presenting a rabbit along with a loud noise. The child is traumatized. But Mary Cover Jones (1924) reverses it: she pairs the rabbit with pleasant experiences (food). Gradually, the fear disappears. This principle — that behavior can be reshaped through conditioning — remains central to behavioral and cognitive-behavioral therapy today.

**Why Milgram's study was revolutionary:**
Most psychologists predicted that only 1% of people would deliver lethal shocks under orders. Milgram found 65%. The finding shocked the field because it revealed that ordinary people — not sadists, not monsters, but ordinary folks responding to a situation — could harm others. It taught psychology that *situation* shapes behavior as much as *personality*.

## Named researchers and key figures

**Wilhelm Wundt (1832–1920):** German physician and experimental scientist, opened the first psychology laboratory at Leipzig in 1879. Believed consciousness had structure that could be studied through introspection and measurement. Pioneer of experimental psychology.

**William James (1842–1910):** American philosopher and psychologist. Skeptical of introspection, emphasized the *function* of consciousness (what it does, not what it is). Founded functionalism. Published *The Principles of Psychology* (1890), still influential.

**John B. Watson (1878–1958):** American psychologist at Johns Hopkins. Declared that psychology should study only observable behavior, not consciousness. Founded behaviorism. His slogan: "Give me a dozen healthy infants... and I'll make them into whatever you want."

**B.F. Skinner (1904–1990):** American psychologist. Showed how behavior is shaped by reinforcement and punishment. Invented the operant conditioning chamber (Skinner box). Argued that even human behavior could be understood through environmental control.

**Sigmund Freud (1856–1939):** Austrian neurologist. Founded psychoanalysis. Proposed that behavior is driven by unconscious desires and early childhood experiences. Influential in clinical psychology despite many ideas being unsupported.

**Carl Rogers (1902–1987):** American psychologist. Founded client-centered therapy. Believed therapists should provide unconditional positive regard. Humanistic perspective emphasizes the whole person and their potential for growth.

**Jean Piaget (1896–1980):** Swiss psychologist. Studied cognitive development in children. Discovered that children think differently at different ages. Showed that development involves active construction of understanding, not passive reception.

**Stanley Milgram (1933–1984):** American social psychologist. Conducted obedience studies showing that people will harm others under authority. His research catalyzed ethical guidelines for psychology research.

**Noam Chomsky (1928–):** American linguist. Critiqued behaviorism, showing that language learning requires innate mental structures. Helped spark the cognitive revolution by demonstrating that behavior alone cannot explain human capacities.

**Margaret Floy Washburn (1871–1939):** First woman awarded a PhD in psychology (1894). Wrote *The Animal Mind: A Textbook of Comparative Psychology*, which was standard in the field for over 20 years.

**Mary Whiton Calkins (1863–1930):** American psychologist. Did all requirements for a Harvard PhD under William James but was denied the degree because she was a woman. Conducted important memory research on primacy and recency effects.

**Martha Bernal (1931–2001):** First Latina to earn a psychology doctorate (1962). Conducted research with Mexican American children on educational psychology.

**Mamie Phipps Clark and Kenneth Clark:** African American psychologists. Their doll-preference studies with Black children were instrumental in the *Brown v. Board of Education* Supreme Court desegregation case (1954). Opened the first child guidance center in Harlem.

## Common misconceptions and corrections

**"Psychology is just common sense."**
Partially true. Some findings confirm intuition (studying helps learning). But many contradict intuition: people are worse at learning if they study too long without breaks (spacing effect); eyewitness testimony is unreliable; people are far more obedient to authority than most expect. Science is valuable because it reveals where intuition misleads.

**"Psychologists read minds."**
False. Psychologists cannot determine what someone is thinking without being told or inferring from behavior.

**"All psychology is therapy."**
False. Clinical psychology is one subfield. Most academic psychologists study cognition, development, social behavior, perception, etc., and never provide therapy.

**"The mind is the same in all cultures."**
False. Psychology developed in Western, educated, industrialized, rich, democratic (WEIRD) societies and was often tested only on college students. Modern psychology recognizes that culture profoundly shapes how people think, perceive, behave, and develop.

**"Freud proved that childhood trauma causes adult problems."**
Partially true. Freud was influential and some intuitions he had are correct (early experiences matter, unconscious processes exist). But many specific claims (psychosexual stages, the Oedipal complex) are not supported by evidence. Research confirms that early experiences matter, but not in the ways Freud proposed.

**"Behaviorism was wrong."**
Partially true. Behaviorism was incomplete, but not wrong. It revealed that behavior can be shaped systematically through reward and punishment. These principles remain central to education, therapy, and organizational management.

**"Neuroscience has replaced psychology."**
False. Psychology and neuroscience are different levels of analysis. Understanding a neuron does not explain why someone falls in love or why they're afraid of spiders. Psychology studies mind and behavior; neuroscience studies the brain. Both are needed.
