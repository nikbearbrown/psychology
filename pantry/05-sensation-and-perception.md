## Concept Architecture

### Concept 1: Sensation (Transduction, Absolute Threshold)

**Scene anchor:** Mouse detecting individual photons at night; girl recovering sight after cataract surgery.

**Mechanism:** Sensory receptors as specialists; transduction as energy translation; absolute threshold as the 50% detection point, not a crisp boundary.

**Trade-off:** Sensitivity vs. noise—thresholds are set by evolution to optimize signal-to-noise ratio, not to maximize detection.

**Etymology:** Transducere (Latin, "to lead across"); sensation from sensatio (Latin, "sensing").

**Worked example:** Hearing threshold testing at 1,000 Hz, establishing the 50% detection point at 10 dB.

**Misconceptions:** 
- Subliminal messages do not control behavior (laboratory findings ≠ real-world persuasion).
- Below threshold does not mean no neural processing.
- Thresholds vary by individual and context.

### Concept 2: Just-Noticeable Difference (Weber's Law, Relativity of Perception)

**Scene anchor:** Phone screen in daylight vs. in a dark theater.

**Mechanism:** Weber's law as a constant proportion; JND = k × I. Different k values for different senses (heaviness ≈ 0.02, brightness ≈ 0.08, pitch ≈ 0.003).

**Trade-off:** Discrimination ability vs. information load—filtering by JND prevents sensory overload but can miss subtle changes.

**Worked example:** Conversation inaudible in loud restaurant, audible when noise decreases. Same signal, different baseline, different perception.

**Misconceptions:**
- JND is not a fixed number; it scales with baseline.
- Below-JND changes are still processed but not consciously reported.

### Concept 3: Attention, Adaptation, and Top-Down Perception

**Scene anchor:** Party with name-capture; sleeping mother awakened by infant's cry.

**Mechanism:** Bottom-up processing (stimulus-driven, automatic) vs. top-down processing (goal-driven, controlled); inattentional blindness as allocation of attention; sensory adaptation as filtering of constants.

**Trade-off:** Bottom-up responsiveness vs. top-down efficiency—need flexibility between the two.

**Worked example:** Gorilla in the basketball-counting video (inattentional blindness); sleeping mother hearing infant's cry but not the truck (signal-detection theory + sensory set).

**Misconceptions:**
- Attention is not purely voluntary; bottom-up capture happens automatically.
- Lack of conscious attention does not mean lack of neural processing.
- Adaptation is a feature (filtering), not a failure (weakening).

## Scale Shift

The chapter-level scale shift occurs in the Integration section: moving from the mechanics of individual receptors and thresholds to the system-level design principle that perception is *relative and constructed*, not a mirror of physical reality. This is the moment where the student sees that the nervous system's job is not to represent the world as a physicist would but to extract behavioral information.

## Voice Anchoring

The chapter uses Attenborough × Feynman voice (fry): 

- **Cold opens are scene-based, not abstract.** Every concept begins with a tangible scenario (mouse at night, concert hall, party, sleeping mother) before introducing terminology.
- **Mechanisms are explained from first principles.** Transduction is described as energy translation before the word is used. Weber's law emerges from the observation of relative change before the equation is shown.
- **Etymology illuminates.** Words like *transducere*, *sensation*, *threshold* are introduced with their roots to show that jargon is usually just Latin for the simple thing it describes.
- **Trade-offs are named explicitly.** Every concept section states what is optimized and what is paid for that optimization.
- **First-person voice is used to signal pedagogy.** "I find myself returning to..." in the "Still puzzling" section models the honest admission that some questions remain open.

## Remaining Work

This chapter intentionally defers detailed coverage of:
- The full anatomy of the eye and ear (would require 1,000+ words). These are described functionally ("the fovea contains cones"; "hair cells in the basilar membrane") rather than anatomically.
- Touch, taste, smell, vestibular sense, proprioception (mentioned in the source material as secondary systems; scoped out to focus on vision, hearing, and the three core concepts).
- Gestalt principles in detail (figure-ground, grouping, continuity, closure are named in the source but would require their own section; the chapter prioritizes transduction, threshold, and the top-down/bottom-up distinction as the core conceptual load).
- Specific color and pitch theories (trichromatic vs. opponent-process; temporal vs. place theory) are mentioned in sources but deferred because they require deeper anatomical detail and are not essential to the chapter's core message: that sensation is translation and perception is interpretation.

These could be the basis for later chapters or expanded versions of Concepts 2 and 3. The current chapter prioritizes *conceptual depth* over *breadth*.

## Fact-Checking Notes

- **30 miles for candle flame detection:** Okawa & Sampath (2007) cites this as a classical threshold measurement.
- **20 feet for clock ticking:** Galanter (1962) cited in the source material.
- **Gorilla study (Simons & Chabris, 1999):** Nearly half of observers miss the gorilla. This is a widely replicated finding and a canonical example in perception research.
- **Weber's law constants:** Values for k (0.02 for heaviness, 0.08 for brightness, 0.003 for pitch) are standard in psychophysics.
- **Girl with cataract removal (2003, India):** Peck (2012) documents the case. The phenomenon of learning to see after congenital blindness shows that perception requires both sensation and prior experience.

All major claims are linked to primary sources or well-established research findings in the chapter text.
