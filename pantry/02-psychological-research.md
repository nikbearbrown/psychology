### Cold Open & Replication Crisis

**Primary Source:** Open Science Collaboration. (2015). "Estimating the reproducibility of psychological science." *Science*, 349(6251), aac4716.
URL: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aac4716

**Key Finding:** 36% of psychology studies replicated (97 out of 270 attempted replications achieved statistically significant results in the predicted direction). This meta-analysis of 100 studies published in top journals (2008-2012) revealed systemic issues with effect sizes and publication bias.

**Why This Opening:** The replication failure is emotionally real for students (contradicts authority/textbooks), contemporary (2015, ongoing), and sets up all three concepts. It's not an attack on psychology but a demonstration of how science self-corrects.

### Concept 1 — The Scientific Method

**Primary Source on Milgram:** Milgram, S. (1963). "Behavioral study of obedience." *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, 67(4), 371-378.

**Follow-up Context:** Milgram, S. (1974). *Obedience to Authority*. Harper & Row. (Book-length treatment explaining the theory, experiments, and ethical implications.)

**Falsifiability Anchor:** Popper, K. (1959). *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*. [Historical standard on falsifiability in science; the chapter uses this concept without deep philosophy.]

**Etymology:**
- Hypothesis: Greek *hupothesis*, "foundation" or "base"; hypo- (under) + thesis (proposition)
- Experiment: Latin *experiri*, "to try" or "to test"
- Theory: Greek *theoreia*, "looking at" or "speculation"; related to *theoros*, "spectator"

**Deduction vs. Induction:**
- Deduction: General principle → Specific case. "All humans are mortal; Socrates is human; therefore Socrates is mortal."
- Induction: Specific cases → General principle. "I've observed 100 ravens and all were black; therefore all ravens are black." (Famous counterexample: not all ravens are black.)

**Freud Unfalsifiability Issue:** 
Documented in Popper's critique and repeated throughout philosophy of science. Any behavior can be explained post-hoc as unconscious motivation, making the theory unfalsifiable.

### Concept 2 — Research Designs

**Descriptive Research — Jane Goodall:**

Primary Source: Goodall, J. (1986). *The Chimpanzees of Gombe: Patterns of Behavior*. Harvard University Press.

Naturalistic observation in the field, 1960 onward. Goodall's methodological innovation: combining narrative observation (which critics feared was non-objective) with ethological rigor (systematic categorization of behaviors). The naming of individuals (instead of numbering them) was criticized as undermining objectivity, but Goodall's position was that naming reflected the individual personalities she observed — a position now standard in primatology.

**Trade-off:** Enormous depth (individual personalities, tool use, cultural transmission) but limited sample (one population in one location, long-term attrition as chimpanzees died).

---

**Correlational Research — Sleep and GPA:**

Primary Source: Lowry, M., Dean, K., & Manders, K. (2010). "The link between sleep quantity and academic performance for the college student." *Sentience: The University of Minnesota Undergraduate Journal of Psychology*, 3 (Spring), 16-19.

Finding: r = -0.29 (weak to moderate negative correlation) between sleep deprivation (fewer than 5 hours/night) and GPA.

**Critical Point for Students:** The correlation is real but small. This means knowing sleep hours gives you limited ability to predict GPA (you'd explain only about 8% of the variance). Other factors matter much more.

**Confounding Variables Inventory:**
1. Stress/Anxiety: Students anxious about grades sleep poorly AND perform worse on exams.
2. Study Hours: Students who sleep less may study more (sleep deprivation by choice, not circumstance).
3. Caffeine Use: Students drinking caffeine to stay awake may have worse academic performance independent of sleep.
4. Underlying Health Issues: Insomnia, sleep apnea, ADHD all correlate with both poor sleep and worse academic outcomes.

**Illusory Correlation — Moon & Behavior:**

Primary Source: Rotton, J., & Kelly, I. W. (1985). "Much ado about the full moon: A meta-analysis of lunar-lunacy research." *Psychological Bulletin*, 97(2), 286-306.

Finding: Meta-analysis of 37 studies found no relationship between lunar phase and human behavior, crime, hospital admissions, or psychiatric symptoms. The belief persists despite null evidence because:
- **Confirmation bias:** People remember full-moon nights when something unusual happened.
- **Availability heuristic:** Vivid examples come to mind easily.
- **Intuitive plausibility:** The moon controls tides; we're mostly water; therefore...

---

**Experimental Research — Milgram's Experimenter Presence:**

Milgram's variations:

1. **Baseline condition** (experimenter in room): 65% obedience (highest shock)
2. **Telephone condition** (experimenter gives orders by phone): 21% obedience
3. **Experimenter absent, peer administers shocks** (participant told to ask peer if unsure): 93% administered shocks (but this is a different measure — not personal responsibility)

**Independent Variable:** Physical presence of authority
**Dependent Variable:** Shock voltage at which obedience ceased

**Mechanism Identified:** Authority worked through multiple channels:
- Legitimate status (scientist, Yale, white coat)
- Proximity (physically present, makes refusal harder)
- Scripted refusals ("You have no choice") — shifted agency from participant
- Incremental escalation (small steps from 15V to 450V)

Remove any of these and obedience decreased.

**Note-Taking Example (Worked):**

Hypothesis based on: Mueller, P. A., & Oppenheimer, D. M. (2014). "The pen is mightier than the keyboard: Advantages of longhand over laptop note taking." *Psychological Science*, 25(6), 1159-1168.

Finding: Students who handwrote notes performed better on conceptual questions than students who typed, even though typed-note-takers took more notes. Proposed mechanism: Handwriting is slower, forcing selective encoding of main ideas rather than transcription.

**Confounds to Control:**
- Internet distraction for laptop group (use software blocking)
- Note quantity (measure and control statistically)
- Typing skill variation (match groups on typing speed)
- Student familiarity with devices (counterbalance — some students test with laptop first, others with handwriting)

---

**Ecological Validity Trade-off:**

| Setting | Strength | Weakness |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Lab (Milgram) | Causal proof, control, replication | Artificial, low real-world match, demand effects |
| Field (Goodall) | High ecological validity, rich context | No control, low generalizability, observer bias |

---

**Longitudinal vs. Cross-Sectional:**

**Longitudinal Example:** The Cancer Prevention Study-3 (CPS-3), American Cancer Society. Follows 300,000+ participants for 20+ years, measuring diet, exercise, exposures, and cancer incidence. **Strength:** You know actual change in same individuals. **Weakness:** Attrition (people die, move, stop responding); confounds from historical events (medical advances, cultural changes).

**Cross-Sectional Example:** Measuring political attitudes across age groups (20, 40, 60) in 2024. **Strength:** Fast, inexpensive. **Weakness:** Can't tell if 60-year-olds are more conservative because they aged or because they grew up in a more conservative era (cohort effect).

### Concept 3 — Replication Crisis

**Primary Source on Crisis Scope:**

Shrout, P. E., & Rodgers, J. L. (2018). "Psychology, science, and knowledge construction: Broadening perspectives from the replication crisis." *Annual Review of Psychology*, 69, 487-510.

Key Contributors:
- Bem, D. J. (2011). "Feeling the future: Experimental evidence for anomalous retroactive influences on cognition and affect." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 100(3), 407-425. [ESP study — replication failures]
- Aschwanden, C. (2018, December 6). "Psychology's replication crisis has made the field better." *FiveThirtyEight*. [Frames crisis as self-correction]

---

**Why Replication Fails:**

1. **Small sample sizes:** Many psychology studies use N = 30-100. To reliably detect a small effect (d = 0.2), you need N = 300+. Small-sample studies can find effects by luck.

2. **P-hacking and multiple comparisons:** If you test 20 hypotheses, ~1 will show p < 0.05 by chance. Bem tested many models; only the statistically significant ones were published.

3. **Convenience sampling:** 90% of psychological subjects are college students (Arnett, J. 2008. "The neglected 95%: Why American psychology needs to become less American." *American Psychologist*, 63(7), 602-614). College students are WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic). Findings may not generalize.

4. **Publication bias:** Studies with significant results are published 5x more often than null results. This inflates the replicability of published findings.

5. **Flexibility in analysis (p-hacking):** Researchers can:
 - Exclude outliers (or not)
 - Test main effects or interactions
 - Transform variables (log, squared)
 - Stop data collection when p < 0.05
 - Choose which covariates to include

 Each choice is defensible alone; together they inflate false positives.

**Ego Depletion Case Study:**

Original findings: Baumeister, R. F., & Vohs, K. D. (2003). "Self-control and self-control depletion." *Psychological Bulletin*, 126(2), 247-259. [Proposes "ego depletion" — willpower as a limited resource.]

Replication Failures: Hagger, M. S., et al. (2016). "A multi-lab pre-registered replication of the ego-depletion effect." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 11(4), 546-573. [36 labs, large samples, pre-registered — mostly null results.]

**Why Original Seemed Strong:**
- Intuitive mechanism (willpower runs out, like energy)
- Many studies appeared to show it
- But: small effects (d = 0.4-0.6), small sample sizes, and flexibility in dependent variables (was depletion measured through persistence, performance, choice, or self-report?)

**Why Replication Failed:**
- Larger samples revealed effect size was smaller or zero
- Pre-registration prevented p-hacking
- Multiple labs tested same hypotheses (reduced false positives from researcher variation)

**Current Status:** Some ego-depletion effects have survived replication; others haven't. The phenomenon is real in narrow conditions but doesn't generalize as broadly as originally claimed.

---

**Open Science Responses:**

1. **Pre-registration** (Open Science Framework, aspredicted.org): Researchers post hypotheses, sample size justification, and analysis plan *before* data collection. Changes are tracked. Reduces p-hacking.

2. **Open Data & Code:** Researchers share raw data and analysis scripts. Others can audit, reanalyze, check for errors.

3. **Meta-analyses:** Combine effect sizes from many studies, weight by sample size and quality. Gives more accurate picture than any single study.

4. **Registered Reports:** Journal accepts study before results known, based on quality of design. Published regardless of outcome, eliminating publication bias.

5. **Large, representative samples:** Shift away from N=30 college-student studies toward N=1000+ diverse-population studies.

---

**Milgram Obedience Replications:**

The obedience effect *has* held up across replications in different countries (Germany, Australia, Netherlands, Italy). Why?

- **Large effect:** About 65% obedience is a robust phenomenon, not a small effect hiding in noise.
- **Clear mechanism:** Presence, authority, scripting — these elements are specified and can be varied.
- **Ethical scrutiny:** Because the study is controversial, many researchers have attempted replications to understand it better.
- **Strong incentive:** Psychologists wanted to replicate Milgram because it was important, not just novel.

However: Recent replications (Burger, 2009) found slightly lower obedience (61%) when researchers used more modern protections and stopped if participants showed high stress. The core phenomenon held, but nuances emerged.

## Voice Calibration

**Attenborough × Feynman adjustment for psychology:**

- **Cold open in scene:** Milgram in the lab, Goodall in the field, the replication crisis at OSC. Not abstract concepts.
- **Real people, real experiments:** Name Milgram, Goodall, Bem, Ainsworth. Make science personal.
- **Mechanism-first:** Why did ego depletion fail to replicate? What were researchers doing wrong that they didn't see? (Answer: small samples, p-hacking, publication bias)
- **Honest about limits:** We still don't know why replication rates differ across subfields.
- **No jargon without gloss:** "p-hacking" (testing many hypotheses and publishing the ones that worked), "attrition" (people dropping out), "confounding variable" (a third variable explaining the correlation).
