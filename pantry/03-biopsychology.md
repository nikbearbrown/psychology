# Biopsychology — Research Pantry

## Phineas Gage — Primary Sources

- **Macmillan, M. (1999). The Phineas Gage Information Page**. http://www.uakron.edu/gage — This is the canonical academic source on Gage. Macmillan has done meticulous historical research showing that many popular accounts of Gage's case have been exaggerated or romanticized. The myth has been revised multiple times.

- **Harlow, J. M. (1848, December). Passage of an iron rod through the head. Boston Medical and Surgical Journal.** — The original case report. Harlow saw Gage and documented the physical injury with remarkable detail for 1848.

- **Damasio, A. R., Grabowski, T. H., Frank, R., Galaburda, A. M., & Damasio, H. (1994). The return of Phineas Gage: Clues about the brain from the skull of a famous patient. Science, 264(5162), 1102-1105.** — Modern neuroscience team used Gage's skull to reconstruct the damage path. Showed the damage was even more extensive than previously thought.

**Key teaching point**: Gage's case is foundational in neuroscience because it's arguably the first clear evidence that specific brain regions are responsible for specific functions. But the case is also a teaching moment about how myths get built in science. Popular accounts of Gage being a completely different person post-injury are exaggerated. Macmillan's work is essential for teaching honestly.

## Neuron Structure and Function

- **Herculano-Houzel, S. (2009). The human brain in numbers: a linearly scaled-up primate brain. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 3, 31.** — Establishes the 86 billion neuron count. Also argues against the old myth that there are 10x more glia than neurons.

- **Azevedo, F. A., et al. (2009). Equal numbers of neuronal and nonneuronal cells make the human brain an isometrically scaled-up primate brain. Journal of Comparative Neurology, 513(5), 532-541.** — Independent confirmation of the glia-neuron ratio being roughly 1:1, not 10:1.

**Ratio of axons in corpus callosum**: 200 million axons connecting the hemispheres. This is from standard neuroscience textbooks and is well-established.

## Action Potential and Ion Movement

The mechanism of action potentials is well-established. The sodium-potassium pump, the role of the myelin sheath and nodes of Ranvier, the all-or-none principle — these are core findings in neurophysiology, established by Hodgkin and Huxley in the 1950s. Not disputed.

## Neurotransmitters and Behavior

### Dopamine
- **Berridge, K. C., & Robinson, T. E. (1998). What is the role of dopamine in reward: Hedonic impact, reward learning, or incentive salience? Brain Research Reviews, 28, 309-369.** — Critical review of dopamine's role. Shows that dopamine is not simply "the pleasure chemical" — it's more involved in motivation and learning.

- **Gardner, E. L. (2011). Addiction and brain reward and antireward pathways. Advances in Psychosomatic Medicine, 30, 22-60.** — Addiction as dysregulation of dopamine and reward pathways.

### Serotonin and Depression
- **Cowen, P. J. (2008). Serotonin and depression: pathophysiological mechanism or marker of depression? Journal of Psychiatric Research, 42(15), 1223-1231.** — Modern critique of the "serotonin hypothesis" of depression. Shows the relationship is more complex than low serotonin → depression.

### The Serotonin Hypothesis and SSRIs
The finding that SSRIs take weeks to work despite immediate serotonin increase is real and still not fully explained. This is worth noting as a limitation of the simple serotonin hypothesis.

## Brain Imaging

- **Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI)** has become the dominant neuroimaging tool for localizing brain function in humans. The mechanism is well-established: active neurons use oxygen, blood flow increases, changes in magnetic properties allow detection.

- **Electroencephalography (EEG)** records electrical activity from scalp electrodes. Temporal resolution in milliseconds. Spatial resolution in centimeters (poor but often sufficient).

- **Positron emission tomography (PET)** uses radioactive tracers. Good for metabolic activity and neurotransmitter receptor binding.

- **Computerized tomography (CT)** uses X-rays. Good for structural imaging, poor for functional imaging.

## Brain Structure and Lateralization

- **Gazzaniga, M. S. (2005). Forty-five years of split-brain research and still going strong. Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 6(8), 653-659.** — Classic review of split-brain research. Gazzaniga's work established that the hemispheres specialize but that claims about "left-brain vs. right-brain personalities" are overblown.

- **Banich, M. T., & Heller, W. (1998). Evolving perspectives on lateralization of function. Current Directions in Psychological Science, 7(1), 1-2.** — Shows that research on hemispheric dominance has produced inconsistent results and that hemispheric interaction is more important than specialization.

- **Ehret, G. (2006). Hemisphere dominance of brain function — which functions are lateralized and why? In 23 Problems in Systems Neuroscience (pp. 44-61).** — Thoughtful treatment of what lateralization actually is and isn't.

## Memory and the Hippocampus

- **Squire, L. R. (2009). The legacy of patient H. M. for neuroscience. Neuron, 61, 6-9.** — H.M.'s case revealed the distinction between procedural and declarative memory and the role of the hippocampus in consolidation.

- **Hardt, O., Einarsson, E. Ö., & Nader, K. (2010). A bridge over troubled water: Reconsolidation as a link between cognitive and neuroscientific memory research traditions. Annual Review of Psychology, 61, 141-167.** — Modern understanding of memory consolidation and reconsolidation.

## Genetics and Gene-Environment Interaction

- **Tienari, P., Wynne, L. C., Sorri, A., et al. (2004). Genotype–environment interaction in schizophrenia spectrum disorder: long-term follow-up study of Finnish adoptees. British Journal of Psychiatry, 184, 216-222.** — Classic study showing interaction between genetic risk and environmental stress for schizophrenia.

- **Miguel, P. M., Pereira, L. O., Silveira, P. P., & Meaney, M. J. (2019). Early environmental influences on the development of children's brain structure and function. Developmental Medicine and Child Neurology, 61, 1127-33.** — Epigenetics and environmental effects on developing brain.

- **Short, A. K., & Baram, T. Z. (2019). Early-life adversity and neurological disease: Age-old questions and novel answers. Nature Reviews Neurology, 15, 657-69.** — Mechanisms by which early stress alters brain development.

## Myths Corrected

### The 10% Brain Myth
The claim that we use only 10% of our brains is false. Every part of the brain is active over the course of a day. The source of the myth is unclear, but likely origins include:
- A misquote of early neuroscience work
- A misinterpretation of glia-neuron ratios (once thought to be 10:1, now known to be roughly 1:1)

This is well-documented as a myth in neuroscience literature.

### Left-Brain/Right-Brain Personality
The idea that "left-brain people" are logical and "right-brain people" are creative is not supported by research. While hemispheric specialization exists, the hemispheres are integrated, and both are involved in complex tasks. This myth is widespread in popular psychology but rejected by neuroscience research.

## Notes on complexity and honest uncertainty

The chapter acknowledges:
1. The serotonin hypothesis of depression is oversimplified. SSRIs work but we don't fully understand why they take weeks.
2. The hard problem of consciousness remains unsolved.
3. Lateralization research has produced inconsistent results.
4. Evolutionary explanations for behavior are useful but limited.
5. Heritability statistics are population-specific and can be misinterpreted.

These acknowledgments follow the Feynman principle: name what we don't yet understand rather than papering over gaps.
