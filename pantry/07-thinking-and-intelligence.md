# Pantry: Chapter 7 — Thinking and Intelligence

## Research notes

### Cold open
- Daniel Kahneman's "Linda problem" / conjunction fallacy, from Tversky & Kahneman (1974) "Judgment under uncertainty: heuristics and biases" *Science* 185(4157):1124–1131
- 85% of subjects choose "bank teller and feminist" as more probable than "bank teller," which violates basic probability (P(A and B) ≤ P(A))
- Demonstrates representativeness bias and availability bias operating before conscious reasoning

### Concept 1: Cognition, concepts, prototypes, schemas
- Cognition defined as thinking inclusive of perception, learning, problem-solving, judgment, memory
- **Natural concepts**: learned from experience (direct: snow in Vermont vs. indirect: snow from photos from Caribbean)
- **Artificial/formal concepts**: defined by explicit rules (triangle: three sides, three angles summing to 180°)
- **Prototypes**: best exemplar of a concept; fast categorization tool; source of systematic errors
- **Schemas**: clusters of related concepts organized thematically (restaurant schema, elevator event schema)
- **Event schemas/cognitive scripts**: routines that become automatic; difficult to override (texting while driving, checking phone)
- Trade-off: speed vs. accuracy; schemas enable fast navigation, also enable persistent errors

Sources:
- Bartlett, F.C. (1932) *Remembering: A study in experimental and social psychology*
- Goldstone, R.L., & Kersten, A. (2003) "Concepts and categorization" *Handbook of Psychology* IV:599–622
- Bayer & Campbell (2012) "Texting while driving on automatic" *Computers in Human Behavior* 28:2083–2090

### Concept 2: Problem-solving, algorithms, heuristics, biases
- **Algorithm**: step-by-step procedure that guarantees correct answer if followed precisely (recipe, long division)
- **Heuristic**: mental shortcut, rule of thumb, usually works but fails systematically (working backward, breaking into steps)
- **Cognitive biases**: systematic deviations from accurate reasoning
  - **Anchoring bias**: fixate on initial information when making decisions (realtor showing bad apartments first)
  - **Availability heuristic**: judge probability by how readily examples come to mind (plane crash vs. car crash)
  - **Representativeness bias**: judge probability by similarity to stereotype/prototype; ignore base rates (quiet person = librarian)
  - **Confirmation bias**: seek, interpret, remember information confirming existing beliefs (unfair professor schema)
  - **Hindsight bias**: believe past events were more predictable than they were ("I knew it was coming")
- Five conditions trigger heuristic use: too much information, time pressure, unimportant decision, little information, available heuristic comes to mind
- Heuristics are *faster than careful reasoning*; necessary for practical decision-making; systematic failures are not character flaws

Sources:
- Kahneman, D. (2011) *Thinking, Fast and Slow*
- Tversky & Kahneman (1974) "Judgment under uncertainty"
- Aronson, E. (Ed.) (1995) *The Social Animal*

### Concept 3: Intelligence theories
- **Charles Spearman (1900s)**: single general intelligence *g*; all cognitive abilities expressions of underlying capacity
- **Raymond Cattell (1940s)**: two-factor model
  - *Fluid intelligence*: ability to see complex relationships, solve novel problems (not dependent on prior learning)
  - *Crystallized intelligence*: acquired knowledge, ability to retrieve and use learned information
  - Fluid declines with age; crystallized often improves
- **Robert Sternberg (1980s)**: Triarchic Theory
  - *Analytical intelligence*: academic problem-solving, reasoning, evaluation (what IQ tests measure)
  - *Practical intelligence*: "street smarts," real-world problem-solving using experience
  - *Creative intelligence*: inventing novel solutions, original work
- **Howard Gardner (1983)**: Multiple Intelligences (at least 8)
  - Linguistic, logical-mathematical, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, spatial, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic
  - Influential in education; criticized by cognitive psychologists for broadness, lack of empirical factor structure
  - Gottfredson (2004): appealing in democratic societies because "everyone can be smart in some way"
- **Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) Theory (Schneider & McGrew 2018)**: hierarchical
  - Level 1: general intelligence (*g*)
  - Level 2: broad abilities (fluid reasoning, short-term memory, processing speed)
  - Level 3: narrow, directly measurable abilities (memory span, reading fluency)
- Cultural variation in intelligence: fishing skill on island culture, storytelling in Irish culture, collective harmony in collectivist cultures
- *Cultural intelligence*: ability to understand and relate to people in another culture

Sources:
- Cattell, R. (1963) "Theory of fluid and crystallized intelligence" *Journal of Educational Psychology* 54(1):1–22
- Sternberg, R.J. (1988) *The Triarchic Mind*
- Gardner, H. (1983) *Frames of Mind: The Theory of Multiple Intelligences*
- Cianciolo, A.T., & Sternberg, R.J. (2004) *Intelligence: A Brief History*
- Schneider, W.J., & McGrew, K.S. (2018) "The Cattell-Horn-Carroll theory" in *Contemporary Intellectual Assessment* pp. 73–163

### Concept 4: Intelligence testing
- **Alfred Binet (early 1900s)**: French psychologist; developed test for French government to identify children who would struggle in school; introduced *mental age* concept
- **Lewis Terman (Stanford)**: standardized Binet's test, tested thousands of children across ages, created norms; developed *Stanford-Binet Intelligence Scale* (1916); introduced IQ = (mental age / chronological age) × 100
- **David Wechsler (1930s)**: believed intelligence = verbal + nonverbal; created test combining subtests; developed *Wechsler-Bellevue Intelligence Scale*; modern versions: WAIS-IV (adults), WISC-V (children), WPPSI-IV (preschool)
- **Norming and standardization**: test administered to large representative sample to establish baseline; administration, scoring, interpretation consistent across all uses
- **IQ scoring**: mean = 100, standard deviation = 15
  - 68% score between 85–115 (1 SD from mean)
  - 95% score between 70–130 (2 SD)
  - ~2.2% score below 70 (intellectual disability threshold with adaptive functioning deficits)
  - ~2.2% score above 130 (gifted threshold)
- **Flynn effect** (James Flynn): each generation scores ~3 points higher per decade through 20th century; argues this reflects improvements in nutrition, education, abstract-reasoning exposure, test familiarity—not deeper intelligence increase
- **Cultural bias**: tests normed on specific populations; reflect cultural knowledge and reasoning patterns more familiar to some groups; *Larry P. v. Riles* (1979) found IQ tests had "discriminatory impact against Black children" when used for special education placement
- **What IQ predicts well**: academic performance in school (original design goal), some life outcomes (income, health); weaker prediction for practical success, creativity, leadership, happiness
- **WISC-V subtest structure**: 14 subtests → 5 indices → Full Scale IQ
  - Indices: Verbal Comprehension, Visual Spatial, Fluid Reasoning, Working Memory, Processing Speed
  - Reflects understanding that intelligence = multiple abilities across cognitive realms

Sources:
- Boake, C. (2002) "From Binet-Simon to Wechsler-Bellevue" *Journal of Clinical and Experimental Neuropsychology* 24(3):383–405
- Terman, L.M. (1916) *The Measurement of Intelligence*
- Wechsler, D. (1958) *The Measurement of Adult Intelligence*

### Concept 5: Nature and nurture—genes and environment
- **Twin studies**: Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart (Bouchard et al. 1990) found identical twins raised apart showed higher IQ correlation than fraternal twins raised together; suggests genetic component
- **Genetic component**: ~50–60% of variation in IQ across individuals explained by genetic differences
- **Environmental component**: substantial; improved nutrition, education, abstract reasoning exposure all raise IQ scores (Flynn effect)
- **Range of reaction**: genetic potential sets a range; environment determines where within that range individual develops
- **Poverty and brain development** (Kishiyama et al. 2009): children living in poverty show reduced activation in prefrontal cortex—region involved in reasoning, executive function; chronic stress literally changes brain structure
- **Arthur Jensen controversy (1969)**: published "How Much Can We Boost I.Q. and Achievement" in *Harvard Educational Review*; argued IQ determined by genetics; controversial conclusion that Level II (abstract) intelligence differed by race
- Jensen's claims not supported by evidence; his data explained by socioeconomic differences, stereotype threat, test cultural bias
- **Socioeconomic differences in IQ**: average 10–15 points; respond to environmental change (adoption studies, educational interventions)
- **Ethnic group differences in IQ**: shrink substantially when socioeconomic status controlled; stereotype threat is well-documented cause of performance depression
- **Heritability vs. genetic determinism**: high heritability ≠ unchangeable (height = 80% heritable, but average height increased 150 years with nutrition improvement)

### Concept 6: Learning disabilities
- **Definition**: specific neurological impairments in particular cognitive area(s); NOT global intellectual disability; can co-occur with average or above-average overall intelligence
- **Dyslexia** (most common learning disability): neurological mechanism for sound processing does not work properly; cannot connect letters to sounds; reading is slow, effortful; some memorize word shapes; some have strong comprehension if read to; letter reversals common; ~10% of children
- **Dysgraphia**: difficulty with written expression; physical act of writing challenging (letter formation, coordination); translating thought to written language difficult; student may speak fluently but struggle on written tests; occupational therapy sometimes helpful
- **Dyscalculia**: difficulty with mathematics; trouble recognizing quantities without counting, memorizing math facts, understanding numerals; specific to numerical reasoning; can excel in other areas
- **Comorbidity**: 30–70% of children with ADHD also have learning disability; often not identified until school age when reading/writing/math required
- **Treatment**: not about lowering standards; about removing barriers to access
  - Dyslexia: intensive phonics instruction, explicit letter-sound connection teaching
  - Dysgraphia: accommodations (typed responses, extra time), assistive technology
  - Dyscalculia: manipulatives, concrete representations, visual approaches rather than abstract
- **Key distinction from intellectual disability**: global limitations (ID) vs. specific domain difficulty (LD); intelligence intact in unaffected areas

Sources:

- Curtiss, S. (1981) "Dissociations between language and cognition" *Journal of Autism and Developmental Disorders* 11(1):15–30

## Myth corrections

1. **Left-brain/right-brain personality myth**: Both hemispheres do everything. Left hemisphere is dominant for language in most people, but both hemispheres process visual, auditory, spatial information. No such thing as "left-brained logical person" vs. "right-brained creative person."

2. **IQ is fixed at birth myth**: Twin studies show heritability, but heritability ≠ unchangeable. Adoption studies show 10–20 point IQ increases when children move from poor to wealthy environments. Flynn effect shows generation-to-generation increases. Environmental factors substantially affect intelligence.

3. **Race determines intelligence myth**: Ethnic group IQ differences are explained by socioeconomic factors, stereotype threat, test cultural bias—not genetics. When socioeconomic status controlled, gaps shrink dramatically. Jensen's claims scientifically unsupported.

4. **IQ tests measure overall intelligence myth**: IQ tests measure academic problem-solving, verbal reasoning, processing speed, working memory. They do NOT measure creativity, practical intelligence, emotional intelligence, wisdom, motivation, character. Predictive mainly for academics, not for real-world success.

5. **Learning disabled = intellectually disabled myth**: Dyslexia, dysgraphia, dyscalculia are specific neurological differences, not global intellectual limitations. A person with dyslexia might be brilliant at math, reasoning, verbal thinking—just cannot process letters efficiently.

## Trade-offs by concept

1. **Concepts/schemas**: Speed vs. accuracy; fast categorization creates persistent biases
2. **Algorithms vs. heuristics**: Precision vs. speed; algorithms guarantee correctness but are slow; heuristics are fast but fail systematically
3. **Intelligence theories**: Simplicity vs. comprehensiveness; Spearman's *g* is simple but misses genuine multifaceted nature; Gardner's 8 intelligences more comprehensive but lacks empirical support
4. **IQ testing**: Reliability and predictive validity for academics vs. cultural specificity and narrow scope; tests predict academic performance well but measure only slice of intelligence
5. **Nature-nurture**: Genetic influence is real; environmental effects are also substantial; interaction complex
6. **Learning disability identification**: Early identification helps intervention; but labels can stigmatize; must balance support against stereotyping

## Key mechanism for integration

The conjunction fallacy (Linda problem) illustrates all the machinery:
- **Concepts/schemas**: "feminist bank teller" activates schema that fits Linda's description
- **Prototype matching**: Linda matches prototype of feminist, not prototype of bank teller
- **Heuristic (representativeness)**: Judge probability by similarity to stereotype
- **Bias (availability)**: Vivid detailed description makes "feminist bank teller" more available mentally
- **Failure of algorithmic reasoning**: 85% of subjects know math of probability but ignore it in favor of intuitive judgment

Returns to this at end of chapter: understanding the machinery does not free you from it, but lets you notice when you are using it.
