# Pantry Notes — Chapter 16: Therapy and Treatment

## Research sources and primary citations

### Historical treatment

- Pinel, Philippe. La Salpêtrière (1795) — the founding moment of moral treatment and the removal of chains from psychiatric patients. Documented in historical records; Pinel did not publish extensively on the method itself.
- Dix, Dorothea Lynde. *Life and Labor of Dorothea Lynde Dix* (Tiffany, 1891 ed.). Primary advocate for asylum reform in the United States; led to creation of the first American mental asylums, though conditions in those asylums later became notorious.
- Blumberg, J. (2007). "A Brief History of the Salem Witch Trials." *Smithsonian Magazine*, October 24. — Documents the conflation of mental illness with witchcraft in early modern Europe; estimates suggest tens of thousands of mentally ill people were killed or imprisoned as witches.

### Deinstitutionalization

- National Institutes of Health. (2013). *Important events in NIMH history.* — Timeline of the 1963 Mental Retardation Facilities and Community Mental Health Centers Construction Act, signed by President Kennedy, which initiated the deinstitutionalization movement.
- Torrey, E. F. (1997). *Out of the Shadows: Confronting America's Mental Illness Crisis.* — Cites that in 1955, 558,239 severely mentally ill patients were institutionalized; by 1994, this had dropped by 92% as a proportion of population, though the actual number of deinstitutionalized people without housing increased due to population growth and inadequate community services.

### Psychotherapeutic approaches

#### Psychodynamic and psychoanalysis

- Freud, S. (1914). *Remembering, Repeating, and Working Through.* — On transference in psychoanalysis.
- Shedler, J. (2010). "The Efficacy of Psychodynamic Psychotherapy." *American Psychologist*, 65(2), 98–109. — Meta-analytic review showing psychodynamic therapy efficacy comparable to CBT for depression and anxiety disorders, though typically longer in duration.

#### Behavior therapy

- Jones, M. C. (1924). "A Laboratory Study of Fear: The Case of Peter." *Pedagogical Seminary*, 31, 308–315. — The foundational case showing desensitization of a fear response through gradual exposure paired with incompatible pleasant stimulus (food). The original experiment that demonstrated the mechanism later systematized by Wolpe.
- Wolpe, J. (1958). *Psychotherapy by Reciprocal Inhibition.* — Formalized systematic desensitization; the mechanism is that relaxation and anxiety are reciprocally inhibitory (cannot occur simultaneously).
- Pavlov, I. P. — Classical conditioning work underlying behavioral approaches; Wolpe's systematic desensitization is grounded in Pavlovian principles.

#### Cognitive therapy

- Beck, A. T., Rush, A. J., Shaw, B. F., & Emery, G. (1979). *Cognitive Therapy of Depression.* The Guilford Press. — Foundational text; Beck's observation was that depressed patients reported automatic negative thoughts that preceded mood changes, and that examining and challenging these thoughts reduced depression.

#### Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)

- Butlera, A. C., Chapmanb, J. E., Formanc, E. M., & Becka, A. T. (2006). "The Empirical Status of Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy: A Review of Meta-Analyses." *Clinical Psychology Review*, 26, 17–31. — Synthesis of meta-analyses showing CBT efficacy for PTSD, generalized anxiety disorder, depression, and social phobia.
- DeRubeis, R. J., Hollon, S. D., Amsterdam, J. D., Shelton, R. C., Young, P. R., Salomon, R. M., et al. (2005). "Cognitive Therapy vs Medications in the Treatment of Moderate to Severe Depression." *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 62(4), 409–416. — Direct comparison: CBT success rate 43%, medication success rate 50%, placebo 25%. The study is important for showing both are effective and the gap between them.
- Alavi, A., Sharifi, B., Ghanizadeh, A., & Dehbozorgi, G. (2013). "Effectiveness of Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy in Decreasing Suicidal Ideation and Hopelessness of the Adolescents with Previous Suicidal Attempts." *Iranian Journal of Pediatrics*, 23(4), 467–472. — Application of CBT to adolescent suicidality.

#### Humanistic therapy

- Rogers, C. (1951). *Client-Centered Psychotherapy.* Houghton-Mifflin. — Foundational text establishing the therapeutic conditions (unconditional positive regard, genuineness, empathy) as mechanisms of change.

#### Evidence comparing approaches (the Dodo bird verdict)

- Wampold, B. E. (2007). "Psychotherapy: The Humanistic (and Effective) Treatment." *American Psychologist*, 62(8), 857–873. doi:10.1037/0003-066X.62.8.857 — The major contemporary articulation of the contextual model; argues that the therapeutic relationship and common factors account for more variance in outcome than specific technique.
- Abbass, A., Kisely, S., & Kroenke, K. (2006). "Short-term Psychodynamic Psychotherapy for Somatic Disorders: Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Clinical Trials." *Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics*, 78, 265–274. — Finds no significant difference in efficacy between psychodynamic and other therapies for somatic disorders.
- Chorpita, B. F., Daleiden, E. L., Ebesutani, C., Young, J., Becker, K. D., Nakamura, B. J., et al. (2011). "Evidence-Based Treatments for Children and Adolescents: An Updated Review of Indicators of Efficacy and Effectiveness." *Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice*, 18, 154–172. — Updates the findings on comparative efficacy across modalities for child and adolescent treatments.
- Chambless, D. L., & Ollendick, T. H. (2001). "Empirically Supported Psychological Interventions: Controversies and Evidence." *Annual Review of Psychology*, 52, 685–716. — Critical examination of what "empirically supported" means and how the field designates treatments as evidence-based.

### Biomedical treatments

#### Antipsychotics

- Charpentier, Paul. Chlorpromazine synthesis, 1954. — Documented in pharmaceutical history; first antipsychotic; revolutionary for treating psychosis (hallucinations and delusions).

#### Antidepressants and the chemical imbalance theory

- Kirsch, I. (2008). *The Emperor's New Drugs: Exploding the Antidepressant Myth.* Basic Books. — Critical examination of SSRI efficacy; argues that much of the SSRI benefit in trials is due to placebo effect. Cites meta-analyses from the FDA's own data showing effect sizes smaller than often reported.

#### Device-based treatments

- Pagnin, D., de Queiroz, V., Pini, S., & Cassano, G. B. (2004). "Efficacy of ECT in Depression: A Meta-Analytic Review." *Journal of ECT*, 20, 13–20. — Meta-analysis showing ~85% improvement rate for severe treatment-resistant depression treated with ECT.
- Mayo Clinic. (2012). *Tests and Procedures: Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation.* — FDA approval 2008; used for treatment-resistant depression when other treatments have failed.
- Donahue, A. B. (2000). "Electroconvulsive Therapy and Memory Loss: A Personal Journey." *The Journal of ECT*, 162, 133–143. — Patient perspective on ECT; documents both efficacy and the memory side effects that make it a last-resort treatment.

#### Combination treatment

- Cuijpers, P., Sijbrandij, M., Koole, S. L., Andersson, G., Beekman, A. T., & Reynolds, C. F. (2014). "Adding Psychotherapy to Antidepressant Medication in Depression and Anxiety Disorders: A Meta-analysis." *World Psychiatry*, 13(1), 56–67. — Shows that psychotherapy combined with antidepressant medication is more effective than either alone for depression and anxiety.

### Substance use and addiction treatment

- Simpson, D. D. (1981). "Treatment for Drug Abuse." *Archives of General Psychiatry*, 38, 875–880. — Finding that addiction treatment requires minimum three months for positive outcome.
- National Institute on Drug Abuse. (2012). *Principles of Drug Addiction Treatment: A Research-Based Guide.* 3rd ed. — Comprehensive review of evidence-based approaches to substance use disorder treatment.
- Muñoz-Cuevas, F. J., Athilingam, J., Piscopo, D., & Wilbrecht, L. (2013). "Cocaine-Induced Structural Plasticity in Frontal Cortex Correlates with Conditioned Place Preference." *Nature Neuroscience*, 16, 1367–1369. — Mechanisms of addiction at the neurobiological level.

### Treatment access and disparities

- Alegría, M., Chatterji, P., Wells, K., Cao, Z., Chen, C. N., Takeuchi, D., et al. (2008). "Disparity in Depression Treatment Among Racial and Ethnic Minority Populations in the United States." *Psychiatric Services*, 59(11), 1264–1272. — Documents treatment disparities by race and ethnicity, even when controlling for income and insurance.
- Sue, D. W. (2001). "Multidimensional Facets of Cultural Competence." *Counseling Psychologist*, 29(6), 790–821. — Framework for cultural competence in therapy; emphasizes moving beyond Eurocentric approaches.
- Clement, S., Schauman, O., Graham, T., Maggioni, F., Evans-Lacko, S., Bezborodovs, N., et al. (2014). "What is the Impact of Mental Health-Related Stigma on Help-Seeking? A Systematic Review of Quantitative and Qualitative Studies." *Psychological Medicine*, 44, 1-17. — Meta-analytic review of stigma as a barrier to treatment.

### Epidemiology of treatment

- U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. (2017). *Mental Health: Results from the 2016 National Survey on Drug Use and Health.* — 18.9% of U.S. adults experienced mental illness in 2017; 14.8% received treatment.
- National Institute of Mental Health. (2017). *Any Disorder Among Children.* — ~13% of children ages 8–15 experience mental illness per year.
- World Health Organization estimate (cited in book): approximately two-thirds of people with mental disorders receive no treatment at all globally.
- U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development. (2011). *The 2010 Annual Homeless Assessment Report to Congress.* — 26% of homeless adults in shelters experience mental illness; demonstrates the service gap.

## Narrative and structural design notes

### Cold opens and scenes

**Chapter cold open:** Freud's Vienna consulting room, 1900. The choice of the couch, the mechanism of free association, the early hypothesis about talking as cure. This grounds the abstract concept of psychotherapy in a real moment of invention, showing that Freud was trying to solve a concrete problem (people with symptoms that seemed to have no physical cause) with a novel method (talk). The scene transitions to the modern moment (contemporary RCTs) to show the arc from intuition to measurement.

**Concept 1 cold open (psychotherapies):** The clinic scene — a therapist choosing among four frameworks for a new patient. This shows that the choice of approach is not abstract but practical and consequential. The four frameworks (psychodynamic, behavioral, cognitive, humanistic) are then explained as competing hypotheses about the mechanism of distress.

**Concept 2 cold open (biomedical):** Charpentier and chlorpromazine, 1954. A moment of serendipity (meant as antihistamine, discovered to treat psychosis) that opened the second major modality of treatment. This scene shows medication as an empirical discovery, not a theory-driven development. It also establishes the trade-off: medications work but the mechanism is not fully clear.

**Concept 3 cold open (evaluation and evidence):** The Wampold finding as a puzzle — different therapies produce similar outcomes. This is counterintuitive and forces the reader to think about what "different" might mean if the results are the same. The concept then unfolds the framework for understanding efficacy vs. effectiveness and what the Dodo bird verdict actually implies.

### Trade-offs (per concept)

- **Psychodynamic:** Depth vs. duration and cost. Can address patterns organized across a lifetime, but takes months or years.
- **Behavioral:** Measurability vs. breadth. Clear mechanistic effect for specific fears and habits, but may not address underlying meaning or broader patterns.
- **Cognitive:** Middle ground. Faster and more measurable than psychodynamic, more comprehensive than pure behavior, but relies on cognitive capacity and does not change real-world constraints.
- **CBT:** Breadth and empirical support vs. depth/existential questions. Strongest evidence base, but criticized as mechanistic.
- **Humanistic:** Authenticity and relational depth vs. measurability and prevalence. Difficult to research, less prominent in evidence-based medicine, but captures something true about healing.
- **Medication:** Speed and accessibility vs. side effects and discontinuation questions. Works quickly, but side effects are real, and long-term maintenance may be necessary.

### Worked examples

Each concept has one worked example:

1. **Concept 1 (psychotherapies):** The anxiety patient choosing among four frameworks. Shows how different frameworks generate different questions and hypotheses about the same presenting problem.

2. **Concept 2 (medication):** SSRI and the chemical imbalance story. Addresses the misleading narrative that depression is caused by low serotonin and SSRIs fix it. Explains what we know (SSRIs work, serotonin is involved, mechanism is more complex) and what we don't (why it takes weeks, what the actual mechanism is).

3. **Concept 3 (evidence):** Reading a meta-analysis. Shows how to interpret a finding (67% improvement with EMDR vs. 60% with CBT) by examining sample size, comparison group, outcome measures, population, duration. This is a critical-reading skill applied to the research literature.

### Integration section

Brings together the three concepts through a patient choice scenario. Shows that the choice of treatment involves efficacy evidence, the therapeutic relationship, real-world constraints (access, money, time), and the patient's own preferences and theory of their problem. The integration also highlights the honest unknowns: why therapeutic relationship predicts outcome so strongly, why SSRIs take weeks, why some people respond and others do not.

### Exercises (graduated)

- **Warm-up (16.1–16.3):** Naming mechanisms, understanding medication function, reading basic meta-analytic data.
- **Application (16.4–16.6):** Designing a treatment plan for a real case, assessing barriers in a specific scenario, interpreting a real study finding.
- **Synthesis (16.7–16.8):** Integrating multiple modalities in a complex case (PTSD with treatment trade-offs), modeling what would have to be true for the Dodo bird verdict to be overturned.
- **Challenge (16.9–16.10):** Imagining a future with neuroimaging biomarkers, and the book-level reflection on the arc of psychology from introspection to treatment.

### Chapter-level scale shift and moral arrival

The scale shift occurs in the integration and synthesis sections, moving from specific mechanisms (how SSRIs work, how CBT restructures thoughts, how exposure extinguishes fear) to the population level (why some people don't get treatment, what barriers exist, why the therapeutic relationship matters as much as technique). The moral arrival is in the final section: psychology is younger and messier than physics, but its measured findings affect billions of lives. The discipline has learned to measure outcomes, to remain humble about mechanisms, and to recognize the relational dimension of healing alongside the technical.

### Voice anchoring

This chapter is the book's closer. The voice is grounded in the earlier chapters' scaffolding (Freud in Chapter 11 on personality, the nervous system in Chapter 3, the diagnosis framework in Chapter 15) but moves to a more reflective register. The chapter is explicitly about the limits of understanding — we have treatments that work but do not fully understand why. This is Feynman's move: name what we do not understand rather than pretend the textbook closes the case. The author's voice is first-person when appropriate ("I do not fully understand...") but clinical and measured when discussing evidence, honoring the field's commitment to measurement and skepticism.

### Connection to book arc

This chapter completes the arc begun in Chapter 1. Psychology started by asking "How does the mind work?" (introspection, then behaviorism, then cognitivism). It then asked "What is the mind's biological basis?" (neuroscience, brain anatomy, neurotransmitters). It then asked "What are the major categories of human distress?" (diagnosis, disorders). Now it asks "How do we help?" The answer — through several approaches, measured by evidence, understood incompletely, but effective — is the destination of the entire book's journey. The reader finishes understanding not just the discipline but its relationship to human flourishing.
