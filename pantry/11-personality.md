## Specification moves (vague terms clarified)

1. **Personality** — Not character judgment or moral evaluation. The scientific term refers to enduring patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that (a) are consistent across time, (b) vary systematically between people, and (c) predict meaningful outcomes. Observable and measurable.

2. **Trait** — A characteristic way of behaving, thinking, or feeling. Traits are dimensions (you fall somewhere on a spectrum), not types (you are not simply "a person with trait X"). The Big Five are broad traits that subsume more specific facets.

3. **Psychosexual stages** — Freud's developmental theory proposing that personality forms through a sequence of childhood stages, each centered on a different body region. Lacks empirical support; included for historical context and as an example of an unfalsifiable theory.

4. **Defense mechanisms** — Unconscious processes Freud proposed to reduce anxiety from conflict between id and superego. Examples: repression (pushing thoughts out of awareness), projection (attributing your feelings to others), reaction formation (expressing the opposite of what you feel). These are theoretically plausible but difficult to test empirically.

5. **Self-actualization** — Maslow's term for realizing your full potential, becoming the best version of yourself. Vague but clinically useful; the goal of humanistic therapy. Hard to operationalize and measure.

6. **Self-concept** — Rogers's term for your beliefs and feelings about who you are. Two aspects: real self (who you believe you are) and ideal self (who you want to be). Congruence (similarity) is associated with better mental health. Measurable through questionnaires.

7. **Heritability** — The proportion of variance in a trait explained by genetic differences. Heritability of 0.50 for conscientiousness means that half the differences between people in conscientiousness can be attributed to genetic variation. Important: heritability is a population statistic, not a statement about individuals.

8. **Reciprocal determinism** — Bandura's framework: personality emerges from the ongoing interaction between person (thoughts, beliefs), behavior, and environment. None determines the others alone; all three are mutually influential.

9. **Self-efficacy** — Your belief in your ability to succeed at a task or in a domain. Domain-specific (you might have high self-efficacy in math but low in public speaking). Predicts persistence, effort, and achievement. Changeable through experience and feedback.

10. **Big Five factors** — Openness to experience (curiosity, creativity), Conscientiousness (responsibility, organization), Extraversion (sociability, assertiveness), Agreeableness (cooperation, compassion), Neuroticism/Emotional stability (anxiety/calm). Abbreviated OCEAN. Discovered through factor analysis; replicated across cultures; moderate to strong heritability; predict academic success, job performance, mental health, relationship satisfaction.

11. **Factor analysis** — A statistical technique that identifies underlying dimensions or latent constructs from patterns of correlations among variables. If many personality items correlate highly with one another but not with other items, they may reflect a single underlying factor. Researchers debate how many factors best describe personality; five emerged as a consensus.

12. **Selective migration** — The process by which people move to environments that match their personalities. Agreable people settle near family; open people move to diverse, innovative cities. Over time, regions accumulate residents with similar personality profiles.

## Myth corrections (explicit)

1. **Myers-Briggs has scientific validity.** False. MBTI has weak test-retest reliability. If you take it again in weeks, you often get a different type. The four dimensions do not map to Big Five structure. It does not predict behavior, job performance, or relationships better than chance. It is popular but scientifically weak.

2. **Personality is fixed.** Partially false. Big Five factors are relatively stable in adulthood, but not immutable. Conscientiousness increases in young adulthood. Agreeableness increases through middle age. People change through therapy, experience, and effort. Traits are resistant to change but not unchangeable.

3. **Handwriting analysis measures personality.** False. Graphology has no scientific support. Personality is not encoded in letter size or slant.

4. **Astrology predicts personality.** False. Birth date and astrological sign do not correlate with personality traits. This is a pseudoscience.

5. **The marshmallow test is a perfect predictor of life success.** Partially false. The original study (1989) showed strong associations. The modern replication (2018) found much weaker associations, highly sensitive to family background and cognitive ability. The trait is less stable, more contextual than originally believed.

6. **Personality is entirely genetic.** False. Heritability of 0.50 means half the variance, not that genes are everything. The other half comes from environment, experience, and culture.

7. **Personality is entirely environmental.** False. Twin studies show substantial heritability. But genes are not destiny; identical twins raised apart are similar but not identical in personality.

## Trade-offs summary (per concept section)

| Perspective | What You Gain | What You Lose |
|---|---|---|
| **Psychodynamic** | Rich theory of inner life; explains unconscious motivation | Lacks falsifiability; poor prediction of behavior; untestable concepts |
| **Trait (Big Five)** | Reliable measurement; cross-cultural validation; strong prediction of real outcomes | Limited depth on mechanism; reductionist; loses narrative richness |
| **Social-cognitive** | Dynamic, contextual, changeable; situation matters; hope for growth | Precision in prediction; complex; harder to standardize |
| **Humanistic** | Emphasizes growth, choice, self-actualization; optimistic | Vague mechanisms; not scientific; difficult to measure |

## Questions for further exploration

1. Why did the Big Five emerge as the consensus across independent factor analytic studies? Is it because five factors are truly fundamental, or partly because of researcher choices in statistical modeling?

2. How much of the universality of the Big Five across cultures reflects true human universals versus cultural translation artifacts (people understanding questionnaire items through their own cultural lens)?

3. What personality dimensions might non-Western cultures highlight that are not captured by OCEAN? How would you design a study to discover them?

4. Can personality change be sustained? Therapy often produces short-term changes; do they stick? Under what conditions?

5. Why is MBTI so popular despite weak scientific support? What does this tell us about the gap between scientific and popular psychology?

6. How do we explain the weakening of associations in the marshmallow test replication? What does this say about longitudinal personality research more broadly?

7. Is there a personality profile for mental health, or does mental health depend more on how personality traits interact with life circumstances and coping skills?

### Warm-up example (Big Five description)

**Friend/Family member: Sarah (older sister)**

- **Openness:** Medium-high. She reads widely, likes travel, enjoys trying new restaurants. But she prefers to plan trips carefully; spontaneity makes her uncomfortable. Maybe 60th percentile.
- **Conscientiousness:** Very high. She keeps a detailed calendar, never misses deadlines, is the person everyone calls when they need something organized. 85th percentile.
- **Extraversion:** Medium. She is comfortable in social situations and makes friends easily, but she also needs quiet time to recharge. Not a party person but not withdrawn either. 55th percentile.
- **Agreeableness:** High. She is kind, listens well, puts others' needs before her own sometimes to a fault. She avoids conflict. 75th percentile.
- **Neuroticism:** Medium. She worries about things, especially deadlines and whether she has done things well enough. But she does not catastrophize; she problem-solves. 60th percentile.

## Limitations and uncertainties

1. **Big Five convergence:** I remain uncertain whether five is the "true" number of personality dimensions or partly an artifact of factor-analytic choices. Cross-cultural research suggests at least five, but HEXACO adds a sixth (honesty-humility). The question remains somewhat open.

2. **Projective test validity:** The Rorschach with Exner scoring has acceptable reliability, but whether it actually measures personality or anxiety in the way proposed is unclear. I have represented the consensus (weak validity) but the debate continues.

3. **Mechanism of humanistic therapy:** I stated that unconditional positive regard helps people develop congruence, but the evidence is correlational. What actually heals in humanistic therapy—the regard itself, or the opportunity to reflect, or the relationship, or something else—is not fully clear.

4. **Personality change magnitude:** People do change on Big Five dimensions across the lifespan, but the changes are modest. I do not fully understand why therapy, coaching, or intention produces only small changes for most people when the stakes feel high. This deserves more attention.

5. **Cross-cultural personality measurement:** The Big Five replicates across cultures, but whether this reflects universals or successful cultural translation of Western constructs remains somewhat open. Indigenous personality research may be discovering dimensions we have missed.
