# Psychology

## Title
Psychology — How the Mind Works, How We Know, and What We Can't Yet Explain

## Subtitle
The standard introductory survey — research methods, biology, consciousness, sensation and perception, learning, memory, intelligence, development, emotion, personality, social behavior, industrial-organizational psychology, stress and health, disorders and treatment — written so the mechanism behind each finding is visible on the page.

## Author
Nik Bear Brown.

## Audience

This book is written for the undergraduate taking their first psychology course — typically a freshman or sophomore in their second semester, often fulfilling a behavioral-science requirement, often considering psychology as a major. They have heard pop-psychology claims their whole life. They are about to find out which of those claims is supported by careful research, which is partly true under specific conditions, and which is folklore.

Specifically:

- Psychology majors taking PSY 101 / Intro to Psychology.
- Pre-med, nursing, public-health, social-work, education, and criminal-justice students for whom psychology is a required prerequisite.
- Liberal-arts students fulfilling a behavioral-science distribution requirement.
- Anyone who has wondered whether they are an introvert, why they remember some things and forget others, and what the actual science says about the difference between sadness and depression.

This is not a clinical-psychology textbook (though Chapter 16 covers therapy and treatment). It is not a research-methods textbook (though Chapter 2 covers methods). It is the standard introductory survey: 16 chapters covering the breadth of contemporary psychology, with the underlying methods made visible at every step.

## Scope

The sixteen chapters cover, in order:

1. **Introduction to psychology** — what psychology is, the major perspectives, the history.
2. **Psychological research** — the scientific method as applied to behavior, descriptive vs. correlational vs. experimental designs, ethics.
3. **Biopsychology** — neurons, neurotransmitters, brain structure, the endocrine system, the genetic foundations of behavior.
4. **States of consciousness** — sleep, dreaming, hypnosis, meditation, drugs.
5. **Sensation and perception** — how the senses work, how the brain assembles them into experience.
6. **Learning** — classical conditioning, operant conditioning, observational learning.
7. **Thinking and intelligence** — concepts, problem-solving, intelligence testing, language.
8. **Memory** — encoding, storage, retrieval, why memory fails.
9. **Lifespan development** — physical, cognitive, and social development from infancy through old age.
10. **Emotion and motivation** — theories of emotion, hunger, sex, achievement.
11. **Personality** — psychodynamic, humanistic, trait, social-cognitive perspectives; assessment.
12. **Social psychology** — attribution, conformity, prejudice, attraction, helping, aggression.
13. **Industrial-organizational psychology** — the workplace as the laboratory; selection, motivation, leadership, work-life integration.
14. **Stress, lifestyle, and health** — the stress response, coping, the link between psychological state and physical health.
15. **Psychological disorders** — diagnosis, anxiety disorders, mood disorders, schizophrenia, personality disorders.
16. **Therapy and treatment** — psychotherapeutic approaches, biomedical treatments, evidence on what works.

Out of scope: graduate-level neuroscience, advanced statistics for psychology research (those belong in the methods sequence), specialized clinical training.

## Voice notes for this book

The Attenborough × Feynman v1.1 voice applies, with adjustments for psychology's particular shape.

**Cold opens earn their place.** A chapter on memory opens with H.M., the man whose hippocampi were surgically removed in 1953 to stop epileptic seizures and who could not form a single new long-term memory for the rest of his life. A chapter on social psychology opens at the Stanford Prison Experiment in 1971 — and the chapter then names what was wrong with that experiment as well as what it taught us. Real people, real experiments, real consequences.

**Methods are taught as part of every finding.** Psychology has a replication crisis (covered explicitly in Chapter 2). Every claim in the book is presented with the kind of evidence that would justify believing it. "Studies have shown" is not enough; the book names the experiment, the design, the sample size, and the limitation. When a finding has been challenged or failed to replicate, the chapter says so.

**Notation taught.** Statistical terms (correlation coefficient, effect size, p-value, sample size) get glossed in plain English when they appear. Psychology students often arrive without statistics; the book teaches as much as it needs as it goes, and references the workshop's introductory-statistics-bundle for deeper background.

**Grounded examples.** Real cases: Phineas Gage, H.M., Genie. Real experiments: Bandura's Bobo doll, Milgram, the Stanford prison study, Loftus's misinformation effect. Real disorders described as people experience them, not as decontextualized symptom lists.

**Honest about what we don't know.** Psychology is younger and messier than physics. The chapters say so. Where consciousness, free will, the mind-body relationship, or the boundary of mental disorder are genuinely unsettled, the chapter names the unsettled thing rather than pretending the textbook closes the case.

**Pop-psychology myths are corrected by name.** Left-brain/right-brain personality. The 10% of the brain. Subliminal advertising. The Mozart effect. The five stages of grief as a strict sequence. Each gets named, situated against the actual research, and corrected — not as a sidebar, but in the prose where the related correct mechanism is taught.

## Prerequisites assumed

- Reading comprehension at college level.
- Basic familiarity with research vocabulary (sample, control, hypothesis) — re-introduced where needed.
- Willingness to revise prior beliefs about how minds work.
- No prior psychology required.

## Author byline convention

Author is Nik Bear Brown. First-person voice when taking a position. "I find myself returning to H.M.'s case every time I teach memory — the loss is so total and the preservation of older memories so partial that it forces every student to revise what they thought memory was..."
