# Bookmap: Chapter 13 Source Analysis

*What a textbook author can extract from sources on Industrial-Organizational Psychology*

---

## Ideas harvest for the author

### **Puzzles that hook curiosity**
- The Hawthorne paradox: productivity increased when conditions worsened (observation-changes-behavior)
- The satisfaction paradox: people who like their jobs do not reliably perform better (r ≈ 0.30)
- The prediction paradox: a test that perfectly predicts success can still be wrong if the person leaves
- The adaptation effect: why a raise does not make people happier (hedonic treadmill)
- The charisma risk: how the best motivation (vision, inspiration) can also be most dangerous

### **Specification moves (clarifying vague terms)**
- "Predictive validity" — not just "the test works" but *which individuals it distinguishes* and *against what measurement of success*
- "Organizational culture" — not values on a poster but shared beliefs revealed through stories, symbols, what gets rewarded/punished
- "Job satisfaction" — measured correlation with work factors; not tightly linked to performance; adapted to over time
- "Engagement" — distinct from satisfaction; absorption and forward momentum rather than contentment
- "Psychological safety" — not niceness but belief that mistakes and questions are safe; created by specific leader behaviors
- "Expectancy theory" — three independent multipliers, not additive; any component at zero kills motivation

### **Mechanisms to explain in detail**
- How structured interviews reduce noise and reveal signal (vs. unstructured conversation)
- Why personality types have no validity while personality traits do (categories vs. spectrums)
- How Herzberg reverses intuition: paying more does not motivate; autonomy and growth do
- Why expectancy theory fails when organizations think it is simple (three factors, all necessary, all often overlooked)
- How psychological safety is created and destroyed (specific behaviors, not just leader presence)
- How organizational culture operates in leader's absence (internalized norms, stories, what feels normal)

### **Worked examples that make concepts concrete**
- Robert Jordan: rejected from police because IQ too high (illustrates: organizations consider retention likelihood)
- Call center bonus: low expectancy (unclear standards) × low valence (small bonus) × low instrumentality (metric not controllable) = no motivation
- Telework decision: wait times went up, so program ended — but unmeasured factors (commitment, trust, ergonomics) also changed
- Lewin's groups: democratic leadership produced engaged groups functioning in leader's absence; authoritarian suppressed aggression temporarily then released it

### **Common misconceptions to correct**
- "Personality types predict job performance" (MBTI has no validity; Big Five traits do modestly)
- "High job satisfaction causes high performance" (r ≈ 0.30; bidirectional; other factors matter more)
- "Pay people more and they will be happier" (they adapt; work content matters more)
- "Charismatic leaders are always better" (they inspire but carry risk of inspiring toward bad goals)
- "Organizational culture is the values on the poster" (revealed in what is rewarded, told, attended to)

### **Trade-offs to name**
- Predictive validity vs. candidate experience (high-validity tests can be invasive)
- Structured assessment vs. personal connection (structure improves prediction but feels less personal)
- Standardized jobs vs. autonomy (standardization allows measurement; autonomy improves engagement)
- Transformational vs. transactional leadership (inspiration vs. clarity; risk vs. stability)
- Easy-to-measure outcomes vs. hard-to-measure ones (wait times easy; commitment hard)

### **Scale-shift examples**
- One hire's job-fit difference: 20% lower performance across 1,000 workers = massive value
- Leadership style: democratic vs. authoritarian shows measurable difference in small groups; scale to organization and culture difference enormous
- Psychological safety: one team asks questions/catches errors; other hides problems until crises
- Measurement choices: decision looking good on easy metrics but bad on hard ones signals wrong optimization target

### **Adjacent concepts worth integrating**
- **Fairness and justice**: how employees perceive decisions as fair; link to commitment
- **Stress and health** (Chapter 14): health costs of poor job design, unclear roles, lack of control
- **Bias and discrimination**: how assessment tools encode bias (legal/ethical constraints)
- **Change management**: implementing changes (e.g., ending telework) while maintaining psychological safety
- **Diversity and inclusion**: how hiring, leadership, culture affect people from different backgrounds

## Limitations to flag

- **Hawthorne effect overstated**: reanalysis suggests smaller effect than claimed
- **Bidirectional causality**: satisfaction ↔ performance; culture ↔ success; leadership ↔ cohesion (unclear which direction stronger)
- **Western context bias**: most research on wealthy Western organizations; motivation/leadership norms differ across cultures
- **Historical context**: field emerged from management-control context (Taylor); some applications still aim to extract more work
- **Measurement challenges**: most important outcomes (psychological safety, commitment) hardest to measure
- **MBTI persistence**: no validity evidence yet widely used; research-practice gap
