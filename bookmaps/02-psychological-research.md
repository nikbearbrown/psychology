## Ideas, Mechanisms, Analogies, and Extensions

## What This Chapter Teaches (Scene-Anchored)

The chapter opens with the Open Science Collaboration's 2015 finding that only 36% of psychology studies replicate. From this authentic crisis, we build outward: *What does "replicate" mean? What makes research believable? How do we know what's true about behavior?* The chapter answers by teaching the scientific method as a cycle (not a line), then distinguishing three research designs by what they reveal and what they hide, then explaining why the replication crisis happened — not as failure but as science doing its job to correct itself.

The mechanism is simple: you can't separate method from finding. Every claim in psychology comes with assumptions about how it was tested. Learn to see those assumptions and you read research critically forever.

---

## Core Moves This Chapter Makes

### Move 1: Hook with authentic puzzle (Replication crisis)

**What works:** The 36% figure is shocking, emotionally real, and immediate. It contradicts the authority of published research that students have been taught to trust. This creates productive discomfort.

**Why it works:** It's not a hypothetical problem but a historical event (2015, ongoing). It involves real scientists and real papers, not abstractions. It sets up a mystery: why would most studies fail to replicate?

**Analogy available here:** Science as self-correcting machinery. The replication failures aren't a scandal; they're the system working, finding problems and fixing them. This is harder to see than "fraud! publish a retraction!" but more honest.

---

### Move 2: Unfold the scientific method — specify what "test" means

**What the chapter specifies:**

- **Theory** (general explanation) vs. **Hypothesis** (specific, testable prediction). Milgram didn't just wonder "are people evil?" He predicted: "Two-thirds of ordinary people will deliver severe shocks when told to by an authority figure."

- **Falsifiability** (the prediction could be wrong). Freud's theories are not falsifiable; anything can be explained as unconscious. Milgram's hypothesis is falsifiable; the data could have said no. This distinction changed what counts as science.

- **The cycle** (not linear). You don't do theory once. Theory generates hypothesis, hypothesis predicts observation, observation feeds back to revise theory. Deduction and induction alternate.

**Why it matters:** Without these distinctions, students think science is "collect data and see if your hunch was right." With them, they see that science is a conversation with reality, one you can lose.

---

### Move 3: Deep-dive on three research designs, each with trade-offs

**Descriptive research (Goodall):**
- **Reveals:** Behavior as it actually happens. Tool use, culture, individual personality, mothering. Richness.
- **Hides:** Causation. Why do some mothers teach and others don't? Generalizability. Do these patterns hold elsewhere?
- **Trade-off:** Depth vs. breadth.

**Correlational research (sleep and GPA):**
- **Reveals:** Relationships, prediction. If you know someone slept poorly, you can guess their GPA will be lower.
- **Hides:** Direction of causation. Does sleep cause GPA? Or does low GPA cause stress, which causes poor sleep? Or does a third variable (anxiety) drive both?
- **Trade-off:** Speed (surveys are fast) vs. clarity (correlation is ambiguous).

**Experimental research (Milgram):**
- **Reveals:** Causation. The experimenter's presence *causes* increased obedience.
- **Hides:** Real-world relevance. The lab is artificial. Would the same obedience appear if the experimenter weren't a scientist but a construction supervisor?
- **Trade-off:** Proof of causation vs. realism.

**Why structure it this way:** Each design answers different questions. Once you see this, you stop asking "which is best?" and start asking "which is right for what I want to know?"

---

### Move 4: Synthesize — explain the replication crisis through the lens of method

**The crisis is not "psychology is junk." It's:**

1. **Small samples** (N = 30) can't reliably detect even real effects. You find what you're looking for by luck.

2. **P-hacking** (testing many hypotheses, publishing the ones that worked) inflates false positives.

3. **Convenience sampling** (college students) makes findings non-generalizable.

4. **Publication bias** (null results hidden in file drawers) makes published literature look more consistent than reality.

5. **Researcher degrees of freedom** (flexible choices in analysis) give researchers unconscious power to find what they expect.

None of this requires dishonesty. It's how incentives work.

**The solution:** Pre-registration (lock your hypothesis before data), open data (transparency), larger samples (power), and willingness to publish null results.

**Why it works:** This frames the replication crisis not as a reason to distrust psychology but as a reason to understand *how* to read it. You're learning to ask hard questions, not to become cynical.

## Analogies That Earn Their Place

### The scientific method as a conversation
**Analogy:** Science is not you talking *at* reality (proclaiming a theory). It's you talking *with* reality (proposing, reality responding). If reality says "no" through replication failure, you revise.

**Breaks down:** Reality doesn't speak in words. It speaks in data. And data can be misinterpreted.

**When it works best:** Helping students see that Milgram's findings don't "prove" obedience is universal — they show that obedience appears under specific conditions. The conversation continues.

---

### Small samples as lucky guesses
**Analogy:** If you flip a coin 10 times, you might get 8 heads by chance. A small study with N = 30 is like flipping a small coin. You can get a false positive by luck. A large study with N = 1000 is like flipping 1000 times — luck averages out.

**Breaks down:** Unlike coin flips, study results depend on real effects, sample composition, measurement, and dozens of other factors. The analogy oversimplifies.

**When it works best:** Explaining why replication failures aren't fraud. They're inevitable with small samples.

---

### Correlation as binoculars
**Analogy:** A correlation lets you *see* a relationship (like binoculars let you see a distant object). But seeing something isn't the same as understanding it. You see two things moving together. Why? You can't tell from the binoculars alone.

**Breaks down:** You can look at binoculars and figure out their mechanism. Correlation doesn't give you that access.

**When it works best:** Making memorable the ice-cream-and-murder example. Students see the relationship but understand it without assuming causation.

## Extensions & Connections Forward

### Extension 1: What makes a good researcher?
Not someone who never fails. Someone who:
- Pre-registers hypotheses
- Reports effect sizes, not just p-values
- Collects large samples
- Shares data for replication
- Celebrates replication failures as learning

This is a character question, not a method question. It matters.

---

### Extension 2: How does bias work in research?
Not conscious fraud (though that happens). Unconscious bias:
- Researcher expects result X, unconsciously favors interpretations supporting X
- Analysis has flexibility; researcher chooses flexible options that yield X
- Some results are more publishable; researcher submits those

**Teaching point:** Honesty doesn't eliminate bias. Transparency and replication do.

---

### Extension 3: The generalizability problem
College students are WEIRD: Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic. Most psychology is tested on them. Do findings generalize to other cultures, ages, socioeconomic statuses?

**Research:** Henrich, J., Heine, S. J., & Norenzayan, A. (2010). "The weirdest people in the world?" *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 33(2-3), 61-83.

**Question for students:** Which psychological findings would you expect to generalize across cultures? Which might not?

---

### Extension 4: Replication in the wild
Many famous psychology findings have now been tested at scale:

- **Stanford Prison Experiment** (Zimbardo, 1971): Replication attempts suggest the original was less "natural" and more shaped by experimenter expectation than believed. The phenomenon may be real but not as universal as framed.

- **Implicit Association Test (IAT)** (Greenwald et al., 1998): Predicts discrimination better than explicit bias measures, but predictive validity is weaker than originally claimed. Useful, but not magic.

- **Ego depletion** (Baumeister et al., 2003): Most replications failed. The effect may exist in narrow conditions but doesn't generalize.

- **Obedience (Milgram, 1963):** Replicated across decades and countries. The phenomenon is real, though nuances emerge.

**Question:** What makes some findings robust and others fragile?

---

### Extension 5: Open questions in research ethics
- **Is deception ever justified?** Milgram deceived participants (they thought the shocks were real). He gained important knowledge. Was it worth the harm? Different people answer differently.

- **How do you balance rigor and kindness?** A truly random sample of "people" requires including vulnerable populations (homeless, incarcerated, etc.). But these groups are often targets of exploitative research. How do you research with integrity?

- **Who benefits from research?** If psychology is mostly studied on college students in wealthy countries, who controls the knowledge? What biases does that introduce?

## Specification Moves (The Chapter's Hidden Backbone)

### What "replicate" really means
Not "get the same result again." It means "run the same study with a new sample, following the same protocol, and see if the statistical conclusion holds." Many replications find smaller effects than originals, but still significant. That's success, not failure.

### What "correlation coefficient r = -0.29" actually tells you
It's the strength of a linear relationship. Specifically: it explains about r² = 0.08 or 8% of the variance. That means 92% of the variation in GPA comes from other factors. Sleep is related but not dominant.

### What "ecological validity" means
Not "the study isn't real" but "we don't know if findings in this artificial setting apply to real life." Milgram's lab is artificial. Whether the obedience he found appears in other settings is a separate question.

### What "p < 0.05" actually means
It means: "If there were no real effect, there's a less than 5% chance we'd see a result this extreme by random variation." It does *not* mean: "The effect is real" or "It will replicate" or "It's important."

## Misunderstandings to Prevent

### Misunderstanding 1: Replication crisis = psychology is fake
**Counter:** Physics has replication crises too (cold fusion). The issue is incentive structures and statistical practices, not that psychologists are dishonest.

### Misunderstanding 2: Experiments are always better than correlations
**Counter:** You can't experiment on everything. You can't randomly assign people to childhoods or political systems. For some questions, correlation is what you get.

### Misunderstanding 3: Big sample size = automatically valid
**Counter:** A biased sample (only college students) stays biased, no matter how many. Sample size and sample representativeness are different.

### Misunderstanding 4: Peer review catches all errors
**Counter:** Peer reviewers are overworked, anonymous, and motivated to be permissive (they don't want reviewers blocking *their* papers). Peer review is a filter, not perfection.

### Misunderstanding 5: The replication crisis means we should ignore psychology
**Counter:** Understanding how science works — including its flaws — makes you a *better* consumer of research, not a dismisser of it.

## If Teaching This Chapter

### Discussion prompts
- "If a study fails to replicate, does that prove it was wrong to begin with?" (Answer: No. It might have been a real effect in the original sample but not generalizable. Or it might have been a false positive.)
- "Why would a researcher p-hack?" (Answer: Not intentionally. But the incentives — publish novel findings or perish — create unconscious pressure to find what's publishable.)
- "Is Milgram's obedience study ethical?" (Answer: By modern standards, no. By 1963 standards, it was approved. But modern students can think through what protections would be needed.)

### Hands-on activities
- **Design an experiment:** Students propose a hypothesis (maybe something from their own experience), specify IV and DV, identify confounds, design control group.
- **Pre-register a study:** Walk through registering a hypothesis on OSF. Students see how it locks in the prediction.
- **Critique a replication failure:** Give students a paper and a replication attempt. Why did the replication fail? (Small sample? Different population? Different operational definition?)

## Summary: What a Student Should Take Away

1. **The scientific method is a cycle, not a line.** Observation feeds theory, theory generates hypothesis, hypothesis predicts observation, observation revises theory.

2. **Three designs, three trade-offs.** Descriptive reveals what happens but not why. Correlation reveals relationships but not causation. Experiment proves causation but may lack real-world relevance.

3. **The replication crisis is not a scandal; it's science working.** When 64% of studies fail to replicate, the system is finding problems and fixing them.

4. **Methods determine credibility.** You read research not by trusting the conclusion but by understanding how it was tested. Was the sample large? Diverse? Pre-registered? Transparent?

5. **Replication, not reputation.** A famous researcher's name doesn't guarantee a finding will hold. A careful, transparent, large-sample study does.
