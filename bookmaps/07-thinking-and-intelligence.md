# Bookmap: Chapter 7 — Thinking and Intelligence

## Source analysis: Source chapter structure (7 original files, ~18K words)

This bookmap analyzes the source chapter structure and harvests writing ideas, structural patterns, and gaps for the Attenborough × Feynman (narrative-explanatory) treatment.

---

## File-by-file breakdown

### File 1: Introduction + References (m82203)

*Content*: Chapter hook (questions about problem-solving, concept formation, language, intelligence variation); introduction to cognitive psychology; preview of chapter topics.

**What this teaches**: 
- Cognitive psychology is the study of how we think, not just observable behavior
- Intelligence is complex—"street smarts" raises questions about IQ test validity
- High-level cognitive processes are what distinguish humans

**Narrative opportunity**: The source does not name a specific person or puzzle. It announces what the chapter will cover in abstract terms. Attenborough-style narrative would open with a specific case: H.M. (memory), Genie (language), or—better for this chapter—Kahneman's Linda problem. Specific, named, puzzling.

**Structural note**: Source opens with three abstract questions. Attenborough voice opens with a scene.

**Harvest**:
- Pattern: The source lists three substantive questions at the start. Could be repurposed as the "genuine puzzle" that motivates the entire chapter, not as an announcement.
- Angle: "What makes humans distinct" is a valuable framing; could structure around the machinery that enables both intelligence and error.

### File 2: Cognition, Concepts, Prototypes, Schemas (m82205)

*Content*: Detailed explanation of concepts, prototypes, natural vs. artificial concepts, schemas (role and event schemas).

**What this teaches**:
- Concepts are categories built from observation or definition
- Prototypes are the best examples, used for fast categorization
- Schemas organize related concepts; event schemas are routines
- Schemas enable efficient behavior but can be difficult to override

**Narrative strengths**:
- "File cabinet" metaphor for concepts (accessible, concrete)
- Elevator event schema example (vivid, relatable)
- Texting-while-driving schema (real consequence, contemporary, personal)
- Role schema example: firefighter showing how assumptions shape judgment before knowing the person

**Weaknesses in source**:
- Very definition-heavy; abstract framing
- No cold open tying concepts to a puzzle or surprise
- Firefighter example is mentioned but not developed as a teaching moment about stereotype and prototype
- The power of schemas is illustrated but not deeply explored (why are they so hard to change?)

**Harvest**:
- **Puzzle angle**: Why does your brain label someone as "leadership" before they say anything? (connects to prototype bias in hiring)
- **Scene opportunity**: Could open with job interview or hiring decision where prototype shapes perception
- **Mechanism**: Explain *why* schemas are hard to override (automaticity, dual-process theory)
- **Trade-off**: Fast thinking vs. accurate thinking—schemas enable one, create risk of the other
- **Misconception to address**: "Prototypes are rules." Penguins are birds but do not match bird prototype; prototype is shortcut, not definition

**Structural note**: Source presents concepts in order (concepts → prototypes → schemas). This is logical but abstract. Attenborough would show a concrete scenario first, *then* name the machinery.

### File 3: Language and Thought (m82206)

*Content*: Language components (lexicon, grammar, phonemes, morphemes, semantics, syntax), language development (babbling stage, critical period, case of Genie), language and thought (Sapir-Whorf, Boroditsky's time research, color naming research).

**What this teaches**:
- Language has specific components and developmental stages
- There is a critical period for language acquisition
- Language may influence thought (linguistic determinism)

**Narrative strengths**:
- Case of Genie (powerful, tragic, real)
- Sapir-Whorf hypothesis is historically situated
- Boroditsky's Mandarin time research is concrete and surprising
- Dani color naming research (testing linguistic relativity)

**Weaknesses in source**:
- Language section feels adjacent to the main chapter on thinking and intelligence
- Not well integrated with rest of chapter
- No clear connection to problem-solving or intelligence theories

**Note on voice**: This section is more declarative than narrative. Could be restructured to emphasize mechanism (how does language shape thought?) rather than listing examples.

**Harvest**:
- **Genie case** as illustration of critical period and language necessity for cognitive development
- **Boroditsky research** as demonstration that language does influence some cognitive processes (but not all; culture matters)
- **Mechanisms of linguistic influence**: Not total determinism (Dani can perceive colors without words for them), but language affects processing speed and memory
- **Why this matters for intelligence**: Language is intertwined with thinking; but language is not the limit of thought

**Decision point**: This entire section might be de-emphasized in Attenborough treatment. Language is important, but it is somewhat separate from thinking (concepts/schemas/heuristics) and intelligence (IQ, theories). The source chapter conflates thinking broadly with language specifically. Attenborough version would keep Genie as an example of critical period, but focus on core thinking machinery.

### File 4: Problem-solving and Biases (m82207)

*Content*: Problem-solving strategies (trial-and-error, algorithms, heuristics, working backward, breaking into steps), pitfalls to problem-solving (mental set, functional fixedness, biases: anchoring, confirmation, hindsight, representative, availability).

**What this teaches**:
- Different strategies suit different problems
- Heuristics are mental shortcuts; they usually work but fail systematically
- Cognitive biases are predictable patterns of error

**Narrative strengths**:
- Concrete problem-solving examples (broken printer, route detour, wedding planning)
- Functional fixedness well-illustrated (candle problem, Apollo 13)
- Biases are named and exemplified (anchoring with housing market, representative with professor stereotype)
- Connection to dual-process theory implicit (fast heuristic, slow algorithmic)

**Weaknesses in source**:
- Still quite abstract and definition-heavy
- Puzzles presented but solutions not deeply explored
- No through-line about *why* humans use heuristics (efficiency in face of complexity)
- Biases presented as individual failings rather than systematic features of how thinking works

**Harvest**:
- **Opening puzzle**: Kahneman's conjunction fallacy (Linda problem) as illustration of heuristic failure
- **Mechanism**: Heuristics are *faster than careful reasoning*; necessary for practical life; fail in predictable ways when conditions change
- **Connection to concepts/prototypes**: Representativeness bias is prototype matching applied to probability judgment
- **Availability bias**: Vivid examples override statistical thinking
- **Anchoring bias**: First piece of information becomes reference point for all subsequent judgments
- **Hindsight bias**: Outcome makes past feel inevitable (important for understanding causality discussion in later chapters)
- **Confirmation bias**: You notice what confirms your schema, filter out contradictions (connects to concept sections)

**Structural opportunity**: The source presents problem-solving strategies, then biases as separate section. Could be integrated: heuristics as strategies that usually work, biases as the conditions under which they fail.

### File 5: Intelligence Theories (m82208)

*Content*: Intelligence definitions over time (Spearman's *g*, Cattell's fluid/crystallized, Sternberg's triarchic, Gardner's multiple intelligences, emotional intelligence, CHC theory), creativity (divergent vs. convergent thinking).

**What this teaches**:
- Intelligence is not a single thing
- Multiple theories exist; each captures something real
- No single theory is complete
- Creativity is related to intelligence but distinct

**Narrative strengths**:
- Theories presented chronologically and comparatively
- Real-world examples for each theory (Sternberg: novel solutions in camping, street smarts)
- Triarchic theory is well-illustrated (analytical, practical, creative)
- Gardner's theory mentioned with both strengths (inclusive) and criticisms (empirically weak)
- Emotional intelligence addressed with both promise (Goleman) and skepticism (Locke)

**Weaknesses in source**:
- Abstract presentation of each theory
- No cold open showing why we care about what intelligence is
- The Oprah example (opening of m82208 section) is powerful but isolated; not used to structure the later discussion
- Limited discussion of what each theory explains well vs. poorly
- No genuine engagement with the question: what does intelligence *actually* measure?

**Harvest**:
- **Oprah opening** (born in poverty, abuse, develops exceptional ability) as illustration that intelligence is multifaceted and responsive to opportunity
- **Why multiple theories exist**: Intelligence is genuinely multifaceted; different theories emphasize different aspects
- **Spearman's insight** (there is something general underlying cognition) is valuable even if not the whole story
- **Cattell's insight** (fluid and crystallized intelligence differ with age) explains otherwise puzzling age differences
- **Sternberg's insight** (real-world success requires practical intelligence, not just analytical) explains why high IQ does not guarantee success
- **Gardner's insight** (intelligence is more varied than academics recognize) is appealing and has influenced education, even if empirically weak
- **The CHC integration** (hierarchical: general at top, broad abilities in middle, narrow measurable abilities at bottom) is a sophisticated synthesis
- **Mechanism**: Each theory responds to limitations of previous theories; together they show that intelligence is multifaceted

**Structural note**: Source presents theories in order. Attenborough would open with Oprah's case, use it to motivate the question ("what is intelligence?"), then use the theories to answer parts of the question, acknowledge what each misses, and return to Oprah at the end.

### File 6: Intelligence Testing (m82209)

*Content*: History of IQ testing (Binet, Terman, Wechsler), norming and standardization, bell curve and standard deviations, Flynn effect, IQ test structure (WAIS, WISC), intellectual disability diagnosis, gifted identification, uses and limitations of IQ testing, case studies (*Atkins v. Virginia*, Candace's case).

**What this teaches**:
- IQ tests are reliable and predictive for academics
- IQ scores follow a normal distribution (bell curve)
- Each generation scores higher (Flynn effect)
- IQ tests have cultural bias; implications for diagnosis and educational placement
- IQ tests do not measure all forms of intelligence

**Narrative strengths**:
- Historical development is clear (Binet → Terman → Wechsler)
- Flynn effect is surprising and well-explained
- Bell curve is well-illustrated
- *Atkins v. Virginia* case is real, consequential, and illustrates limits of IQ testing in justice system
- Candace's case (failing student with IQ 68) shows how testing can identify students who need support
- Cultural bias (*Larry P. v. Riles*) is concrete legal outcome

**Weaknesses in source**:
- Very information-heavy; risk of losing mechanism in details
- Flynn effect explained with multiple possibilities; does not deeply explore the implication: IQ is measuring something about the interaction between person and environment, not a fixed trait
- No deep exploration of what IQ tests actually measure vs. what they miss
- Cultural bias section is important but could be connected more clearly to earlier discussion of concepts/schemas (test items reflect cultural knowledge)

**Harvest**:
- **Cold open opportunity**: Return to Kahneman's Linda problem. IQ tests measure the kind of reasoning shown in that problem. But that one type of reasoning is not "intelligence" in the full sense.
- **Flynn effect mechanism**: IQ scores are not measuring fixed capacity; they measure performance in a context. Improving nutrition, education, abstract-reasoning exposure raises scores. This shows that what we call "intelligence" is responsive to environment.
- **What IQ predicts well**: Academic performance (the original design goal), some life outcomes (income, health—weak correlations)
- **What IQ does not predict well**: Practical success, creativity, leadership, happiness
- **Bell curve insight**: Understanding standard deviations matters for interpretation. A score of 130 is not "twice as intelligent" as 100; it is 2 SDs above average, meaning top 2% of population.
- **Norming and standardization matter**: Tests must be administered consistently to large representative samples. If sample is not representative (e.g., all white children in 1970s California), test is biased.
- **Cultural specificity**: Test items reflect cultural knowledge. Knowing the word for "regatta" is not about intelligence; it is about cultural exposure. But IQ tests weight such knowledge.

**Structural note**: Source is fact-heavy. Attenborough treatment would reduce information density and increase mechanism: What is actually being measured? Why does it work for some purposes and not others? What does the Flynn effect tell us about what intelligence is?

### File 7: Genetics, Environment, Learning Disabilities (m82211)

*Content*: Nature-nurture debate for intelligence (twin studies, heritability, range of reaction, poverty effects on brain development), Arthur Jensen's controversial claims about genetics and race, socioeconomic differences in IQ, learning disabilities (dyslexia, dysgraphia, dyscalculia), distinction between learning and intellectual disabilities.

**What this teaches**:
- Intelligence has both genetic and environmental components
- Heritability does not mean unchangeable
- IQ differences between ethnic groups are explained by environment and bias, not genetics
- Learning disabilities are specific neurological differences, not global intellectual limitations

**Narrative strengths**:
- Oprah Winfrey opening (born into poverty and abuse, develops remarkable abilities) illustrates resilience and environmental responsiveness
- Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart is concrete (identical twins separated at birth, correlated IQs)
- Range of reaction concept explained clearly
- Kishiyama study (poverty and prefrontal cortex activation) shows mechanism: stress literally changes brain structure
- Jensen controversy is historically situated and critiqued
- Stereotype threat research is powerful (identical performance gap disappears when test is framed as "problem-solving" instead of "ability")
- Learning disability cases (dyslexia, dysgraphia, dyscalculia) are clearly distinguished from intellectual disability
- Genie case (from earlier in chapter) provides evidence for critical period and language-thought connection

**Weaknesses in source**:
- Nature-nurture discussion is somewhat abstract; range of reaction concept introduced but not deeply explored
- Jensen section could be more critical
- Stereotype threat mentioned briefly but not used as a central mechanism
- Learning disabilities section is somewhat disconnected from rest of chapter (feels like appendix)

**Harvest**:
- **Oprah opening** (reintroduced from earlier section): born in poverty and abuse, yet develops exceptional intelligence. Why? Genetic resilience? Environmental supports? Motivation? Probably all three, interacting. This is real data on nature-nurture interaction.
- **Heritability is not genetic determinism**: High heritability ≠ unchangeable. Height is 80% heritable, but average height increased 150 years with nutrition. Intelligence is ~50% heritable, but Flynn effect shows scores rise when environment improves.
- **Range of reaction mechanism**: Each person has a genetic range (possible IQs they could reach given different environments). Environment determines where within that range they develop. Same environmental improvement (e.g., from poverty to wealth) raises all three, but each reaches different absolute level.
- **Poverty and brain development (Kishiyama)**: Chronic stress of poverty changes prefrontal cortex structure—not through lack of education but through neurobiological stress response. This is not motivation; this is biology.
- **Group differences in IQ are not genetic**: When socioeconomic status is controlled, ethnic group differences shrink. Stereotype threat explains remaining differences. Genetic claims do not fit data and have dark history (eugenics, forced sterilization).
- **Stereotype threat mechanism**: Performance depression when aware of negative stereotypes. Same person, same test; framing as "problem-solving" (no stereotype threat) vs. "ability measure" (stereotype threat) produces different results. This is psychological, not biological.
- **Learning disabilities vs. intellectual disabilities**: Critical distinction. LD = specific area affected (dyslexia, dyscalculia) with overall intelligence intact. ID = global cognitive limitation. Interventions completely different.
- **Dyslexia mechanism**: Letter-sound processing does not work properly neurologically. Not low motivation, not low intelligence; neurological difference in how letters are processed. Some develop compensation strategies (memorizing word shapes); others never fully overcome decoding difficulty despite strong reasoning.

**Structural note**: Learning disabilities section could be separated into its own concept section or kept as part of intelligence testing. Attenborough treatment would keep it integrated, showing how understanding intelligence helps us recognize when someone struggles with specific domains while excelling in others.

### Angles the source covers adequately:
- History of intelligence testing
- Bell curve and standard deviations
- Multiple theories of intelligence
- Learning disabilities vs. intellectual disabilities

### Angles the source touches but does not deeply explore:
- **Heuristics as adaptation**: Why do humans use mental shortcuts? Because we evolved in environments with incomplete information and no time for deliberation. The shortcuts that sometimes lead us astray are the same shortcuts that let us survive.
- **Prototype bias in real contexts**: How prototypes shape hiring, educational opportunity, justice system outcomes (not just academic exercises)
- **Creativity as form of intelligence**: Source mentions but does not deeply explore. What makes creativity hard to measure? What does it tell us about intelligence?
- **Emotional intelligence**: Mentioned skeptically; actual evidence mixed
- **Cultural intelligence**: Named but not developed

### Angles the source does not cover:
- **How to use IQ testing well**: What questions can it answer? What are its limits? When should it be used? When should it not?
- **How to recognize your own biases**: Metacognition. How do you notice when you are using a heuristic vs. careful reasoning?
- **Historical harm from IQ testing**: Eugenics, forced sterilization, discrimination. Not adequately addressed.
- **Why understanding intelligence matters**: What difference does it make in practice? How should society respond to the evidence that intelligence is multifaceted, environmentally responsive, and culturally variable?

## Final assessment

The source chapter is comprehensive and well-sourced. The main challenge for Attenborough treatment is **reducing abstraction** and **increasing mechanism**. The source tells the reader what each concept is; Attenborough treatment should show the reader how each concept works in a concrete scenario, then name it.

The source also conflates **thinking broadly** (concepts, schemas, problem-solving) with **intelligence specifically** (IQ, theories, testing). Attenborough treatment would tighten the focus: Thinking is the machinery. Intelligence theories are different ways of conceptualizing what thinking enables. IQ testing is one (narrow) way of measuring thinking. Understanding the machinery lets you recognize both the value and the limits of any measurement.

**Key structural choice**: Whether to include language section (Genie, Sapir-Whorf, Boroditsky). Probably de-emphasize it. Language is important, but it is tangential to core thinking mechanisms and intelligence theories. Keep Genie as an example of critical period, but use that example to support the larger claim that thinking develops in interaction with environment—not as a separate section on language.
