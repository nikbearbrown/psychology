**Source files analyzed:**

## Concepts chosen for deep treatment

### 1. **What psychology is** (Concept 1 in chapter)
**Source material:** Primarily 02-m82162.md

**Source scaffolding:**
- Definition: "scientific study of mind and behavior"
- Emphasis on the scientific method, hypothesis testing, replication
- Distinction between psychology and philosophy
- Psychology as both biological and social science
- Emphasis on critical thinking skills developed in psychology education

**How transformed:**
- Added cold open (introducing ambiguity of studying the mind through behavior)
- Added etymology (psyche + logos)
- Added **introspection-reliability trade-off** as central tension
- Added the 10% brain myth as worked example (grounding the abstract in a concrete misconception)
- Added contrast with psychiatry and other disciplines
- Shortened "why study psychology" section to essentials; deferred career details to Chapter 2 context notes

**What works:**
- The definition is clear and students understand why psychology is a science
- The examples of fields that don't count as psychology are helpful
- The emphasis on scientific method is appropriate for Chapter 1

**What was changed:**
- Removed "quiz" questions (end-of-section review). The chapter now has integrated exercises.
- Removed "Link to Learning" callout to external video (kept in chapter but as footnote option)
- Reframed "critical thinking" not as a skill to learn but as a discipline psychology teaches by example

### 2. **The history of psychology** (Concept 2 in chapter)
**Source material:** Primarily 03-m82163.md

**Source scaffolding:**
- Wundt and structuralism (introspection, reaction time)
- William James and functionalism (adaptation, Darwin)
- Freud and psychoanalytic theory (unconscious, childhood)
- Gestalt psychology (whole-ness, perception)
- Behaviorism (Pavlov, Watson, Skinner)
- Humanism (Maslow, Rogers)
- Cognitive revolution (Chomsky, Neisser)
- Feminist psychology (Weisstein, Crawford & Marecek)
- Multicultural and cross-cultural psychology (WEIRD populations, Clarks, Sumner, Bernal, Sanchez)

**How transformed:**
- Reordered for narrative flow and causal connection (Wundt → James as reaction to Wundt → Freud as alternative → Behaviorism as reaction to introspection-focused schools → Humanism as reaction to behaviorism → Cognitive revolution as integration)
- Added cold open showing James criticizing introspection
- Added **trade-off** for each school: what it revealed vs. what it missed
- Elevated female and non-white contributors (Margaret Floy Washburn, Mary Whiton Calkins, Martha Bernal, Inez Beverly Prosser, Francis Cecil Sumner, Mamie Phipps Clark, Kenneth Clark) from sidebar to main narrative
- Added named researchers with dates and specific contributions
- Simplified the Feminist Psychology and Multicultural Psychology sections into a more concise narrative about the field's biases and corrections
- Removed detailed "learning objectives" for each historical period; these are now embedded in prose

**What works:**
- The narrative arc (introspection → frustration with introspection → behaviorism → frustration with behaviorism → return to mind) is clear
- Each school's contribution is named
- Trade-offs show that no school was "right" or "wrong," but incomplete
- The history of exclusion is named rather than hidden

**What was challenged:**
- The source material treats Feminist Psychology and Multicultural Psychology as separate sections at the end, as though they were afterthoughts. The revision integrates them into the main narrative as corrections and expansions of the field's scope.
- The source material emphasizes that Freud's ideas are "controversial" but then says some modern iterations are "empirically demonstrated to be effective." This tension is acknowledged in the chapter but not fully resolved — that's appropriate for a chapter that should show unsettled questions.

**Source notes on what was deferred:**
- Deep detail on Piaget's stages is deferred to Chapter 9 (Lifespan Development)
- Deep detail on psychoanalytic theory beyond Freud's basic ideas is deferred to Chapter 11 (Personality) and Chapter 16 (Therapy)
- The "Link to Learning" about Carl Rogers's therapeutic approach is deferred

## Structural decisions

### Why three concepts instead of a different breakdown?

The chapter could have been organized as:
1. (A) What is psychology? (B) Why is it a science? (C) What does it study? (D) Major perspectives (E) Subfields
2. (A) Defining psychology (B) History (C) Contemporary practice (D) Careers

Instead, I chose:
1. (A) **What psychology is** — Definition, core concepts, epistemology, what it is not
2. (B) **The history** — How psychology developed, major schools, their trade-offs
3. (C) **Contemporary subfields** — What psychologists actually do now

This structure:
- Frontloads the definition and disciplinary identity (students need to understand what they're studying)
- Uses history as a lens to show how the field resolved tensions and expanded
- Ends with contemporary practice, showing that history is not over — the field is still evolving

### Integration section rationale

Rather than summarizing each concept separately, the Integration section shows how the three concepts connect: the definition makes sense in light of the history; the history explains why there are multiple contemporary subfields; and the contemporary practice reflects the integration of historical insights.

## Critical decisions made

1. **On Freud:** The source material is conflicted about Freud (controversial but influential). The chapter acknowledges this: Freud was historically important, some ideas are supported, many are not. This honesty is appropriate.

2. **On bias in psychology:** The source material treats the historical exclusion of women and people of color as a sidebar. The chapter integrates it into the main narrative, showing that the field's early biases shaped what we know and that correction is ongoing.

3. **On scientific rigor:** The chapter emphasizes that psychology is a science, but a young one. It's rigorous but incomplete. This is honest without undermining the discipline.

4. **On pop-psychology:** Rather than dismissing pop-psychology as "just wrong," the chapter shows that pop-claims often contain a grain of truth (usually misunderstood or oversimplified). This is more pedagogically useful than mockery.

5. **On careers:** Careers information was deferred. While important, it's not central to *understanding* what psychology is. A reader who finishes this chapter should understand the discipline's scope and history, not primarily its career paths.
