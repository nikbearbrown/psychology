# Bookmap: Chapter 14 Source Analysis

## Source Overview

This chapter draws on primary sources spanning from Hans Selye's 1936 foundational observation of the general adaptation syndrome through contemporary research on telomeres, social support, and discrimination. The intellectual architecture rests on Selye, Cannon, and Lazarus's process-based model of stress appraisal, with mechanisms grounded in psychoneuroimmunology (Kiecolt-Glaser, Cohen, Segerstrom).

---

## Core Concepts and Their Sources

### The General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye, 1936–1976)

**What the source teaches:** Selye's observation that diverse stressors (cold, surgery, toxins) produce an identical physiological pattern (adrenal enlargement, thymus shrinkage, gastric ulceration) reveals that the body has a *nonspecific* response to threat, not individual responses to individual stressors. This three-stage model (alarm, resistance, exhaustion) describes the temporal arc of stress response.

**How the chapter uses it:** As the organizing framework. Selye provides the empirical foundation for understanding why chronic stress is damaging — exhaustion stage depletes resources. His observation is the intellectual ancestor of every modern stress study.

**What it doesn't do:** Selye's model is response-based; it does not address appraisal or cognitive factors. Later work (Lazarus) corrects this gap.

**Mechanism the student learns:** The body responds to stress holistically, not with individual patches. When the response is sustained, tissues wear out.

---

### Appraisal and Coping (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984)

**What the source teaches:** Stress is not the event; it is the *process* of encountering an event, appraising its threat and one's capacity to manage it, and responding accordingly. Two appraisals matter: primary (How threatening is this?) and secondary (What resources do I have?). Coping is the mental and behavioral effort to manage the stress and emotions.

**How the chapter uses it:** As the corrective to stimulus-based definitions of stress. The same job is stressful to one person and energizing to another based on appraisal. This is why individual differences matter and why stress management is possible — appraisals can be changed.

**What it doesn't do:** Lazarus does not describe the physiology. That comes from Cannon and Selye. Lazarus explains why physiology differs across people encountering the same event.

**Mechanism the student learns:** Meaning matters. How you interpret a threat changes your response to it. This is one of the most empowering insights — stress is not fixed by the event; it depends on appraisal.

---

### The Psychoneuroimmunology Framework

**Key sources:** Kiecolt-Glaser & Glaser (2005) on immune suppression; Segerstrom & Miller (2004) meta-analysis; Cohen et al. (1998) on stressors and colds.

**What they teach:** Stress suppresses immune function through two pathways: direct (sympathetic nerves to immune organs) and endocrine (cortisol inhibition of lymphocytes). This suppression is measurable at the level of antibody response, white blood cell counts, and susceptibility to infection.

**How the chapter uses it:** To explain the mechanism linking stress appraisal to disease. Once stress is appraised as threat, physiological cascades suppress immunity. The mechanism is not magical or metaphorical; it is anatomical and hormonal.

**What it doesn't do:** These studies do not address individual differences in how much immune suppression occurs or who is most vulnerable to infection once immune function is suppressed.

**Mechanism the student learns:** Stress "gets under the skin." There is a direct biological pathway from chronic mental stress to physical disease via immune suppression.

---

### Cardiovascular Disease and Personality (Friedman, Rosenman; Chida & Steptoe; Chang et al.)

**What the sources teach:** The original Friedman-Rosenman work identified Type A personality (competitive, rushed, hostile) as predicting heart disease. Later meta-analyses (Chida & Steptoe) showed it is not Type A broadly but specifically the *hostility component*. One longitudinal study (Chang et al.) followed medical students for decades: high anger at age 30s predicted 6× higher heart attack risk by age 55.

**How the chapter uses it:** To illustrate that personality traits linked to stress do predict physical disease, but the mechanism is specific — it is not ambition or time urgency but rather hostility and the social conflict hostility creates.

**What it doesn't do:** These studies are correlational. They show association, not causation. Depression predicts heart disease, but it is unclear whether depression *causes* disease or whether both emerge from common physiological pathways (inflammation, etc.).

**Mechanism the student learns:** The transactional model — hostility → social conflict → increased stress and cardiovascular reactivity → disease. Personality shapes behavior; behavior shapes social outcomes; social outcomes feed back into physiology.

---

### Social Support and Mortality (Berkman & Syme, 1979; Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010)

**What the sources teach:** Berkman & Syme followed nearly 7,000 people for 9 years and found that social isolation predicted mortality with effect sizes comparable to smoking and physical inactivity. Holt-Lunstad's meta-analysis of 148 studies confirmed: strong social relationships predict 50% greater survival likelihood.

**How the chapter uses it:** As evidence that social integration is a health determinant comparable in magnitude to traditional medical risk factors. Isolation is not just uncomfortable; it is dangerous to health.

**What it doesn't do:** These studies do not definitively answer the direction of causality — do social connections protect health, or do healthier people maintain better relationships? Likely bidirectional, but the causal mechanism is not fully clear from these epidemiological studies alone.

**Mechanism the student learns:** Relationships matter for survival. This is not metaphorical. A person with adequate social support has better health outcomes, lower mortality risk, and faster recovery from illness than an isolated person.

---

### Perceived Control (Marmot et al., 1997; Infurna & Gerstorf, 2014)

**What the sources teach:** The Whitehall studies of British civil servants found that job *control* predicts heart disease risk more strongly than job *demand*. A high-demand job with control is less damaging than a low-control job. Infurna & Gerstorf showed perceived control predicts health both directly (physiological) and indirectly (through encouraging exercise and healthy behaviors).

**How the chapter uses it:** As evidence that appraisal of control matters as much as objective circumstances. A stressor you believe you can influence is less damaging than an identical stressor you perceive as uncontrollable.

**What it doesn't do:** These studies do not resolve whether perception of control is accurate (does the person actually have control?) or whether illusory control (overestimating control) is equally protective. Some evidence suggests both real and illusory control protect, but the optimal amount of control perception is unclear.

**Mechanism the student learns:** Control is protective. Circumstances that remove control (low-status jobs, poverty, discrimination, captivity) are uniquely damaging to health.

---

### Stress and Cellular Aging (Epel et al., 2004; Shalev et al., 2013)

**What the sources teach:** Telomere shortening is a marker of cellular aging. Chronic stress accelerates telomere shortening. Mothers of chronically ill children show telomere lengths equivalent to 9–17 years of aging. Childhood violence exposure predicts telomere erosion years later.

**How the chapter uses it:** To make visible the cellular cost of chronic stress. Stress doesn't just produce unpleasant feelings; it ages the body at the cellular level. This is visible, measurable, and irreversible (once shortened, telomeres do not lengthen).

**What it doesn't do:** Telomere length is a marker of aging, but whether shortening *causes* disease or is a byproduct of the physiological processes that cause disease is not settled. The causal chain is still being worked out.

**Mechanism the student learns:** Stress has a literal cost at the cellular level. Chronic stress makes you older faster. This is one reason why chronic stress is particularly damaging in childhood — there are more years ahead to be affected.

## Ideas Harvest — Angles and Moves for Teaching Stress

### Hook Candidates

1. **The observation move (Selye):** Notice something everyone else missed. Selye was looking at sex hormones but observed that diverse stressors produced identical physical changes. Teachable: Looking carefully at anomalies (why do different stressors cause the same pattern?) can reveal fundamental mechanisms.

2. **The personal stakes move:** Open with stress the student is experiencing now (exams, relationships, finances). Use their own appraisal to teach the process-based model. Teachable: Stress is immediate and personal; theory explains your own experience.

3. **The structural move:** Start with health disparities (people in lower-SES groups have higher rates of cardiovascular disease and hypertension). Then explain: Is it just health behaviors, or is chronic stress from discrimination, low control, and resource scarcity damaging in itself? Teachable: Individual stress management cannot overcome structural inequality.

### Specification Moves Ready to Deploy

1. **"Stress" is vague; appraisal makes it specific.** Before you can manage stress, you must specify: What am I appraising as threatening? Do I actually believe I lack the capacity to manage it, or is that an assumption I can test?

2. **Cortisol is not stress.** Cortisol is a hormone that increases during stress and has both useful and harmful effects. High cortisol *during* a crisis is adaptive. High cortisol *all the time* is damaging. Conflating stress (psychological appraisal) with cortisol (a hormone) obscures the actual mechanism.

3. **Control is not binary.** Even in an uncontrollable situation, there are often aspects you can influence. The question is not whether you have total control but whether you can influence *something* in a way that matters.

### Mechanism Explanations Ready to Deploy

1. **The HPA axis.** Hypothalamus → pituitary → adrenal glands, a three-part relay that works much slower than the sympathetic nervous system but sustains longer. Easy to draw; illuminating when students see the anatomy.

2. **Immune suppression.** Cortisol inhibits lymphocyte production directly. Sympathetic nerves physically innervate thymus and lymph nodes. Not mysterious; anatomically specific.

3. **The transactional model of hostility.** Hostile → defensive → others react negatively → isolation/conflict increases → hostility deepens → cardiovascular reactivity increases. The cycle is the mechanism. Breaking the cycle requires changing behavior, not just thinking positive.

### Trade-offs to Highlight

1. **Acute stress is sometimes helpful; chronic stress is always damaging (eventually).** The same system that protects in short bursts destroys in long runs.

2. **Control has costs.** Feeling in control can lead to overestimating control, which can prevent necessary help-seeking. Illusion of control may protect mental health short-term but fail long-term.

3. **Social support is not equally accessible.** The research shows support is protective, but access is unequal. Individual coping cannot solve structural isolation.

### Common Mistakes to Correct

1. "Stress causes ulcers." — Most ulcers are bacterial. Stress exacerbates them in some people but does not cause them directly.

2. "Just relax." — Some stress requires action (changing jobs, addressing abuse). Relaxation without problem-solving leaves the stressor in place.

3. "Negative people get sick; positive people stay healthy." — Oversimplified. Positivity that prevents necessary action (ignoring a lump, staying in an abusive relationship) is harmful. Accurate appraisal matters more than positivity.

4. "Type A personality causes heart disease." — Not quite. It is specifically the hostility component and the social conflict it generates.

5. "Stress is in your head." — Stress is in your brain (cognition), nervous system (sympathetic activation), endocrine system (cortisol), and immune system (suppression). The mind-body division is false.

### Gaps and Extensions

1. **Why telomeres?** Why is cellular aging the marker? Could stress cause disease through other mechanisms (inflammation, atherosclerosis, neurodegeneration) that don't involve telomeres? Multiple mechanisms likely operate; telomeres are one visible marker.

2. **Recovery.** How long does it take to recover from chronic stress? If someone removes themselves from a high-stress job, do their immune markers improve immediately or take weeks/months? This is practically important and not well-researched.

3. **Resilience.** What makes some people resilient to stress others find crushing? Genetics, childhood experience, current support, coping skills — all matter, but the relative weights and interactions are not well understood.

4. **Prevention versus intervention.** Is it better to reduce chronic stressors (social policy, workplace reform) or to build individual coping skills? Likely both, but the balance is not settled.

### Adjacent Chapters and Cross-References

- **Chapter 15 (Psychological Disorders):** Many disorders emerge from or are exacerbated by chronic stress (depression, anxiety, PTSD). The stress-disorder link is bidirectional.
- **Chapter 16 (Therapy and Treatment):** Many therapeutic approaches work partly by reducing stress or changing appraisal of stressors.
- **Chapter 13 (Industrial-Organizational Psychology):** Workplace stress is a major stressor. Job strain, burnout, and work-life integration are applied stress topics.
- **Chapter 12 (Social Psychology):** Social relationships, discrimination, and social belonging all connect to stress.

## Teaching Decisions Made in This Chapter

1. **Emphasized appraisal over events.** The chapter could focus on what causes stress (traumatic events, major life changes). Instead, it emphasizes how appraisal of threat and capacity determines stress response. This makes the topic more actionable — students can change appraisals; they cannot always change events.

2. **Used Selye as a gateway to modern psychoneuroimmunology.** Selye's observations from rats are old, but they lead directly to contemporary understanding of immunity and stress. His three-stage model still organizes thinking.

3. **Named the hostile component, not Type A broadly.** The original Type A findings did not replicate. Rather than leave students confused, the chapter specifies what actually predicts cardiovascular disease: hostility and the social conflict it creates.

4. **Integrated inequality from the start.** Stress research has historically focused on individual differences (how people cope). Recent work highlights structural factors (discrimination, low control, low status) that create chronic stress. Both matter.

5. **Deferred positive psychology (happiness) to the synthesis.** The chapter teaches what stress is and how it damages health before pivoting to resilience and well-being. This order (damage first, then protection) makes protection more meaningful.
