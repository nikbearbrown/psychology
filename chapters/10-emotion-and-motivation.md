# Chapter 10 — Emotion and Motivation

## Cold open

Paul Ekman stepped out of an aircraft in 1967 into the Fore highlands of Papua New Guinea, a culture so isolated from the outside world that its people had never seen a foreigner. No Western media. No photographs. No film. When Ekman showed a Fore highlander a photograph of a human face and told a story — "His child has just died" or "He is about to fight" or "He is looking at a dead pig" — the man pointed to the expression that matched. Then Ekman showed the same faces to college students in San Francisco, to Japanese and Brazilian students, to a hospitalized psychiatric patient. Across all of them, the same stories matched the same expressions.

That consistency echoed across a century of research. Whether you were born in Manhattan or in the forest, your face tells the same story when you feel fear, anger, sadness, disgust, surprise, or happiness. The machinery was visible and universal.

But the same Fore highlander, asked to voluntarily produce an expression of anger, made his face do something the American college student wouldn't. Culture shapes when you show what you feel and how you shape it for an audience. The universal substrate — the muscles, the neural firing — gets dressed in cultural rules that say *which emotions are safe to display, when, and to whom*.

That tension — between what appears to be built into us and what appears to be learned — is what this chapter teaches. You will learn that emotion and motivation are not simple feelings. They are the convergence of three systems: the body's arousal, the mind's interpretation, and the culture's rules for display. You will learn that the mechanisms are visible if you look closely. And you will learn that two people facing identical circumstances can feel entirely different things because of how their minds label what their bodies are doing.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- **Explain** how three competing theories of emotion (James-Lange, Cannon-Bard, Schachter-Singer two-factor) account for the relationship between body arousal and feeling.
- **Distinguish** biological hunger from behavioral hunger; apply the concepts of homeostasis, leptin, and ghrelin to the regulation of eating.
- **Analyze** what happens when homeostatic hunger regulation breaks down — the costs of obesity, the mechanisms and consequences of eating disorders.
- **Describe** how the hypothalamus coordinates sexual motivation and behavior, and recognize that sexual orientation has biological foundations.
- **Evaluate** Maslow's hierarchy of needs against evidence, and distinguish intrinsic from extrinsic motivation.
- **Apply** the facial feedback hypothesis and emotion-regulation strategies to predict changes in emotional experience.

### Prerequisites

Chapter 3 (Biopsychology) — the brain's structure and the autonomic nervous system. Chapter 9 (Lifespan Development) — childhood development and attachment. Basic familiarity with the concept of homeostasis: a system returns to balance when pushed away from it.

### Why this chapter matters

You spend your life chasing and avoiding. You chase food when hungry, sex when aroused, status when competing. You avoid pain, rejection, shame. The machinery that drives those pursuits is ancient. It is also fragile. When hunger stops signaling, you starve or gorge. When the labeling system misinterprets arousal, you feel wrong. When culture forbids expression, you regulate — sometimes into health, sometimes into disorder. This chapter teaches the mechanism because the mechanism breaks in ways that matter. If you understand how homeostasis works, you understand what happens when weight set-points shift. If you understand how labeling works, you understand why the same racing heart feels like terror in one moment and excitement in another.

## Concept 1 — Theories of Emotion: The Body Speaks, But What Does It Say?

### The puzzle

You are walking home at dusk when you round a corner and nearly step on a coiled snake. Your heart races. Your breathing quickens. Your palms sweat. Adrenaline floods your system. The threat is real.

Three minutes later you are sitting in the dark theater and the soundtrack surges with violins. On the screen a figure moves toward the darkness. Your heart races. Your breathing quickens. Your palms sweat. Adrenaline floods your system. The threat is imaginary.

In both cases, your body is doing the same thing. But one feels like fear and the other feels like excitement mixed with delight. Why? The puzzle is not the arousal. The puzzle is how you got from the arousal to the feeling.

### James-Lange: We feel because we tremble

William James was an American psychologist writing in the 1880s, and he proposed something that sounds backwards. You do not tremble because you are afraid, he argued. You are afraid *because* you tremble. Emotion, in James's account, flows from the body to the mind. The physiological response comes first. The feeling follows after.

In the modern language: you encounter a threat, your sympathetic nervous system fires, your heart races and your skin prickles. *Then*, your brain registers the body's state and labels it fear. Different physiological patterns — different patterns of heart rate, breathing, skin conductance, muscle tension — would be interpreted as different emotions. James-Lange says the body's arousal is specific to emotion type. The brain reads the body's message and translates it into feeling.

This is testable. If James-Lange is right, then people with spinal-cord injuries that block the body's signals from reaching the brain should feel emotions less intensely, because the arousal signals can't get through. Research on people with spinal-cord paralysis does find reduced emotional intensity, consistent with James-Lange. But the research is not decisive. The feelings do not disappear; they just diminish. Something else is happening.

### Cannon-Bard: We tremble and feel together

Walter Cannon and Philip Bard proposed an alternative in the 1920s. Emotion is not the result of the body's response. Emotion and the body's response are *simultaneous and independent*. The same stimulus — the snake, the movie — activates both the brain's emotional center (the limbic system, particularly the amygdala) and the body's arousal system (the sympathetic nervous system). They fire together, but they are separate events. You see the threat and you *feel* fear *at exactly the same moment* that your heart starts to race. The feeling is not downstream of the racing heart; it is running in parallel.

This explains why James-Lange has trouble. The body's response and the emotion are correlated, but one is not causing the other. They are more like two notes struck on a piano simultaneously — they sound together, but the first note does not cause the second.

Cannon-Bard also pointed out that the body's arousal patterns are not nearly specific enough for James-Lange. Fear, anger, and excitement produce similar autonomic responses. Your heart races in all three states. If the feeling came purely from reading the body's state, you should not be able to tell them apart. But you can.

### Schachter-Singer: It is not what you feel, but what you think about what you feel

Stanley Schachter and Jerome Singer proposed a two-factor theory in 1962. The body's arousal is real, they said, but it is ambiguous. Your body does not come pre-labeled. What matters is what your *mind* makes of the arousal in the context where you find yourself.

They tested this with a clever experiment. Male college students volunteered for what they thought was a study of a vitamin injection's effects on vision. Half the men got an injection of epinephrine — a drug that mimics sympathetic nervous system activation. Heart racing, hands trembling, flushed skin, all of it. The other half got a saline placebo.

Among the men who received epinephrine, some were told what to expect: *You might feel your heart race, your hands might tremble, you might feel flushed. That is normal and temporary.* They knew the arousal was coming from the drug.

Others were *not* told what to expect. Or they were told false expectations — *You might itch, or your feet might go numb.* They felt the arousal but had no explanation for it.

After the injection, the men sat in a waiting room with another man (actually an actor working with the experimenters). The actor behaved in two different ways across different runs of the study. Sometimes he acted euphoric — joking, throwing paper balls, laughing, acting like he was having a wonderful time. Other times he acted angry — complaining about the questionnaire, getting visibly frustrated, acting increasingly hostile.

The result was decisive. The men who *knew* their arousal came from the drug reported no change in emotion. *I feel the drug, not happiness or anger.* The men who felt unexplained arousal interpreted it through the context. With the euphoric actor, they felt euphoric. With the angry actor, they felt angry. Same arousal. Different emotions, depending on the cognitive label they applied.

Schachter and Singer showed that emotion requires two factors: physiological arousal and a cognitive interpretation of what that arousal means. The interpretation happens *in context*. The same pounding heart feels like terror at the edge of a cliff, excitement at the start of a race, and love when you are with someone you care about. The body is the same. The context and the label change everything.

### Appraisal theory: Your thoughts come before the feeling

Richard Lazarus and Magda Arnold proposed a different sequence. Thought comes before emotion, not after. You do not feel fear and then think *I am afraid*. You appraise the situation — *This threat is serious and I cannot escape* — and the appraisal *produces* the fear. Your evaluation of the circumstance determines what you feel.

This explains something Schachter-Singer struggles with: the speed. Fear from a genuine threat happens instantly. You do not pause to interpret the arousal. You appraise and you fear, almost simultaneously. The cognitive process is fast, automatic, often unconscious. But it is there. You *think* (quickly, automatically) that the situation is dangerous, and that appraisal produces the fear response.

Two people facing the same circumstance will have different emotions if they appraise it differently. A public-speaking assignment could feel terrifying if you appraise it as a judgment of your worth, or exciting if you appraise it as an opportunity. The circumstance is the same. The appraisal is the variable. The feeling follows the appraisal.

### What the evidence supports

These theories are not all right and all wrong. They are partially right and partially incomplete.

James-Lange captures something true: the body does feed back to the brain. Research on the facial feedback hypothesis shows that if you hold your face in a smile, you will actually feel slightly happier. If you suppress a frown, you will feel slightly less sad. The body's state does influence the mind's state.

But James-Lange is incomplete. The emotion does not emerge *only* from the body's reading. Spinal-cord patients still feel emotions even when arousal signals are blocked. The speed is wrong too. You fear the snake before your heart finishes its first race.

Cannon-Bard is right that emotion and arousal are independent processes. The amygdala can fire and produce fear before the sympathetic nervous system even activates. Research by Joseph LeDoux shows that some emotional signals take a fast path from the thalamus directly to the amygdala, bypassing the cortex entirely. Fear from a genuine threat is *not* downstream of arousal. It is partly independent of it.

But Cannon-Bard underestimated the role of context. Schachter-Singer showed that identical arousal in different contexts produces different emotions. The cognitive label does matter.

And appraisal theory captures the speed and the variety. Your automatic appraisal of a situation happens fast enough to feel immediate. And the appraisal does produce different emotions for different people.

The honest current reading: emotion emerges from the convergence of three systems. The amygdala and limbic structures produce rapid emotional responses based on learned associations — this is fast, automatic, often unconscious. Simultaneously, the sympathetic nervous system produces arousal. And simultaneously, the cortex engages in appraisal — *What does this mean? Is it safe? Should I approach or avoid?* These three systems run in parallel. Sometimes the amygdala's fear wins the race. Sometimes the cortex's appraisal reframes the situation. Sometimes the arousal is ambiguous enough that context and labeling determine what you feel. All three theories captured part of the truth.

### Emotion regulation: When you control the feeling

You are in a meeting and something makes you angry. You want to respond with cutting sarcasm, but you know that will damage a relationship you care about. So you regulate. You might reappraise — *This is not a personal attack, just a disagreement about tactics.* You might suppress the expression — *I will not let my face show what I feel.* You might even remove yourself from the situation temporarily.

Emotion regulation is the process of managing emotional experiences and expressions. Research shows that reappraisal (changing how you think about the situation) is more effective at changing how you actually feel. Suppression (hiding the expression) is less effective — the feeling persists even though the face does not show it. And suppression has costs. People who chronically suppress emotions experience worse health outcomes, higher stress, and more depression.

Much of emotion regulation happens automatically, without conscious thought. You do not deliberately choose to reappraise every situation. The reappraisal happens as a habit, a learned pattern. This is adaptive when the pattern is effective, maladaptive when it is not. Some people chronically reappraise threats as opportunities. Others chronically appraise neutral situations as threats. The regulation style becomes part of personality.

### Common misconceptions

**Emotions are pure biology.** They are not. They are biology plus cognition plus culture. Take away any one, and the emotion changes.

**You can read someone's emotion accurately from their face.** You cannot. The same facial expression can mask different feelings. A smile can hide sadness. A still face can hide fear. And display rules — cultural norms about which emotions are acceptable to show — mean that the expression you see may not match the feeling at all. A Japanese person and an American might make the exact same expression, but the American's is a true expression while the Japanese person's is a social display, and the feelings behind them are different.

**Arousal determines emotion.** No. Arousal is necessary but not sufficient. Two people with identical arousal in different contexts will feel different emotions. The context, the appraisal, and the labeling all matter as much as the body's state.

## Concept 2 — Hunger and Homeostasis: When the Body's Balance Breaks

### The puzzle

Your body wants to stay at a certain weight. Not forever — weight changes across the lifespan and in response to circumstances. But at any given moment, your body seems to have a target. You eat until you feel full. You feel hungry again a few hours later. You might lose weight if you diet, but it is hard; hunger nags at you. You might gain weight if you overfeed, but your appetite dampens slightly; fullness comes sooner.

This stability in weight — even in the face of variation in intake and activity — suggested that the body had a thermostat. The idea is called *set-point theory*. Your body has an ideal weight, genetically determined, and it defends that weight through changes in hunger and metabolism. If you drop below your set point, hunger increases and metabolism may slow slightly, pushing you back up. If you rise above your set point, hunger decreases and metabolism may increase, pushing you back down.

Set-point theory is elegant, intuitive, and partly wrong. The evidence does not support the rigid defense of a fixed weight. People who lose weight and keep it off do not show the compensatory metabolic changes set-point theory would predict. And the set point itself is not fixed — it shifts with age, stress, and diet composition. What is real is homeostasis: the body's tendency to return to balance. What is real is the machinery of hunger and satiety. The set point is not.

### The machinery of hunger

When your stomach is empty, it contracts. You feel hunger pangs. But the hunger signal is not coming from the stomach alone. The real signals come from your blood. When blood glucose drops, your pancreas and liver send chemical messages — hormones — that activate hunger. Glucose is the brain's preferred fuel. Low glucose means the brain is not getting what it needs. The hunger signal is *Fuel now.*

The specific hormones matter. Ghrelin is produced by the stomach when it is empty and signals *hungry*, the sensation of appetite and the desire to eat. Leptin is produced by fat cells and signals *full*, the sensation of satiation — the sense that you have eaten enough and should stop.

These signals converge in the hypothalamus, the brain's appetite control center. The hypothalamus integrates the signals from ghrelin, leptin, and blood glucose, and it makes a decision: initiate eating or continue with whatever you are doing. This is homeostasis at work. The signals are continuous, not binary. Hunger is not an on-off switch; it is a dimmer that gets brighter as fuel drops and dimmer as fuel is restored.

The system works well for most people most of the time. But it is vulnerable to disruption. And when it disrupts, the disruptions are severe.

### Obesity and the shift in set point

In the United States, roughly 40% of adults are obese — that is, have a body mass index (BMI) of 30 or higher. BMI is weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared. It is a crude measure; it does not distinguish fat from muscle, and it does not account for variation across ethnicities. But it is useful for population-level analysis. Obesity is a risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, type-2 diabetes, liver disease, sleep apnea, arthritis, and several cancers. The health burden is real.

What is less clear is why the prevalence has increased so dramatically over the past 40 years. The set-point theory would predict that people got lazier or ate more. But the data do not support that. People are not dramatically less active, and caloric intake has not increased as much as weight has. Something else is shifting the homeostatic balance.

The leading hypothesis involves the food environment. Processed foods are high in calories, low in satiety — they do not activate the leptin signal strongly because they lack the fiber and protein that normally produce fullness. So people eat more and stay hungry. The foods are also high in sugar and refined carbohydrates, which produce rapid blood-glucose spikes and crashes, creating cycles of hunger and overeating. Over time, leptin signaling itself may be disrupted — chronic overeating can lead to *leptin resistance*, where fat cells produce leptin but the brain stops responding to it. The signal becomes noise.

Socioeconomic factors also matter. People in low-income neighborhoods often have limited access to fresh food and abundant access to cheap, high-calorie processed foods. Time poverty — working multiple jobs, commuting long distances — leaves little time for food preparation or physical activity. The body's homeostatic system is not broken. The environment is broken, and it is pushing weight set-points upward across entire populations.

### Eating disorders: When hunger and fullness misfire

At the other extreme, eating disorders represent profound disruptions of the hunger-satiety system.

Anorexia nervosa is characterized by severe restriction of food intake, often to the point of starvation. People with anorexia often have distorted body images — they perceive themselves as overweight even when they are dangerously thin. The restriction can lead to bone loss, heart failure, kidney failure, cessation of menstruation, and in severe cases, death. Research suggests that anorexia involves a combination of factors: genetic predisposition (twin studies show heritability), psychological factors (perfectionism, need for control, obsessive thought patterns), and cultural factors (the thin ideal promoted in media, fashion, and entertainment).

Bulimia nervosa is characterized by cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors — vomiting, laxative abuse, or excessive exercise. Like anorexia, bulimia is associated with distorted body image and can produce severe health consequences: electrolyte imbalance leading to heart problems, esophageal and tooth damage from stomach acid, and psychological distress.

Binge eating disorder, recognized more recently as a distinct disorder, involves cycles of eating large quantities followed by guilt and shame, but *without* compensatory behaviors. The psychological distress is real, but the physical damage is less severe than bulimia.

The etiology of eating disorders is complex. Genetic factors account for some risk. Neurobiological factors matter — people with eating disorders show differences in brain regions involved in reward processing and impulse control. Psychological factors matter — perfectionism, anxiety, depression, and need for control are more common in people with eating disorders. And cultural factors matter enormously — eating disorders are more common in Western cultures that emphasize thinness as a beauty ideal. They are more common in White females from industrialized societies. This is not because other populations lack the vulnerability. It is because the cultural message that drives the disorder is not directed at them as forcefully.

Understanding eating disorders requires understanding that the hunger system is not purely biological. It is psychological — what you think about food and your body matters. It is cultural — what your society tells you about thinness, control, and worth matters. And it is biological — the brain's reward and impulse-control systems are involved. All three layers are real.

### Common misconceptions

**Hunger is purely physiological.** It is not. Hunger is modified by what you have learned about food, what your culture teaches about eating, and what you think about your body.

**Obesity is caused by laziness or overeating.** This is wrong and harmful. Obesity results from complex interactions between genes, food environment, and behavior. The same food environment will produce obesity in some people and not others, depending on genetic susceptibility. And the food environment itself is changing — the availability of ultra-processed foods has no historical precedent. Individual willpower is not sufficient to counteract this environmental pressure.

**Eating disorders are a choice.** They are not. Eating disorders involve changes in brain structure and function, psychological vulnerabilities, and cultural pressures. People with eating disorders are not weak or vain. They are experiencing a disorder of motivation and perception, just as people with diabetes experience a disorder of glucose regulation.

## Concept 3 — Achievement, Sex, and Maslow's Ladder: The Ladder Climbs, but Not How Maslow Said

### The puzzle

Why do people work? Why do some people pursue academic achievement relentlessly while others coast? Why do some people need to accomplish great things while others are content with modest stability? And why do some people pursue sex while others do not?

These questions point to psychological motives — wants and needs that drive behavior toward goals that have nothing directly to do with survival. Hunger is about surviving. These motives are about mattering.

### Need for achievement and the overcomplicated truth

Psychologist David McClelland studied achievement motivation by looking at what people wrote in stories. He gave people ambiguous scenes — a person sitting at a desk, a father and son — and asked them to write stories about what was happening. McClelland coded the stories for *thematic apperception*: the themes that appeared most frequently. People high in achievement motivation wrote stories about people overcoming obstacles, reaching goals, doing things well. People low in achievement motivation wrote stories about relationships, leisure, simple happiness.

Achievement motivation predicts behavior. People high in achievement motivation pursue challenging tasks, persist in the face of failure, and seek feedback. They become entrepreneurs, academics, competitive athletes.

But achievement motivation is not static. It responds to experience. Research by Deborah Tannen and others shows that girls are socialized differently from boys around achievement. Girls are praised for being "nice" and "helpful"; boys are praised for being "smart" and "strong." As a result, girls tend to develop what psychologist Carol Dweck calls a *fixed mindset*: the belief that ability is fixed, unchangeable. When they fail, the failure is evidence of low ability. So they avoid challenges that might reveal lack of ability. Boys tend to develop a *growth mindset*: the belief that ability is developed through effort. When they fail, the failure is a temporary setback; they can get better through work. So they pursue challenges.

This is not innate. It is learned through socialization. And it has consequences. The same girl, praised as "smart," will take on more challenges than the girl praised as "nice." The mindset is the lever.

### Intrinsic versus extrinsic motivation: The overjustification effect

Suppose you love baking. On weekends, you experiment with new recipes, spend hours perfecting technique, bake for friends just for the pleasure of it. Then a friend suggests that you sell baked goods at the farmer's market. You start to do so for a few hours a week. A month in, you notice something: you no longer want to bake for fun. The intrinsic motivation — the joy of doing it for its own sake — has been displaced by extrinsic motivation — doing it for payment.

This is the *overjustification effect*: when you add external rewards to an activity you already enjoy, the external reward can actually reduce the intrinsic motivation. Why? Because your mind re-labels the activity. *I bake because I enjoy it* becomes *I bake because I get paid.* The narrative changes, and with it, the motivation.

The effect is real but not universal. It depends on the kind of reward and whether you expected it. Tangible rewards (money, prizes) are more likely to undermine intrinsic motivation than intangible rewards (praise, recognition). And if you did not expect the reward, intrinsic motivation often stays intact.

Why does this matter? Because intrinsic motivation produces better work — more creative, more persistent, more resilient. Students who are motivated to learn for its own sake develop deeper understanding than students motivated by grades. Workers who find meaning in their work are more engaged than workers motivated by salary alone. Schools that emphasize grades and testing, trying to motivate through external rewards, often reduce the intrinsic love of learning. The mechanism is more subtle than "students will work harder for grades." The real story is "students will relabel why they work, and the relabeling changes what they are motivated to do."

### Sexual motivation: The hypothalamus and hormones

Sexual behavior is motivated by the hypothalamus. Lesions to specific areas of the hypothalamus in male rats eliminate the *ability* to copulate — the mechanics break. Lesions to other areas eliminate the *motivation* — the males no longer seek out females, even though they still *could* mate if females were forced upon them. The motivation and the ability are dissociable.

In both sexes, testosterone is important. All genders produce testosterone; the amount differs. People with higher circulating testosterone tend to report higher sexual desire. People who are given testosterone replacement therapy report increased sexual interest. Testosterone is not the only hormone involved — prolactin, estrogen, and oxytocin all play roles — but it is central to motivation.

But unlike hunger, which is relatively constant (you get hungry every few hours), sexual motivation is highly variable and context-dependent. The same person can have zero sexual interest in one context and high interest in another. Cultural, psychological, and relationship factors matter enormously. The hypothalamus provides the substrate. The mind and the culture shape how that substrate is expressed.

Sexual orientation — the emotional, romantic, and sexual attraction to specific genders or sexes — has biological foundations. Twin studies show that sexual orientation is heritable; identical twins are more likely to share sexual orientation than fraternal twins. Genetic studies have identified regions of DNA associated with sexual orientation (though no single "gay gene" exists; sexual orientation is polygenic, involving multiple genes). Brain imaging shows differences in structure and activation patterns between people of different sexual orientations. Hormone levels during development appear relevant — people who were exposed to unusual hormone levels in utero are more likely to report non-heterosexual orientation.

But biology is not destiny. The same genetic and hormonal substrate can be expressed in different ways depending on culture, experience, and choice. In some cultures, same-sex sexual behavior is part of expected development. In others, it is forbidden. The underlying capacity is the same; the expression varies.

What is robust across all research is that sexual orientation is not a choice and cannot be changed by effort or therapy. Conversion therapy — the attempted use of psychological, behavioral, or biomedical interventions to change sexual orientation — has no evidence of effectiveness. It has strong evidence of harm. The American Psychological Association, the American Medical Association, and nearly every major mental-health organization have issued statements against it. People with LGBTQ identities do not need to be changed. They need to be accepted.

### Maslow's hierarchy: The ladder and the evidence

Abraham Maslow proposed that human motivation could be organized into a hierarchy. At the base are physiological needs: food, water, sleep, sex. The hierarchy climbs: safety needs, belonging needs, esteem needs, and at the top, self-actualization — the realization of your full potential.

Maslow's hierarchy is intuitive. Of course you need to eat before you can worry about love. Of course you need safety before you can pursue meaning. The picture is memorable — a pyramid with five levels.

But the evidence does not support the strict ordering. People go hungry to pursue love or honor. Parents sacrifice safety for their children. Artists starve for their vision. The hierarchy is not ordinal — it is not true that lower needs must be satisfied before higher ones matter. What is true is that biological needs have to be met *enough* for people to have the energy for other pursuits. But "enough" is not the same as "fully satisfied." You can pursue meaning on partial satisfaction of biological needs.

The other problem: self-actualization is vague. What does realizing your full potential mean? Maslow never specified. Is it happiness? Creativity? Moral excellence? Achievement? Different cultures would answer differently. And the evidence suggests that what motivates people is not a single climb up a pyramid but a complex negotiation among multiple motivations that vary with context, culture, and individual differences.

What Maslow got right is that humans are motivated by more than hunger and survival. What he got wrong is the picture of an orderly hierarchy. The reality is messier and more interesting: people juggle multiple motivations simultaneously, and the balance among them shifts with context and culture.

### Self-determination theory: A better framework

Edward Deci and Richard Ryan proposed an alternative. Rather than a hierarchy, they propose that people are motivated by three basic psychological needs: autonomy (the sense that you are acting by choice), competence (the sense that you can succeed at what matters), and relatedness (the sense of connection to others).

This explains why intrinsic motivation is so powerful. Activities that satisfy all three needs — that you chose, that you can do well, and that connect you to people you care about — are highly motivating. It explains why the overjustification effect happens. If you bake because you love it, and then you bake for payment, the autonomy shifts. *I choose to do this* becomes *I do this because I am paid.* The motivation changes because one of the needs is undermined.

And it explains cultural variation. Different cultures emphasize autonomy, competence, and relatedness differently. Individualist cultures (like the United States) emphasize autonomy heavily; the narrative is *choose who you want to be*. Collectivist cultures emphasize relatedness more; the narrative is *find your role in the group*. But all cultures have all three needs. The balance differs, not the presence.

### Common misconceptions

**Need for achievement is fixed.** It is not. It is shaped by socialization, experience, and how you think about yourself.

**Extrinsic motivation is always bad.** It is not. External rewards can be useful when they are unexpected, when they do not change how you think about yourself, and when the activity is not intrinsically motivating to begin with. The problem is that external rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation, so they should be used carefully and sparingly.

**Maslow's hierarchy is law.** It is not. It is an intuitive model, but the evidence does not support strict ordering. People pursue meaning and connection even when basic needs are not fully met.

## Integration — Emotion, Motivation, and Why You Choose What You Choose

Return to the morning you read the news and learned something disturbing. Your heart rate climbed. You felt angry. You posted a response on social media. Three hours later, you were still thinking about it — the anger still fresh.

What happened was the convergence of three systems. The amygdala registered the content as threatening or unjust, producing rapid emotional activation — fear or anger. Simultaneously, your cortex appraised the situation — *This is wrong, and I should do something about it.* Simultaneously, your autonomic nervous system produced arousal — heart racing, breathing quickened. All three happened nearly at once.

Then you did something: you posted. Why? Because your motivation system was engaged. You had a need to express, to belong, to be heard. The behavior was driven by multiple motives simultaneously: the desire to be right (achievement), the desire to be part of a community (relatedness), the desire to express what you believe (autonomy). All three were activated.

Now consider what happened after. You checked for responses. Some people agreed. That felt good — relatedness and competence, validated. Some people disagreed. That felt bad. You reappraised the situation — *Some people understand, some do not, that is normal* — or you did not reappraise and you stayed angry. Your emotion-regulation strategy determined whether the subsequent emotions were adaptive or maladaptive.

This is how emotion and motivation work together. Motivation drives the behavior. Emotion provides information about whether the behavior is working. If relatedness needs are met, you feel good and you continue. If they are not met, you feel bad and you change strategies. Over time, emotion and motivation become habit — you no longer consciously choose to check for responses; you just do it. The habit is automatic.

The systems have evolved to work together. Motivation without emotion would be directionless; you would not know which goals matter. Emotion without motivation would be meaningless; you would feel things but have no drive to do anything about them. Motivation and emotion together create agency: the sense that you can act, that your actions matter, that the world responds to what you do.

## Chapter Summary

Emotions are not simple feelings. They are the convergence of rapid amygdala responses, cortical appraisal, autonomic arousal, and learned regulation strategies. Three major theories each captured part of the truth: James-Lange showed that the body feeds back to the mind; Cannon-Bard showed that emotion and arousal are independent systems; Schachter-Singer showed that context and labeling matter. The current understanding is that all three contribute. Emotion emerges from the interaction of systems running in parallel.

Hunger and satiety are regulated by homeostatic mechanisms involving ghrelin (signaling hunger), leptin (signaling fullness), and glucose sensing. The system works well when the food environment supports it, but modern processed foods and lifestyle factors have disrupted this regulation, leading to epidemic obesity. At the other extreme, eating disorders represent profound disruptions of appetite regulation, with multiple contributing factors: genetic predisposition, psychological vulnerabilities, and cultural pressure.

Sexual motivation is rooted in the hypothalamus and influenced by testosterone and other hormones. Sexual orientation — the pattern of sexual and romantic attraction — has biological foundations (genetics, brain structure, hormone exposure), but also cultural and individual expression. Sexual orientation is not a choice and cannot be changed through any intervention.

Motivation is not a simple hierarchy. Maslow's pyramid is intuitive but not empirically supported. A better model is self-determination theory: people are motivated by autonomy (feeling that you are choosing), competence (feeling that you can succeed), and relatedness (feeling connected to others). Intrinsic motivation — doing something for its own sake — produces better learning and engagement than extrinsic motivation, though adding external rewards can sometimes undermine intrinsic motivation through the overjustification effect.

The chapter closes with the principle that emotion and motivation work together. Motivation without emotion is directionless. Emotion without motivation is inert. Together, they produce agency — the sense that you can act and that your actions matter.

## What would change my mind

If evidence demonstrated that sexual orientation *could* be changed through psychological or behavioral intervention with lasting effects and without psychological harm, the conclusion that orientation is not changeable would need revision. To date, no such evidence exists. More broadly, if future research revealed that the amygdala's responses to threat were entirely learned (rather than partly innate), the conclusion that emotion has a rapid, automatic component would need revision. The evidence points in the opposite direction, but the scientific commitment is to evidence, not to conclusions.

## Still puzzling

The exact mechanism by which appraisal produces emotion remains unclear. How does thinking about a situation *so quickly* produce a feeling, often before conscious awareness? The cognitive appraisal happens fast enough to be automatic, but appraisal is itself a cognitive process that should take time. The speed is striking and not yet fully explained. Also unsettled: whether there are truly universal emotions or whether emotion categories themselves are cultural constructions. Ekman's universals in facial expression are robust, but the question of whether the underlying emotional experience is universal or culturally shaped remains genuinely open.
