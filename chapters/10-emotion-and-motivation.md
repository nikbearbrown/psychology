# Chapter 10 — Emotion and Motivation
*The body speaks first. The mind argues about what it said.*

---

In 1967, Paul Ekman flew to the Fore highlands of Papua New Guinea to see people who had never seen a foreigner. No Western media, no photographs, no film. He showed a Fore man a photograph of a human face and told him a story: "His child has just died." The man pointed to the expression that matched. Ekman ran through six stories — fear, anger, sadness, disgust, surprise, happiness — and the same faces matched the same stories.

Then he flew home and showed the same photographs to college students in San Francisco. Same results.

Then he showed the photographs to Japanese students, Brazilian students, a hospitalized psychiatric patient. Same results.

The conclusion seemed clear: across cultures so different that they had never encountered each other, the same six facial expressions carried the same emotional meaning. The machinery was universal.

But then he asked the Fore highlander to *produce* an expression of anger. The man made a face. It was recognizable as anger — the same muscles, the same configuration. But an American looking at it would have found it subtly off, performed differently than they would perform it. Culture does not build the machinery. Culture dresses the machinery in rules about when to show what, to whom, how forcefully.

That tension — between what appears wired into the body and what is shaped by learning and context — is the central problem this chapter attacks. The machinery of emotion turns out to be three systems running simultaneously. And the mechanism of motivation turns out to be not a pyramid but a negotiation. Both are more interesting, and more practically useful, than the intuitive picture.

---

## Three theories and what each one got right

The puzzle starts with arousal. You round a corner and nearly step on a snake. Your heart races, your breathing quickens, adrenaline floods your system. Three minutes later you're in a dark theater watching a horror film. Your heart races, your breathing quickens, adrenaline floods your system. Same body state. One is fear, the other is something between fear and delight. The arousal is identical. The emotion is not. How do you get from the body's response to the feeling?

Three theories were proposed, in sequence, each one correcting a mistake in the one before.

### James-Lange: the feeling follows the body

William James proposed in the 1880s something that sounds backwards: you do not tremble because you are afraid. You are afraid *because* you tremble. The emotion is downstream of the body. Your sympathetic nervous system fires — heart racing, skin prickling, stomach tightening — and your brain registers that state and labels it *fear*. Different body states would produce different emotions. Fear has its own physiological signature; anger has a different one; sadness a different one still. The brain reads the body's message and translates it into feeling.

This is testable. If James-Lange is right, people with spinal-cord injuries that block body signals from reaching the brain should feel weaker emotions, because the body can't report its state. And that is, roughly, what research finds — emotional intensity does decrease with higher spinal lesions. The body feeds back to the brain. James-Lange captured something real.

What it missed: the emotions don't disappear with spinal damage. They diminish, but they persist. And the body's arousal is not specific enough to generate all the distinctions we make. Fear and anger and excitement all produce roughly similar autonomic signatures — racing heart, quick breathing, heightened alertness. If the feeling came purely from reading the body, you should not be able to tell them apart. You can.

### Cannon-Bard: body and feeling run in parallel

Walter Cannon and Philip Bard proposed in the 1920s that the emotion and the body's arousal are simultaneous and independent. The threat activates the limbic system — particularly the amygdala — which generates the emotional response. Simultaneously, it activates the sympathetic nervous system, which generates the arousal. These two events run in parallel. You feel fear and your heart races at the same time. One does not cause the other. They are two notes struck simultaneously.

Joseph LeDoux's later work confirmed the basic architecture. The amygdala has a fast pathway from the thalamus that bypasses the cortex entirely — you can be afraid before you have consciously processed what you're afraid of. The snake produces fear before your cortex has finished identifying it as a snake. Cannon-Bard was right that the emotional response is not downstream of the body's arousal; the amygdala fires first.

What Cannon-Bard missed: context matters. Identical arousal in different situations produces different emotions. The body's state is not irrelevant — but it is also not self-interpreting. Something else fills in the meaning.

### Schachter-Singer: arousal plus labeling

Stanley Schachter and Jerome Singer ran a clever experiment in 1962. Male college students were told they were participating in a study of a vitamin injection's effects on vision. Half received an injection of epinephrine — a drug that produces sympathetic nervous system activation: racing heart, flushing, trembling. The other half received a placebo.

Among the epinephrine group, some were told exactly what to expect: *you will feel your heart race and your hands tremble — that is the drug, it's temporary.* They had an explanation for the arousal. Others were told nothing, or given false expectations about what they'd feel. They had unexplained arousal — a racing heart with no account of why.

In the waiting room, each participant sat with an actor. In some sessions the actor behaved euphorically — joking, laughing, throwing paper airplanes. In other sessions he behaved angrily — complaining, getting hostile, eventually walking out.

The result: the participants who knew their arousal was from the drug felt nothing in particular. *That's the epinephrine.* The participants with unexplained arousal interpreted it through whatever was happening around them. With the euphoric actor, they felt euphoric. With the angry actor, they felt angry. Same body state. Different emotions. The difference was what the mind made of the arousal — the cognitive label applied in context.

Schachter and Singer showed that emotion requires two factors: physiological arousal and a cognitive interpretation of what that arousal means. The same pounding heart is terror at the edge of a cliff, excitement at the start of a race, love in the presence of someone you care about. The body provides the energy. The mind and the context provide the meaning.

### The synthesis: three systems in parallel

The honest reading of this evidence is that all three theories captured something real, and none captured everything.

The amygdala fires fast and automatically in response to learned threats, often before conscious awareness — this is Cannon-Bard's contribution. The body's arousal follows and feeds back to the brain, modulating the emotional experience — this is James-Lange's contribution. The cortex appraises the situation and labels the arousal in context — this is Schachter-Singer's contribution. The three systems run simultaneously. Sometimes the amygdala's fear wins the race and you respond before you've thought about it. Sometimes the cortex's appraisal reframes the situation and changes what you feel. Sometimes the arousal is genuinely ambiguous and context determines the emotion entirely.

### What this means for regulation

The constructive nature of emotion has a practical consequence: you can intervene in the construction.

Emotion regulation is the process of managing emotional experience and expression. Two major strategies are well-studied. *Reappraisal* — changing how you think about a situation — works on the appraisal pathway. Instead of interpreting the racing heart before a presentation as fear of failure, you interpret it as excitement and readiness. The body state is the same. The label changes. And the emotional experience genuinely shifts — not just how you describe it, but how it feels.

*Suppression* — hiding the expression — works on the output rather than the process. You feel angry but do not let your face show it. This is less effective at changing the underlying experience; the feeling persists even when the face does not show it. And suppression has costs: people who chronically suppress emotions show worse health outcomes, higher stress, and more depression. The body keeps its own account.

The interesting finding from the facial feedback hypothesis is that the influence runs both ways. If you hold your face in a position that resembles a smile, you feel slightly happier. The expression does not just follow from the emotion — it feeds back and shapes it. The Cannon-Bard architecture of independent streams understates the interaction. Body and mind are in continuous conversation.

## Hunger: when homeostasis works and when it breaks

Your body wants to stay near a certain weight. Not forever — weight changes — but at any given time, your body behaves as if it has a target. You eat until full. You get hungry again a few hours later. You can lose weight by eating less, but hunger nags. You can gain weight by overeating, but fullness comes sooner. There is something like a thermostat at work.

The machinery runs on two hormones and one feedback loop. When your stomach is empty, it produces ghrelin — the hunger signal: *eat now.* As you eat, fat cells produce leptin — the satiety signal: *enough.* Both signals converge on the hypothalamus, which integrates them with blood glucose levels and makes a continuous adjustment to appetite. Hunger is not binary. It is a dimmer that gets brighter as fuel drops and dimmer as fuel is restored.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: homeostatic hunger loop — shows stomach → ghrelin → hypothalamus and fat cells → leptin → hypothalamus, with blood glucose as a third input; arrows showing that hypothalamus output modulates appetite and metabolism; student should see this as a continuous feedback system, not an on/off switch] -->

This system works well under the conditions it evolved in: food that required effort to obtain, variable in availability, moderate in caloric density. Modern processed foods violate all three conditions simultaneously. High caloric density, constant availability, engineered for palatability, low in the fiber and protein that activate the leptin signal. The result is that people eat more without feeling full. Over time, chronic overeating can produce *leptin resistance* — fat cells keep producing leptin, but the hypothalamus stops responding to it. The signal becomes noise. The thermostat is still producing readings; the furnace has stopped listening.

This is the best current account of obesity's epidemic increase over the past forty years. The people haven't changed. The food environment has. Roughly 40% of American adults are now obese — a body mass index above 30 — with associated risk for cardiovascular disease, stroke, type-2 diabetes, and several cancers. Set-point theory, the older account that posited a fixed defended weight, does not explain the data well; people who lose weight don't show the compensatory metabolic changes the theory predicts, and the apparent set point shifts with the food environment. What's real is homeostasis. What's broken is the food environment that homeostasis was calibrated for.

At the other extreme, eating disorders represent the hunger-satiety system undermined from a different direction — not by environmental abundance but by psychological distortion of how the signals are interpreted.

Anorexia nervosa involves severe restriction to the point of starvation, accompanied by a distorted body image in which the person perceives their dangerously thin body as overweight. The restriction can produce bone loss, cardiac arrhythmia, kidney failure, and death. The causes are multiple and interact: genetic predisposition (the heritability is substantial in twin studies), psychological factors including perfectionism and need for control, and cultural factors — the thin ideal promoted in Western media and fashion. Anorexia is not a choice to be thin. It is a disorder of perception and motivation in which the homeostatic signals are overridden by a distorted cognitive appraisal of the body.

Bulimia nervosa involves cycles of bingeing followed by compensatory behaviors — purging, laxative use, excessive exercise. The physical consequences are severe: electrolyte imbalance, cardiac risk, esophageal damage from stomach acid. The psychological profile overlaps with anorexia: perfectionism, body-image distortion, anxiety.

The important point for both: the causes are biological, psychological, and cultural simultaneously. The neurobiological profile is real — eating disorders involve measurable differences in reward processing and impulse control in the brain. The psychological vulnerabilities are real. The cultural pressure is real. None of these is sufficient alone. The disorder emerges from their combination.

## Motivation: not a pyramid, a negotiation

Abraham Maslow proposed in 1943 that human motivation could be arranged as a hierarchy. Physiological needs at the base: food, water, sleep. Safety above that. Then belonging, then esteem, then at the peak, self-actualization — the realization of your full potential. The picture is a pyramid with five levels, and the idea is that you cannot climb to a higher level until the lower level is satisfied.

The hierarchy is intuitive. It is also not supported by evidence.

People go hungry for love. Parents sacrifice their safety for their children. Artists starve for their vision. The strict ordering is wrong. What is true is that biological needs must be met *enough* to leave resources for other pursuits — but "enough" is not "fully satisfied." You can pursue meaning on a half-empty stomach. You cannot pursue much of anything when you're starving. The threshold matters; the hierarchy does not.

A better framework comes from Edward Deci and Richard Ryan's self-determination theory. Rather than a hierarchy, they propose three fundamental psychological needs that operate simultaneously: *autonomy* — the sense that you are acting by genuine choice; *competence* — the sense that you can succeed at what matters; and *relatedness* — the sense of meaningful connection to others.

What makes intrinsic motivation so powerful is that it satisfies all three simultaneously. When you do something because you genuinely want to, because you're good at it (or becoming good at it), and because it connects you to people you care about — those activities produce high engagement, persistence, and quality of work. Remove any one need, and motivation deteriorates.

This explains the *overjustification effect*, one of the more surprising findings in motivation research. If you love baking and bake on weekends purely for the pleasure of it, your motivation is intrinsic. Now suppose you start selling at a farmer's market. A month in, you notice you no longer want to bake just for fun. The intrinsic motivation has been partially displaced by extrinsic motivation. Why?

Because the narrative has changed. *I bake because I enjoy it* becomes *I bake because I get paid.* The autonomy is compromised — you are no longer acting purely from internal choice; there is now an external constraint shaping the activity. Deci and Ryan's framework predicts this precisely: when external rewards undermine the perception of autonomy, intrinsic motivation decreases.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: self-determination theory — three overlapping circles labeled autonomy, competence, and relatedness; intersection labeled intrinsic motivation; arrows showing that external rewards can shrink the autonomy circle, producing the overjustification effect; extrinsic motivation shown outside the intersection] -->

The effect is real but not universal. Unexpected rewards do not undermine intrinsic motivation as much as expected ones. Verbal praise tends to undermine less than tangible rewards like money. And for activities that were not intrinsically motivating to begin with, external rewards don't have anything to undermine. The practical implication: external rewards should be used carefully and sparingly for activities you want people to continue caring about, because you can kill the thing you're trying to encourage.

### Achievement motivation and the mindset variable

David McClelland showed in the 1950s that people differ stably in achievement motivation — the drive to accomplish difficult things, to overcome obstacles, to do things well. He measured it through thematic analysis of stories people wrote about ambiguous scenes. High-achievement people wrote about characters overcoming challenges and reaching goals. Low-achievement people wrote about relationships and contentment.

Achievement motivation predicts behavior: people high in it seek challenging tasks, persist through failure, and want feedback. But it is not fixed. Carol Dweck's work on mindset showed that the belief about whether ability is fixed or growable is itself a powerful motivational variable. A *fixed mindset* — the belief that ability is innate and unchangeable — produces avoidance of challenges: if you fail, you've proven you lack ability. A *growth mindset* — the belief that ability develops through effort — produces approach: failure is information about what to practice next.

The mindset is learned through the kind of praise and feedback children receive. Praise for being smart ("you're so smart") promotes a fixed mindset — the ability is the thing, and it might be lost. Praise for effort ("you worked hard at that") promotes a growth mindset — the process is the thing, and more process produces more growth. The same child, praised differently, will make different choices about which challenges to attempt.

### Sexual motivation and what biology determines

Sexual motivation is rooted in the hypothalamus. Specific hypothalamic lesions in rats eliminate the *motivation* to seek sex while leaving the *ability* to perform intact — or vice versa. The motivation and the mechanics are dissociable systems.

Testosterone is central to sexual motivation across sexes. People with higher circulating testosterone report higher sexual desire; testosterone replacement increases desire in people whose levels are low. But unlike hunger, which cycles reliably every few hours, sexual motivation is highly variable and context-sensitive. The hypothalamus provides the substrate. Culture, psychology, and relationship all shape how the substrate is expressed.

Sexual orientation — the pattern of emotional, romantic, and sexual attraction — has clear biological foundations. Twin studies show substantial heritability. Genetic association studies have identified multiple chromosomal regions involved (no single gene; it is polygenic). Brain imaging shows structural and functional differences across orientations. Prenatal hormone exposure affects the probability of non-heterosexual orientation. The biological evidence is robust.

What the biology does not determine is how orientation is expressed. In some cultures, same-sex behavior is part of expected development. In others it is suppressed. The capacity is similar; the expression varies with the social environment.

What the evidence does not support is that sexual orientation can be changed through intervention. Conversion therapy — any attempt to change orientation through psychological, behavioral, or biomedical means — has no demonstrated effectiveness. It has substantial demonstrated harm: increased rates of depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation. Every major psychological and medical organization has issued statements against it. The orientation is not the problem; the stigma is.

## How emotion and motivation work together

The morning you read disturbing news and find yourself still angry three hours later: here is what happened. The amygdala registered the content as unjust and fired — rapid, automatic, emotional activation. Simultaneously, your cortex appraised it: *This is wrong, and I should do something.* Simultaneously, your sympathetic nervous system produced arousal. You posted a response on social media — motivated by the need to express (autonomy), to be heard (competence), to be part of a community of people who share your values (relatedness). You checked for responses. Some agreed; the relatedness need was met, and you felt good. Some disagreed; the competence challenge was activated, and you felt worse.

Whether you stayed angry or moved on depended on your emotion-regulation strategy. Reappraisal — *This is how disagreement works; some people understand, some don't* — would have shifted the emotion. Without reappraisal, the original appraisal held, and the anger persisted. Over time, if you check responses enough, the pattern automatizes: you no longer deliberately check; you just do it. The motivation has become a habit. The three systems — emotion, motivation, habit — have merged into a single behavioral pattern.

This convergence is not dysfunction. It is how the systems are supposed to work. Motivation without emotion is directionless — you would not know which goals matter. Emotion without motivation is inert — you would feel things but have no drive to act on them. Together, they produce agency: the sense that what you do changes what happens next.

The places where the systems break are instructive. When homeostatic regulation of hunger is disrupted by a food environment it was not calibrated for, the result is population-level obesity. When the appraisal pathway is systematically distorted — when the body is persistently appraised as wrong, as too large, as requiring correction — the result is an eating disorder. When the motivation to achieve is undermined by external rewards applied to activities that were intrinsically satisfying, the result is disengagement.

The mechanism is the same in all three cases: one of the systems is working as designed, but the environment it is working in is wrong for it. The hunger system is working — it just encounters the wrong food. The appraisal system is working — it just processes the wrong cultural signal. The motivation system is working — it just responds to the wrong reward structure. Understanding the mechanism tells you where the intervention should go.

## What would change my mind

If evidence demonstrated that sexual orientation could be changed through psychological or behavioral intervention with lasting effects and without psychological harm, the conclusion that orientation is not changeable would need revision. To date, no such evidence exists. More broadly, if future research revealed that the amygdala's responses to threat were entirely learned rather than partly innate, the rapid-automatic account of emotion would need revision. The evidence points in the opposite direction, but the commitment is to evidence, not to conclusions.

---

## Still puzzling

The exact mechanism by which appraisal produces emotion so quickly remains unclear. How does thinking about a situation fast enough to feel immediate produce a feeling — often before conscious awareness? The cognitive appraisal happens at the speed of reflex, but appraisal is itself a cognitive process that should require processing time. The speed is striking and not yet fully explained. Also unsettled: whether the six basic emotions Ekman identified are truly universal categories, or whether emotion categories themselves are culturally constructed and the cross-cultural similarity in expression reflects surface similarity over deeper variation in experience.
