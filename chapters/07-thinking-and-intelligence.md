# Chapter 7 — Thinking and Intelligence

## Cold open

Daniel Kahneman is in a laboratory at Stanford in the 1980s, watching something peculiar happen. He has given his subjects a profile: "Linda is thirty-one years old, single, outspoken, and bright. She majored in philosophy. As a student, she was deeply concerned with issues of discrimination and social justice, and also participated in antinuclear demonstrations."

The question is simple. Which is more probable: (A) that Linda is a bank teller, or (B) that Linda is a bank teller *and* a feminist?

This is a question where the math is certain. If Linda is something *and* something else, she cannot be more probable than if she is just one of those things. The probability of two conditions both being true is always lower than the probability of just one condition being true. It's basic set logic.

But Kahneman watches. His subjects read the description. Eighty-five percent choose answer B — that Linda is a bank teller *and* a feminist.

This is called the *conjunction fallacy*: we judge a claim as more probable when it is more specific, when it should be less probable. The human mind, presented with vivid details and a coherent story, treats the probability not as a mathematical question but as a question of *fit*. How well does "bank teller and feminist" match the description of Linda? How well does "bank teller, period" match it? The details make the conjunction feel more *real*, more *likely*, and that feeling overwhelms the arithmetic.

Thinking, it turns out, is not a single process. Some of it is fast and automatic—the parts of your mind that made you choose answer B without conscious deliberation. Some of it is slow and deliberate—the part that, when you learned the arithmetic, forced you to revise your answer. Understanding which type of thinking you're using, when, and why, is the foundation of cognitive psychology. Understanding what "intelligence" means and how to measure it is the foundation of what intelligence testing tries to do—and where it often fails.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- **Define** cognition and describe how concepts, prototypes, and schemas help organize thinking.
- **Explain** the difference between algorithmic problem-solving and heuristic shortcuts, and **identify** common decision biases that arise from heuristic thinking.
- **Compare** major theories of intelligence—Spearman's *g*, Cattell's fluid and crystallized intelligence, Sternberg's triarchic theory, and Gardner's multiple intelligences—and **evaluate** what each explains well and what it leaves out.
- **Describe** what IQ tests measure, how they are scored and normed, and **critique** the role they play in education and clinical settings.
- **Examine** the evidence for genetic and environmental contributions to intelligence, and **explain** why IQ test scores differ systematically across socioeconomic and ethnic groups.
- **Distinguish** intellectual disabilities from learning disabilities, and **give** examples of specific learning disorders like dyslexia and dyscalculia.

### Prerequisites

Familiarity with the basic ideas of probability and statistics from earlier chapters—the idea that scores on a population follow a distribution, that we can talk about averages and variation around the average. Willingness to question what you thought you already knew about your own mind.

### Why this chapter matters

You are constantly making decisions with incomplete information. You are constantly using shortcuts that usually work but sometimes fail. You have been told your whole life whether you are "smart," often reduced to a single number on a test. This chapter teaches you what that number actually measures, what it leaves out, and what the science really says about where intelligence comes from. More than that, it teaches you to notice when your own mind is using the fast, automatic shortcuts—the heuristics—and when you should stop and do the arithmetic. That difference between automatic and deliberate thinking is the machinery underneath cognition.

## Concept 1 — How thinking organizes itself: concepts, prototypes, and schemas

When you wake up tomorrow morning, your brain does not start fresh. It does not ask itself, "What is this experience I am having? What is this set of objects and sensations I should arrange into meaning?" Instead, your brain immediately reaches into its filing system and pulls out a concept: *kitchen*, *breakfast*, *Monday*. These are not neutral observations. They are structures, built from your own experience and the experience of thousands of people who spoke English before you, that instantly tell you what to pay attention to and what to ignore.

A *concept* is a mental category—a grouping of things that share a common feature or set of features. The concept *dog* contains Chihuahuas and Great Danes, poodles and mutts, because we have agreed that the essential features of dogness—four legs, fur, domestication, a certain kind of bark and shape of face—define the category. The concept *furniture* groups chairs, tables, couches, desks; the concept *color* groups red and blue and millions of shades in between. Your brain has thousands of these categories, constantly active, constantly filtering experience.

How did you build these categories? Some came from direct experience. If you have lived your whole life in Vermont, you have a rich, detailed concept of *snow* built from seeing it, feeling it, tasting it, shoveling it, sliding in it. If you grew up on a Caribbean island, you may have built your concept of snow from pictures and films—indirect experience, but experience nonetheless. The crucial thing is that your concept of snow, whatever it is, came from *observation*. We call these *natural concepts*—categories built naturally, from the way the world presents itself to you.

But not all concepts come from observation. *Triangle* does not come from seeing triangles in nature and finding a pattern. A triangle is a concept defined by an explicit set of rules: a closed shape with exactly three sides and three angles whose sum is 180 degrees. This is an *artificial concept*, or *formal concept*—defined not by observation but by a specification. You do not learn triangles from the world showing them to you. You learn triangles by someone teaching you the rule.

The machinery gets more sophisticated. Within the concept *dog*, your brain stores a *prototype*—the best example of dogness, the dog that comes to mind first. If your first dog was a golden retriever, that retriever might be your prototype for the entire category. When you hear the word *dog*, the golden retriever is what flashes through your mind before you consciously think about all the other kinds of dogs that exist. Prototypes are the brain's way of doing fast categorization. You don't have to check whether something matches the definition of *dog* by going through a mental checklist. You compare it to your prototype and see how much it resembles that best example. A German shepherd looks like a dog to you because it resembles your mental golden retriever. A Chihuahua looks like a dog, too, though it looks less similar to your prototype—it is a more distant match, a prototype that is also a dog.

This is fast, and it usually works. It also creates the structure for systematic errors. There is a cultural concept called *leadership* that has a prototype: we think of a tall man with a deep voice and confidence. When we evaluate people for leadership positions, we unconsciously measure them against this prototype. Women and short men and people with quieter voices get rated as less good leaders, not because they lack the competence but because they do not match the prototype in our heads. The prototype is *faster than conscious evaluation*. It shapes what we see before we even know we are looking.

Groups of related concepts cluster into larger mental structures called *schemas*. A *schema* is a cluster of related concepts organized around a central theme. Your schema for *restaurant* includes the concept *menu*, *server*, *food*, *payment*, *sitting down*. When you walk into an unfamiliar restaurant in an unfamiliar country, your restaurant schema activates. You know what to expect, what sequence of events will probably happen, what role you are supposed to play. If the restaurant does not follow the script, if the server does not arrive when you expect or if you are supposed to pay before eating instead of after, you notice the violation because the schema had set up a specific expectation.

Event schemas—also called *cognitive scripts*—are schemas for routines. Your event schema for getting on an elevator is precise and automatic: you wait for the doors to open, you step in, you turn around to face the doors, you look for your floor button. You never face the back of the elevator. If you did, it would feel deeply wrong, not because there is a law against it but because the script you learned—the event schema—says you turn around. When you walk into an elevator facing backward, the other people in the car notice. It's a violation of the script, and it triggers discomfort.

Here is where schemas show their power and their danger in a single example: texting while driving. Your event schema for driving is well-practiced, automatic, deeply embedded. When you hear your phone ring or buzz, a different event schema—the schema for checking your phone—activates. The two schemas compete. Texting while driving is now so common that many drivers have built a blended schema: driving *and* checking the phone, simultaneously, without thinking. The schema is so strong that even when drivers know the danger, even when they consciously decide not to text while driving, many reach for the phone anyway. The schema is faster than conscious intention. The event schema tells the hand to move before the conscious mind finishes its reasoning.

The trade-off is this: concepts, prototypes, and schemas make fast thinking possible. They are the machinery of intuition, of quick judgment, of moving through the world without consciously deliberating about everything. They are also the machinery of persistent error. The prototype that makes fast judgment possible is the same prototype that causes you to hire people who look like previous hires. The schema that makes routine action automatic is the same schema that makes the dangerous action routine.

### Worked example — How a schema shapes expectation

You walk into a coffee shop for the first time. Your schema for *coffee shop* activates immediately. You expect that you will order at the counter, or that a server will come to your table, or that you will order on a tablet. You expect that you will receive a menu—or if this is a basic coffee place, you expect that the menu is written on the wall. You expect prices to be reasonable, the environment to be relatively quiet, and other customers to be reading, working on laptops, or having conversations.

Now imagine you walk into a coffee shop in Tokyo. Your schema for coffee shop activates, but many of the script's expectations are violated. In a traditional *kissaten*, or Japanese coffee shop, the proprietor hand-roasts the beans and brews each cup with careful attention. You sit in quiet respect, often alone or in small groups. There is no food. The coffee is expensive. The proprietor may spend five minutes on a single pour-over cup. This is not how your schema said coffee shops work. The expectation is violated at every step.

But here's the mechanism: because you have a schema for coffee shops, you can *learn* a new schema. You experience the Japanese kissaten not as chaos but as a *different kind of coffee shop*—a schema that your brain can build and remember. Someone without a schema for coffee shops at all would be confused by both the American coffee chain and the Japanese kissaten equally.

### Common misconceptions

**Prototypes are not rules.** A prototype is not a checklist of required features. It is the best example. A penguin is a bird, even though it does not match most people's prototype for bird—the prototype is usually something like a robin or an eagle, something that flies. The definition of bird includes penguins. The prototype does not.

**Schemas are not always conscious.** You do not consciously think through the coffee-shop script every time you enter a café. The schema activates automatically, beneath awareness. Only when the script is violated do you become conscious of it.

**Event schemas are difficult to change, even when you want to.** The more automatic a schema becomes, the harder it is to override it with conscious intention. This is why habits are hard to break and why people continue to text while driving despite consciously deciding not to.

## Concept 2 — Problem-solving strategies: algorithms, heuristics, and the biases that follow

You have a problem. You want to mail a report to your supervisor by the end of the business day, and it is urgent. Your printer will not work. What do you do?

One approach is *trial and error*: you try one solution (restart the printer), and if it does not work, you try another (check the ink level), and another (check if it's connected), until something works. Trial and error will eventually solve the problem if you keep going long enough, but it is inefficient—you might try many things before you find the right one.

A better approach is an *algorithm*. An algorithm is a step-by-step formula, a procedure you follow precisely and in order, that guarantees you will reach the correct answer. A recipe is an algorithm. A long-division problem is an algorithm. The instructions for assembling furniture from a box are an algorithm. Algorithms are everywhere because they work: if you follow the steps exactly, you get the result exactly.

But algorithms are slow. And not every problem has an algorithm. What do you do when there is no step-by-step formula?

You use a *heuristic*—a mental shortcut, a rule of thumb, a general strategy that usually works and saves you time and effort. For the printer problem, a useful heuristic is *working backwards*. You know the deadline is end of business day. You know it takes your supervisor time to open mail. You work backward from the deadline and realize you need to mail the package by 2 p.m. to guarantee it arrives by tomorrow morning. That backward reasoning gives you a deadline: mail by 2 p.m. Now the problem is simpler—not "how do I fix my printer?" but "how do I get a printed report in someone's hands by 2 p.m.?" You could take a photograph of the document and email it. You could walk to the library and print it there. You could ask a colleague if you can use their printer. The heuristic of working backward narrows the problem and opens new solutions.

Heuristics are fast. They usually work. They are how we navigate a world that has far too much information for algorithmic processing. But they have a cost: they sometimes fail in systematic, predictable ways. These failures are called *cognitive biases*—tendencies to judge or decide in ways that consistently depart from accurate reasoning.

One common bias is the *anchoring bias*. You are apartment hunting with a friend. A realtor shows you a series of run-down apartments for $1,600 a month. Then the realtor shows you a really nice apartment for $2,000. Suddenly, $2,000 does not feel expensive—it feels like a good deal relative to the run-down $1,600 apartments. The first price you saw—the *anchor*—has shifted your judgment about what seems reasonable. The realtor chose the anchor on purpose, to make the higher price feel acceptable. Anchoring bias causes you to fixate on an initial piece of information when making a decision, and subsequent information gets interpreted relative to that anchor.

Another is the *availability heuristic*. You judge something as more probable, more common, or more important if an example of it comes readily to mind. After you see a news story about a plane crash, you become worried about flying, because the crash is now vivid and available in your memory. You do not think about the statistics—that car crashes are far more frequent than plane crashes, that driving to the airport is more dangerous than the flight. The vivid example shapes your judgment of probability.

The *representativeness bias* causes you to make judgments based on how similar something is to a stereotype or prototype. You meet a quiet, careful person with thick glasses who likes crossword puzzles. You assume this person is more likely to be a librarian than a farmer, because this person fits your prototype of a librarian. You do not think about base rates—the actual proportion of farmers and librarians in the population. If the population has far more farmers than librarians, the base rate should overwhelm the stereotype. A random farmer is more likely than a random librarian to fit your mental picture of a library worker simply because there are so many more farmers.

The *confirmation bias* makes you seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms what you already believe. If you think your professor is unfair, you notice all the instances where the professor seems harsh. You remember the sarcastic remark from three weeks ago. You do not consciously remember the three times the professor stayed late to help you. The bias filters your memory through what you already believe, which means your belief becomes more certain the more you think about it—even though the belief might be wrong.

The *representativeness bias* operates in conjunction with base-rate neglect: you ignore the actual frequency of events in a population. There are many more car accidents than plane crashes, many more farmers than librarians. But people are more likely to *remember* a plane crash than a car accident, and more likely to *think* of a librarian than a farmer when a description matches a stereotype. You forget the base rates and let the vivid example or the stereotyped description guide your judgment.

Finally, *hindsight bias* is the tendency to believe that an event you just experienced was predictable, even though it really was not. After the stock market drops, people say "I knew it was coming." After a political upset, people say "of course that was going to happen." Hindsight makes the outcome feel inevitable, which feels like it should have been obvious in advance. This is a form of overconfidence: your past judgments seem worse than they were because you cannot actually remember how uncertain they were before the outcome was known.

All of these biases have something in common: they are *faster than careful reasoning*. The heuristic does not require you to look up base rates or carefully consider the data. It gives you an answer immediately. For most of life, that speed is valuable. You have far too much information to process, and you need to make decisions quickly. The heuristic shortcuts let you do that. The cost is that those shortcuts sometimes lead you astray in ways that feel completely intuitive while they are happening.

### Worked example — Seeing the availability heuristic at work

During a drought, water agencies want to convince people to use less water. They could present statistics: "Per capita water usage in this county is 250 gallons per day, compared to 100 gallons per day in water-rich regions. We need to reduce by 40 percent." Statistics are abstract. Instead, the agency can show you vivid images: empty reservoirs, brown lawns, the concrete sides of drying lakes. The vivid image makes drought *available*—easy to remember, easy to imagine. You are more likely to change your behavior because of the available image than because of the abstract statistic.

The water agency is using the availability heuristic in reverse: they are making the problem so vivid that you treat it as more serious and more probable than the statistics alone would suggest. This is not manipulation (though it can be). This is how human reasoning actually works. We evolved to make decisions in environments where vivid, immediate evidence was the most reliable information. An ancestor who waited for statistical base rates while running from a predator did not pass on genes.

### Common misconceptions

**Heuristics are not always wrong.** A heuristic is a shortcut that usually works. The availability heuristic causes you to overestimate the probability of vivid events, but it also helps you quickly assess which problems are most urgent. The representativeness heuristic causes you to stereotype, but it also allows you to quickly categorize ambiguous situations.

**Biases are not character flaws.** They are not stupidity or irrationality. They are the systematic results of how fast thinking works. Highly intelligent people are just as susceptible to them as everyone else. Daniel Kahneman, who discovered the conjunction fallacy, is himself vulnerable to the biases he discovered. Knowing about the bias does not automatically free you from it.

**Slowing down helps, but it is not enough.** If you know about anchoring bias, you are less anchored, but you are not free from it. The bias operates beneath conscious awareness. Even people who study decision-making fall prey to the same biases in situations where they are distracted or tired.

## Concept 3 — Theories of intelligence: What is it, really?

A father watches his four-and-a-half-year-old son read words aloud: "Go, Pig! Go!" The father had not been teaching his son to read. He had simply answered questions about letters and words, everywhere they appear. Now his son is reading on his own. Is this exceptional intelligence? Is this simply where childhood language development naturally goes? The father does not know. He wonders what intelligence actually is.

This question has puzzled psychologists for more than a century, and the answer has changed multiple times as different researchers have emphasized different aspects of the problem.

**Charles Spearman**, a British psychologist in the 1900s, proposed that intelligence is a single, general factor—he called it *g*, for general intelligence. All the different mental abilities—reasoning, vocabulary, pattern recognition, processing speed—are expressions of a single underlying capacity. Some people have more *g*, some have less, and the differences are measurable and comparable across individuals. This theory is appealing for its simplicity: if there is one intelligence, then there is one number that captures it.

But other researchers saw the problem immediately: people vary in different cognitive ways. Someone might be brilliant at reasoning about abstract concepts but struggle with verbal expression. Someone else might be an excellent writer but mediocre at mathematics. If intelligence is truly a single factor, how can these differences exist?

**Raymond Cattell** proposed a two-factor model in the 1940s. He split intelligence into *fluid intelligence*—the ability to see complex relationships and solve novel problems—and *crystallized intelligence*—acquired knowledge and the ability to retrieve and use what you have learned. Fluid intelligence is what you use when you navigate a detour on a familiar route because road construction forced you off your usual path. It is fast, adaptive, problem-solving in the moment. Crystallized intelligence is what you use to answer questions on a test: you have already learned the information, and you are simply retrieving it. Fluid intelligence declines with age, as the brain slows. Crystallized intelligence often improves with age, because you accumulate knowledge. A seventy-year-old may not solve novel spatial puzzles as fast as a twenty-year-old, but the seventy-year-old knows far more.

**Robert Sternberg** took a different approach in the 1980s. He proposed the *triarchic theory of intelligence*—three forms of intelligence that work together: *analytical intelligence*, *practical intelligence*, and *creative intelligence*. Analytical intelligence is what IQ tests measure: the ability to analyze, evaluate, judge, and contrast information. When you read a novel for literature class and compare the motivations of two characters, you are using analytical intelligence. Practical intelligence is "street smarts"—the ability to find solutions that work in everyday life using the knowledge you have gained from experience. A person with high practical intelligence knows how to get things done in the real world, even without formal education. Creative intelligence is the ability to invent novel solutions to problems or create original work. Sternberg's point was that the real world demands all three types, and none of them is sufficient on its own. A person might score very high on analytical intelligence—perfect on standardized tests—but be helpless at solving practical problems.

**Howard Gardner** went further and proposed *Multiple Intelligences Theory*: not three types of intelligence, but *at least eight*. Linguistic intelligence (the ability to use language), logical-mathematical intelligence (the ability to reason with numbers and symbols), musical intelligence, bodily-kinesthetic intelligence (the ability to control and coordinate the body), spatial intelligence, interpersonal intelligence (the ability to understand and relate to other people), intrapersonal intelligence (the ability to understand your own emotions and motivations), and naturalistic intelligence (the ability to understand and categorize the natural world). Gardner's theory has been extremely influential in education, and many educators now try to recognize and develop all eight forms of intelligence. But cognitive psychologists have criticized it for being too broad, for defining intelligence so expansively that almost any skill becomes an intelligence, and for lacking empirical support—the eight forms do not actually factor out as eight independent things in the way the theory suggests they should.

The most comprehensive model to date is the *Cattell-Horn-Carroll theory*, which arranges abilities in a three-level hierarchy. At the top is general intelligence (*g*, as Spearman proposed). Below that is a second level of broad abilities—fluid reasoning, short-term memory, processing speed—which are somewhat independent of each other. Below that is a third level of narrow, specific abilities that can be directly measured, like memory span or reading fluency. This model preserves both Spearman's insight that there is something general underlying all cognitive performance and Cattell's insight that intelligence is not a single thing but multiple things that correlate with each other.

What is intelligence? The evidence suggests that it is something like this: there is a general capacity—*g*—that influences how well you perform on a wide range of cognitive tasks. But this general capacity is not destiny. Different contexts demand different forms of intelligence. A culture that values the ability to navigate by stars and read animal tracks will identify as intelligent people who might score low on a standardized test. A culture that values collective harmony will define intelligence differently than a culture that values individual achievement. Oprah Winfrey, who began life in poverty and abuse, developed the practical and interpersonal intelligence needed to become a media mogul. Her journey is not captured by any single IQ number.

The trade-off is this: *g* is measurable, and it is predictive—people with higher *g* tend to do better on academic tasks, earn higher incomes, and have longer lifespans. But *g* is only a partial picture of what makes a mind work. Creativity, practical wisdom, emotional understanding, the ability to work with others—these are not captured by standard intelligence tests, and they often matter more in real life than *g* does.

### Worked example — How different intelligences apply to the same problem

Imagine you are a team leader managing a project that is falling behind schedule. You need to figure out why and what to do about it.

Using *analytical intelligence*, you gather data: How many tasks are completed versus planned? What is the rate of task completion by team member? Are there dependencies where one person's delay blocks others? You analyze the numbers and identify where the bottleneck is.

Using *practical intelligence*, you talk to the team members. You learn that one person is overwhelmed but has not asked for help because they are afraid of looking incompetent. You learn that two people are waiting for information from an external department, but no one has followed up. You learn that another person actually has capacity and would welcome more work to feel productive. You figure out what will actually get the project back on track.

Using *creative intelligence*, you notice that the standard approach to scheduling—dividing tasks evenly and following the original plan—does not match the actual skills and constraints of the people you have. You propose a different task allocation that leverages what each person is actually good at, and you suggest reaching out to the external department through a back-channel contact who can prioritize your request. You innovate around the constraint rather than accept it.

The project succeeds not because you are high in one form of intelligence, but because you use all three types.

### Common misconceptions

**Intelligence is not fixed.** While there is a genetic component to intelligence (more on this later), intelligence is also shaped by experience, education, and opportunity. A person's performance on an intelligence test reflects their current functioning, not their unchangeable capacity.

**High intelligence does not guarantee success.** Sternberg has shown that practical intelligence is often a better predictor of real-world success than analytical intelligence. A brilliant academic might fail at business because they lack practical sense. A person of ordinary analytical intelligence but strong practical and interpersonal intelligence might build a successful organization.

**Intelligence is not one thing.** The fact that multiple theories exist is not a sign of confusion. It is a sign that intelligence is genuinely multifaceted. A person might be high in analytical intelligence and low in interpersonal intelligence. They might be a strong creative thinker but weak at practical problem-solving. The theories are not wrong; they are emphasizing different aspects of a complex phenomenon.

## Integration — The conjunction fallacy, revisited

Return to Daniel Kahneman's question about Linda. Eighty-five percent of people say that Linda is more likely to be a bank teller *and* a feminist than to be a bank teller, period. This is mathematically impossible. If Linda is something *and* something else, she cannot be more probable than if she is just one of those things.

Why does the error happen? Because of how thinking works.

When you read the description of Linda—intelligent, outspoken, socially concerned—your brain activates concepts related to feminism. Your schema for "feminist bank teller" becomes available. You run a quick heuristic judgment: how well does this person match my prototype of a feminist? How well does she match my prototype of a bank teller? A bank teller, in the stereotype, is more conventional, less outspoken. A feminist, in the stereotype, is exactly what Linda sounds like. Your prototype-matching heuristic says: "Bank teller and feminist" fits better than "bank teller" alone.

This is *representativeness bias*—judging probability by similarity to a prototype. It is also *availability bias*—you are focusing on the vivid, detailed description of Linda, not on base rates (what percentage of bank tellers are feminists, anyway?). Together, these biases override the mathematical truth.

But here is the deeper lesson: your fast thinking—automatic, intuitive, based on prototypes and schemas—is not wrong. It is incredibly useful most of the time. A quick heuristic judgment lets you navigate the world without conscious deliberation about everything. The problem is knowing when to trust your intuition and when to stop and do the math.

Kahneman's research showed that even when subjects are told about the conjunction fallacy, they continue to fall prey to it. The bias operates beneath conscious awareness. Even trained statisticians make this error when they are not paying attention. This is not a failure of intelligence. This is how human reasoning actually works.

The machinery of thinking—concepts, prototypes, schemas, heuristics, and the biases that follow—is designed for speed. Your brain had to evolve to make decisions about survival and reproduction in environments with incomplete information and no time for deliberation. A hunter who waited for complete statistical certainty while deciding whether to flee a predator did not pass on genes. The heuristics that sometimes lead you astray are the same heuristics that let you move through a complex world quickly enough to survive.

Understanding this machinery does not free you from it. But it lets you notice when you are using it, and when it might be leading you astray.

## Concept 4 — Measuring intelligence: IQ tests and what they actually measure

Alfred Binet, a French psychologist in the early 1900s, was asked by the government to develop a test to identify children who would struggle in school. Binet built a test that asked children questions, presented problems, and measured their ability to reason and answer. He called the measure the *mental age*—the age at which an average child would score at your level. If you were a nine-year-old but you scored like a twelve-year-old, your mental age was twelve.

The next innovation came from Lewis Terman at Stanford, who standardized the test. Terman gave the test to thousands of children of different ages and created a standard: the average score for each age. He calculated the *intelligence quotient*, or *IQ*, as mental age divided by chronological age, multiplied by 100. (An IQ of 100 is average, by definition.) The test was *normed* and *standardized*—administered consistently to a large population so that any future score could be compared to this standard baseline.

The test that came to dominate the field was developed by David Wechsler in the 1930s. Wechsler believed that intelligence was not just verbal reasoning but a combination of verbal and nonverbal skills. His test, and its modern descendants (the WAIS for adults, the WISC for children), combines subtests that measure vocabulary, reasoning, spatial ability, processing speed, and working memory. A single IQ score emerges from all these subtests, but the test also reports sub-scores for each domain.

Here is what IQ tests measure well: they are reliable—if you take the test twice, you get roughly the same score. They are predictive—people with higher IQ scores tend to do better in academic settings, earn more income (though the correlation is not overwhelming), and have longer lifespans. They identify children who need academic support and children who need more advanced material. In clinical settings, they help identify intellectual disability.

But the question of what intelligence really is—what these tests are actually measuring—is more complicated than the test makers often admit.

In 1912, a phenomenon began to be noticed: each generation seemed to score higher on IQ tests than the previous generation. This is called the *Flynn effect*, named after James Flynn, who documented it carefully. In the United States and other developed countries, IQ scores rose about 3 points per decade through the twentieth century. If IQ is fixed at birth, and if the genetic inheritance of intelligence is stable across generations, this pattern should not exist.

Flynn himself argues that higher scores do not mean that newer generations are more intelligent in any deep sense. Instead, they reflect improvements in nutrition, education, exposure to abstract reasoning (through schools and media), and test-taking familiarity. IQ tests are not measuring intelligence in isolation. They are measuring intelligence in a particular context, at a particular time, in a particular population.

This becomes even clearer when you look at differences in average IQ scores across groups. In the United States, average IQ scores differ by ethnic group. White students, on average, score higher than Black students; Asian students, on average, score higher than White students. These differences have been documented repeatedly.

Are these differences genetic? Arthur Jensen argued in a controversial 1969 paper that they were. He proposed that intelligence came in two types (Level I and Level II), and that Level II—the abstract reasoning that matters most—differed by race. His conclusions were seized upon by scientists and political groups seeking justification for racism. The evidence did not support the conclusion. Jensen's own data could be explained by socioeconomic differences, by stereotype threat (the performance depression that occurs when you are aware of negative stereotypes about your group), by differences in educational quality, and by the cultural specificity of the tests themselves.

The cultural specificity problem is genuine. IQ tests are normed on specific populations. The test items reflect knowledge and reasoning patterns that are more familiar to some cultural groups than others. When the state of California was using IQ tests to place students in special education, African American students were dramatically overrepresented in these placements. A court case, *Larry P. v. Riles* (1979), found that the tests were "culturally biased" and that they "have a discriminatory impact against Black children." The state had to stop using the tests in this way.

What does IQ predict best? Academic performance in school. IQ tests were designed to predict who would do well in school, and they do that job reasonably well. But beyond academics, the predictive power weakens. Practical intelligence, creativity, motivation, emotional understanding—these vary independently of IQ. A person with a 140 IQ is not necessarily going to be a better manager, entrepreneur, or artist than a person with a 110 IQ. The test measures a narrow slice of cognition.

### Worked example — Understanding the bell curve and standard deviations

IQ scores follow a bell curve—a normal distribution centered on 100, with a standard deviation of 15 points. This means:

- 68 percent of the population scores between 85 and 115 (one standard deviation from the mean).
- 95 percent scores between 70 and 130 (two standard deviations).
- About 2.2 percent scores below 70, and about 2.2 percent scores above 130.

An IQ score of 70 or below, combined with significant limitations in adaptive functioning, is used to diagnose intellectual disability. An IQ score of 130 or higher, combined with other evidence of exceptional ability, is used to identify gifted children.

The bell curve looks precise, but it conceals important uncertainties. Any single IQ score has measurement error. A test result of 105 might represent true ability anywhere from 100 to 110. Second, the curve itself changes over time (the Flynn effect). The distribution that was normed in 1995 is different from the distribution in 2010. When tests are renormed, average IQ can shift.

### Common misconceptions

**IQ is not fixed at birth.** Twin studies show that IQ is heritable—genetic factors influence it. But heritability does not mean unchangeable. Environmental factors, especially in childhood, have substantial effects on IQ.

**IQ tests do not measure all forms of intelligence.** They measure certain types of reasoning and knowledge that are relevant to academic performance. They do not measure practical intelligence, creative intelligence, emotional intelligence, or many other forms of cognitive ability.

**A single IQ number does not capture the full picture.** Modern IQ tests provide sub-scores (verbal comprehension, perceptual reasoning, working memory, processing speed). These sub-scores often matter more than the overall IQ number. A person might have average overall IQ but be exceptionally high in one domain and low in another.

**IQ scores are not culturally neutral.** The tests are normed on specific populations and reflect cultural values about what counts as "intelligence." This does not mean the tests are useless, but it does mean they should be interpreted with caution when comparing across cultures or socioeconomic groups.

## Concept 5 — Where intelligence comes from: Genes and environment, forever entangled

The question of whether intelligence comes from nature or nurture has been debated for centuries. Is a person born with a fixed capacity? Or is intelligence shaped by upbringing and experience? The honest answer, supported by modern research, is both—and the interaction is complex.

Twin studies provide evidence for the genetic component. In the *Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart*, researchers followed identical twins who were separated at birth and raised by different families. The twins showed similar IQ scores despite having entirely different environments. If environment were all that mattered, we would expect separated identical twins to have very different scores. They do not. Identical twins correlate more highly with each other than fraternal twins do, even when raised apart. There is a genetic component to intelligence.

But genetics does not determine destiny. A child born with genetic potential for high intelligence will not develop that intelligence in an environment with severe deprivation, poor nutrition, no access to education, or chronic stress. Research on poverty and its effects on brain development shows that children growing up in poverty have measurably different brain structure in the prefrontal cortex—the region involved in reasoning and executive function. This is not because poor people have different genes. It is because the chronic stress of poverty literally changes how the brain develops. Mark Kishiyama studied children living in poverty and found that their brains showed reduced activation in the same regions that show reduced activation in people with damage to the lateral prefrontal cortex.

*Range of reaction* is the concept that captures this interaction: each person has a genetic potential (a range of possible intelligence levels they could reach, given different environments), but whether they reach the upper end or the lower end of that range depends on their environment. A child with high genetic potential who grows up in a stimulating environment—with books, conversations, education, intellectual challenges—will likely reach toward the upper end of their range. A child with high genetic potential who grows up in an unstimulating environment might reach the lower end of their range. The tragedy is not merely that poor children have fewer resources; it is that the chronic stress of poverty actually changes how their brains develop.

Oprah Winfrey provides a counterexample to determinism in either direction. She was born into poverty and abuse. By every environmental measure, her circumstances suggested a trajectory of limited opportunity. But she developed strong practical intelligence, emotional intelligence, and interpersonal skill. She had the genetic potential, and she found the environmental supports—adults who saw her talent, education when it was available, people who believed in her. She also had resilience, motivation, and drive. The interaction of genetics, environment, and motivation combined to produce an extraordinary life.

The research on socioeconomic status and intelligence is also informative. Differences in IQ scores between economically advantaged and disadvantaged groups are substantial—about 10-15 IQ points on average. Are these differences genetic? The evidence says no. Adoption studies show that when children from disadvantaged backgrounds are adopted into wealthy families, their IQ scores increase. The effect is not large enough to close the entire gap, but it is large enough to show that environment matters substantially. When educational interventions improve school quality or provide enrichment programs, IQ scores improve. The differences we observe are not fixed at birth—they respond to environmental change.

Why, then, do ethnic groups differ in average IQ? The evidence points to factors like educational opportunity, socioeconomic status, stereotype threat, and test cultural bias—not genetic differences. When you account for socioeconomic status, the ethnic differences shrink dramatically. Stereotype threat—the performance reduction that occurs when people are aware of negative stereotypes about their group—is well-documented and substantial. When test-takers are told that a test measures ability (activating stereotype threat), Black students perform worse; when they are told it is just a problem-solving task (not measuring ability), the performance gap disappears. The difference is purely psychological, not biological.

The temptation to attribute group differences to genetics has a dark history. IQ tests have been used to justify eugenics programs, forced sterilization, and discrimination. The genetic claims have repeatedly been used to suggest that inequality is natural and immutable. In reality, genetics tells a more complex story: people differ genetically along many dimensions, but those differences do not fall neatly along racial or ethnic lines, and genetic differences do not explain the IQ gaps we observe between groups.

### Worked example — Understanding heritability

Heritability is a measure of how much of the variation in a trait, across a population, is due to genetic variation. If a trait has high heritability, it means that genetic differences explain most of the variation. If it has low heritability, environmental differences explain more of the variation.

But heritability is often misunderstood. High heritability does NOT mean that a trait is unchangeable. Height has high heritability—genetic factors explain about 80 percent of the variation in height across a population. But average height has increased substantially over the past 150 years as nutrition improved. The variation in height is genetically driven. The overall level of height can be environmentally changed.

The same principle applies to intelligence. Intelligence has moderate heritability—genetic factors explain roughly 50-60 percent of the variation across individuals. But this does NOT mean that IQ scores are unchangeable, or that environmental interventions do not work, or that group differences are genetic. It means that within a population, genetic differences contribute to differences in IQ. Across time or across groups that have experienced major environmental changes, environmental factors dominate.

### Common misconceptions

**Heritability is not the same as genetic determinism.** Just because intelligence is heritable does not mean it is fixed at birth or unchangeable.

**"The environment" is not a single thing.** When we say environment matters, we mean dozens of things: prenatal nutrition, family stress, access to education, quality of schooling, exposure to challenging ideas, books in the home, peer influence, motivation. Not all of these can be easily changed, but they are variable across individuals and across societies.

**Group differences are not genetic.** When average IQ differs between groups, the evidence points to environmental factors—opportunity, bias, stereotype threat, cultural fit. Genetic explanations do not fit the data.

## Concept 6 — Learning disabilities: When cognition works differently, not deficiently

A student sits in class and reads the sentence on the board aloud: "The big dog ran home." But what she reads is, "The dig bod ran home." The letters are being processed in the wrong order. The student is not struggling because she is not intelligent. Her IQ is average. She has dyslexia, a neurological difference in how letters are processed.

A learning disability is a neurological condition that affects a specific area of cognitive function, while leaving overall intelligence intact. This is crucial: a student with dyslexia has difficulty processing letters and sounds, but this does not mean the student is intellectually disabled. A student might read two years below grade level but be excellent at math and reasoning. A student might struggle with written expression but be a brilliant verbal thinker.

Dyslexia is the most common learning disability. The neurological mechanism for sound processing does not work properly. The brain cannot connect letters to sounds the way it normally does. As a result, reading is slow and effortful. Some dyslexic individuals cope by memorizing the shapes of words, which works until they encounter a new word. Others develop strong comprehension despite their struggle with decoding—they understand what is read to them, but cannot read it themselves.

Dysgraphia is difficulty with written expression. The student understands concepts and can speak fluently, but putting thoughts on paper is extraordinarily difficult. The physical act of writing is challenging—the coordination between hand and letter formation does not work smoothly. The organizational challenge of translating thought into written language is also difficult. A student with dysgraphia might speak brilliantly in class but struggle terribly with written tests.

Dyscalculia is difficulty with mathematics. The student might have trouble recognizing how many objects are in a small group without counting them, or difficulty memorizing math facts, or trouble understanding what numerals mean. Unlike intellectual disability, which is a global cognitive limitation, dyscalculia is specific to numerical reasoning. A student might excel in reading and verbal reasoning while struggling with every mathematical concept.

The key distinction: learning disabilities are specific, neurological differences in how certain information is processed. Intellectual disabilities are global limitations in overall cognitive ability. A person with a learning disability is not intellectually disabled—they have normal intelligence in some domains and specific difficulty in others.

Learning disabilities are often not identified until a child reaches school age, because the disability only becomes apparent when the student is expected to read, write, or do math. A bright child with dyslexia might do fine in preschool, where reading is not required. In first grade, the struggle becomes obvious. Teachers and parents often misinterpret the struggle as laziness or low motivation, rather than recognizing it as a specific neurological difference.

Treatment varies. For dyslexia, intensive phonics instruction—direct, explicit teaching of the connection between letters and sounds—helps some students. For dysgraphia, accommodations like allowing typed responses instead of written ones, or allowing more time, can let the student demonstrate their knowledge. For dyscalculia, manipulatives and concrete representations of numerical concepts help more than abstract instruction.

The crucial point: identifying a learning disability is not labeling a student as deficient. It is identifying a specific area where the student's brain works differently, so that instruction can be adapted to how that brain actually works. A student with dyslexia is not less intelligent than a student without dyslexia. The dyslexic student's brain processes letters differently. Instruction that ignores this difference and simply expects the student to work harder will not help. Instruction that teaches through the student's strengths and provides explicit support in the area of difficulty will help.

### Worked example — Identifying a learning disability vs. low intelligence

A teacher suspects that a student is struggling with math because of intellectual disability or learning disability. How can she tell the difference?

*For intellectual disability*, the student would struggle across all academic areas: reading, writing, math, reasoning, vocabulary. The struggle would be global, affecting overall cognitive function.

*For dyscalculia*, the student might read fluently and write well, understand scientific concepts, but struggle specifically with numbers. The difficulty is isolated to the mathematical domain.

A psychologist can administer cognitive testing—an IQ test that includes subtests of different abilities. If all subtests are low, global intellectual disability is likely. If the IQ score is average overall but there is a specific low score on the arithmetic or quantitative reasoning subtest (while other subtests are normal or high), a specific learning disability is likely.

The distinction matters because the intervention is completely different. A student with global intellectual disability needs curricula designed for a different developmental level. A student with dyscalculia needs the standard curriculum taught in a way that works with their brain—manipulatives, visual representations, concrete examples—rather than the standard abstract approach.

### Common misconceptions

**Learning disabilities are not the same as intellectual disabilities.** A person with dyslexia is not mentally retarded. A person with dyscalculia can be brilliant at abstract reasoning.

**Learning disabilities are neurological, not psychological.** They are not caused by poor motivation or emotional problems. They reflect differences in how the nervous system processes certain information.

**Accommodations do not lower standards.** Allowing a student with dyslexia to use audiobooks is not "cheating" or "lowering expectations." It is removing the barrier that prevents the student from accessing the content. The student is still expected to master the same material.

## Chapter summary

Thinking is not a single process. It is a collection of processes—fast intuitive ones and slow deliberate ones—and you use them all, often simultaneously.

Concepts, prototypes, and schemas organize your thinking automatically, letting you navigate the world without conscious deliberation about everything. They are the machinery of intuition, and they usually serve you well. They also create systematic biases—you judge things by their similarity to a prototype even when base rates would give you a better answer, and you seek information that confirms what you already believe.

Problem-solving comes in two forms: algorithms (step-by-step procedures that guarantee a correct answer) and heuristics (mental shortcuts that usually work quickly but sometimes fail). Heuristics are how you navigate real-world complexity, but they fail in predictable ways. Anchoring bias, availability bias, representativeness bias, confirmation bias, hindsight bias—these are not character flaws. They are the systematic consequences of how human reasoning actually works.

Intelligence is not a single thing. Spearman's *g*, Cattell's fluid and crystallized intelligence, Sternberg's triarchic intelligence, Gardner's multiple intelligences—these theories emphasize different aspects of something complex. The best evidence suggests that there is a general capacity (*g*) that influences performance across a range of cognitive tasks, but that this general capacity is only one part of what makes a mind work. Creativity, practical wisdom, emotional understanding, the ability to work with others—these are not captured by IQ tests, and they often matter more in real life.

IQ tests measure something real: they predict academic performance and show some correlation with life outcomes. But they are not measuring "intelligence" in any deep sense. They are measuring performance on a particular test, at a particular time, in a particular population. They are culturally specific. They are influenced by education, opportunity, stereotype threat, and test-taking familiarity. The Flynn effect shows that average IQ scores rise when societies invest in education and improve nutrition. Differences in IQ between ethnic groups narrow substantially when you account for socioeconomic factors. The evidence does not support genetic explanations for group differences.

Intelligence, like height, is influenced by both genes and environment. Genes set a range of possibilities. Environment determines where within that range a person develops. A child born with genetic potential for high intelligence will not develop that intelligence in severe deprivation. A child born with modest genetic potential can reach high levels of intelligence in a stimulating environment. The interaction is complex and real.

Learning disabilities are specific neurological differences in how certain information is processed—difficulty with letters, or writing, or numbers—while overall intelligence remains intact. They are not intellectual disability. They are not low motivation. They are neurological. Identifying them accurately is the first step toward helping a student learn effectively.

Understanding this does not make you free from the biases that thinking creates. But it makes you aware of them. It gives you the option to slow down and think deliberately, to check your intuitions against the data, to notice when you are being anchored or succumbing to availability bias. It lets you judge an IQ score for what it actually is—a measure of performance on a particular test—rather than as a judgment of your intelligence or your worth. And it gives you compassion for minds that work differently, recognizing that dyslexia and dyscalculia and other learning differences are neurological, not failures of effort or ability.

## What would change my mind

I find the current evidence for a genetic component to intelligence to be substantial. My reading would shift if adoption studies began to show no environmental effect, or if identical twins reared apart showed no correlation. I remain uncertain about the right way to understand learning disabilities like dyslexia and dyscalculia. They are neurological—that part is solid—and they are not global intellectual limitations.
