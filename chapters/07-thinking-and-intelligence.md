# Chapter 7 — Thinking and Intelligence
*Why your brain gives you the wrong answer and feels completely sure about it.*

---

Daniel Kahneman put a simple question to his subjects. They had been given a profile: Linda is thirty-one, single, outspoken, bright, a philosophy major who participated in antinuclear demonstrations and cared deeply about social justice. Then the question: which is more probable — (A) Linda is a bank teller, or (B) Linda is a bank teller *and* a feminist?

The arithmetic is not ambiguous. A conjunction cannot be more probable than either of its parts. If Linda is something *and* something else, she cannot be more likely than if she is just the first thing. This is not a subtle statistical point. It is as certain as saying that the number of people who own both a car and a bicycle cannot exceed the number of people who own a car. The math is airtight.

Eighty-five percent of subjects chose (B).

This is called the conjunction fallacy, and what makes it interesting is not that people are stupid. What makes it interesting is that the error is universal, systematic, and feels completely right while it's happening. The description of Linda fits a feminist. It does not fit a bank teller. So "bank teller and feminist" feels more probable than "bank teller" — because it's a better *story*, a more coherent *picture*, even though it's a less probable *event*. The mind is running a different calculation than the one it was asked to run, and it doesn't know the difference.

That's the subject of this chapter. Thinking is not a single process. It's two processes running simultaneously — a fast one that generates answers by matching patterns and constructing stories, and a slow one that can check those answers if you stop and make it. Most of the time you trust the fast one. Most of the time that's fine. But the fast one fails in specific, predictable ways. Understanding those failures is the beginning of understanding your own mind.

## The filing system

Before getting to the failures, it's worth understanding the machinery.

Your brain doesn't encounter the world raw. Between the sensory signal and the conscious thought is an enormous amount of prior organization — categories, templates, stored patterns that tell you what you're looking at before you've finished looking. These are *concepts*: mental groupings of things that share features. *Dog* contains Great Danes and Chihuahuas. *Furniture* contains chairs and tables and things that might be either. You have thousands of these categories, constantly active.

Within each category, your brain stores a *prototype* — the best example. The prototype for "bird" in most Western minds is something like a robin: small, brown, sings, flies. A penguin is a bird, legally — it meets the definition. But it's a bad match for the prototype, which is why "penguin" takes a fraction longer to classify as a bird than "robin" does. The brain checks incoming things against the prototype, and the mismatch triggers a momentary friction.

Prototypes do fast work. They let you classify an object in milliseconds without running through a checklist. They're also how you form first impressions, recognize faces, and decide whether someone "seems like a doctor." Fast and mostly correct. Fast and sometimes catastrophically wrong — because the same prototype that lets you recognize a dog instantly is the one that causes you to hire people who look like previous successful hires, and disqualify people who don't match the template.

Related concepts cluster into *schemas* — organized networks of knowledge about how things work. Your restaurant schema tells you: enter, wait to be seated or go to the counter, receive a menu, order, receive food, eat, pay, leave. You don't think through these steps. The schema runs automatically. When you enter an unfamiliar restaurant in another country and the script is different — you pay first, or there's no menu, or you're supposed to seat yourself — you notice the violation immediately, because the schema had set up an expectation that wasn't met.

Event schemas are cognitive scripts for routines. Your elevator script: wait for doors, enter, turn around to face the doors, find the floor button. If you walked into an elevator and stood facing the back wall, you would feel something was wrong, and so would everyone else in the car. Not because there's a rule against it. Because the script says otherwise, and scripts are powerful enough to override voluntary behavior — which is why people continue to reach for their phones while driving even after consciously deciding not to.

The trade-off: schemas and prototypes make fast, efficient cognition possible. They are the infrastructure of intuition. They are also the infrastructure of stereotyping, of confirmation bias, of every cognitive shortcut that usually works and sometimes spectacularly doesn't.

## Two ways to solve a problem

When you face a problem, you have two broad strategies.

An *algorithm* is a step-by-step procedure that, if followed correctly, guarantees a correct answer. Long division is an algorithm. A recipe is an algorithm. The strength of an algorithm is certainty: follow the steps, get the result. The weakness is speed: algorithms are slow, and not every problem has one.

A *heuristic* is a mental shortcut — a rule of thumb that usually produces a good enough answer quickly. You don't have an algorithm for deciding whether to trust a stranger. You don't have an algorithm for estimating whether you'll enjoy a film. You use heuristics: Does this person make eye contact? Does the film have actors I like? These shortcuts work most of the time. They fail in predictable ways.

The predictable failures have names.

**Anchoring bias**: the first number you hear distorts all your subsequent estimates. In classic experiments, subjects were asked to estimate various quantities — the percentage of African nations in the UN, the age of a famous building — after first hearing a random number generated by a spinning wheel. The random number was irrelevant. Subjects' estimates were dragged toward it anyway. In apartment hunting, the first apartment you see sets the anchor; every subsequent apartment is judged relative to that anchor, not relative to the market. Realtors use this deliberately, showing overpriced properties first.

**Availability heuristic**: you estimate the probability of something by how easily examples come to mind. After a plane crash makes the news, people become afraid of flying and more comfortable driving — even though driving is statistically more dangerous per mile. The crash is vivid, recent, available in memory. The daily toll of traffic deaths is abstract. Availability substitutes for actual probability, and vivid events distort the calculation. This heuristic isn't irrational — in environments without statistics, vivid events really were more reliable guides to probability than base rates. But in a world saturated with selected news coverage, availability is a systematically biased sample.

**Representativeness bias**: you judge probability by how similar something is to a prototype. Linda sounds like a feminist. A bank teller sounds like someone conventional. "Bank teller and feminist" matches the prototype better than "bank teller" alone, so it feels more probable. The conjunction fallacy is representativeness overriding arithmetic. Related: you hear that a quiet, careful person with glasses likes crossword puzzles and you guess "librarian" rather than "farmer" — even though there are many more farmers than librarians. The person matches the librarian prototype better. But base rates should dominate, and you're not thinking about base rates.

**Confirmation bias**: once you believe something, you seek evidence that confirms it and notice evidence that contradicts it less. A manager who thinks an employee is unreliable will notice every late email and forget every prompt response. The belief filters perception, and filtered perception reinforces the belief. This is not a mild distortion. It is the primary mechanism by which wrong beliefs become entrenched.

**Hindsight bias**: after an outcome, you believe you would have predicted it. After the stock market crashes, you "knew it was coming." After the election upset, it "seemed obvious in retrospect." Hindsight replaces the genuine uncertainty you experienced before the outcome with the false certainty you feel after it. This matters because it makes your past judgments seem worse than they were, while making you feel overconfident about your current judgments.

These biases share a property: they operate below the speed of deliberate thought. The conjunction error happens before you think about set theory. The anchor distorts your estimate before you notice you're being influenced. Knowing about a bias helps — people who've studied anchoring are less anchored — but it doesn't eliminate it. The fast process runs anyway. What you can do is notice when you're in a situation where it's likely to mislead you, and deliberately invoke the slow process.

## What intelligence is

In 1904, Charles Spearman noticed something. People who scored well on one mental test tended to score well on others. People who scored poorly on vocabulary tended to score poorly on spatial reasoning. There was a positive correlation across cognitive tasks — if you were good at one mental thing, you tended to be good at others. Spearman called the underlying factor *g*, for general intelligence.

The implication was significant: if there's a single general factor underlying all cognitive performance, then a single number could capture something meaningful about a person's mind. That's the theoretical foundation of the IQ test.

But Spearman's insight was immediately complicated by observation. People vary in different cognitive ways. A person who reasons brilliantly about abstract structures might write poorly. A gifted writer might struggle with mathematics. If *g* is all that's there, how do these individual profiles exist?

Raymond Cattell's answer, developed in the 1940s, was to split *g* into two kinds. *Fluid intelligence* is the capacity to reason with novel information — to see patterns, solve new problems, adapt to unexpected situations. It's what you use when you take an unfamiliar route because yours is blocked. *Crystallized intelligence* is accumulated knowledge and the ability to retrieve and deploy it — vocabulary, general knowledge, practiced skills. It's what you use on a trivia question.

The distinction matters because they have different trajectories. Fluid intelligence peaks in the twenties and declines through life — the brain processes new information more slowly with age. Crystallized intelligence tends to increase through middle age and hold relatively steady, because you keep accumulating knowledge. A seventy-year-old is worse than a twenty-year-old at novel pattern recognition. The seventy-year-old knows far more.

Robert Sternberg's triarchic theory added a dimension that standard IQ testing conspicuously ignores. He proposed three forms of intelligence: *analytical* (what IQ tests measure — comparing, evaluating, analyzing information), *practical* (what people call street smarts — finding solutions that work in actual life, navigating real organizations, understanding unwritten rules), and *creative* (generating novel ideas, making unexpected connections, inventing solutions). A person can be extremely high in analytical intelligence and be helpless at practical problems. A person with moderate analytical intelligence but high practical intelligence often outperforms the high-*g* person in real settings.

Howard Gardner pushed further, proposing at least eight distinct intelligences: linguistic, logical-mathematical, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, spatial, interpersonal, intrapersonal, and naturalistic. The educational appeal is obvious — it offers a language for recognizing abilities that standard testing ignores. The empirical problem is also real: Gardner's multiple intelligences don't factor out as cleanly independent capacities in psychometric data. They correlate with each other, which is exactly what you'd expect if Spearman's *g* is real.

The most comprehensive synthesis is the Cattell-Horn-Carroll model, which arranges cognitive abilities hierarchically: general *g* at the top, broad factors (fluid reasoning, short-term memory, processing speed, and others) at the second level, and narrow specific abilities at the third. This preserves Spearman's insight that something general underlies performance, while acknowledging that intelligence is multifaceted — different people have different profiles across the broad factors, and those profiles matter.

What is intelligence? The honest answer: it is whatever produces the correlations Spearman noticed. There is something that causes performance on one cognitive task to predict performance on others. That something is measurable, heritable, and predictive of academic and professional success. It is not the whole story of a mind. It does not capture creativity, practical wisdom, interpersonal skill, or motivation. But it's real, and ignoring it would be dishonest.

## What IQ tests actually measure

Alfred Binet, in Paris in the early 1900s, was asked to develop a test that could identify children likely to struggle in school. He built one: a series of tasks measuring reasoning, vocabulary, and problem-solving. He calculated a child's *mental age* — the chronological age at which average performance matched the child's score. A nine-year-old who scored like a twelve-year-old had a mental age of twelve.

Lewis Terman at Stanford standardized the test on a large American population and introduced the intelligence quotient: mental age divided by chronological age, multiplied by 100. Average becomes 100 by definition. David Wechsler, in the 1930s, built the tests that dominate clinical and educational practice today — the WAIS for adults, the WISC for children — combining subtests of verbal comprehension, perceptual reasoning, working memory, and processing speed into a single score, with sub-scores for each component.

IQ tests are reliable: you get roughly the same score on two administrations. They are predictive: higher IQ correlates with academic performance, and to a lesser degree with income and longevity. They identify children who need more support and children who need more challenge. In clinical settings, they're essential for diagnosing intellectual disability and learning disabilities. These are real virtues.

But here is what the tests do not do.

James Flynn, a New Zealand philosopher, noticed in the 1980s that average IQ scores had been rising about three points per decade across the twentieth century in developed countries. If IQ measures something fixed and largely genetic, this should be impossible. Genetic change doesn't happen that fast. What changed were environments: better nutrition, more years of formal schooling, more exposure to abstract reasoning in everyday life, more familiarity with the format of tests. The scores rose because the environment changed, not because humans became genetically more intelligent. This is the *Flynn effect*, and it reveals that IQ tests are measuring performance in a particular cultural context, not some abstract cognitive capacity floating free of history and circumstance.

Average IQ scores also differ across ethnic and socioeconomic groups. In the United States, white Americans score higher on average than Black Americans; Asian Americans score higher on average than white Americans. Arthur Jensen argued in 1969 that these differences had a genetic basis. The evidence doesn't support this. When you control for socioeconomic factors, the gaps shrink dramatically. Adoption studies show that children from disadvantaged backgrounds raised in wealthy families show substantial IQ gains. Stereotype threat — the performance impairment that occurs when you're aware of a negative stereotype about your group — accounts for a meaningful portion of the gap; when testing conditions eliminate stereotype threat, the gap narrows. The tests are normed on specific populations and reflect the kind of reasoning those populations practice. They are not culturally neutral instruments.

What IQ tests predict best is performance in school-like settings. They were designed to do exactly that, and they do it reasonably well. The question is whether school-like performance is what we mean when we say someone is intelligent. If it is, the tests work. If we mean something broader — practical wisdom, creativity, emotional understanding, the ability to read a room and adapt — the tests are partial at best.

## Where intelligence comes from

Twin studies established that intelligence is heritable. Identical twins raised apart — separated at birth, different families, different environments — have IQ scores that correlate substantially. Fraternal twins correlate less. If environment were all that mattered, separated identical twins would be as different as any two strangers. They're not. Genes contribute.

But here is what heritability does not mean. Height is about 80% heritable — genetic factors explain most of the variation in height across a population. Average human height has increased by about four inches over the past century. The genes didn't change. Nutrition did. High heritability does not mean high fixedness. The genetic contribution sets a range of possible outcomes. The environment determines where within that range a person actually develops.

Mark Kishiyama's research on children in poverty showed that chronic economic stress produces measurable differences in prefrontal cortex function — the region involved in reasoning, planning, and executive control. Not because poor children have different genes. Because the chronic stress of poverty, the cortisol and the disrupted sleep and the cognitive load of scarcity, literally changes how the developing brain is shaped. A child with genetic potential for high intelligence, raised under those conditions, may never develop that potential. The range of reaction is real: the same genotype, expressed in different environments, produces different phenotypes.

Adoption studies confirm the environment's role. Children adopted from disadvantaged backgrounds into economically and educationally enriched families show substantial IQ gains. The gain isn't large enough to close every gap, but it's consistent and meaningful. Intelligence is not destiny. It is a capacity that can be developed or suppressed by circumstance.

The reason group differences in average IQ almost certainly don't reflect genetic differences is this: the environmental differences between groups in the United States are enormous and persistent. Differences in school quality, in access to medical care, in chronic stress, in test familiarity, in stereotype threat — all of these produce measurable cognitive effects. When these factors are controlled for, the group gaps shrink. Genetics predicts individual variation within a relatively uniform environment. It does not predict group differences between populations with vastly different histories and circumstances.

## When cognition works differently

There's a category of cognitive difference that's worth distinguishing carefully from low intelligence: *learning disabilities*.

A student with dyslexia has difficulty reading. Letters are processed in the wrong order, or the connection between letters and sounds doesn't form reliably. Reading is effortful and slow. But this student's IQ, tested with non-reading tasks, may be average or above average. The difficulty is specific to one kind of processing — the translation of written symbols into sounds — not a global limitation.

Dysgraphia is specific difficulty with written expression. Dyscalculia is specific difficulty with numerical reasoning. These are neurological differences in how particular kinds of information are processed. They are not intellectual disabilities, which are global limitations across cognitive domains. A child with dyscalculia might be a fluent, sophisticated reader and a poor mathematician. A child with intellectual disability would struggle across both.

The distinction matters practically. A student with dyslexia doesn't need a simpler curriculum. They need a different route to the same content — audiobooks instead of assigned reading, more time on written tests, explicit phonics instruction that makes the letter-to-sound connection a deliberate rule rather than an automatic one. A student with global intellectual disability needs the curriculum itself adapted to a different developmental level. Getting the diagnosis wrong — treating a learning disability as low intelligence, or missing a learning disability and attributing the difficulty to low motivation — means the intervention is wrong.

The deeper point: intelligence is not a single dimension along which minds can be ranked. It is a collection of capacities that are somewhat correlated with each other, somewhat independent, and variably affected by both genetic and environmental factors. A person who can't read well is not necessarily less intelligent than a person who can. They have a different profile of strengths and weaknesses. What looks like low intelligence in a standard school setting may be a mismatch between the setting's demands and the person's actual cognitive profile.

## Back to Linda

Return to the conjunction fallacy. Eighty-five percent of people say Linda is more likely to be a bank teller and a feminist than a bank teller alone. This is arithmetically impossible. It's also completely understandable once you see the mechanism.

The description of Linda activates your prototype for "feminist." It's a good match. The description doesn't activate your prototype for "bank teller." "Bank teller and feminist" is a better story than "bank teller alone" — it's coherent, it fits, it explains why someone like Linda would end up where she is. The fast process is not calculating probability. It's calculating *fit*. How well does this scenario match the description? And "feminist bank teller" matches better than "bank teller."

The slow process, the one that knows set theory, can correct this. But the slow process has to be invoked. It has to notice that a probability judgment is being made and that the fast process is answering a different question. For most subjects in Kahneman's experiment, the fast answer arrived so quickly and felt so right that the slow process never entered the room.

This is not a bug in human cognition. It is the design. The fast, pattern-matching, story-constructing process handles most of what you need to do: recognize people, navigate spaces, interpret conversation, make quick decisions when time is short. The slow process is expensive — it takes effort, time, and working memory. You can't run it constantly. The heuristics that produce the conjunction fallacy are the same heuristics that let you read a social situation in seconds, recognize danger before you've consciously analyzed it, or understand what someone means before they've finished their sentence.

Understanding this doesn't free you from it. But it gives you something: a list of situations where the fast process is known to mislead, and the option — when you're not too tired, not too rushed, not too confident — to stop and ask whether you're being anchored, or availability-biased, or whether you're matching a prototype instead of calculating a probability.

That's not a cure. It's what's available.

## What comes next

Chapter 8 covers memory — how information is encoded, stored, and retrieved, and where each stage can go wrong. The connection to this chapter is direct: memory is not a recording, it is a reconstruction, and reconstruction uses the same schemas and prototypes that govern thinking. What you remember is shaped by what you expected to happen, which means the errors of memory and the errors of thinking have the same architecture underneath them.

Chapter 12 covers social psychology — how the presence of others shapes behavior. The cognitive biases you've met here don't disappear in social contexts; they intensify. Confirmation bias operates on our beliefs about other people. Representativeness governs first impressions. Availability shapes how we estimate risk in groups. The thinking machinery runs the social machinery.
