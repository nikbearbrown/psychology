# Chapter 9 — Lifespan Development
*What childhood predicts, and what it doesn't.*

---

In 1972, researchers in Dunedin, New Zealand enrolled every child born in the city during a twelve-month window — 1,037 children — and began following them. Every few years they called the same people back to the lab, measured and tested and interviewed them, and watched as they grew from infants into children into teenagers into adults.

In 2011, when the subjects were in their early thirties, Terrie Moffitt and colleagues published a finding that stopped the field: children who showed low self-control between the ages of three and eleven — measured by teacher reports, parent reports, and observational tests — were, three decades later, measurably worse off. More health problems. Lower income. More criminal convictions. The effect held even after controlling for IQ and social class.

The headlines read: *Your childhood self-control predicts your adult success.* That is not quite what the science says. What the science says is more interesting and more careful: the capacities a child develops in the first decade of life, shaped by the people around them and their own emerging temperament, *correlate* with outcomes they will face decades later. Not cause, in the simple sense. Not destiny. A measurable probabilistic relationship, precise enough to see in a population of a thousand people followed over thirty years.

This is the tension at the heart of lifespan development: we change throughout our lives, yet the foundations matter. Biology gives us a starting point. Environment shapes how that starting point develops. The science can measure the shape of the relationship between them — not to say your future is written in childhood, but to understand the mechanisms well enough to intervene.

---

## What development means

The word comes from Latin *developare* — to unfold, to unwrap. An acorn unfolds into an oak tree following a genetic program shaped by sunlight and soil. A child unfolds according to a genetic program shaped by caregivers, culture, education, nutrition, and circumstance. The metaphor is useful and dangerous: it implies a predetermined unfolding. The actual process is messier. Development is not predetermined. It is constrained by biology and continuously shaped by experience.

Psychologists organize development into three domains, not because these are truly separate systems but because each requires different measurement and illuminates different mechanisms.

**Physical development** is growth in the body and brain — height, weight, motor skills, neural maturation. A six-month-old cannot sit up alone; a twelve-month-old usually can; an eighteen-month-old runs. Something changed in the substrate. We can measure it precisely.

**Cognitive development** is the change in how people think, learn, and reason. A three-year-old cannot understand that pouring water from a tall glass into a short glass leaves the same amount. A seven-year-old can. The difference isn't in motivation or instruction. It's in the logical operations the child's mind can perform.

**Social-emotional development** is how people form relationships, regulate emotion, understand themselves, and navigate social roles. An infant recognizes her mother's face. A toddler says "me do it." An adolescent struggles with identity. An older adult reflects on a life lived. These are not merely cultural milestones. They are systematically ordered, cross-culturally present, and meaningfully predictive.

Three foundational questions run through every developmental theory, and how a theory answers them shapes what it can explain.

*Continuous or discontinuous change?* Does development proceed smoothly, or in stages with qualitative shifts? Height grows continuously. Object permanence — the understanding that hidden things still exist — emerges discontinuously around seven to eight months. Most developmental processes mix both patterns. Language grows continuously (more words each month) and has discontinuous shifts (the vocabulary explosion, the acquisition of syntax). The tension is not resolved by choosing a side. It is managed by describing where each applies.

*Universal course or many courses?* Some developmental patterns appear everywhere — all children form attachments, all children acquire language. Others are culturally specific — when a child is expected to become independent, how emotions are expressed, what a successful relationship looks like. Both are true. The categories come from which aspect of development you're measuring.

*Nature or nurture?* This framing is wrong. A better question: *In what ways do genetic predispositions and environmental inputs interact to produce the person you become?* A child born with genes for easy sociability will develop differently in a warm, engaged family than in an isolated, neglectful one. Same genes, different outcomes. A child with a difficult temperament pulls different responses from her environment than an easy-going peer. The environment is a partner, and the child is an active participant, not a passive recipient.

## Attachment: the first relationship

In 1952, Harry Harlow separated infant monkeys from their biological mothers and gave them two artificial mothers: one bare wire with a feeding bottle, one soft cloth with no bottle. All the monkeys went to the cloth mother for comfort. None went to the wire mother, even though it was the only food source. When frightened, they clung to the cloth. When exploring, they returned to the cloth for reassurance. They were not driven by hunger alone. They needed something Harlow called *contact comfort* — the physical experience of closeness to a warm, soft caregiver.

This experiment, disturbing by today's ethical standards, overturned the dominant theory of the time: that attachment was simply a learned association between caregiver and food. It wasn't. Attachment was about security, proximity, the reassurance that someone responsive was there.

John Bowlby, building on Harlow's work, proposed in 1969 that attachment is a biological bond between infant and caregiver — evolved because it kept infants close to the people most likely to protect them. A baby who cried when separated from her mother was more likely to be found if lost. A baby who sought proximity was less likely to wander into danger. Attachment is not something parents do to infants. It is a relationship, co-constructed.

Mary Ainsworth developed a method to measure the quality of this relationship: the Strange Situation, a twenty-minute procedure in which an infant and caregiver enter a room, a stranger appears, the caregiver leaves and returns twice. What matters is not any single moment but the *pattern* across the procedure: Does the infant use the caregiver as a base for exploration? How distressed is the child when the caregiver leaves? How is the child comforted on return?

From thousands of observations, Ainsworth identified patterns.

**Secure attachment**: the child explores freely when the caregiver is present, becomes distressed when she leaves, seeks proximity on return, accepts comfort, and returns to play. The caregiver has been consistently sensitive to the child's cues — not intrusive, not neglectful, but attentively responsive.

**Anxious-resistant attachment**: the child is clingy even with the caregiver present, becomes very distressed at separation, and on return seeks contact but also resists — arching away, hitting. The caregiver has been *inconsistently* responsive. The child has learned to cry harder and stay closer, unsure whether calling will bring comfort.

**Avoidant attachment**: the child explores freely, shows little distress at separation, and ignores the caregiver on return. The caregiver has been emotionally unavailable. The child has learned: *I will take care of myself, since you aren't taking care of me.*

**Disorganized attachment** (identified later by Mary Main): the child shows contradictory behaviors — approaching with head turned away, freezing, appearing frightened. This pattern emerges when the caregiver is both a source of comfort and a source of fear. The attachment system is caught in an unsolvable conflict.

Do these patterns predict later outcomes? Yes, with caveats. Secure attachment in infancy correlates with better social skills, lower anxiety, and more stable relationships in adulthood. Disorganized attachment correlates with the highest rates of later difficulty. But these are probabilistic correlations, not destinies. A child with insecure attachment can develop secure relationships later through a responsive teacher, a stable family transition, or therapeutic intervention. Attachment patterns can change. What secure attachment in infancy provides is a foundation, an increased likelihood of a good trajectory — not a guarantee.

Cultural variation complicates the picture further. When the Strange Situation was administered in Japan, where mothers typically keep infants physically very close, more infants showed anxious-resistant patterns — not because Japanese mothers are unresponsive, but because the procedure is genuinely unusual: Japanese infants rarely experience separation. In Germany, where independence is culturally valued, more infants showed avoidant patterns — not because German mothers are rejecting, but because the cultural expectation is that children learn to manage separation calmly. The distribution of attachment styles reflects cultural practices around proximity and separation, not just caregiver quality.

## Cognitive development: the logic of childhood

In Jean Piaget's laboratory in Geneva, a five-year-old watches him pour equal amounts of water into a tall thin glass and a short wide glass. He asks: "Are these the same?" "Yes," the child says. He pours the water from the tall glass into another short glass. Now two short glasses sit before her, and she is certain: the one on the left has more, because it looks fuller.

She is not wrong about what she sees. She is wrong about what the seeing means. Piaget called this *non-conservation* — the inability to understand that volume remains constant despite change in appearance. And he took from thousands of such observations a sweeping claim: children don't simply know less than adults. They think *differently*. Their minds operate according to a different logic.

Piaget proposed four stages. The *sensorimotor stage* (birth to roughly two years) is the period when infants learn through senses and action — touching, looking, listening, manipulating. The critical achievement of this stage is **object permanence**: the understanding that objects continue to exist when out of sight. Before about seven months, an infant whose toy is hidden will not search for it. After it develops, the infant searches — she knows the toy is there, just occluded. This is not trivial. It is the foundation for understanding that other people have inner states you can't see, that the world continues to exist when you're not looking at it.

The *preoperational stage* (roughly two to seven years) introduces symbolic thinking — a stick becomes a sword, a doll becomes a patient, words represent absent things. But the child cannot yet perform mental operations — cannot reason about transformations without concrete objects in hand. Hence non-conservation. Hence *egocentrism*: the inability to take another person's perspective. A preoperational child assumes everyone sees, thinks, and feels what she sees, thinks, and feels. If she would like a toy for her birthday, her grandmother would like the same toy.

The *concrete operational stage* (roughly seven to eleven) brings the ability to reason logically about concrete things. The child understands conservation — pouring water doesn't change the amount. She understands *reversibility* — if you pour it back, you're back to the original amount. She can classify objects by multiple criteria simultaneously. But her logic still requires something tangible. Ask her to reason about hypotheticals — "Suppose all cats were blue; would my cat be blue?" — and she resists. That's not how cats work.

The *formal operational stage* (roughly twelve onward) introduces abstract reasoning. The adolescent can think about hypotheticals, reason about possibilities that don't yet exist, construct logical arguments from premises, think about her own thinking. This is when arguments become possible — and when teenagers become maddening to debate, because they now have the machinery to construct counterarguments.

#### The mechanisms beneath the stages

Piaget wasn't just cataloguing what children could and couldn't do. He was proposing mechanisms. When a child encounters new information, she *assimilates* it into an existing *schema* — a mental model of how the world works. A two-year-old's schema for "dog" is built from her family's golden retriever: four-legged, furry, friendly, barks. When she sees a poodle, she assimilates: "Dog!" When she sees a sheep, she might say "Dog!" too. But her mother corrects her, and she has to *accommodate* — revise her schema. Not all four-legged furry things are dogs. Sheep are different. Over thousands of assimilations and accommodations, her model of the world becomes more accurate and more fine-grained.

Development, for Piaget, is this incremental process: encounter something, assimilate what fits, accommodate when something doesn't, build better mental models.

#### What subsequent research revealed

Piaget's observations were brilliant. His stages proved less rigid than he described.

When researchers used *nonverbal* methods — watching where infants look rather than asking them to explain — they found evidence of object permanence in infants as young as three or four months, years before Piaget claimed it emerged. His stages described what children could *explain*, not always what they could do. There's a gap between implicit understanding and the ability to articulate.

The stages also overlap more than Piaget proposed. A child doesn't shift suddenly from preoperational to concrete operational at seven. She shows logical reasoning earlier on familiar, practiced problems and fails on unfamiliar, abstract ones. Development is messier and more continuous than stage labels suggest.

Most importantly, Lev Vygotsky — a Russian psychologist writing contemporaneously — proposed something Piaget underweighted: development is *social*. A child doesn't build knowledge by acting on objects in isolation. She learns through interaction with more skilled others — parents, teachers, older peers. Vygotsky called the space between what a child can do alone and what she can do with help the *zone of proximal development*. Learning happens in that zone, when an adult or peer *scaffolds* the task — provides just enough support that the child succeeds, then gradually withdraws as competence grows. This is why a six-year-old can solve a puzzle with guidance that she can't solve alone, and why the guided experience doesn't just solve the immediate problem — it builds a capacity.

Cross-cultural research has validated Vygotsky's emphasis. Piaget's stages describe development in schooled, Western populations more accurately than in non-schooled populations where children learn through direct participation in adult work. Children who learn to measure, count, and reason in the context of weaving or carpentry or farming develop these capacities differently than children who learn them in classrooms — not worse, but through different routes.

## Where the domains intersect

The Dunedin children in their early thirties had arrived at that point through thirty years of development in all three domains simultaneously.

Physical development had shaped their brains — chronic stress in childhood activates the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, elevates cortisol, and alters how the prefrontal cortex develops. Children whose early environment was chaotic or threatening arrived at adulthood with measurably different stress-regulation systems. That is a physical consequence of social experience.

Cognitive development had shaped their educational trajectories — children with better self-control learned more, built academic skills that accumulated into educational advantage, which opened doors to higher income and more stable careers. The effect is not because smarter children have better self-control. The effect runs through attention, persistence, and the ability to delay gratification long enough to complete work.

Social-emotional development had shaped their relationships — children with better self-control navigated peer relationships more successfully (waiting your turn, not lashing out), formed more stable friendships, and built the social capital that matters economically and psychologically into adulthood.

The Dunedin finding is not that childhood self-control is the single cause of adult success. It is that self-control at three years old is a marker for a cluster of developing capacities that compound across all three domains over thirty years. The correlation is real. The mechanism is multiple.

And crucially — the researchers did not conclude that the childhood self-control gap is fixed. They concluded the opposite: if we understand the mechanisms, we can intervene. Programs that build self-control in childhood — structured play, responsive caregiving, explicit emotional-regulation practice — show measurable effects on long-term outcomes. The science is not fatalistic. It is actionable.

## What research designs can and can't show

The Dunedin Study is longitudinal — the same people measured repeatedly over time. This is expensive and slow. But it's the only design that can show you how individual trajectories actually unfold. Cross-sectional studies, which compare different age groups at one point in time, are faster but confounded by cohort effects: the forty-year-olds being studied today grew up in a different world than the twenty-year-olds, and differences between them may reflect history as much as development.

Adoption studies — comparing adopted children to both biological relatives (genetic influence) and adoptive relatives (environmental influence) — provide natural experiments in separating nature and nurture. But adoptive families are not a random sample of all families. They are wealthier, more educated, more stable than average. The "environment" an adopted child receives is systematically different from the population average.

Each design answers some questions and obscures others. Good developmental science triangulates: it uses multiple designs and looks for findings that hold across all of them. Where they converge, confidence is higher. Where they diverge, something interesting is happening.

## What early experience does and doesn't determine

The Dunedin Study found that childhood self-control predicts adult outcomes. Some researchers, reading this, concluded that childhood is destiny. The researchers who conducted the study explicitly disagreed.

Prediction is not determination. A child with low self-control at three has a higher probability of certain adult outcomes. Many such children have good outcomes anyway. Many children with high self-control face unexpected setbacks. The prediction is aggregate, probabilistic, and visible only at the level of a population followed over decades — not at the level of any individual child.

Moreover, the mechanisms are not sealed after childhood. The brain retains plasticity. Relationships formed later in life can partially repair attachment patterns formed early. Educational opportunities can develop cognitive capacities that were constrained in childhood. Therapeutic work can build emotional regulation in adulthood. The arc bends toward whatever environment a person currently inhabits, shaped by the starting point childhood provided but not fully determined by it.

This is what lifespan development science actually claims: *foundations matter, but the arc is not fixed.* Understanding the mechanisms — how attachment works, how cognitive stages build on each other, how self-regulation develops — gives us leverage. Not to predict individuals, but to design environments, relationships, and interventions that improve the probabilities.

That is the work. The Dunedin Study did not tell us what children will become. It told us what we should be building.
