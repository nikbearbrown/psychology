# Chapter 16 — Therapy and Treatment
*Several things work. We don't fully know why. That's the honest answer.*

---

Vienna, 1900. Sigmund Freud's patient lies on the couch facing away from him — a deliberate choice. He wanted her to feel less observed. She talked through her symptoms — the paralysis, the choking sensation, the disturbances of vision — and reported that something shifted when she did. She called it the talking cure.

Freud had no control group. No measurement instrument. No follow-up protocol. He had observation and intuition and a theory about why it worked: unconscious conflicts, repressed from childhood, released through confession into language. His theory was elegant and largely wrong. His observation — that people sometimes improve when they talk about their suffering with someone who listens — was correct and has never been refuted.

One hundred and twenty years later, we have the opposite problem. We have hundreds of randomized trials, meta-analyses synthesizing thousands of studies, neuroimaging that shows us the depressed brain before and after treatment. We have several therapies that work about equally well, a class of medications that helps most people who take them, and an honest acknowledgment that we still don't fully understand the mechanism by which any of it works.

This chapter teaches three things. What the major treatment approaches are and what each proposes. What the evidence actually shows, including what it doesn't show. And the thing that unsettles the field most: the relationship between therapist and patient predicts treatment outcome as powerfully as the specific technique used — which raises the question of whether the techniques are doing what their theories claim.

---

## The four frameworks and what each bets on

Imagine you are a therapist in 1960 and someone walks into your office saying they cannot sleep, that everything feels grey, that they see no point. You have four frameworks to choose from. They disagree fundamentally about what is wrong.

**Psychodynamic therapy** — Freud's inheritance — says the problem is hidden. The person has impulses and memories so painful or unacceptable that they have been pushed into unconsciousness, where they continue to operate, shaping mood and behavior from below. The job is *uncovering*: through free association, through examining resistance, through tracking what the patient avoids talking about, the therapist helps the person gradually become aware of what they have buried. When unconscious material surfaces and is integrated, symptoms ease.

The trade-off is steep. Psychodynamic therapy can be genuinely deep — it can reach patterns that have organized an entire life — but it is slow and expensive. A typical course runs six months to several years. The mechanism is also the hardest to test. How do you measure what is, by definition, unconscious? When a symptom improves, how do you know whether the theory proposed actually caused the improvement, or whether something else did?

**Behavior therapy** bets on the opposite: the problem is visible, not hidden. It is a learned pattern, and learned patterns can be unlearned. A phobia of elevators is not a symbol for repressed childhood material about entrapment. It is a conditioned fear response — the brain learned to associate elevators with anxiety, and now elevators reliably trigger it. The mechanism is *relearning*.

Mary Cover Jones discovered this in 1924 with a boy named Peter who was terrified of rabbits. She slowly brought the rabbit closer while Peter ate snacks he liked. The brain cannot sustain simultaneous fear and comfort, so the fear response extinguished. Joseph Wolpe refined this into systematic desensitization: build a hierarchy of feared situations from least to most anxiety-provoking, teach relaxation, work up the hierarchy while relaxed. The fear response extinguishes at each step.

The advantage: fast, measurable, no theory about unconscious causes required. The limitation: behavior therapy addresses the conditioned response but not the meaning the person assigns to it. A person might overcome an elevator phobia and still hold the core belief that they are powerless in confined spaces. The behavior has changed; the belief system has not.

**Cognitive therapy**, developed by Aaron Beck in the 1960s, says the problem is *meaning*. Not repressed impulse, not conditioned response, but the interpretation a person places on events. Beck noticed that depressed people had a characteristic distortion: they overgeneralized from failure ("I failed this exam, therefore I am stupid and will always fail"), catastrophized about the future, and attributed bad outcomes to permanent personal deficiencies. The mechanism is *cognitive restructuring* — the therapist and patient work like detectives, examining the evidence for and against the person's negative beliefs.

If you failed one exam, does that mean you will fail out of college? Examine the evidence: you have passed other exams; you are capable of learning; this exam was unusually difficult. The more accurate thought produces a less depressed mood. The mood is not directly the target — the thought is the lever.

**Cognitive-behavioral therapy** (CBT) is the synthesis. It takes Beck's cognitive mechanism and adds behavioral exposure. A therapist treating social anxiety might teach relaxation (behavioral), then examine the catastrophic thoughts about social situations (cognitive), then have the patient practice social situations in real life while using the skills (behavioral again). The research evidence for CBT is the strongest of any named therapy for the broadest range of conditions: depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, eating disorders, OCD.

**Humanistic therapy**, associated most with Carl Rogers, proposes something philosophically different. Rogers believed distress arises from a gap between the person's *actual self* and their *ideal self* — who they are versus who they feel they ought to be. The mechanism is not technique at all. It is *acceptance*: the therapist practices unconditional positive regard, radical acceptance of the person exactly as they are, and this acceptance creates the space in which the gap can close. People, Rogers believed, naturally move toward growth and health when given genuine permission to be themselves.

Humanistic therapy is difficult to test — what does "authenticity" mean, and how do you measure it? It has faded from prominence in a research culture that prizes quantifiable outcomes. But it captured something that the research has since confirmed by a different route: the quality of the relationship between therapist and patient predicts outcome at least as well as the specific technique being used.

### What the evidence shows they have in common

In 1992, Bruce Wampold examined a strange pattern in the treatment literature. Study after study compared different therapies — CBT versus psychodynamic, behavioral versus humanistic — and found they produced similar outcomes. About 40 to 45% of people with depression achieved remission with CBT. About the same percentage achieved remission with psychodynamic therapy. About the same with behavioral activation. Different theories, different techniques, similar results.

Wampold called this the *Dodo bird verdict*, after the dodo in *Alice in Wonderland* who declares: "All have won, and all shall have prizes."

The verdict is unsettling because it implies that the theories underlying each therapy — Freud's unconscious, Skinner's conditioning, Beck's cognitive distortions, Rogers' authentic self — cannot all be correct in the ways they claim. Or perhaps they are each partially correct, each reaching a real aspect of how change happens. What Wampold found across all approaches was one consistent predictor of outcome: the therapeutic alliance — the quality of the working relationship between therapist and patient. Patients who felt understood, accepted, and working collaboratively improved more, regardless of which technique was used.

This is either a statement about the power of good relationship, or a statement about our inability to measure the thing that actually distinguishes the therapies, or both. The research does not settle it.

## Medication: what it does, what it explains, and what it doesn't

In 1954, a French naval surgeon synthesized a compound called chlorpromazine while looking for an antihistamine. What he found instead was that people with psychosis — experiencing hallucinations and delusions that had, until that moment, been largely untreatable — showed dramatic improvement. Their hallucinations quieted. The delusions released their hold. The medication did not cure psychosis, but it made it manageable.

This was a revolution. Not because it explained what psychosis was, but because it demonstrated that a chemical substance could reach into the brain and alter a symptom that had been considered beyond medicine's grasp.

The mechanism modern practitioners understand works like this. Neurons communicate by releasing neurotransmitters into the synapse between them. The receiving neuron has receptors for those neurotransmitters. When serotonin is released and binds to the next neuron's receptors, it has an effect. After the transmission, the releasing neuron normally reuptakes — reabsorbs — the serotonin to recycle it. A selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor, an SSRI like fluoxetine or sertraline, blocks this reuptake. The serotonin remains in the synapse longer, available to bind to more receptors, producing more of its effect.

The hypothesis: more serotonin availability reduces depression.

Here is what is honest about this: the hypothesis is partially wrong, or at least incomplete, and we know it. SSRIs block serotonin reuptake immediately. If elevated serotonin were the mechanism, improvement should follow within hours. Instead, SSRIs typically take two to four weeks to produce clinical improvement. Something else is happening downstream — changes to gene expression, synaptic plasticity, neurogenesis in the hippocampus — that takes time. The "chemical imbalance" theory of depression, the popular framing that depression is caused by low serotonin that SSRIs correct, has been substantially revised in the research literature. Serotonin is *involved* in depression. The causal story is more complex than a simple deficit.

This does not mean SSRIs don't work. They do. For about 60% to 70% of people with major depressive disorder, symptoms measurably improve. The practical reality is that we have a treatment that works, deployed through a mechanism we understand partially but not completely. This is not unusual in medicine — aspirin was used for decades before anyone understood how it inhibited prostaglandin synthesis. Treatments can precede mechanism.

The trade-off of medication is concrete. Medications work faster than most psychotherapies — often within weeks. They require less time investment from the patient. They can be combined with therapy for greater effect. But they ask the body for a payment. SSRIs can cause sexual dysfunction, weight gain, and emotional blunting — some patients describe feeling numb, flattened, less themselves. Antipsychotics, the descendants of chlorpromazine, can cause tardive dyskinesia, involuntary movements of the mouth and extremities that can be permanent with long-term use. Mood stabilizers like lithium require blood monitoring because the therapeutic and toxic doses are not far apart.

There is also the discontinuation question. Many people who stabilize on medication eventually want to stop taking it. Some can. Others relapse within weeks or months. Whether this means the underlying condition was never treated — only its symptoms suppressed — or whether the medication was doing necessary ongoing work, the research does not cleanly resolve. Both stories are sometimes true for different people.

For severe treatment-resistant cases, device-based approaches are available. Electroconvulsive therapy, despite its historical reputation, is administered under general anesthesia with carefully controlled parameters. It is remarkably effective for severe treatment-resistant depression — roughly 85% of patients improve — and the mechanism remains partially mysterious. Transcranial magnetic stimulation is less invasive and has fewer side effects but less robust evidence. Both are reserved for cases where other treatments have failed.

## Reading treatment evidence: the gap between efficacy and effectiveness

When a researcher says a therapy "works," they mean one of two things. The distinction matters.

*Efficacy* is what you measure in a randomized controlled trial under ideal conditions. You recruit participants with a diagnosed disorder. You assign them randomly to receive either the active treatment or a control condition. You measure outcomes with a standardized instrument before and after. If the treated group improves more than the control, the treatment has efficacy. The effect size (Cohen's d) tells you how much more. A d of 0.5 means the average treated person improved by half a standard deviation more than the average untreated person — for depression, this is the difference between remaining severely depressed and having mild symptoms.

*Effectiveness* is what you measure in the real world: real therapists in real clinics, treating people who may have multiple comorbid conditions, unstable housing, limited access to follow-up care. Efficacy in trials is typically larger than effectiveness in practice. A therapy might show 40% remission in a trial but 25% in a community clinic. The gap exists because trials screen participants carefully, train therapists to deliver the treatment with high fidelity, and measure outcomes systematically. In the clinic, therapists vary in skill, people drop out, comorbid problems complicate the picture, and measurement is irregular.

The Dodo bird verdict numbers illustrate this. In recent meta-analyses:

- CBT for depression: approximately 43% remission in trials.
- Psychodynamic therapy for depression: approximately 40%.
- Behavioral activation: similar.
- Combined therapy plus medication: better than either alone for moderate to severe depression.

The similar rates across therapies are trial numbers, not clinic numbers. In practice, the therapist's skill and the quality of the working alliance matter enormously. A moderately trained CBT therapist with a good alliance will likely produce better outcomes than an expert CBT therapist with a poor alliance.

What makes reading the evidence hard is not the finding itself. It is that people seeking treatment want to know what will happen to *them*, and trials only tell you what happens to populations on average. A 43% remission rate for CBT means that if you followed 100 people with depression through CBT, about 43 would achieve full remission. It says nothing about whether you will be in that 43 or the 57. Your outcome depends on the severity of your depression, your specific therapist's skill, your level of engagement, and factors the research hasn't yet identified. The trial gives you odds. It does not give you a guarantee.

## What the evidence doesn't explain

The honest corner of the field contains several important unknowns.

Why do SSRIs take weeks to work when they block reuptake immediately? The answer must involve something downstream from synaptic serotonin levels — changes to receptor density, neurogenesis in the hippocampus, or altered gene expression — but the exact mechanism is not established. We deploy a treatment whose mechanism is partially opaque, which is uncomfortable but not unusual in medicine.

Why does the therapeutic relationship predict outcome so reliably? One possibility is that a good relationship creates the conditions under which any technique can work — the patient engages more, practices more, trusts the process more. Another is that the relationship is itself the mechanism of change — that being understood and accepted by another person produces psychological change that technique cannot. Wampold argues for the second. The research cannot yet settle which is true.

How much of treatment efficacy is placebo? In antidepressant trials, placebo typically produces 30 to 40% of the improvement the medication produces — meaning a substantial fraction of medication's benefit may be expectancy, the belief that help is coming. In psychotherapy trials, there is no placebo control — you cannot deliver sham therapy without being deceptive — so we cannot measure how much of therapy's benefit comes from the specific techniques versus the expectancy and the attention of a caring person. This does not invalidate therapy. It means that hope and the experience of being taken seriously are powerful medicines, and good therapists harness them regardless of the theory they work within.

Who will respond to what? Clinicians cannot yet reliably predict which patient will benefit from CBT versus psychodynamic therapy versus medication versus their combination. The research on moderators and predictors of treatment response is growing, but it remains underdeveloped relative to its clinical importance. We allocate treatments largely by evidence-based guidelines (CBT for depression, exposure for phobias, medication for psychosis) and trial-and-error rather than by personalized prediction.

## How to choose

Given this evidence — several approaches work comparably, the relationship matters as much as the technique, we don't fully understand the mechanisms — how should someone seeking treatment decide?

Start with the evidence for your specific condition. Some conditions have a clear first-line recommendation: exposure-based therapy has overwhelming evidence for specific phobias; CBT has strong evidence for depression and anxiety disorders; medication is typically necessary for psychosis; lithium and mood stabilizers for bipolar disorder. These recommendations are based on effect sizes across hundreds of trials, not arbitrary preferences.

Then consider the practical constraints. CBT typically runs 12 to 20 sessions. Psychodynamic therapy typically runs six months to several years. Medication is ongoing. These are not equivalent commitments. Access, cost, insurance coverage, availability of competent practitioners in your area — these shape what "best treatment" means in practice. A therapy with a d of 0.6 that you cannot access is worse than a therapy with a d of 0.4 that you can.

Then consider the relationship. The research says this matters as much as anything. A therapist you trust, who makes you feel understood, who you are willing to be honest with — this predicts outcome independent of technique. If that relationship isn't present, find a different therapist.

What the evidence does not support is the false binary that has structured too much of the cultural conversation about mental health: *either* biological *or* psychological, *either* medication *or* therapy, *either* scientific *or* relational. A person who benefits from CBT is using their brain's plasticity to restructure thought patterns — that is simultaneously psychological and biological. A person who benefits from an SSRI and from a good therapeutic relationship is benefiting from altered neurotransmitter function and from being heard. The mechanisms are not competing. They coexist, and their combination is typically more effective than either alone.

## The arc and the honest close

Psychology began in 1879 with Wundt's introspection — the attempt to understand the mind from the inside. It moved through behaviorism's insistence that only observable behavior counts, through the cognitive revolution's recovery of internal processes, through neuroscience's mapping of the brain's architecture, through the careful description of disorders, and arrived here: at the question of what helps when the mind suffers.

The trajectory reveals something unexpected. Understanding how the mind works in detail is not sufficient for knowing how to help people when it goes wrong. You can understand the principles of classical conditioning and still not know why some people fail to benefit from exposure therapy. You can understand how SSRIs alter serotonin reuptake and still not know why the same medication helps one person profoundly and another barely at all. You can understand cognitive distortions and still encounter a person whose distorted thoughts accurately reflect a genuinely constrained situation, where restructuring the thought does not change the underlying reality.

This is not a failure of psychology. It is what psychology actually looks like in 2024. The treatments work — we can measure that. The mechanisms are partially understood — we can describe the pathways while acknowledging what the descriptions don't explain. The relationship matters more than we expected — we can document that without fully understanding why. The treatments work better in trials than in practice — we know the gap and are trying to close it.

What the field has learned, across all the measurement and theory and revision, is what Freud's patient intuited in 1900: something changes when you talk about suffering with someone who listens. We have spent a century and a quarter trying to understand precisely what changes, and precisely why, and we have partial answers. The partial answers are enough to help many people. The remaining questions are enough to keep the field working.
