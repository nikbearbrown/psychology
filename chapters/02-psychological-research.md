# Chapter 2 — Psychological Research
*The discipline of being wrong on purpose.*

---

In August 2015, a group of researchers published a result that should have been humiliating and instead turned out to be one of the most honest things the field had ever done.

They had taken 100 studies — published between 2008 and 2012, cited thousands of times, taught in classrooms, used to justify clinical and policy decisions — and tried to run them again. Same methods. Same measures. Same logic. Just: does it happen a second time?

Thirty-six replicated.

Thirty-six percent.

That number is not a scandal about bad people. It is a number that tells you something precise about what "proof" had meant in psychology for a century, and what it will have to mean from now on. The gap between those two meanings — between what looks like evidence and what actually is evidence — is what this chapter is about.

---

## The machine that makes knowledge

Here is the simplest version of how science works: you have an idea about why something happens, you figure out what you would have to observe if that idea were true, you go look, and then you update your idea based on what you saw. Then you do it again.

That's it. Four moves, in a cycle. Not a line with an ending — a cycle, because looking always generates new questions.

The philosopher's names for these moves are *theory*, *hypothesis*, *observation*, and *revision*. But those words can make the cycle sound more formal than it is. A theory is just a proposed explanation — a story about mechanism. A hypothesis is just a prediction: *if my story is right, then this specific thing should happen*. The observation is where you test whether it happened. And revision is the part where you either feel good about your story for now, or you figure out what needs to change.

The critical feature of this machine is not any of the individual steps. It's the direction of authority. Reality gets to say no.

You don't get to decide in advance that your explanation is correct. You don't get to notice only the observations that confirm it. You don't get to keep a theory just because you like it. The observation is sovereign. If your prediction fails, you have to revise. If it keeps failing in ways you can't account for, you have to abandon the theory and start over.

This sounds obvious. It's not.

### The falsifiability criterion

Consider Sigmund Freud's account of the unconscious. All human behavior, he argued, originates in unconscious wishes — the id — which get mediated by social reality through the ego and the superego. It's an elegant architecture, and in some form it's filtered into how most people in the Western world talk about motivation and self-deception. But here's a problem: what observation would prove it wrong?

If you ask someone why they did something and they say "because I wanted to," Freud's system says: that's your ego talking; the real motive is unconscious. If they say "I have no idea why I did it," Freud says: you see, the unconscious is at work. The theory can absorb any possible observation. A theory that can absorb any observation doesn't make predictions — it makes interpretations.

For a hypothesis to be scientific, there must be some possible observation that would prove it wrong. This is Karl Popper's criterion of *falsifiability*, and it is the single most important idea in the philosophy of science for practicing researchers.

Falsifiability does not mean a hypothesis is probably false. It means you're willing to be wrong. It means you have described, in advance, what evidence would constitute a refutation. Milgram's obedience hypothesis was falsifiable: if most participants had refused to shock others, or if the experimenter's presence turned out not to matter, the prediction would have been falsified. It could have said no.

Freudian theory, as constructed, cannot be falsified. That's not a comment on whether the unconscious exists; it's a comment on what kind of knowledge Freudian theory can produce.

### The two directions of reasoning

The cycle runs in two directions, and you need both.

*Deductive reasoning* moves from theory to observation. You have a general principle, and you deduce what must be true in a specific case. If sleep deprivation impairs memory consolidation (theory), and these students slept only four hours (specific case), then their recall scores tomorrow should be lower (prediction you can test).

*Inductive reasoning* moves from observation to theory. You gather specific cases and look for a pattern that suggests a general principle. You notice that people wash their hands more frequently after touching objects they find disgusting. You observe this enough times to propose a hypothesis: disgust triggers a drive toward physical cleansing. Then you design a deductive test.

Neither direction alone is sufficient. Deduction without induction is sterile — you're testing what you already assumed. Induction without deduction is anecdotal — you're seeing patterns that might be coincidence. The cycle requires both, turning continuously.

## The three things research can tell you

Not all research questions are the same kind of question, and the answer to each requires a different tool.

Some questions are *what* questions: what do chimpanzees actually do in the wild, what kinds of relationships do infants form with their caregivers, what does a panic attack feel like from the inside. These questions call for observation and description.

Some questions are *whether* questions: whether sleep and academic performance move together, whether depression rates are higher in urban or rural populations, whether people who exercise report more life satisfaction. These questions call for correlation.

Some questions are *why* questions: why does obedience increase when an authority figure is physically present, why does sleep deprivation impair recall, why do infants become distressed when a caregiver leaves. These questions call for experiment.

The distinction matters because the tools have different powers and different limits, and conflating them is where most reasoning errors about research begin.

### Description

When Jane Goodall arrived at Gombe Stream in Tanzania in 1960, she had no experimental hypothesis. She watched. She named the chimpanzees she studied — a methodological choice her critics called unscientific, because names imply individuality, and individuality complicates generalizations. She watched anyway, for decades, recording what they ate and how they traveled and how mothers taught their young and how they treated the dead.

That sustained, systematic attention revealed facts nobody had predicted: that chimpanzees make and use tools, that they have individual personalities, that culture — learned behavior transmitted across generations — exists in nonhuman primates.

<!-- → [IMAGE: Goodall at Gombe — chimp using a grass stem as a termite-fishing tool, with caption noting this was the first documented tool use in a non-human primate and the observation that prompted Louis Leakey to say "we must redefine tool, redefine man, or accept chimpanzees as humans"] -->

Descriptive research has enormous power. It is rich in context, faithful to real behavior in real conditions, and capable of revealing phenomena that no one thought to hypothesize. Its limit is equally clear: it cannot answer causal questions. Watching Goodall's mother chimpanzee teach her infant to fish for termites does not prove that the teaching caused the learning. Maybe the infant would have figured it out anyway. Maybe the mother's presence made the infant curious. The observation is real. The mechanism is invisible.

### Correlation

A study tracking college students found that those who slept fewer than five hours on weeknights had GPAs about 0.29 points lower than those who slept more than seven hours. The relationship is measured precisely using a *correlation coefficient*, written as *r*, which runs from −1 to +1. Here, *r* is around −0.29: negative because the variables move in opposite directions (more sleep, higher GPA), and moderate in strength (close to zero, not close to −1).

<!-- → [CHART: scatterplot of sleep hours vs. GPA with a fitted regression line, showing a weak negative slope; point of emphasis should be the wide scatter around the line, illustrating that correlation ≠ determinism even when real] -->

Correlational research scales. You can survey thousands of students, measure dozens of variables, and ask which ones move together. This is useful. It generates hypotheses. It reveals patterns worth explaining.

Its limit is the limit all introductory statistics students eventually hit: *correlation does not imply causation*. The sleep-GPA relationship has at least three plausible explanations. Maybe poor sleep impairs the cognitive processes needed for academic work. Maybe academic stress causes both anxiety and poor sleep. Maybe a third variable — say, a depressive episode — drives both sleep problems and academic difficulty simultaneously. The correlation is the same in all three cases. The cause is different.

This is the ice-cream-and-murder-rate problem. Through the summer months, ice cream sales and homicide rates both increase. The correlation is real and measurable. Should you ban ice cream? No — temperature is the *confounding variable*, the third variable that drives both. Once you account for temperature, the ice-cream-murder correlation vanishes.

People also perceive correlations that don't exist — *illusory correlations*. Many people believe human behavior becomes stranger during a full moon. The belief is intuitive: the moon governs tides, we are mostly water, therefore we are governed too. A meta-analysis of 40 studies found no relationship. The illusory correlation persists because of *confirmation bias* — we vividly remember the odd night when something strange happened and the moon was full, and forget the unremarkable full moons without number.

### Experiment

To prove that something *causes* something else, you must control the comparison.

In 1963, Stanley Milgram wanted to know whether an authority figure's physical presence caused higher obedience — not just correlated with it, but caused it. So he ran two conditions. In one, the experimenter stood in the room giving instructions. In the other, the experimenter left the room and gave the same instructions by telephone. Everything else was identical: the same participant, the same actor playing the learner, the same shock apparatus, the same scripted escalation.

Obedience dropped sharply when the experimenter left. Because the only thing that differed between the conditions was the experimenter's physical presence, and the outcome differed in a predictable direction, Milgram could say: presence causes obedience, not merely accompanies it.

<!-- → [TABLE: Milgram's conditions side by side — experimenter present vs. telephone vs. absent entirely; columns: condition, % who went to maximum voltage, what this tells us about the mechanism] -->

This is the logic of the experiment. You have an *independent variable* — the thing you manipulate — and a *dependent variable* — the thing you measure. You have an *experimental group* that receives the manipulation and a *control group* that does not. Everything else is held constant. If the groups differ on the dependent variable, that difference must come from the independent variable, because it's the only thing that differs.

For this logic to hold, you must *randomly assign* participants to conditions. If the most obedient people systematically ended up in the experimenter-present condition, you'd confuse personality with presence. Random assignment distributes personality traits, intelligence, and every other unmeasured characteristic roughly equally across groups. The groups are, on average, the same in every way except the manipulation.

You also control for experimenter bias through *blinding*. A single-blind experiment keeps participants ignorant of their condition. A double-blind experiment keeps both participants and the person measuring outcomes ignorant until the data are analyzed. If the researcher knows which condition each participant is in and believes the manipulation should increase the outcome, she might unconsciously treat the groups differently — smile more, speak more gently, give inadvertent cues. Blinding closes that door.

The trade-off is *ecological validity* — the degree to which lab findings reflect real-world behavior. Milgram's finding is probably real: authority structures do cause increased compliance. But would the same mechanism operate if the authority figure wore a janitor's uniform instead of a white coat? In a factory instead of a Yale laboratory? With a population not drawn from 1960s New Haven? Probably something similar, but the exact magnitude and conditions are questions for replication.

## Why so many findings didn't replicate

In 2011, a social psychologist named Daryl Bem published a paper in one of the field's most prestigious journals claiming evidence for extrasensory perception. Participants were shown two curtains and asked to guess which hid a photograph. Bem reported that participants guessed correctly more often than chance — particularly when the hidden photo was erotic.

The finding was not a hoax. Bem ran the statistical analyses correctly, by the standards of the field at the time. He found what he found.

What he had done, without necessarily intending to, was *p-hacking*: running many tests, many conditions, many comparisons, and publishing the ones that crossed the significance threshold. The standard threshold is *p < 0.05* — meaning there is less than a 5% chance the result occurred randomly, assuming no real effect. But if you run 20 independent tests, on average one will cross that threshold by chance alone, even if none of the effects are real. Run a hundred tests and five will look significant. Publish those five and file away the 95.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: visual showing a grid of 100 hypothesis tests under the null (no real effect), with 5 highlighted as "significant by chance" — illustrates why p-hacking inflates the false-positive rate even with honest researchers] -->

The statistical threshold itself is not the problem; the problem is treating a significance test as proof rather than as one piece of evidence. A p-value below 0.05 means: *if there were no real effect, results this extreme would occur less than 5% of the time*. It does not mean the effect is large, important, or will replicate.

Several forces combined to produce a literature full of false positives.

**Incentives rewarded novelty.** Publishing that something works gets you cited, promoted, tenured. Publishing that something doesn't work — or that someone else's result failed to replicate — gets you ignored at best, and resistance at worst. Researchers had unconscious incentives to find what they were looking for.

**Samples were small and unrepresentative.** The majority of published psychology studies used college students because they were available. College students are younger, more educated, more Western, and more WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) than the human populations psychology claims to describe. Effects that hold in a sample of 30 Yale undergraduates may not hold in a sample of 500 adults across demographics, income levels, and cultures.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: world map or population pyramid illustrating the WEIRD skew — what share of psychology studies come from North America and Western Europe vs. their share of global population; reinforces why sample representativeness is a validity threat, not a technicality] -->

**There were too many degrees of freedom.** *Researcher degrees of freedom* are the small choices an investigator makes during data collection and analysis: which outliers to exclude, which of several measures to use as the dependent variable, whether to test men and women separately or together, when to stop collecting data. None of these choices are inherently dishonest. But each one is a fork in the road, and if researchers — consciously or not — make choices that favor significant results, the false-positive rate climbs far above 5%.

**Underpowered studies missed real effects and amplified false ones.** *Statistical power* is the probability that a study will detect a real effect if it exists. With small samples, power is low. Small studies that do find significant results are more likely to have found inflated effects — flukes at the noisy edge of the distribution. Those flukes get published. The many non-significant attempts with the same question go into the file drawer. The published literature ends up biased toward large, unreproducible effect sizes.

The combination produced a field that had become very good at finding what it expected to find, and not sufficiently honest about whether those expectations were correct.

### The correction

The response has been real. *Pre-registration* now lets researchers publicly commit to their hypotheses, sample sizes, and analysis plans before collecting data, so they can't adjust decisions after seeing results. Journals that participate in *Registered Reports* review the study design and commit to publication before seeing data — removing the incentive to find something significant.

*Open data* practices require researchers to share their raw data and analysis code so others can audit the work. *Effect sizes* — reporting not just whether an effect was significant but *how large* it was — make it harder to trumpet a reliable but tiny effect as a discovery. *Meta-analyses* pool the results of many studies on the same question, weight them by quality and sample size, and ask what the evidence actually shows when you look at all of it rather than just the studies that got published.

None of these practices make psychology easy or certain. They make it honest about its uncertainties. That's different from saying the field is broken. The 2015 replication paper is not a scandal; it's science working as designed — testing itself, finding where the foundations needed repair, and beginning the repair.

## Ethics: the rules that make trust possible

Scientific knowledge depends on the willingness of people to participate in research. That willingness depends on trust. And trust depends on the field maintaining obligations to participants that are not negotiable even when they're inconvenient.

Four commitments anchor research ethics in psychology.

*Informed consent* means participants are told what they're agreeing to before they agree. What will happen to them, how their data will be used, that they can stop at any time without penalty. They have to actually understand it — not just sign a form they haven't read.

*Protection from harm* means researchers have an affirmative obligation to minimize risks — physical, psychological, social — and to avoid harm that isn't justified by the knowledge to be gained. Milgram's participants showed real signs of distress during his experiments. Many reported lasting effects. Whether the knowledge gained justified the harm is a question the field is still debating sixty years later.

*Deception and debriefing*: sometimes research requires concealing its true purpose. Milgram couldn't tell participants they were in a study of obedience to authority; they would have behaved differently. But deception comes with an obligation to debrief — to tell participants, after the study, what was actually going on, why deception was necessary, and to address any distress it may have caused.

*Institutional review* is the governance structure that enforces the other three. Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) — committees of researchers, ethicists, and community members — review study designs before research begins. They have authority to approve, require modifications, or reject proposals that don't adequately protect participants. They are not optional. No IRB approval, no research.

<!-- → [TABLE: four ethical commitments — informed consent, protection from harm, deception/debriefing, IRB oversight — columns: commitment, what it requires of the researcher, what happens when it's violated] -->

These rules exist not just to protect participants — though they do — but because science that violates its own ethical norms corrupts the knowledge it produces. People who are deceived, harmed, or coerced don't behave the way they would freely, and findings from coerced or harmed populations aren't generalizable anyway. Ethics and scientific validity are not in tension; they reinforce each other.

## What you can now do

You started this chapter knowing that "research shows" was supposed to mean something. Now you know what it has to mean for the claim to be credible.

*Is the claim causal or correlational?* If someone says "coffee consumption is associated with reduced Parkinson's risk," that's a correlational claim. If they say "regular exercise causes a reduction in depressive symptoms," that's a causal claim — and it requires experimental evidence, with random assignment and a control group, to earn the word *causes*.

*Is the sample representative?* A finding from 40 undergraduates at a single university is a hypothesis, not a fact about humanity. The question is whether the sample matches the population the claim is supposed to describe.

*Has it been replicated?* One study is a finding. A dozen independent replications with similar results, using different samples and slightly different methods, are something closer to knowledge. Any important finding should be treated as provisional until the literature shows it's consistent.

*Were there researcher degrees of freedom?* Was the analysis pre-registered, or could the researchers have adjusted their approach after seeing the data? This doesn't mean unregistered research is wrong — most research isn't pre-registered yet — but it's a question worth asking, especially for surprising or counterintuitive findings.

*What's the effect size?* Statistical significance means: this result would be unlikely by chance. Effect size means: how large is the relationship or difference, and does it matter in practice? A study can be statistically significant with an effect so small it has no practical meaning.

These questions are not grounds for cynicism about psychology. They're the tools for reading it well — the same tools the field is now applying to its own history, not to tear itself down, but to build the next century on firmer ground.

### Warm-up

**2.1** Name the four moves of the scientific method and explain in one sentence why the cycle has no permanent ending point.

**2.2** A researcher claims: "People who own pets live longer." Is this a causal claim or a correlational one? What single word in the claim signals which it is, and what kind of research design would be required to earn that word?

**2.3** State one hypothesis that is falsifiable and one that is not. For each, name the specific observation that would either refute it (if falsifiable) or explain why no such observation exists (if not).

### Application

**2.4** You read that students who take handwritten notes score higher on exams than students who type. (a) Identify a plausible confounding variable. (b) Design an experiment — name the independent variable, dependent variable, experimental group, and control group — that would test whether note-taking method *causes* the difference. (c) Why must participants be randomly assigned to conditions rather than allowed to choose?

**2.5** Jane Goodall's chimpanzee observations and Stanley Milgram's obedience experiments both produced important findings, but through entirely different methods. (a) What type of research is each? (b) What causal question could Milgram answer that Goodall could not? (c) What did Goodall's method preserve that Milgram's could not?

**2.6** A p-value of 0.03 appears in a study claiming that brief meditation reduces test anxiety. (a) What does p = 0.03 actually mean — in plain language, not statistical jargon? (b) What does it *not* tell you about the effect? (c) What additional piece of information would you want before concluding the finding is practically meaningful?

### Synthesis

**2.7** The Bem (2011) ESP study was published in a peer-reviewed journal using standard statistical methods and passed peer review. (a) Explain specifically how p-hacking could produce a significant result even when no real effect exists. (b) What would a pre-registered version of Bem's study have required him to do differently? (c) If the same study were run today under Registered Reports, and the results came back non-significant, what would happen to the paper?

**2.8** A headline reads: "Coffee drinkers have 34% lower rates of Parkinson's disease." (a) What research design almost certainly produced this finding? (b) Construct two alternative causal explanations that do not involve coffee preventing Parkinson's. (c) What would an experiment capable of testing causation require — and why would it almost certainly be unethical to run?

### Challenge

**2.9** The replication crisis hit social psychology harder than cognitive psychology. Using what you know about how each subfield typically runs studies — sample sizes, types of dependent variables, how much flexibility researchers have in analysis — construct an argument for why this difference in replication rates might be predictable rather than surprising. Then name one piece of evidence that would challenge your argument.

**2.10** Design a study to test whether social media use causes increased anxiety in adolescents. You must address: the research design you'd use and why; at least two researcher degrees of freedom you'd pre-register to eliminate; why random assignment is difficult here and what you'd do instead; and how you'd distinguish a genuine causal effect from a correlation driven by a third variable.
