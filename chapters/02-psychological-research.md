## TL;DR

Psychology became a science when researchers stopped asking *what do people think* and started asking *how do I prove it*. The gap between those questions — filled by research designs, controls, and the discipline to admit when you were wrong — is what this chapter teaches you.

## Cold open: The replication problem lands (2015)

In August 2015, a consortium of researchers called the Open Science Collaboration published a result that shook the discipline. They had taken 100 studies from three top psychology journals published between 2008 and 2012 — papers that had collectively been cited thousands of times, that psychologists taught in classes, that had shaped practice and policy — and tried to replicate them. To reproduce the studies exactly, using the same methods, the same measures, the same logic.

Only 36 of the 100 replicated.

Thirty-six percent.

That means that if you had taken a finding from psychology research, picked it at random in 2010, and asked "will this hold up if someone else runs the same study?" — odds were better than two-to-one that the answer was no. Not *maybe* no. Not *partially* no. The effect vanished. The phenomenon that had seemed solid enough to cite, teach, build on — simply did not appear when the experiment was run again.

This did not mean all psychology was junk. It meant something else: psychology had been running for a century on methods that looked rigorous but had hidden flaws. The field had become very good at finding what it expected to find. It had become less good at asking whether those expectations were correct.

That gap — between what looks like proof and what *is* proof — is what you're about to learn to see. Understanding it will change how you read research forever. Not because psychology is broken, but because fixing it is the most honest thing the field could do. And the fixes depend entirely on understanding what separates evidence from coincidence, what can be measured from what merely sounds true, and why replication — the willingness to be wrong again — is science's secret superpower.

### Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- **Explain** the four essential moves of the scientific method (theory, hypothesis, observation, revision) and show how they form a cycle rather than a line.
- **Distinguish** between description, correlation, and experimental causation — recognizing what each reveals and what each cannot prove.
- **Evaluate** a research design by naming its independent and dependent variables, identifying threats to validity, and judging whether the conclusion is earned.
- **Assess** ethical obligations in research: informed consent, protection from harm, deception and debriefing, and the governance that enforces them.
- **Read** a replication failure without despair — understanding it as science working as designed to correct itself.

### Prerequisites

Basic comfort with the idea that feelings are not facts. Willingness to distrust your intuition about how often things happen. No math background required.

When Chapter 5 says "people remember emotionally vivid events better," you'll need to know what evidence would have to support that claim — and how that evidence could be wrong. ## Concept 1 — The anatomy of the scientific method: Theory, hypothesis, observation, revision

In 1963, Stanley Milgram walked into his laboratory at Yale with a question that had been burning in the world for eighteen years. How could ordinary German citizens have participated in genocide? How could people who were not sadists, not military officers, not ideologues — just people — have turned the machinery of bureaucracy toward mass murder?

Milgram's answer was not that Germans were uniquely evil. His hypothesis was darker and more precise: *ordinary people will inflict severe pain on innocent others when directed to do so by an authority figure*.

To test it, he built a machine that delivered electrical shocks. He recruited male volunteers, told them they were in a study of learning, and put them in a room with a man strapped to a chair. That man — actually an actor, not receiving real shocks — was wired to the machine. The participant was told to teach the man a list of word pairs. If the man got one wrong, the participant would deliver a shock. With each wrong answer, the voltage went up. The experimenter, wearing a gray coat and speaking in a flat, bureaucratic tone, would say: *You have no choice; you must continue.*

Milgram expected maybe 1 or 2 percent would go to the maximum voltage of 450 volts. Most people, he thought, would refuse.

Two-thirds of them went all the way.

That experiment — flawed in ways the field is still unpacking — revealed something true about obedience to authority that has held up in dozens of replications, in different countries, with different populations. The mechanism was visible: the experimenter's presence, the stepped progression of small increments, the scripted refusals so the participant never had to choose directly, the white coat signaling authority. Each element mattered. Remove one and the effect weakened.

This is how science works in the real world. You start with a puzzle — not a mathematical equation, but a genuine question drawn from history or common experience or a gap in what you already know. You translate that puzzle into a *theory*, a general explanation for how the world works. Milgram's theory was that obedience is a learned response to authority structures, not a personality trait. From the theory you derive a *hypothesis* — a specific, testable prediction. If my theory is correct, then *this* should happen. Milgram's hypothesis was precise: ordinary volunteers will inflict pain when told to do so by an experimenter.

Then you conduct the *observation* — the experiment, the measurement, the data collection. You measure reality and compare it to your prediction. In Milgram's case, the observation was: measure the voltage at which each participant stops, record the behaviors and reactions, note whether the experimenter's presence affects the outcome.

Finally comes *revision*. You look at your data and ask: did my prediction match what I observed? If yes, you've supported your hypothesis. If no, you've learned something else — perhaps something more interesting. Either way, you now have a new hypothesis to test. You've completed one cycle of the scientific method.

The critical move is that science is *not* linear. It's circular. You don't do theory once and then stop. You test the hypothesis, revise based on results, generate new hypotheses that push deeper into the mechanism, test those, revise again. Theories stand as long as they predict what you observe. The moment they don't, they must be modified or abandoned.

### The distinction between deduction and induction

The cycle has two directions. When you move from *theory to observation* — "If my theory is true, then I predict this result" — you're using *deductive reasoning*. You begin with a general principle and deduce what must be true in a specific case. If all living things require energy to survive (theory), and ducks are living things (specific case), then ducks require energy (prediction). Then you test that prediction.

When you move from *observation to theory*, you're using *inductive reasoning*. You gather specific observations and look for patterns that suggest a more general principle. You notice that people tend to wash their hands more frequently after touching something they find disgusting, and from a handful of such observations you form a hypothesis: *disgust triggers a drive to cleanse*. You'd then test that hypothesis deductively — predict what should happen, run an experiment, see if you were right.

Science uses both directions. Deduction without induction becomes sterile — you're just testing what you already assumed. Induction without deduction becomes anecdotal — you're seeing patterns that might be coincidence. Together, they form the cycle: specific observations suggest general theories, which generate testable hypotheses, which lead to new observations, which revise the theories.

Milgram's method moved both directions. He began with observations — the historical puzzle of how ordinary people participated in atrocity — induced a theory from those observations, then deduced a specific prediction that he could test in the lab.

### What makes a hypothesis scientific: Falsifiability

Not every prediction is scientific. Consider Sigmund Freud's theory of the id, ego, and superego — the three parts of personality. Freud predicted that all human behavior originates in the unconscious wishes of the id, mediated by social reality through the ego and the superego. The theory is elegant and has shaped generations of therapists. But here's the problem: *there is no observation that could prove Freud wrong*. If you ask someone why they did something and they say "because I wanted to," Freud would say "that's your ego talking; your real motive is unconscious." If they say "I don't know why I did it," he'd say "see, the unconscious is at work." The theory is unfalsifiable. It can be tweaked to explain anything.

For a hypothesis to be *scientific*, it must be falsifiable — there must be some possible observation that would prove it wrong. Milgram's hypothesis was falsifiable. If participants had refused to shock others (and many did in different conditions), his prediction would have been falsified. If changing the experimenter's location or removing the white coat eliminated obedience, he would have learned something. The experiment could have said *no* to his theory.

Falsifiability is not the same as being likely to be true. It means you're willing to be wrong. Science is a conversation with reality, and reality only speaks to you if you're willing to hear the word *no*.

### Worked example — Testing whether sleep affects exam performance

**Puzzle:** You notice that when you sleep poorly before exams, your performance suffers. Is that always true? Can it be proven?

**Theory:** Sleep quality influences cognitive function, particularly the consolidation and retrieval of recently learned information.

**Hypothesis:** Students who sleep fewer than 5 hours the night before an exam will score significantly lower than students who sleep more than 7 hours.

**Observation:** You would recruit a sample of students, assign them randomly to either a normal-sleep condition or a sleep-deprivation condition (one stays up late, one sleeps normally), give both groups the same exam the next morning, and measure the difference in scores.

**Revision:** If sleep-deprived students score lower, your hypothesis is supported. But you'd ask: how much lower? Is the effect size large enough to matter? Does it affect all types of material equally? Would caffeine reverse it? You'd design a new hypothesis to answer the next question, based on what you learned.

**Is it falsifiable?** Yes. If there were no difference in exam scores between sleep-deprived and well-rested students, your theory would need revision. Maybe sleep quality doesn't affect exam performance the way you predicted. Maybe it matters for some types of learning but not others. The experiment could have said *no*.

### Common misconceptions

**"If I find a correlation, my theory is proved."** Correlation is an observation, not a proof. It tells you two things move together, not why. You'll learn this in Concept 2.

**"Science means eliminating your bias."** Science does not eliminate bias; it *reveals* bias. The scientific method is designed to make your assumptions testable so that reality — not your hopes — decides whether they're true.

**"Replication failure means the original research was fraudulent."** Most replication failures happen because the original study was too small, used a convenience sample, or the effect was smaller than the sample size could detect. That's not fraud; it's a limitation the original researchers didn't know they had.

## Concept 2 — Research designs: Descriptive, correlational, and experimental

There's a famous false correlation: as ice cream sales increase, so does the murder rate. A graph would show them moving together, month by month through summer. Should we ban ice cream? The answer is obvious. Both variables are driven by a third variable — temperature. When it's warm, people buy ice cream *and* spend time outside where conflicts are more likely.

But here's what's instructive: the observation is *real*. Ice cream sales and murder rate *do* move together. What's not real is the cause. That confusion — between noticing a relationship and explaining it — is where most research error lives.

Psychologists have three main tools for studying behavior. They differ in what they can show and what they must sacrifice.

### Descriptive research: Rich but not causal

When Jane Goodall walked into the Gombe Stream National Park in Tanzania in 1960, there was no hypothesis about chimpanzee behavior. She watched. For months and years, she watched. She recorded what the chimpanzees ate, how they traveled, how they interacted with each other, how mothers taught their young. She gave them names instead of numbers — a choice critics said undermined objectivity. But the depth of observation Goodall provided changed how we understood primate intelligence. Chimpanzees use tools. They have individual personalities. They grieve their dead.

That's *descriptive research* — systematic observation of behavior in its natural setting or in controlled conditions where the goal is to describe what happens, not explain why.

Descriptive research has enormous power. The richness of detail is unmatched. When Goodall watched a mother chimpanzee teaching her infant how to fish for termites, spending months perfecting the technique, she revealed something about the transmission of culture that no laboratory experiment could touch. The observation is real. The context is preserved.

But descriptive research cannot answer causal questions. Watching Goodall's mother chimpanzee teach her infant doesn't prove that *teaching* caused the infant to learn. Maybe the infant would have learned anyway. Maybe the mother's presence made the infant more curious. You see the relationship, not the mechanism that produces it.

Descriptive research is also limited by the observer's attention and bias. If the researcher expects to see a pattern, she may notice it while ignoring contradictions. To guard against this, researchers use *structured observation* — pre-established categories for what counts as "teaching" or "playing" or "aggression" so that multiple observers coding the same behavior agree on what they've seen. Mary Ainsworth used this when studying infant attachment, placing infants and caregivers in a room with toys, then carefully noting the infant's behavior when the caregiver left and returned. The protocol was precise. The observations were consistent.

### Correlational research: Seeing relationships without causation

A study published in 2010 found that students who slept fewer than 5 hours on weeknights had a GPA about 0.29 points lower than students who slept more than 7 hours. That's a *correlation* — a measured relationship between two variables. The correlation coefficient, written as *r*, ranges from -1 to +1. A correlation of -0.29 means the variables move in opposite directions: as sleep decreases, GPA decreases (or as sleep increases, GPA increases). The negative sign indicates direction, not judgment. The magnitude (how close to -1 or +1) indicates strength. A correlation of -0.29 is weak to moderate.

Correlational research can be conducted quickly and on large samples. Researchers can send out surveys, collect thousands of responses, and calculate how variables relate. The results generalize better than descriptive research because the sample is larger and more diverse.

But correlation has a hard ceiling: *it cannot establish causation*. Maybe sleep deprivation causes lower GPAs. Maybe lower GPA causes stress, which causes sleep deprivation. Maybe a third variable — anxiety about grades — drives both poor sleep and poor performance. The correlation is real. The cause is ambiguous.

This matters because people routinely mistake correlation for proof. When a breakfast-cereal company publishes data showing that people who eat their cereal have lower rates of heart disease, the implication is clear: eat cereal, get healthier. But the correlation could mean that health-conscious people eat cereal. Or that wealthy people eat cereal and have better healthcare. Or that the cereal is correlated with something else entirely that causes the health benefit.

To avoid this trap, researchers sometimes identify a *confounding variable* — a third variable that accounts for the correlation. In the ice cream and murder example, temperature is the confound. Once you control for temperature, the relationship between ice cream and murder disappears.

Sometimes people see correlations that don't exist — *illusory correlations*. Many people believe that human behavior changes with the moon's phases, especially at full moon. The belief is intuitive: the moon controls tides, and our bodies are mostly water, so we're affected too. But a meta-analysis of 40 studies found no relationship between lunar phases and behavior. The illusory correlation persists because we remember vivid examples — the one odd night when something strange happened and the moon was full — while forgetting the many full moons when nothing unusual occurred. This is *confirmation bias*: remembering evidence that supports your belief while ignoring evidence against it.

### Experimental research: Controlling for causation

To prove causation, you must control the variables. In Milgram's obedience study, he was interested in the effect of the *experimenter's presence* on obedience. If his theory about authority was correct, then the authority figure's presence should matter. So he designed a condition where the experimenter left the room and gave orders only by telephone. Obedience dropped. The presence of authority wasn't just correlated with obedience; it *caused* the increase.

This is the logic of an *experiment*. You have two groups: an *experimental group* that experiences a manipulation and a *control group* that does not. Everything else is held constant. The only difference is the variable you're testing — the *independent variable*. You then measure the outcome you care about — the *dependent variable*.

In Milgram's telephone condition, the independent variable was the experimenter's presence (in the room versus on the phone). The dependent variable was the voltage at which obedience stopped. Everything else — the participant, the learner, the shock apparatus, the word pairs — stayed the same.

If the experimental group (experimenter present) shows higher obedience than the control group (experimenter absent), you can attribute the difference to the independent variable because it's the *only* difference between the groups. You've established causation.

For this to work, participants must be *randomly assigned* to conditions. If all the most obedient people happened to end up in the experimenter-present group, you'd confuse personality with the effect of presence. Random assignment ensures that personality traits, intelligence, and other unmeasured variables are distributed equally across groups. With sufficiently large samples, random assignment makes it unlikely that one group is systematically different before the experiment begins.

The experimenter must also control for bias. If the experimenter knew which participant was in which condition and believed that presence would increase obedience, she might unconsciously treat the two groups differently — smile more at the experimental group, speak more gently, give subtle cues. To prevent this, many experiments use *blinding*. In a *single-blind* study, participants don't know which condition they're in. In a *double-blind* study, neither the researcher nor the participant knows the group assignment until the data are analyzed. (In Milgram's case, single-blinding wasn't possible — participants always knew if an experimenter was in the room — but the person who measured the shock voltages was blind to condition.)

### Worked example — Designing an experiment to test whether note-taking method affects learning

**The puzzle:** Some students take notes on laptops, some by hand. Does the method matter for what they remember?

**The hypothesis:** Students who take notes by hand will retain more information than students who take notes on laptops, because handwriting requires more selective processing.

**The independent variable:** Note-taking method (handwritten versus typed).

**The dependent variable:** Score on a test covering the material.

**The experimental group:** Students who type notes on laptops.

**The control group:** Students who handwrite notes.

**Setup:** Recruit a large sample of students. Randomly assign them to type or handwrite while watching the same lecture. Immediately after, both groups take the same exam on the material. Compare scores.

**Why this works:** The only difference between groups is note-taking method. If the handwriting group scores higher, the difference must be caused by the method, not by prior knowledge or intelligence or motivation (those are distributed equally across groups by random assignment).

**Threats:** What if the laptop group gets distracted by the internet? Then it's not note-taking method that matters; it's distraction. You could control this by disconnecting wifi or using software that blocks other applications. What if handwriting is just slower, so those students write fewer notes? Then quantity is a confound. You'd measure notes taken and control for that statistically.

**Confounding variables:** Any difference between groups besides the independent variable. Your job is to think of them before running the study, then prevent them.

### The trade-off between control and realism

Experiments have power: they can prove causation. But that power comes at a cost. In a lab, under controlled conditions, with a captive sample and artificial tasks, behavior may not match the real world. This is *ecological validity* — the degree to which findings in a controlled setting apply to real life.

Goodall's chimpanzees taught freely in the forest; Milgram's participants shocked a stranger in a Yale laboratory. Both observations tell you something, but what? Goodall's findings reflect how chimpanzees actually behave. But they're based on a handful of individuals in one location. Milgram's findings are causal — the experimenter's presence *does* increase obedience — but in the artificial context of a laboratory, with an actor, with no real risk, with a specific authority structure. Would the same obedience appear if the experimenter were not a scientist in a white coat but a janitor in a maintenance uniform? Possibly, possibly not. That's what replication tries to answer.

### Longitudinal versus cross-sectional research

One more distinction worth making. If you want to understand how people *change* — how does personality develop, how do political attitudes shift, how does memory decline with age — you have two main approaches.

*Longitudinal research* follows the same people over time. You test them at age 20, again at 30, again at 40, asking the same questions each time. By following the same individuals, you know you're measuring change in the same people, not comparing different generations with different values and experiences.

The cost: it takes decades. People drop out. Life changes. Between 1990 and 2010, how many of your original participants move, get married, change jobs, have health crises? Attrition is high. And you won't have results for years.

*Cross-sectional research* tests different age groups at the same time. You interview 20-year-olds, 30-year-olds, and 40-year-olds in a single year, then compare. This is fast and cheap.

The cost: you're comparing different generations. Maybe 40-year-olds in 2024 are less progressive than 20-year-olds not because they've aged but because they grew up in a different time. You can't distinguish aging from generational change.

### Common misconceptions

**"An experiment with a small sample is worthless."** A small sample is less powerful — you're less likely to detect a real effect — but it can still reveal causation. What it can't do is generalize confidently to a broader population.

**"If the correlation is very strong, it's probably causal."** No. A perfect correlation of r = 0.99 could still have a confounding variable. The strength of the relationship doesn't tell you about causation.

**"Descriptive research is just storytelling."** Descriptive research is rigorous observation. Goodall's field notes revealed facts about chimpanzee behavior that hypotheses hadn't predicted. Observation comes before theory.

## Concept 3 — The replication crisis and why it happened

In 2011, a social psychologist named Daryl Bem published a paper in the journal *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* claiming to have found evidence for extrasensory perception. In his experiments, participants were shown images of two curtains and asked to guess which one hid a photograph. One curtain was chosen at random. Bem reported that participants guessed correctly more often than chance, and particularly so when the hidden photograph was erotic.

The finding was shocking. Not because extrasensory perception is real — there's no known mechanism for it — but because it was published in a mainstream journal following standard procedures.

What Bem had done was *p-hacking*. He had conducted many statistical tests, looking for any that came out significant (p < 0.05, meaning there's less than a 5% chance the result occurred randomly). Enough tests, and some will come out as hits by pure chance. If you test a thousand hypotheses, about 50 will show statistical significance even if none are real effects. Bem published the ones that worked.

This exemplifies the replication crisis: researchers were finding effects that, when others tried to reproduce them, vanished. Why?

Several reasons combined. First, the incentive structure in academia rewards novelty. Publishing that something works gets you promoted, cited, famous. Publishing that something doesn't work or that someone else's finding failed to replicate gets you resistance. Researchers have unconscious motivation to find what they're looking for.

Second, the statistical threshold (p < 0.05) is arbitrary and easily crossed if you're looking at enough variables. Suppose you measure 20 possible predictors of academic success. By chance, one of them will correlate with grades at p < 0.05 even if there's no real relationship. You publish: "This variable predicts success!" Someone else runs the study with a larger sample or a more representative group, and the correlation vanishes. The original finding was a false positive — a statistical mirage.

Third, the assumption that psychological research uses large, representative samples is wrong. The majority of published studies use college students because they're convenient. College students are younger, more educated, more liberal, more culturally Western than the general population. A finding that works with college students may not generalize.

Fourth, many studies are underpowered. Statistical power is the probability that an experiment will detect a real effect if it exists. With a small sample, you have low power. An effect might be real but too small to detect, leading to a false negative (concluding there's no effect when there is one). But the absence of a published null result means those studies stay in researchers' file drawers. Only the studies with positive findings — whether real or lucky — get published.

Fifth, researchers often have flexibility in how they analyze data. Did you predict the effect would be strongest in men or women? Maybe you didn't specify beforehand. Then you run the analysis both ways and publish the one that worked. That's a *researcher degree of freedom* — a choice that looks small but inflates the false-positive rate.

The replication crisis revealed all of this. And it's happening because science works. When many researchers independently try to reproduce findings, inconsistencies emerge. The field is self-correcting, but correction takes time and honesty.

### How researchers combat the replication problem

The response has been substantial. Researchers now pre-register studies — publicly committing to their hypotheses and analysis plans *before* conducting the experiment, so they can't change their minds based on results. Journals increasingly require authors to report effect sizes (how large the effect is, not just whether it reached p < 0.05) and confidence intervals (a range that likely contains the true effect, with stated uncertainty). Some journals commit to publishing replications and null results, removing the incentive to hide non-findings.

Open Science practices have emerged: researchers share raw data and analysis code so others can audit their work. The Center for Open Science maintains a registry of registered reports, where researchers describe their study plan in advance, get it peer-reviewed, and then conduct the study knowing it will be published regardless of results.

Meta-analyses have become more common. Instead of relying on a single study, researchers compile dozens of studies on the same question, weight them by sample size and quality, and ask: what does the body of evidence actually show? This reveals how much of the published literature represents true effects and how much is noise.

### Worked example — Why the original obedience study replicated but many social psychology findings didn't

Milgram's obedience study, first run in 1963, has been replicated dozens of times across decades and countries. Why did it hold up when many other famous studies didn't?

**The effect was large and consistent.** The phenomenon Milgram found — that about two-thirds of people would deliver high shocks when directed to — appeared in most conditions and most replications. Large effects are more likely to replicate.

**The sample was reasonably large.** For its time, Milgram ran multiple experiments with dozens of participants each. Larger samples are less susceptible to random noise.

**The mechanism was testable and specific.** Milgram had clear predictions about what would increase or decrease obedience — removing the experimenter, moving the location, changing the authority figure. Each variation showed how the effect worked, not just that it happened.

**Later researchers had strong incentive to replicate.** Milgram's finding was controversial, shocking, ethically troubling. People wanted to understand it, challenge it, refine it. Replication wasn't treated as boring validation; it was seen as important.

By contrast, a famous study on "ego depletion" — the idea that willpower is a limited resource that gets exhausted — was published hundreds of times, inspired thousands of follow-ups, and is now thought to be largely false. Why the replication failure?

**The original effects were small.** When you're trying to detect a small effect, you need much larger samples than were typically used.

**There were many degrees of freedom.** How exactly do you measure "depletion"? Through performance on an impossible task? A persistence measure? A choice task? Researchers could adjust the dependent variable until something worked.

**The field was incentivized to believe it.** The idea appealed to conventional wisdom — yes, willpower runs out, that's obvious. Replications that supported it were published eagerly. Replications that found nothing were published reluctantly or not at all.

The solution is not to abandon the findings but to retest them carefully, with larger samples, transparent methods, and pre-registered analysis. Some ego-depletion findings have survived; others haven't. That's science correcting itself.

### Common misconceptions

**"Replication crisis means psychology is fake."** It means psychology standards were looser than they should have been. The crisis is not a failure of science; it's science working as designed.

**"All published research is junk."** No. Some published findings replicate reliably. Others don't. Your job is to learn which is which by understanding the methods, sample sizes, and whether the work has been replicated.

**"If a study didn't replicate, the original researcher was dishonest."** Usually not. Researchers weren't lying. They were working within incentive structures that rewarded novelty, they used statistical practices that inflated false positives, and they didn't always have access to the same populations or resources as original researchers. Replication failures reveal systemic issues, not individual fraud.

**"Big effects always replicate."** Sometimes big effects appear in small, specialized samples and shrink in larger, more representative samples. The effect is real, but its size was overstated.

## Integration — What you can now expect from research, and what you should question

You've learned to distinguish three questions in reading psychological research:

1. **What is the claim?** Not just "this therapy works" but "this therapy, for this condition, in this population, measured this way, by these researchers."

2. **What evidence supports it?** Is it descriptive observation, correlational, or experimental? Descriptive research can reveal phenomena. Correlational research can suggest hypotheses. Only experiments can prove causation. Each is valuable for different questions.

3. **Could it be replicated?** Is the sample large enough? Is it representative of the population the researchers want to understand? Were the methods transparent and specific enough that someone else could run the same study? Did the original researchers pre-register their hypotheses or was there flexibility in how they analyzed the data?

When you read that "research shows" something in psychology, you'll now ask: *whose research, under what conditions, with what sample, has it been replicated, and could someone else run the same study and get the same result?*

That's not cynicism about psychology. It's what science looks like when it's honest about its own uncertainties.

## Chapter summary

The scientific method is a cycle: theory generates hypothesis, hypothesis predicts observation, observation reveals whether prediction matched reality, results generate new hypotheses or revise existing theories. The cycle repeats. This is how psychology separated itself from mere opinion — it committed to testing ideas against evidence and accepting when evidence said no.

There are three main research designs. Descriptive research systematically observes behavior, providing rich detail but no causal claims. Correlational research measures relationships between variables, allowing prediction but not proof of cause. Experimental research manipulates variables and measures outcomes, establishing causation by comparing groups that differ only in the variable being tested.

Each design involves trade-offs. Observation is rich but limited in generalizability. Surveys are scalable but shallow. Experiments prove causation but risk ecological invalidity — findings may not reflect the real world.

The replication crisis revealed that many published findings fail to reproduce. This happened because of small samples, flexible analysis choices, researcher degrees of freedom, publication bias, and incentives to find novel results. The response has been substantial: pre-registration, open data, meta-analyses, and commitment to publishing null results. Science is self-correcting, but correction requires honesty about limitations.

You can now read a research finding and ask the questions that distinguish evidence from luck: Is the sample large? Is it representative? Could someone else run the same study and get the same result? Are the hypotheses pre-registered or were there degrees of freedom in analysis? Has the finding been replicated?

These questions are not obstacles to appreciating research. They're the tools for reading it well.

## What would change my mind

I assume that replications of classic studies sometimes fail because of systemic issues in how research is conducted, not because the original findings were false. If instead I discovered that replication failures were mostly due to fraud or incompetence by the original researchers, I would conclude that psychology's integrity problems are deeper than method problems and require stronger oversight.
