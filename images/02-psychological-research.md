### Figure 1: Stanley Milgram's Obedience Study Setup
**Location in chapter:** Concept 1 cold open section

**Description:** Photograph or diagram showing the laboratory setup from Milgram's 1963 obedience study. Shows the experimenter in a gray coat, the "participant" seated at a shock console, and the "learner" (actor) visible through a window or partition, wired to the shock apparatus. The shock machine itself is prominently featured with a large scale labeled from 0 to 450 volts, with verbal labels underneath ("Slight shock" to "Danger: Severe shock").

**Source:** Milgram, S. (1963). "Behavioral study of obedience." *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology*, 67(4), 371-378. [Figure reproduced in many textbooks; original photographs are in Milgram archives at Yale University.]

**Educational Function:** Brings the emotional reality of the study into focus. Students see the white coat, the mechanical apparatus, the distance and proximity of participants. This makes the mechanism (authority, incremental escalation, scripted refusals) visible.

### Figure 2: Milgram's Obedience Results Across Conditions
**Location in chapter:** Concept 1, worked example section

**Description:** Bar chart showing obedience rates (% of participants reaching maximum shock voltage) across Milgram's major experimental conditions:
- Baseline (experimenter in room): ~65%
- Telephone condition (experimenter absent): ~21%
- Experimenter absent, peer administers: ~93%
- Experimenter absent, participant decides voltage: ~2-3%

**Source:** Milgram (1963) and Milgram (1974), *Obedience to Authority*.

**Educational Function:** Shows visually that the effect is large, systematic, and driven by a specific variable (experimenter presence). This supports the argument that causation can be proven through experimental control.

### Figure 3: Jane Goodall Observing Chimpanzees in the Field
**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, descriptive research section

**Description:** Photograph of Jane Goodall in the field with chimpanzees at Gombe Stream National Park, Tanzania. Shows Goodall at a distance, observing naturally behaving chimpanzees. Could show a mother chimpanzee teaching an infant, or tool use (fishing for termites).

**Source:** Goodall, J., & Berman, M. (1999). *Reason for Hope*. [Photographs from Goodall's decades of field work are widely available in archives.]

**Educational Function:** Contrasts with Milgram's lab setting. Shows richness of context, observation in natural habitat, the intimacy of long-term study. Asks: what can you learn here that you can't in a lab?

### Figure 4: Ice Cream Sales vs. Murder Rate — Spurious Correlation
**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, correlational research opening

**Description:** Line graph showing two trends over months (roughly May through September): ice cream sales (left y-axis) and violent crime rate (right y-axis). Both rise in summer, creating apparent correlation. A dashed line or annotation shows temperature as the confounding variable, explaining both.

**Source:** Harper, J. (2013, July 5). "Ice cream and crime: Where cold cuisine and hot disputes intersect." *The Times-Picayune*. [Actual data available from ice cream industry reports and FBI crime statistics by month.]

**Educational Function:** Makes "correlation ≠ causation" concrete and memorable. Students see the spurious relationship, understand the confound, and can explain why both rise in summer without implying causation.

### Figure 5: Sleep Duration and GPA — Negative Correlation Scatterplot
**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, worked example section

**Description:** Scatterplot showing individual data points (each student's sleep hours on x-axis, GPA on y-axis). Points trend from upper-left to lower-right (negative correlation). A trend line shows r = -0.29. Dots scatter around the line, showing that sleep hours alone don't perfectly predict GPA.

**Source:** Lowry, M., Dean, K., & Manders, K. (2010). "The link between sleep quantity and academic performance for the college student." *Sentience*, 3(Spring), 16-19.

**Educational Function:** Shows what a weak-to-moderate correlation looks like visually. Students see the trend but also the scatter, understanding why correlation coefficient is -0.29 (explains ~8% of variance), not -0.9.

### Figure 6: Moon Phases and Hospital Admissions Over One Year
**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, illusory correlations section

**Description:** Line graph or circular diagram showing hospital admission rates across the lunar month (new moon, waxing, full moon, waning). The line is flat, showing no relationship. A superimposed memory/perception bias illustration shows *where* people think they see a peak (full moon), even though actual data shows none.

**Source:** Rotton, J., & Kelly, I. W. (1985). "Much ado about the full moon: A meta-analysis of lunar-lunacy research." *Psychological Bulletin*, 97(2), 286-306.

**Educational Function:** Demonstrates confirmation bias and availability bias. Students see that the data doesn't support the intuitive belief. Asks: why do we believe it anyway?

### Figure 7: Experimental Design: Note-Taking Methods and Learning Outcomes
**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, worked example section

**Description:** Diagram showing experimental design:
- **Top branch:** Participants randomly assigned to handwrite notes → lecture material → exam → score
- **Bottom branch:** Participants randomly assigned to type notes on laptop → same lecture material → same exam → score
- **Comparison:** Bar chart showing handwriting group mean score vs. laptop group mean score.

**Source:** Mueller, P. A., & Oppenheimer, D. M. (2014). "The pen is mightier than the keyboard." *Psychological Science*, 25(6), 1159-1168.

**Educational Function:** Shows the logic of an experiment: two groups, identical except for independent variable, both take same test, scores compared. Makes abstract experimental design concrete.

### Figure 8: Ecological Validity Trade-off — Lab vs. Field
**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, trade-off discussion

**Description:** Diagram or table comparing:
- **Left side (Lab - Milgram):** Controlled setting, artificial task, strong causal inference, low real-world relevance
- **Right side (Field - Goodall):** Natural setting, real behavior, weak causal inference, high real-world relevance
- **Center:** Scaling or continuum showing trade-off

**Educational Function:** Visualizes the central tension in psychological research. No design is "perfect"; each gains something by sacrificing something else.

### Figure 9: Longitudinal vs. Cross-Sectional Study Designs
**Location in chapter:** Concept 2, longitudinal vs. cross-sectional section

**Description:** Two diagrams:
- **Left (Longitudinal):** Same cohort (born 2010) measured at ages 5, 10, 15, 20. Shows one line tracked over time.
- **Right (Cross-sectional):** Different cohorts (born 2010, 2005, 2000, 1995) measured in same year (2024). Shows multiple lines at single time point.
- **Below:** Table showing advantages (longitudinal: true change; cross-sectional: fast) and disadvantages (longitudinal: attrition; cross-sectional: cohort confounds).

**Source:** Standard methodological diagram, appears in most research methods textbooks.

**Educational Function:** Clarifies the difference between following change over time (longitudinal) and comparing groups at one time (cross-sectional). Asks: which design answers which questions?

### Figure 10: The Replication Crisis — OSC 2015 Results
**Location in chapter:** Concept 3, replication crisis opening

**Description:** Visualization of the Open Science Collaboration (2015) findings:
- **Left side:** 100 studies published in top journals (shown as boxes)
- **Color coding:** Green boxes = replicated successfully (~36), Red boxes = failed to replicate (~64)
- **Optional:** Comparison of effect sizes: original studies vs. replication studies show substantial shrinkage

**Source:** Open Science Collaboration. (2015). "Estimating the reproducibility of psychological science." *Science*, 349(6251), aac4716.

**Educational Function:** Makes the scale of replication failure visible. 64% failure is striking, memorable, and raises questions (which we then answer).

### Figure 11: Why Studies Fail to Replicate — Contributing Factors
**Location in chapter:** Concept 3, causes of replication failure section

**Description:** Infographic or diagram showing multiple contributing factors to replication failure, arranged radially around a central "Replication Failure" node:
- Small sample sizes (arrow labeled "Low statistical power")
- P-hacking (arrow labeled "Testing many hypotheses")
- Convenience sampling (arrow labeled "College students unrepresentative")
- Publication bias (arrow labeled "Null results not published")
- Researcher degrees of freedom (arrow labeled "Flexibility in analysis")

**Educational Function:** Shows that replication failure is not a single problem but a constellation of systemic issues. No single fix works; the solution requires addressing multiple factors.

### Figure 12: Effect Size and Sample Size — Why Milgram Replicated
**Location in chapter:** Concept 3, worked example section

**Description:** Diagram comparing two hypothetical studies:
- **Top row (Milgram-like):** Large effect size (d ≈ 1.0), moderate sample (N ≈ 40), replicable ✓
- **Bottom row (Ego depletion-like):** Small effect size (d ≈ 0.3), small sample (N ≈ 30), not reliably replicable ✗

**Shows relationship:** Effect size × sample size = replicability. Can also be expressed as a power analysis curve.

**Educational Function:** Explains why some studies replicate and others don't, without blaming the original researchers. Large effects in reasonable samples are more likely to replicate.

### Figure 13: Open Science Practices — Pre-registration & Transparency
**Location in chapter:** Integration section, open science solutions

**Description:** Timeline or flowchart showing:
1. **Before data collection:** Researcher pre-registers hypothesis and analysis plan on OSF (Open Science Framework) or similar registry. Plan is publicly timestamped and immutable.
2. **During data collection:** Researcher collects data following pre-registered protocol.
3. **After analysis:** Results are posted. Whether significant or not, the study is published because journal committed to it.

**Optional:** Comparison showing traditional (hypothesis chosen after seeing data) vs. pre-registered (hypothesis locked before data).

**Educational Function:** Demonstrates how open science practices address p-hacking and publication bias. Makes transparency concrete.

## Notes on Figure Implementation

- **Photographs vs. Diagrams:** Milgram and Goodall photos are essential for emotional/historical impact. Graphs and diagrams should be clear and simple.
- **Accessibility:** All figures should include alt text describing the image and key findings.
- **Modularity:** Each figure stands alone but also connects to the surrounding text.
- **Scale:** Figures should be sized to emphasize key relationships (e.g., the 64-36 split in replication failures should be visually striking).
