**Figure 7.1: Neural pathways activation during thinking**

## Concept 1: Cognition, concepts, prototypes, schemas

**Figure 7.2: Concept hierarchy and prototype variation**

*Description*: Tree diagram with "living things" at the top. Branches to "animals" and "plants." "Animals" branches to "mammals" and "birds." "Mammals" branches to "dogs," "cats," "whales." The dog branch shows a photograph of a prototypical golden retriever, then photographs of progressively less prototype-matching dogs: German shepherd, Chihuahua, hairless Xoloitzcuintli. Text beside each: "high prototype match," "moderate match," "low match."

*Context*: Shows how prototypes work as a categorization shortcut. Most people recognize the Retriever immediately as a "dog." Recognition gets slower and more deliberate as appearance deviates from the prototype. This is accurate for most comparisons but creates bias when prototype does not match reality (e.g., penguins are birds but do not match bird prototype).

**Figure 7.3: Event schema visualization—The elevator script**

*Description*: Five-panel sequence showing standard elevator behavior: (1) Wait for doors to open, face outward; (2) Step in, turn around to face doors; (3) Look for floor button; (4) Press button, face doors; (5) Wait for arrival, face doors. Sixth panel shows person facing *back* of elevator, with other passengers looking uncomfortable/disturbed. Visual emphasizes that schema is automatic and powerful; violation creates discomfort even when no rule is broken.

*Context*: Demonstrates how schemas create automatic behavior that is difficult to override. Supports later discussion of texting-while-driving schema and how schemas can enable dangerous habits.

## Concept 2: Problem-solving and heuristics

**Figure 7.4: Algorithm vs. heuristic in action**

*Description*: Two columns. Left column (algorithm): flowchart with step-by-step decision tree for fixing a printer (restart? restart works? no → check ink; check works? no → check connection; etc.). Each step inevitable. Right column (heuristic): "Working backwards" visualization. Deadline at bottom (end of business day, 5 PM). Work backward: mail must arrive by 10 AM tomorrow → mail by 2 PM today → need printed copy by 1:30 PM → alternative: email, library printer, colleague's printer. Heuristic opens multiple solutions; algorithm follows single path.

*Context*: Shows the trade-off: algorithms guarantee correctness but are slow; heuristics are fast but require judgment. Most real-world problems use heuristics.

**Figure 7.5: Common cognitive biases illustrated with examples**

*Description*: Grid of four illustrations.

(1) **Anchoring bias**: Realtor showing run-down apartment for $1,600 (anchor), then nice apartment for $2,000. Text: "Without anchor, $2,000 feels expensive. With anchor, $2,000 feels like a deal."

(2) **Availability heuristic**: News headline about plane crash. Person worrying about flying. Text: "Vivid example makes flying feel risky, even though driving is statistically more dangerous."

(3) **Representativeness bias**: Quiet person with glasses doing crossword puzzle. Two options: "Librarian" vs. "Farmer." Text: "Fits librarian stereotype. But base rate: 5 million farmers vs. 160,000 librarians in U.S. More likely to be farmer."

(4) **Confirmation bias**: Person with negative thought bubble about professor ("Professor is unfair"). Same professor in two scenarios: (a) Professor makes sharp comment (remembered, confirms bias). (b) Professor stays late to help student (not remembered, contradicts bias). Text: "Notice what confirms belief; filter out what contradicts it."

*Context*: Makes each bias concrete with specific example. Helps student recognize these patterns in their own thinking.

## Concept 3: Intelligence theories

**Figure 7.6: Intelligence theory comparison matrix**

*Description*: Five-row table comparing Spearman, Cattell, Sternberg, Gardner, and CHC theories.

| Theory | Year | Structure | Strengths | Limitations |
|--------|------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Spearman (*g*)** | 1900s | 1 general factor | Simple; predictive | Misses genuine variation |
| **Cattell** | 1940s | Fluid + crystallized | Explains age differences | Only 2 dimensions |
| **Sternberg** | 1980s | Analytical + practical + creative | Real-world relevance | Difficult to measure creativity |
| **Gardner** | 1983 | 8+ intelligences | Inclusive; educational impact | Lacks empirical support; too broad |
| **CHC** | 2018 | Hierarchical (general → broad → narrow) | Integrates competing theories | Complex; unwieldy |

*Context*: Allows students to compare theories at a glance. Shows that no single theory is "right"—each captures different aspects of intelligence.

**Figure 7.7: Triarchic theory of intelligence in action**

*Description*: Three overlapping circles labeled "Analytical," "Practical," "Creative." Center (intersection) labeled "Effective problem-solving." Example scenario: "Project falling behind schedule."

- **Analytical**: Gather data, analyze bottlenecks, identify root cause
- **Practical**: Talk to team members, learn actual constraints, figure out what will work
- **Creative**: Notice that standard approach does not match actual team; propose innovative allocation

*Context*: Shows that real-world success requires all three types. A person high in one type might fail in another domain.

## Concept 4: Intelligence testing

**Figure 7.8: The bell curve and IQ distribution**

*Description*: Standard normal distribution centered at 100. X-axis: IQ score (55 to 145). Y-axis: Percentage of population. Shaded regions:

- Center: 85–115 (one SD from mean, 68% of population), labeled "Average intelligence"
- 70–85 and 115–130 (two SDs), labeled "Above/below average"
- Below 70: 2.2%, labeled "Intellectual disability (with adaptive functioning deficits)"
- Above 130: 2.2%, labeled "Gifted"

Additional note: "Standard deviation = 15 points. Percentile ranks shown on right axis."

*Context*: Students need to understand the bell curve to interpret IQ scores. Many students have heard "IQ of 130" without understanding what that means in population terms (top 2%).

**Figure 7.9: The Flynn effect over time**

*Description*: Line graph showing average IQ score (y-axis, 85–105) against year (x-axis, 1950–2020). Line slopes upward from ~95 in 1950 to ~102 in 2020, approximately 3 points per decade. Annotations:

- 1950: "Worse nutrition, less formal education, less exposure to abstract reasoning"
- 1980: "TV, video games introduce visual-abstract reasoning"
- 2000: "Internet, smartphones, global information access"
- 2020: "But effects plateau in wealthy nations"

*Context*: The Flynn effect is counterintuitive and important. It shows that IQ is not measuring some fixed capacity. It is measuring performance in a particular context at a particular time. What we measure as "intelligence" responds to environmental change.

**Figure 7.10: WISC-V subtest structure**

*Description*: Pyramid diagram.

- Base: 14 subtests (names: Similarities, Vocabulary, Block Design, Matrix Reasoning, Digit Span, Coding, Symbol Search, etc.)
- Middle tier: 5 indices
  - Verbal Comprehension (Similarities, Vocabulary, Information)
  - Visual Spatial (Block Design, Visual Puzzles)
  - Fluid Reasoning (Matrix Reasoning, Picture Concepts)
  - Working Memory (Digit Span, Letter-Number Sequencing)
  - Processing Speed (Coding, Symbol Search)
- Top: Full Scale IQ (composite of all indices)

*Context*: Shows that modern IQ testing is more nuanced than "one number." The sub-scores matter. A student might have average overall IQ but uneven profile (high in some areas, low in others).

## Concept 5: Nature and nurture

**Figure 7.11: Range of reaction illustrated**

*Description*: Line graph showing three people with different genetic potential (y-axis = IQ, 70–140). X-axis = environmental quality (poor environment → average environment → enriched environment).

- Person A (high genetic potential): line slopes from 85 in poor environment to 130 in enriched environment
- Person B (average genetic potential): line slopes from 75 to 115
- Person C (low genetic potential): line slopes from 65 to 100

Text explanation: "Genetic potential sets a range. Environment determines where within that range a person develops. Same environmental improvement (e.g., from poor to enriched) raises all three, but each reaches different absolute level."

*Context*: Addresses the nature-nurture question directly and visually. Shows that "heritability" does not mean "unchangeable." All three people benefit from enriched environment, but genetic differences persist.

**Figure 7.12: Socioeconomic status and prefrontal activation (Kishiyama et al.)**

*Description*: Two brain scans (fMRI) in same orientation. Left: Child from wealthy background, prefrontal cortex shows strong activation (warm colors). Right: Child from low-income background, prefrontal cortex shows reduced activation (cool colors). Caption: "Same task (executive function test); different brain activation due to chronic stress from poverty. The brain physically develops differently in high-stress environments."

*Context*: Makes concrete the mechanism by which poverty affects intelligence: not through motivation or effort, but through literal changes in brain structure and function caused by chronic stress.

**Figure 7.13: Flynn effect and socioeconomic gains**

*Description*: Two line graphs stacked. Top graph: Average IQ over time in developed nations (rising). Bottom graph: Income inequality, nutrition access, years of schooling completed, percentage of population exposed to abstract reasoning (visual media, education levels). All four factors rising in parallel with IQ.

*Context*: Supports the Flynn effect interpretation. IQ is not rising because people are genetically smarter. It is rising because environments are improving.

## Concept 6: Learning disabilities

**Figure 7.14: Dyslexia in action**

*Description*: Page of text as it appears to non-dyslexic reader: "The big dog ran home." Same text as it might appear to dyslexic reader: "The dig bod ran home" (letters transposed, reversals). Below: Brain activation map during reading. Dyslexic brain shows reduced activation in left hemisphere language areas (Broca's area, Wernicke's area) and unexpected activation in right hemisphere.

*Context*: Shows that dyslexia is neurological, not a reading-comprehension problem or low motivation. The letters are being processed incorrectly at the neural level.

**Figure 7.15: Learning disability vs. intellectual disability**

*Description*: Grid comparing two students.

**Student A (Dyslexia)**: 
- Reading: 2 grade levels below (difficulty with letters/sounds)
- Math: 2 grade levels above (strong)
- Reasoning: 1 grade level above (strong)
- Vocabulary: 1 grade level above (strong)
- Overall IQ: 115 (above average)
- *Profile*: Specific learning disability; global intelligence intact

**Student B (Intellectual Disability)**:
- Reading: 3 grade levels below
- Math: 3 grade levels below
- Reasoning: 3 grade levels below
- Vocabulary: 3 grade levels below
- Overall IQ: 65 (well below average)
- *Profile*: Global intellectual disability; struggle across all areas

*Context*: Clarifies the critical distinction. Student A needs accommodations and explicit support in reading. Student B needs curriculum at a different developmental level across all subjects. Same intervention would not work for both.

**Figure 7.16: Learning disability types and manifestations**

*Description*: Three columns.

**Dyslexia**:
- Image: Letters appearing transposed or reversed
- Manifestation: Slow reading, effortful decoding, poor spelling
- Brain region affected: Left temporal and parietal regions (letter-sound processing)
- Intervention: Explicit phonics instruction, multisensory approaches

**Dysgraphia**:
- Image: Chicken-scratch handwriting, words illegible
- Manifestation: Illegible writing, difficulty translating thought to paper, slow writing
- Brain region affected: Motor cortex, parietal regions (fine motor coordination, motor planning)
- Intervention: Occupational therapy, assistive technology (typing), extra time

**Dyscalculia**:
- Image: Numbers appearing jumbled or confused
- Manifestation: Cannot count without pointing, difficulty memorizing math facts, confusion about numerals
- Brain region affected: Intraparietal sulcus, other parietal regions (magnitude processing)
- Intervention: Manipulatives, concrete representations, visual approaches

*Context*: Helps students recognize different learning disabilities and understand that different interventions apply.

## Integration image

**Figure 7.17: The Linda problem revisited—How thinking machinery creates the conjunction fallacy**

*Description*: Infographic showing how the error happens.

Top: "Linda is 31, single, outspoken, bright, philosophy major, concerned with discrimination and social justice, antinuclear activist."

Four thought bubbles:

1. **Concept activation**: "Feminist" concept activates strongly; "bank teller" concept activates weakly
2. **Prototype matching**: "How well does Linda match prototype of feminist?" (very well). "How well does she match prototype of bank teller?" (poorly). Score: feminist much better.
3. **Heuristic judgment**: "Feminist bank teller" feels more coherent, more "real," fits better with her described personality
4. **Availability bias**: Vivid details make "feminist bank teller" more available in memory than abstract probability

Outcome: 85% choose "feminist bank teller" as more probable, despite math showing it should be less probable.

Caption: "The machinery that makes fast thinking possible is the same machinery that creates systematic errors. Understanding it does not free you from it, but lets you notice when you are using it."

*Context*: Brings together all the machinery of the chapter (concepts, schemas, prototypes, heuristics, biases) to explain a single compelling example. Shows integration of concepts.

## Summary figures

**Figure 7.18: Chapter at a glance—The four types of thinking**

*Description*: Four quadrants.

1. **Fast, automatic**: Concepts, prototypes, schemas, intuitive heuristics
   - Speed: Fast
   - Accuracy: Usually good, systematic errors possible
   - Effort: Low
   - Examples: Recognizing dog, categorizing person as leader, judging probability of Linda problem

2. **Slow, deliberate**: Algorithms, careful reasoning, checking against data
   - Speed: Slow
   - Accuracy: High (if you do it correctly)
   - Effort: High
   - Examples: Long division, debugging code, statistical analysis

3. **Heuristic shortcuts**: Quick rules of thumb
   - Speed: Fast
   - Accuracy: Usually good, fail predictably in specific conditions
   - Effort: Low
   - Examples: Working backward, anchoring, availability bias

4. **Expert intuition**: Fast automatic thinking based on deep knowledge
   - Speed: Fast
   - Accuracy: High (in domain of expertise)
   - Effort: Appears effortless but built on thousands of hours of practice
   - Examples: Chess grandmaster seeing winning move instantly, experienced doctor diagnosing from subtle signs

*Context*: Helps students understand that "intuition" is not monolithic. Some intuitions are reliable, others are biased. The difference is often expertise and domain knowledge.

## Alternative/supplementary images (if space permits)

**Figure 7.A: Timeline of intelligence theory**

Horizontal timeline from 1900 to 2020. At each major point, the theory, theorist, and key contribution.

1900: Spearman → *g*
1940: Cattell → Fluid/crystallized split
1980s: Sternberg → Triarchic theory
1983: Gardner → Multiple intelligences
2018: CHC → Hierarchical integration

**Figure 7.B: IQ test development timeline**

1905: Binet develops first test for French government
1916: Terman develops Stanford-Binet (IQ = mental age/chronological age × 100)
1939: Wechsler develops Wechsler-Bellevue combining verbal and nonverbal subtests
1950s onward: WAIS, WISC, WPPSI versions; periodic renorming
1970s-1980s: Flynn effect documented; cultural bias cases (*Larry P. v. Riles*)
2000s onward: Neuroimaging shows brain basis of IQ components

**Figure 7.C: The three routes to intelligence assessment**

1. **IQ testing** (standardized, time-consuming, trained administrator needed, measurable/comparable)
2. **Portfolio assessment** (real-world work samples, more authentic, harder to compare)
3. **Behavioral observation** (in natural contexts, less biased by test anxiety, time-intensive)

Real assessment often uses all three.
