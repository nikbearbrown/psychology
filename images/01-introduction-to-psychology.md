# Image briefs for Chapter 1: Introduction to Psychology

## Figure placeholders in chapter

### Figure 1 — Wundt's Leipzig laboratory (cold open context)
**Description:** A photograph or illustration of Wundt's psychology laboratory at the University of Leipzig, circa 1879. The space is modest — wooden tables with brass instruments, reaction-time apparatus, a tachistoscope (a device for presenting stimuli briefly), and other measuring equipment. One or two experimenters are visible working with apparatus while a subject sits in an isolated chamber.

**What students should notice:**
- This is a working laboratory, not a grand institution. Psychology's birth was modest.
- The instruments are mechanical and analog, yet capable of measuring mental phenomena to extreme precision (thousandths of a second).
- The isolation of the subject from the experimenter shows deliberate experimental control.
- The apparatus themselves embody the belief that mental phenomena can be measured like physical phenomena.

**Why it matters:** This image grounds the abstract claim that "psychology became a science in 1879" in concrete reality. It shows what scientific psychology actually looked like at its birth.

### Figure 2 — Historical timeline of psychology (Concept 2 context)
**Description:** A horizontal timeline from 1879 to present, with labeled periods and key figures/schools:

- 1879: Wundt opens Leipzig lab
- 1890s: William James (Functionalism), Mary Whiton Calkins
- 1920s: John B. Watson (Behaviorism), B.F. Skinner (Operant conditioning)
- 1930s-1950s: Sigmund Freud's influence (Psychoanalysis), Carl Rogers (Humanism), Abraham Maslow
- 1950s-1960s: Cognitive Revolution (Noam Chomsky, Ulric Neisser), Gestalt psychology
- 1960s-present: Integration of perspectives, Feminist psychology, Multicultural psychology, Neuroscience

**What students should notice:**
- The field has moved from introspection alone to measurement to behavior to mental processes and back again — not in circles, but in spirals that integrate previous insights.
- Behavioral and cognitive approaches overlap in time; they were competing frameworks, not sequential.
- Feminist and multicultural psychology emerged later, as the field recognized its biases.
- No single perspective dominates; modern psychology is eclectic.

**Why it matters:** It visualizes the narrative arc and shows that psychology's history is not a march toward a final truth, but a conversation between perspectives.

### Figure 3 — Contemporary subfields of psychology (Concept 3 context)
**Description:** A radial or circular diagram with "Psychology" at the center and branches radiating outward to major subfields. Each branch is labeled and color-coded:

Central hub: "Psychology — Study of Mind and Behavior"

Branches (radiating):
- Biopsychology (includes neuroscience, biological systems)
- Cognitive Psychology (memory, thinking, perception, language)
- Developmental Psychology (lifespan development)
- Social Psychology (relationships, groups, culture)
- Clinical Psychology (mental illness and treatment)
- Industrial-Organizational Psychology (workplace)
- Health Psychology (behavior and physical health)
- Forensic Psychology (justice system)
- Sport Psychology (athletic performance)
- Evolutionary Psychology (evolutionary origins)

**What students should notice:**
- Psychology is radial, not hierarchical. No subfield is more central than others.
- Some subfields (cognitive, social) seem to overlap in their interests. The diagram should show that overlap.
- The diversity of subfields all share the same core methods (scientific study of behavior and mind).
- Applied subfields (clinical, I-O) exist alongside research subfields (cognitive, developmental).

**Why it matters:** It shows that psychology is not monolithic. There is no single "psychology"; there are multiple psychologies unified by a common method.

### Figure 4 — Contrast: Psychiatry, Psychology, Neuroscience (Integration context)
**Description:** Three overlapping circles (Venn diagram), each labeled with a discipline:

**Psychiatry:**
- MD degree (medical school)
- Can prescribe medication
- Treats mental illness
- Biological focus
- Works in clinical settings

**Psychology:**
- PhD or PsyD degree
- Cannot prescribe (in most states)
- Studies mind and behavior broadly
- Treatments include therapy, behavioral intervention
- Works in research labs, clinics, organizations

**Neuroscience:**
- PhD in neuroscience or related field
- Studies the brain and nervous system at cellular and circuit levels
- Uses neurotechnology (fMRI, EEG, microscopy)
- Focus on neural mechanisms
- Works in research labs, hospitals

**Overlaps:**
- Psychiatry/Psychology: Both treat mental illness; psychiatry has biological focus, psychology has behavioral/psychological focus
- Psychology/Neuroscience: Both study brain and behavior; neuroscience focuses on neural mechanisms, psychology on behavioral outcomes
- Psychiatry/Neuroscience: Both have medical/biological training; psychiatry applies neuroscience to treatment

**What students should notice:**
- These are distinct but overlapping disciplines.
- A person being treated for depression might see a psychiatrist (who prescribes medication), a psychologist (who provides therapy), and have brain imaging done by a neuroscientist (to understand mechanism).
- All three contribute to understanding mental illness.

**Why it matters:** Students often confuse these disciplines. This diagram clarifies the distinctions and the overlaps, and shows that effective treatment often requires all three perspectives.

## Figure notes for instructor

**Re: Wundt's laboratory:**
If using a photograph, be sure it's historically accurate (the original laboratory was destroyed in WWII, but photographs and diagrams exist). Illustrations work well too. The key is to show that early scientific psychology was humble in scale but ambitious in scope.

**Re: Historical timeline:**
A visual timeline is more effective than a text list. Consider adding representative quotes from each era to show how the questions changed:
- Wundt: "The mind has structure; we can measure it."
- Watson: "Forget the mind; study behavior."
- Skinner: "Behavior is shaped by consequences."
- Cognitive psychologists: "The mind is back; it's like a computer."

**Re: Subfields diagram:**
This diagram can be updated each time a major subfield emerges or gains prominence. Keep it current.

**Re: Discipline contrast:**
The Venn diagram is useful in lectures when students ask, "Is my psychiatrist a psychologist?" or "Do neuroscientists do brain surgery?" It clarifies the distinctions.
