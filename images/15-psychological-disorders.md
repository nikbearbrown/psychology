**Description**: Visual representation of the four diagnostic criteria for psychological disorder with examples and counterexamples.

**Content**:

### Figure 3: Anxiety disorder spectrum — From normal to pathological

**Description**: Continuum showing anxiety level (x-axis) and functional impairment (y-axis). 

**Content**:
- Region 1 (green): normal anxiety — person studies for exam, prepares presentation, maintains relationships despite mild worry
- Region 2 (yellow): subclinical anxiety — worry noticeable but does not prevent functioning
- Region 3 (red): anxiety disorder — excessive anxiety causes avoidance and significant impairment

**Examples placed on continuum**:
- Social anxiety: normal shyness → social anxiety disorder
- Fear: normal caution with heights → specific phobia of heights
- Worry: normal concern about health → generalized anxiety disorder with constant uncontrollable worry

### Figure 4: The Diathesis-Stress Model in Depression

**Description**: 3D or overlapping diagram showing how genetic diathesis (vulnerability) and environmental stress interact.

**Content**:
- X-axis: Stress exposure (from low to high)
- Y-axis: Genetic vulnerability (from low to high)
- Z-axis or color gradient: Risk of depression

**Interpretation zones**:
- High diathesis + high stress = very high depression risk
- High diathesis + low stress = manageable (no depression)
- Low diathesis + high stress = some risk (but less than high diathesis group)
- Low diathesis + low stress = very low risk

**Gene example**: 5-HTTLPR short vs. long versions under varying stress.

**Placeholder text**:
```
[FIGURE 4: 3D surface plot or contour map showing diathesis-stress interaction.
X-axis: Life stress level (low to high)
Y-axis: Genetic vulnerability for depression (e.g., short 5-HTTLPR alleles, low to high)
Z-axis/color: Depression risk (blue/low to red/high)
Shows that high-risk genotype only manifests depression when combined with stress;
high stress can trigger depression even in those with lower genetic risk.]
```

### Figure 5: Anxiety conditioning in PTSD and Phobia

**Description**: Flow chart showing classical conditioning mechanism and persistence through avoidance.

**Acquisition phase**:
- Unconditioned Stimulus (trauma, dog bite) → Unconditioned Response (terror, fear)
- Conditioned Stimulus (reminder: sound of helicopter, presence of dog) paired with UCS
- Conditioned Stimulus → Conditioned Response (fear response to reminder)

**Maintenance phase**:
- Avoidance prevents extinction (never encounters CS without severe consequence; never learns CS is now safe)
- Safety behaviors (ritualistic checking in PTSD, avoidance in phobia) maintain the fear

**Placeholder text**:
```
[FIGURE 5: Two-panel flow chart.
Left panel (Acquisition): 
UCS (trauma) + CS (environmental cue) → UCR/CR (fear)
Over repetitions: CS alone → CR (fear conditioned)

Right panel (Maintenance):
Person avoids CS, preventing extinction learning.
New threatening interpretation occurs → avoidance reinforced.
Shows why exposure therapy works: it breaks the avoidance cycle and allows extinction to occur.]
```

### Figure 6: Positive and negative symptoms in schizophrenia

**Description**: Two-column comparison with concrete examples and neurobiological basis.

**Positive Symptoms** (additions to normal experience):
- Hallucinations (auditory: "voices," visual: "seeing things")
- Delusions (false beliefs: persecution, reference, grandeur)
- Disorganized speech (incoherence, tangential)
- Disorganized behavior (bizarre, purposeless)
- Associated with: excess dopamine in mesolimbic pathways

**Negative Symptoms** (reductions in normal functioning):
- Alogia (poverty of speech)
- Avolition (loss of motivation)
- Affective flattening (reduced emotional expression)
- Anhedonia (loss of pleasure)
- Associated with: deficient dopamine in prefrontal cortex

**Placeholder text**:
```
[FIGURE 6: Two-column table with icons/images.
Left: Positive symptoms with speech bubbles, visual distortions, disorganized arrows.
Annotation: "Excess dopamine in mesolimbic reward pathways"
Right: Negative symptoms with faded images, reduced color, flattened expression.
Annotation: "Deficient dopamine in prefrontal cortex"
Bottom: Note that individual may experience both positive and negative symptoms.]
```

### Figure 7: Depression across presentations

**Description**: Grid showing three different presentations of major depressive disorder, each meeting full criteria but expressing differently.

**Presentation A: Classic depressive mood**:
- Describes sadness, hopelessness, tearfulness
- Loss of interest (anhedonia) in hobbies, social contact
- Sleep disturbance, appetite loss
- Guilt, worthlessness

**Presentation B: "Depression without sadness"**:
- Reports emotional numbness, "I feel nothing"
- Anhedonia (core symptom) prominent
- Fatigue, slowness (psychomotor retardation)
- Concentration difficulty, indecisiveness
- May not mention sadness

**Presentation C: Somatic presentation**:
- Dominant complaint: pain, fatigue, physical symptoms
- Patient may not frame as mood disorder
- Loss of appetite and sleep (same as A, but experienced as physical)
- Anhedonia present but attributed to pain/fatigue
- Mood symptoms minimized in self-report

**Caption**: All three meet MDD criteria; depression presents heterogeneously.

**Placeholder text**:
```
[FIGURE 7: Three clinical vignettes in boxes, side-by-side.
Person A: "I feel so sad and hopeless. Nothing interests me anymore."
Person B: "I feel numb. Nothing brings me joy. I'm exhausted."
Person C: "My body aches. I'm too tired to do anything. Food tastes like nothing."
Below each: symptoms checklist showing all three meet full MDD criteria despite different presentation.
Title: "Depression is not just sadness; it varies across individuals and contexts."]
```

### Figure 8: Myths vs. Reality

**Description**: Side-by-side visual refutation of four major myths about mental illness.

**Myth 1: Schizophrenia = Split Personality**
- Myth image: Single person splitting into two
- Reality: One mind with fragmented thought processes
- Image of fMRI showing disorganized neural activity

**Myth 2: People with mental illness are dangerous**
- Myth: threatening image, gun, crime scene
- Reality: Bar chart showing mental illness victims > perpetrators (11:1)

**Myth 3: Depression = Sadness**
- Myth: Tear drop, sad face
- Reality: Spectrum showing anhedonia, numbness, fatigue as core features

**Myth 4: Eating disorders = Choice/Vanity**
- Myth: Mirror, person obsessing
- Reality: Brain imaging, genetic/neurobiological dysregulation, medical complications
