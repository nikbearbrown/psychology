### Figure 1: Piaget's Conservation Task (Concept 3 opening)

**Reference:** Not yet rendered. Should show:
- Two identical glasses, both filled with equal amounts of water. Child says "same."
- Water from one glass poured into a third, shorter glass.
- Child now looks at the two short glasses and indicates the "fuller-looking" one has more water.
- Caption: *A five-year-old who has not yet developed conservation of volume believes that the shorter glass contains more water, even though the same amount was poured. She is not wrong about what she sees; she is limited by the logic available to her.*

**Text anchor:** "Watch the same child look at the short glass. She is very clear: the short glass on the left has more water, because it's fuller-looking."

### Figure 3: Three Attachment Styles in Reunion (Illustration)

**Reference:** Not yet rendered. Should show:
- Three panels, each depicting a mother and infant during reunion (episode 5 or 8 of Strange Situation):
  - **Secure:** Infant approaches mother, seeks proximity, is comforted, returns to play.
  - **Anxious-resistant:** Infant seeks mother but also resists contact (arching away, hitting), ambivalent.
  - **Avoidant:** Infant ignores mother, looks away, moves toward toy or door.
- Short labels and behavioral descriptions under each.
- Caption: *The three primary attachment styles are evident in how an infant responds to reunion with the mother after separation. Secure infants use the mother as a base and are comforted. Anxious-resistant infants are conflicted. Avoidant infants show little affective response.*

**Text anchor:** "From this pattern, Ainsworth identified three attachment styles..."

## Notes on figure rendering

1. **Conservation task (Figure 1)** — should be photo-realistic or clearly illustrated (not cartoon). The child's face should show concentration, not distress.

2. **Strange Situation (Figure 2)** — can be illustrated as simple stick figures or semi-realistic. Behavioral codes (e.g., "cries at separation," "seeks contact at reunion") should be clearly labeled at each episode.

3. **Attachment styles (Figure 3)** — three side-by-side panels. Mother drawn the same in all three; infant posture differs. This visual comparison makes the distinction clear.

4. **Stages timeline (Figure 4)** — a simple horizontal timeline or stacked timeline format, with ages and stage names prominent. Include one example behavior per stage to anchor it.

5. **Assimilation/accommodation (Figure 5)** — the progression should be clear: existing schema → new input → accommodation. Arrows and labels essential.

6. **Pathways diagram (Figure 6)** — a flow chart or network diagram, with self-control as the central source node and three outcome clusters as targets. Color coding (one color per pathway) helps.

7. **Cultural motor development (Figure 7)** — comparative bar chart or timeline. The key insight is the delayed start but accelerated development later. Visual should emphasize the crossover (Aché surpass Western peers by age 9).
