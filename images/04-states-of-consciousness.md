## Section 2: Sleep Stages and Brain Waves

**[FIGURE: EEG Patterns Across Sleep Stages]**
A vertical stacked-trace display showing five rows, each a 10-second EEG segment:

1. **Wakefulness (Beta waves)**: Low amplitude, high frequency (13–30 Hz), highly variable
2. **Stage 1 (N1, Alpha and Theta waves)**: Slightly higher amplitude, lower frequency (4–12 Hz), more synchronized than wake
3. **Stage 2 (N2, Theta with Sleep Spindles and K-complexes)**: Dominant theta activity with brief bursts (spindles) at 12–15 Hz and sharp K-complex peaks
4. **Stage 3 (N3, Delta waves)**: High amplitude, very low frequency (less than 3 Hz), regular rhythm
5. **REM Sleep**: Low amplitude, fast frequency (similar to wakefulness), with rapid eye movement artifact visible as eye-movement signals

Vertical calibration bars indicate amplitude (50 μV). Annotation: "Each stage shows distinctive electrical signatures."

Annotation: "Sleep spindles occur in stage 2 and correlate with memory consolidation."

**[FIGURE: Delta Waves in NREM Stage 3 (Slow-Wave Sleep)]**

## Section 3: Why We Sleep—Mechanisms

**[FIGURE: Memory Consolidation During Sleep]**
A three-panel diagram:

Panel 1 (Wakefulness): Brain with highlighted hippocampus and motor cortex. Arrow labeled "Learning" shows new synaptic connections forming.

Panel 2 (NREM Stage 2): Brain with hippocampus active. Arrow labeled "Replay" shows the learned sequence replaying from hippocampus to motor cortex.

Panel 3 (Next Day): Motor cortex alone is active (hippocampus quiet). Arrow labeled "Retrieval" shows the memory now stored in cortex, no longer dependent on hippocampus.

Annotation: "Sleep consolidates memories by replaying them, gradually shifting storage from hippocampus to cortex."

**[FIGURE: Growth Hormone Secretion Across Sleep and Wake]**
A line graph showing blood growth hormone concentration (y-axis, 0–10 ng/mL) across a 24-hour period (x-axis, wake hours and sleep hours).
- Low during the day while awake
- Sharp spike during the first hour of stage 3 sleep
- Return to baseline as sleep progresses
- Remains low throughout the waking day

Annotation: "Growth hormone is secreted primarily during deep sleep (stage 3), supporting physical growth and restoration."

## Section 4: Sleep Disorders

**[FIGURE: Sleep Apnea: Oxygen Drops and Arousals]**
A multi-channel recording over 2 minutes showing:
- Top channel (respiratory flow): Normal breath, breath, breath, then a 20-second apnea (flat line), then resumption.
- Second channel (oxygen saturation, SpO₂): Normal 95–98%, drops to 80% during apnea, recovers as breathing resumes.
- Third channel (heart rate): Normal 60 bpm, climbs to 85 bpm as oxygen drops, returns to baseline.
- Bottom channel (EEG): Normal sleep, brief arousal/alert signal during the apnea and recovery.

Annotation: "Obstructive sleep apnea causes repeated oxygen drops and micro-arousals, disrupting sleep quality."

**[FIGURE: Narcolepsy: REM Sleep Intrusion During Wakefulness]**
A two-row EEG comparison:

Top row (Normal night sleep, entering REM): Shows the normal progression—N2 sleep waves, then rapid transition to REM (fast waves, REM artifact visible).

Bottom row (Narcolepsy patient during daytime): Shows sudden transition from wakefulness (fast, low-amplitude waves) directly to REM-like activity (rapid eye movements visible, loss of muscle tone) within seconds. No gradual descent through N1, N2, N3.

Annotation: "In narcolepsy, REM sleep can intrude directly into wakefulness, causing sudden sleep attacks and cataplexy."

## Section 5: Altered States—Drugs, Hypnosis, Meditation

**[FIGURE: Dopamine Transmission in Normal State vs. With Stimulant]**
Two synaptic diagrams side by side:

Left (Normal): Presynaptic neuron releases dopamine. Dopamine binds to receptors on postsynaptic neuron. Dopamine reuptake transporter removes dopamine from synapse, recycling it back to presynaptic neuron.

Right (With Cocaine): Cocaine blocks the reuptake transporter (shown as a lock). Dopamine accumulates in the synapse. Prolonged, excess stimulation of postsynaptic receptors.

Annotation: "Stimulants block dopamine reuptake, causing dopamine to accumulate and overstimulate postsynaptic receptors."

---

**[FIGURE: Brain Activity During Hypnosis vs. Normal Wakefulness]**
Two brain images (lateral view, left hemisphere):

Left image (Normal wakefulness): Multiple regions of activation (prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate, parietal cortex, visual cortex).

Right image (Hypnotic state): Highlighted regions show increased activity in anterior cingulate and narrow focus; decreased activity in lateral prefrontal cortex (shown in lighter color).

Annotation: "Hypnosis shows reduced prefrontal activity (less critical evaluation) and increased anterior cingulate activity (narrowed focus)."

---

**[FIGURE: Brain Connectivity in Experienced Meditators vs. Control]**
Two functional connectivity maps (resting-state fMRI):

Top (Control, non-meditator): Default-mode network (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate) shows strong connectivity within itself. Task-positive network (dorsolateral prefrontal, parietal cortex) shows separate connectivity.

Bottom (Experienced meditator): Decreased within-network connectivity in default-mode network. Increased connectivity between default-mode and task-positive networks.

Annotation: "Meditation training reduces default-mode-network activity and increases cross-network connectivity, reducing mind-wandering."

---

**[FIGURE: Neurotransmitter Changes Across Sleep and Wake States]**
A stacked bar chart showing relative activity levels of four neurotransmitter systems (y-axis) across sleep stages (x-axis: Wake, N1, N2, N3, REM).

- **Acetylcholine** (green): High in wake, dips in early sleep, rises again in REM.
- **Norepinephrine** (red): High in wake, low in all sleep stages.
- **Serotonin** (blue): High in wake, low in all sleep stages, especially REM.
- **Dopamine** (yellow): Moderate in wake, lower in sleep, rises in REM.

Annotation: "Different neurotransmitter systems dominate different states. REM's high acetylcholine and low norepinephrine/serotonin produce vivid, illogical dreams."

## Summary Figures

**[FIGURE: Consciousness as a Spectrum]**
A horizontal spectrum from left (fully conscious, alert) to right (deeply unconscious). Points along the spectrum labeled:
- Full wakefulness (alert, aware)
- Light sleep / daydreaming
- NREM stage 2
- NREM stage 3 (deep sleep)
- REM sleep (paradoxical—brain active, body paralyzed)

**[FIGURE: The Neurotransmitter Basis of Addiction]**
A cycle diagram showing:
1. Drug use → dopamine surge → reward
2. Reward → learning/reinforcement → cravings
3. Cravings → tolerance (receptors downregulate, reuptake increases) → need higher dose
4. Higher dose → physical dependence → withdrawal upon cessation
5. Withdrawal discomfort → relapse → restart cycle

Annotation: "Addiction involves reward reinforcement, tolerance, and withdrawal—all rooted in neurotransmitter adaptation."
