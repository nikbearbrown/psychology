# Chapter 14 Images and Figures

## Figure 1: The Fight-or-Flight Response

**Location:** Concept 1 (opening the concept)

**Description:** Diagram showing the sympathetic nervous system response to threat. On the left, a figure confronted by a bear. On the right, a list of physiological changes with arrows:
- Heart rate increases
- Pupils dilate
- Breathing quickens
- Digestion slows
- Blood vessels in limbs dilate (redirecting blood from gut to muscles)
- Muscles tense, ready for action
- Adrenaline and noradrenaline released from adrenal glands

**Caption:** The sympathetic nervous system prepares the body for immediate action when faced with threat. This system works within seconds. The physiological changes are rapid and coordinated across multiple organ systems.

**Teaching note:** This figure establishes the normal, healthy functioning of the stress response. Students should see this as adaptive — the machinery works correctly. The problem emerges only when activation is sustained.

## Figure 2: The HPA Axis — A Slower Parallel Response

**Location:** Concept 1 (machinery section)

**Description:** Flowchart showing the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis:
- Brain (hypothalamus) senses threat → releases corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF)
- CRF travels to pituitary gland → pituitary releases ACTH
- ACTH travels in bloodstream to adrenal glands → adrenal glands release cortisol
- Cortisol circulates throughout body, affecting multiple systems

Timeline annotation: sympathetic response (seconds), HPA response (minutes to hours)

**Caption:** The HPA axis is the endocrine system's response to stress. It works more slowly than the sympathetic nervous system but sustains elevated stress hormone levels longer. Chronic elevation of cortisol damages immune function, cardiovascular health, and brain regions involved in memory.

**Teaching note:** Emphasize parallel systems and timing. Students often confuse "slow" with "weak." The HPA axis, though slower to activate, has powerful sustained effects.

## Figure 3: Selye's General Adaptation Syndrome

**Location:** Concept 1 (progression section)

**Description:** A graph with two axes:
- X-axis: Time (hours/days/weeks)
- Y-axis: Stress hormone level / Physiological activation

The curve shows three distinct stages:
1. **Alarm stage:** Sharp spike upward when stressor first encountered
2. **Resistance stage:** Curve descends but plateaus at elevated baseline; body has adapted but remains mobilized
3. **Exhaustion stage:** Curve declines further as resources deplete; physiological systems begin to fail

Annotations:
- "Useful adaptation" pointing to early resistance
- "Cumulative tissue damage begins" pointing to late resistance
- "Disease vulnerability increases" pointing to exhaustion stage

**Caption:** Selye observed that the body's response to stress unfolds in three stages. The alarm stage is adaptive. The resistance stage shows adaptation. But if stress continues into the exhaustion stage, the body's ability to maintain elevated activation fails, and health consequences emerge.

**Teaching note:** This figure explains why *duration* matters. Acute stress that resolves in the alarm or early resistance stage is largely harmless. Stress that persists into exhaustion causes damage.

## Figure 4: Stress Appraisal — Same Event, Different Stress

**Location:** Concept 1 (appraisal section, worked example)

**Description:** A two-panel diagram showing the same stressor (job loss notice) processed through two different appraisal paths:

**Left panel — Threat Appraisal:**
- Event: Job loss notice
- Primary appraisal: "This is a threat to my security"
- Secondary appraisal: "I have no alternatives; I will lose my home"
- Result: Intense stress, anxiety, avoidance

**Right panel — Challenge Appraisal:**
- Same event: Job loss notice
- Primary appraisal: "This is challenging, but I have handled change before"
- Secondary appraisal: "I have skills; I have savings; I can explore new directions"
- Result: Moderate stress, motivation to act

**Caption:** The same objective event produces different stress responses depending on how it is appraised. Appraisal is the cognitive process that determines whether an event triggers a threat response or a challenge response.

**Teaching note:** This figure is crucial for showing that stress is not simply determined by external events. Cognition — how we interpret events — shapes stress response. This is one of the most actionable insights for stress management.

## Figure 5: How Chronic Stress Suppresses Immune Function

**Location:** Concept 2 (immune system section)

**Description:** A flowchart showing the pathway from chronic stress to immune suppression:

- Chronic stressor (box at top)
- ↓ Activates sympathetic nervous system and HPA axis (multiple arrows)
- ↓ Elevated adrenaline and cortisol
- ↓ Multiple effects:
  - Sympathetic nerves directly reduce thymus and lymph node function
  - Cortisol inhibits lymphocyte production
  - Cortisol increases inflammatory markers (IL-6, TNF-α)
- Result: Weakened immune response
- Outcome: Increased vulnerability to infection, slower wound healing, increased cancer risk, autoimmune flares

**Caption:** Chronic stress weakens immunity through multiple mechanisms. The sympathetic nervous system directly innervates immune organs; stress hormones (particularly cortisol) suppress white blood cell production and increase inflammation. The result is increased susceptibility to infections and diseases.

**Teaching note:** Emphasize that this is not "all in your head." The mechanism is physiological and measurable. This is why people getting sick during stressful periods (exams, grief, job loss) is not coincidence.

## Figure 6: Hostility, Social Conflict, and Heart Disease

**Location:** Concept 2 (cardiovascular section)

**Description:** A cycle diagram showing the transactional relationship between hostility and cardiovascular risk:

Hostility (cynicism, distrust) → Interprets others as threatening → Responds defensively/aggressively → Triggers hostile reactions from others → Increased social strain and conflict → Hostile mood intensifies → Cardiovascular reactivity increases (elevated blood pressure, inflammation, atherosclerosis risk) → Back to heightened hostility

The cycle is labeled with annotations:
- "Behavioral amplification" on the social interaction part
- "Physiological damage" on the cardiovascular reactivity part

**Caption:** Hostile individuals create a self-perpetuating cycle. Their suspicion and defensive behavior provoke antagonistic responses from others, increasing their stress and hostile mood. This cycle amplifies both psychological distress and physiological cardiovascular risk.

**Teaching note:** This illustrates that personality does not directly cause heart disease, but rather creates a social and physiological environment that damages health. It is not the speed and ambition of Type A; it is the hostility component and the social conflict it generates.

## Figure 7: Perceived Control and Stress Outcome

**Location:** Concept 3 (control section)

**Description:** A 2x2 matrix:

|  | **Stressor is Controllable** | **Stressor is Uncontrollable** |
|---|---|---|
| **High Perceived Control** | Best outcome: proactive coping, stress is manageable, health is protected | Good outcome: emotion-focused coping, acceptance, lower reactivity |
| **Low Perceived Control** | Poor outcome: learned helplessness, passivity, depression emerges, health declines | Worst outcome: despair, exhaustion, severe health consequences |

Annotations in each cell show typical coping approach and health trajectory.

**Caption:** Perceived control shapes stress outcomes. A stressor that is actually controllable but perceived as uncontrollable produces worse outcomes than a genuinely uncontrollable stressor that is accurately perceived. Likewise, high perceived control protects health even in high-demand situations.

**Teaching note:** This clarifies why the Whitehall studies of British civil servants found that low-status, low-control jobs were more damaging than high-demand jobs. It is not the demand; it is the demand combined with lack of control.

## Figure 8: Social Support as a Buffer

**Location:** Concept 3 (social support section)

**Description:** A line graph showing stress level over time in two conditions:

- X-axis: Time (weeks during a major stressor)
- Y-axis: Reported stress level (1-10 scale)

Two lines:
1. **With adequate social support** (lower line): Stress rises with stressor onset, peaks at 6–7, then gradually declines as support helps processing and recovery
2. **Without adequate social support** (upper line): Stress rises similarly, peaks higher (8–9), plateaus at elevated level, takes much longer to decline

The lines diverge most noticeably in the recovery phase (weeks 6–12), showing that social support accelerates recovery from stress.

**Caption:** Social support buffers the effects of stress. People with adequate support not only experience lower peak stress but also recover more quickly. The protective effect is both immediate (emotional validation, practical help) and longer-term (faster psychological adjustment).

**Teaching note:** This figure shows support is not just comforting; it has measurable effects on stress trajectory. Isolation extends stress duration; connection accelerates recovery.

## Figure 9: Coping Strategy by Stressor Type

**Location:** Concept 3 (coping section)

**Description:** A decision tree diagram:

```
Stressor encountered
        ↓
    Is this controllable?
    /                \
  YES               NO
   ↓                ↓
Problem-focused   Emotion-focused
coping:           coping:
- Identify problem - Accept reality
- Brainstorm      - Find meaning
  solutions       - Adjust expectations
- Take action     - Seek emotional support
- Address root    - Reappraise situation
  cause           - Manage emotions
   ↓                ↓
Most effective   Most effective
strategy         strategy
```

**Caption:** Matching coping strategy to stressor type is crucial. Problem-focused coping works best for controllable stressors (take action). Emotion-focused coping works best for uncontrollable stressors (you cannot change the event, so managing emotion and finding meaning are most effective).

**Teaching note:** A common mistake is using emotion-focused coping for a controllable problem (avoiding rather than addressing), or using problem-focused coping for an uncontrollable loss (trying to "fix" what cannot be fixed). Flexibility and accurate assessment of controllability are key.

## Figure 10: Exercise as a Stress Buffer

**Location:** Concept 3 (exercise section)

**Description:** A comparison of two groups over time:

Left column: **Chronically stressed individuals who exercise regularly**
- Cortisol levels: elevated but controlled
- Immune markers: maintained near normal
- Telomere length: stable
- Cardiovascular markers: protected
- Mood: improved despite stressor

Right column: **Chronically stressed individuals who do not exercise**
- Cortisol levels: very elevated
- Immune markers: suppressed
- Telomere length: shortened (accelerated aging)
- Cardiovascular markers: degraded
- Mood: poor

Annotations show:
- "Exercise reduces stress reactivity"
- "Exercise enhances recovery"
- "Physical fitness appears protective even under chronic stress"

**Caption:** Physical exercise buffers stress effects across multiple biological systems. Exercising individuals maintain better immune function, more stable cortisol levels, longer telomeres, and better mood despite equivalent stress exposure.

**Teaching note:** This is one of the most action-oriented findings in stress research. Unlike some protective factors (social support, control, personality traits) that are harder to change quickly, exercise is a behavior anyone can start this week.

## Usage Notes

All figures use the following conventions:
- Color coding where relevant (e.g., green for protective factors, red for risk)
- Clear captions that stand alone (student should understand the figure's point from the caption)
- Annotations that explain the "why" of the mechanism, not just the "what"
- Realistic scenarios and data where possible (Whitehall studies, Alameda County study, etc.)

Figures are designed for both understanding the concept and for quick review during study. Each can be simplified (for initial learning) or elaborated (for advanced students exploring mechanisms).
