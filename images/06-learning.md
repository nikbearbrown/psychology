## Figure 6.1 — Classical Conditioning Sequence

**Reference in chapter:** Concept 1, after "Pavlov's experiments with dogs."

**Visual representation:**

```
BEFORE CONDITIONING:
Meat powder (UCS) ──→ Salivation (UCR)
Bell (NS) ────→ No response

DURING CONDITIONING:
Bell (NS) + Meat powder (UCS) ──→ Salivation (UCR)
[Repeated pairings]

AFTER CONDITIONING:
Bell (CS) ──→ Salivation (CR)
```

**What students should see:**
- The unconditioned reflex (UCS → UCR) that exists before learning
- The neutral stimulus that initially produces no response
- The pairing process (NS + UCS together)
- The result: the former neutral stimulus now produces the response (CS → CR)
- The response (CR) is the same as the original (UCR), but the trigger (CS) is new

**Key point to highlight:** The response itself hasn't changed. Salivation is salivation. What changed is what triggers it.

**What students should see:**
- Acquisition: the CR gets stronger with repeated CS-UCS pairings
- Extinction: the CR weakens when the CS is presented without the UCS

- Spontaneous recovery: after a rest period, the CR reappears briefly
- The recovered response is typically weaker than it was before extinction

**Key point to highlight:** Extinction is not erasure. The learning persists; it's just suppressed by newer learning.

## Figure 6.5 — The Bobo Doll Experiment Sequence

**Reference in chapter:** Concept 3, when describing the Bandura experiment.

**Visual representation:**

```
1. MODEL OBSERVES
   [Adult hitting, kicking, punching Bobo doll]
         |
         ↓
2. MODEL CONSEQUENCE
   Condition A: "Good job! Here's a reward."  (Reinforced)
   Condition B: "Stop that! You're bad!"      (Punished)
         |
         ↓
3. CHILD WATCHES
   [Child observes the above]
         |
         ↓
4. CHILD'S MOTIVATION
   Condition A: "I saw the adult get rewarded.
                I'm motivated to imitate."
   Condition B: "I saw the adult get punished.
                I'm less motivated to imitate."
         |
         ↓
5. BEHAVIOR TEST
   Child placed alone with Bobo doll
   Condition A: High aggression imitation
   Condition B: Low aggression imitation
         |
         ↓
6. AFTER REWARD OFFER
   Experimenter offers reward for imitating aggression
   Both groups: High aggression imitation
   (Shows learning occurred in both; performance was different)
```

**What students should see:**
- That observation alone doesn't determine imitation
- That vicarious consequences matter: what happens to the model affects motivation
- That all children learned the behavior (both groups could perform it)
- That motivation to perform is separate from learning

**Key point to highlight:** This demonstrates the four-step process and the critical finding that learning ≠ performance.

## Figure 6.8 — Learning Mechanism Boundaries

**Reference in chapter:** Integration section, when discussing what each mechanism can and cannot do.

**Visual representation:**

```
WHAT CAN BE LEARNED BY EACH MECHANISM

Classical Conditioning:
├─ Emotional responses to new triggers
│  (fear of driving, anxiety in hospitals)
├─ Physiological responses to new triggers
│  (nausea, pupil dilation)
└─ Involuntary reflexes redirected
   (salivation to bell)

Operant Conditioning:
├─ Behavioral frequency changes
│  (more studying, less phone use)
├─ Skills with practice and feedback
│  (typing faster, math accuracy)
├─ Habits and routines
│  (morning exercise, cleaning)
└─ Motivation-driven behavior patterns
   (work effort, compliance)

Observational Learning:
├─ Complex motor skills
│  (sports moves, surgical technique)
├─ Social behaviors and norms
│  (conversation style, politeness)
├─ Cognitive strategies
│  (problem-solving approaches)
└─ Cultural knowledge and preferences
   (language accent, food preferences)

Mechanisms working together:
├─ Learning to drive safely
│  (classical: fear response; operant: reinforcement for safe behavior;
│   observational: modeling by experienced driver)
├─ Addiction recovery
│  (classical: extinction of cravings; operant: reinforcement for abstinence;
│   observational: modeling by others in recovery)
└─ Parenting skills
   (classical: reducing anxiety about parenting; operant: reinforcement for
   good parenting; observational: modeling of effective parents)
```

**What students should see:**
- That each mechanism has a domain where it's effective
- That most real learning involves all three mechanisms
- That understanding which mechanism is operating guides intervention

**Key point to highlight:** No single mechanism explains all learning. Understanding the boundaries shows where to look for intervention points.

## Notes for image creation/use

1. **Acquisition/extinction graphs:** Show the curve, not just the dots. Students should see the *rate* of change.

2. **Quadrant diagrams:** Use the same visual structure (2×2 grid) so students recognize the pattern. Color-code reinforcement (green) vs. punishment (red) if possible.

3. **Sequence diagrams:** Use arrows and icons (stick figures, dolls) to make the process clear. Avoid text-heavy captions.

4. **Compare-contrast figures:** Use parallel structure (two columns, same row layout) so differences jump out.

5. **In worked examples:** Ask students to reproduce these diagrams from memory or to create diagrams for new scenarios.
