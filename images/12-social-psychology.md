## [FIGURE 1: The Asch Line Task]

**Location:** Concept 1 (Cold open)

**Specification:**
Display the four cards from Asch's original line-matching task. Card 1 shows three vertical lines of different lengths labeled A (longest), B (middle), and C (shortest). Card 2 shows a single line labeled X that matches the length of line A.

The figure should make the correct answer visually obvious—this is essential to the puzzle. The reader must be able to see with certainty that A is correct. This reinforces the experimental finding: subjects knew the right answer but said the wrong one anyway because of group pressure.

**Caption:** "The Asch line-matching task. The correct answer is obviously line A, which matches line X in length. In Asch's 1951 experiments, 76% of naive subjects gave the wrong answer when confederates had named line B first, even though the correct answer was plainly visible."

## [FIGURE 2: Attribution Styles (Flowchart or Decision Tree)]

**Location:** Concept 1 (Fundamental attribution error section)

**Specification:**
A diagram showing the flow of attribution when observing behavior:

```
Behavior observed
↓
Is the observer the actor or an observer?
├─ ACTOR (experiencing the behavior) → Points to SITUATION
│  ("I yelled because I'm exhausted, tired, had bad news")
│  [Actor-observer bias: Internal access to situational context]
│
└─ OBSERVER (watching the behavior) → Points to PERSON/DISPOSITION
   ("They yelled because they're angry, mean, irritable")
   [Fundamental attribution error: Lacks situational context]
```

**Caption:** "The actor-observer bias. When explaining your own behavior, you have access to situational information and tend toward situational attributions ('I'm exhausted'). When explaining others' behavior, you lack that context and tend toward dispositional attributions ('They're angry'). The same behavior yields different explanations depending on whether you are the actor or the observer."

## [FIGURE 3: Cognitive Dissonance Reduction Pathways]

**Location:** Concept 2 (Cognitive dissonance section)

**Specification:**
A triangle or three-branch diagram showing the three paths to dissonance reduction:

```
                    DISSONANCE
                  (Belief ≠ Behavior)
                         ↓
                ┌─────────┼─────────┐
                ↓         ↓         ↓
           PATH 1    PATH 2    PATH 3
         Change     Change    Add New
        Behavior    Cognition  Cognition
           ↓         ↓         ↓
        "I quit    "Smoking   "Smoking
        smoking"   isn't so   helps me
                   bad"       stay thin"
                ↓         ↓         ↓
              CONSONANCE RESTORED

Example: Smoker who believes smoking is harmful
- Path 1: Stop smoking (behavior change)
- Path 2: Rationalize: "Filtered cigarettes are safe enough" (belief change)
- Path 3: Add: "Smoking prevents obesity, which is worse" (new cognition)
All three restore cognitive consonance.
```

**Caption:** "Three paths to reducing cognitive dissonance. When beliefs and behaviors conflict (you believe smoking is harmful but you smoke), the psychological discomfort motivates change. You can change the behavior, change the belief, or add a new cognition to bridge the conflict. All three restore internal consonance."

## [FIGURE 6: The Stereotype → Prejudice → Discrimination Pipeline]

**Location:** Concept 4 (Prejudice section)

**Specification:**
A flow diagram showing the progression and the points where intervention is possible:

```
STEREOTYPE (Belief)
"Older adults are slow and incompetent"
    ↓ (confirmation bias, social learning)
PREJUDICE (Feeling)
"I feel uncomfortable around older adults; I view them negatively"
    ↓ (activated in certain contexts)
DISCRIMINATION (Action)
"I avoid hiring older workers; I exclude older adults from my friend group"
    ↓ (repeated, becomes systemic)
INSTITUTIONAL PATTERN
Age-based hiring discrimination, age-segregated housing, etc.

Intervention points:
- At stereotype level: Counter stereotypes with accurate information
- At prejudice level: Reduce aversive feelings through contact & individuation
- At discrimination level: Create policies that document and prevent discriminatory action
```

**Caption:** "From stereotype to discrimination. A stereotype (overgeneralized belief) can activate prejudice (negative feeling), which can lead to discrimination (differential treatment). The progression is not inevitable—people can hold stereotypes without feeling prejudiced, and can resist discriminatory action despite prejudiced feelings. Intervention is possible at each stage."

## [FIGURE 7: In-Group Bias and Outgroup Stereotyping]

**Location:** Concept 4 (In-group bias section)

**Specification:**
A Venn diagram or comparison table showing in-group preference:

```
        IN-GROUP                OUT-GROUP
      (Us, Similar)          (Them, Different)

Traits remembered:  Positive            Negative
Exceptions noted:   Many               Few
Motives attributed: Complex, situational  Simple, dispositional
Behavior given:     Benefit of doubt    Skepticism
Trust level:        High               Lower
```

**Caption:** "In-group bias: we attribute positive traits, remember positive examples, and give benefit of doubt to people similar to us (in-group). For people different from us (out-group), we notice negative examples, assume simple motives, and apply stricter judgment. This bias is nearly universal across cultures and shows up in children as young as three."

```
                    COHESIVE GROUP
                          ↓

                          ↓

                          ↓

                          ↓
              Biased decision-making
                          ↓
                  Poor outcomes
                          ↓
        Blame external factors, not the decision
                          ↓
            Confidence in group maintained
                          ↓

```

Intervention points marked: "Seek outside opinion," "Vote privately," "Assign devil's advocate," "Delay decision."

**Caption:** "The groupthink cycle. A cohesive group prioritizing harmony can suppress disagreement, creating a false illusion of unanimity. This leads to biased decisions. Poor outcomes are blamed on external factors, not the group's reasoning, so the group maintains confidence. The cycle repeats unless interrupted by outside input or internal challenge."

## [FIGURE 9: Kitty Genovese Case Timeline (Revised Account)]

**Location:** Concept 3 (Worked example, bystander effect section)

**Specification:**
A timeline showing what actually happened during the Kitty Genovese attack (1964):

```
~3:15 AM    Genovese is first stabbed; she screams for help
            → Some neighbors hear, some don't (not 38)
            → One man shouts at attacker; attacker flees

~10 min     Attacker returns; Genovese is stabbed again, mortally
            → Some neighbors hear
            → A woman says she calls police (disputed whether she did)
            → Another resident hears, comes downstairs to be with Genovese
            → Genovese is moved indoors; dies before police arrive

Later       Police come to neighborhood
            → Several neighbors provide information
            → Police are able to track down the attacker days later

MYTH: 38 witnesses; none helped; all did nothing
FACT: Actual witnesses were fewer; several intervened; help was insufficient, not absent
```

**Caption:** "Timeline of the Kitty Genovese case (revised). The legend of 38 silent witnesses was an exaggeration. Fewer neighbors actually heard the attack; several did intervene (one shouted, another called police, a third comforted her). The tragedy was not that no one helped, but that help came too late. The myth served a teaching purpose (demonstrating diffusion of responsibility) but distorted the historical record."

## [FIGURE 10: Self-Fulfilling Prophecy in the Classroom]

**Location:** Concept 4 (Self-fulfilling prophecy section)

**Specification:**
A sequence diagram showing how teacher expectations reshape student performance:

```
INITIAL STATE
Teacher: "This student is smart"
Student: Unknown actual ability

TEACHER BEHAVIOR CHANGES
- More eye contact, more questions
- More wait time for answers
- More positive feedback
- Harder questions offered
- More opportunities to respond

STUDENT BEHAVIOR ADAPTS
- More engaged (positive feedback)
- More practice (asked more questions)
- Develops stronger skills (harder questions)
- Higher confidence

OUTCOME
Student performs better
    ↓
Confirms original expectation
    ↓
Expectation becomes self-fulfilling

Note: Same process works in reverse if teacher expects failure.
```

**Caption:** "Self-fulfilling prophecy in education (Rosenthal & Jacobson, 1968). When teachers hold high expectations, they inadvertently change their behavior toward students—more questions, more wait time, more positive feedback. This changed treatment leads students to develop the skills that fulfill the expectation. Stereotypes become self-fulfilling through the mechanism of differential treatment."

## [FIGURE 11: Implicit vs. Explicit Bias (Dual Attitudes Model)]

**Location:** Concept 4 (Implicit bias section)

**Specification:**
A two-layer diagram or comparison:

```
EXPLICIT ATTITUDES (Conscious, Controllable)
┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
│ "I believe in equality"              │
│ "Discrimination is wrong"            │
│ "People should be judged as individuals" │
└──────────────────────────────────────┘
         ↓ Sometimes misaligned ↓
IMPLICIT ATTITUDES (Unconscious, Automatic)
┌──────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Automatic association: Black face → bad │
│ Faster recognition of threatening face │
│ Comfort in same-race social contexts │
│ Implicit preference for in-group traits │
└──────────────────────────────────────┘

Result: Person genuinely believes in equality but harbors implicit biases
discovered only through indirect measures (IAT, behavioral observation)
```

**Caption:** "Dual attitudes model. Explicit attitudes (conscious beliefs) can be genuinely egalitarian while implicit attitudes (automatic associations) harbor bias. Most people score low on explicit racism measures but show evidence of implicit bias on tests like the Implicit Association Test. These are not consciously hypocritical; the two attitude systems can be genuinely separate."

## Asset production notes

- All figures should be clear, labeled, and accessible (high contrast, readable fonts)
- Scientific diagrams should be rendered professionally but simply; avoid unnecessary decoration
- Real data (Asch, Milgram variations) should be shown as bars or line charts with actual numbers
- Conceptual models (dissonance, groupthink) can be flowcharts or cycle diagrams
- Include captions that explain what the figure shows and why it matters for the chapter
- All figures should be alt-text ready (comprehensive captions for screen readers)

---

## Images to avoid or correct in existing textbooks

1. **Asch figure:** Do NOT use ambiguous line drawings. The lines must be obviously different in length. The whole point is that the correct answer is evident.

2. **Milgram figure:** Do NOT show the shock generator as larger-than-life or menacing. It should look like a relatively mundane 1960s piece of equipment. The menace is in what it represents, not in its appearance.

3. **Stanford Prison Experiment figures:** If any are included, contextualize with the critique. The now-debunked "guard brutality" should not be presented as a clean demonstration of situational power.

4. **Stereotype/prejudice section:** Do NOT use caricatured or offensive images as examples of stereotypes. Describe stereotypes in text or show neutral diagrams. The goal is to understand the mechanism, not to reinforce offensive images.

5. **Kitty Genovese:** Do NOT include photos of Genovese or sensationalized crime-scene material. The case should be presented factually in text and timeline, respecting the victim's dignity.
