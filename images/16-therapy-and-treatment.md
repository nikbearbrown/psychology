# Hero image brief — Chapter 16: Therapy and Treatment

## Primary image request

**Scene:** Freud's consulting room, Vienna, circa 1900. A patient lies on the famous couch, which is oriented so that the analyst sits behind the patient's line of sight. A single window allows northern light into the room. Books line the shelves. The furniture is substantial, Austro-Hungarian style. The image captures the moment before talk became a treatment — the setup, the deliberate design, the intellectual seriousness.

**Visual focus:** The couch as the central object. The patient in repose. The analyst's chair positioned behind, slightly out of frame or barely visible. The room's atmosphere: quiet, serious, attentive.

**Mood:** Contemplative, serious, foundational. Not melodramatic. The image should feel like a historical moment of genuine invention.

**What the student should notice:** The deliberate physical arrangement (the couch facing away, the positioning of analyst behind), which embodies Freud's theory that the patient should feel less observed. This design choice reveals his thinking: he wanted to reduce the social pressure on the patient, to make space for unconscious material to surface. The image is about method made physical.

**Caption:** Freud's consulting room, Berggasse 19, Vienna, circa 1900. The couch faced away from the analyst deliberately — a design choice meant to reduce observation and allow unconscious material to emerge. The physical arrangement embodied a theory about how healing works: through reduced social pressure and the safety to speak freely.

## Secondary supporting images (for concepts)

### Concept 1: Four therapeutic approaches

**Image 1.1 — Systematic desensitization hierarchy**
A visual hierarchy diagram showing anxiety levels (y-axis, 0–100) against increasingly fear-inducing situations (x-axis). For the example of elevator phobia:
- Level 1: Seeing a photo of an elevator (anxiety 10)
- Level 2: Standing outside an elevator door (anxiety 25)
- Level 3: Pressing the call button (anxiety 40)
- Level 4: Entering the elevator with the door open (anxiety 60)
- Level 5: Doors closing, brief ride to floor 2 (anxiety 75)
- Level 6: Full ascent to floor 5 (anxiety 85)
- Level 7: Ascending 10 floors (anxiety 95)

The diagram should show horizontal bars at each level with the situation labeled. A line connects the tops of the bars, showing the graduated increase. The visualization makes concrete what "systematic desensitization" means — a step-by-step relearning process.

**What the student should notice:** The method is not "just go ride the elevator." It is incremental, measured, deliberate. Each step allows the nervous system to learn that the situation is safe before escalating. This shows why exposure therapy works: it uses gradualism and the incompatibility of fear and relaxation to retrain the nervous system.

**Caption:** Systematic desensitization hierarchy for elevator phobia. The patient and therapist create a graded list of feared situations, pair each with relaxation training, and move up the hierarchy only when the patient can remain relaxed at the lower level. This method shows behavior therapy's core mechanism: learning through gradual exposure.

### Concept 2: Neurotransmitter function

**Image 2.1 — SSRI mechanism at the synapse**
A simplified diagram of two neurons at the synapse:
- Left neuron (presynaptic): releases serotonin into the synaptic space.
- Synaptic cleft: crowded with serotonin molecules.
- Right neuron (postsynaptic): serotonin receptors on the membrane.
- **Without SSRI:** serotonin released, some binds to receptors, some is reuptaken back into the presynaptic neuron via reuptake transporters (depicted as protein channels in the presynaptic membrane).
- **With SSRI:** SSRI blocks the reuptake transporter, so serotonin that would have been reuptaken stays in the synapse longer, allowing more binding to postsynaptic receptors.

**What the student should notice:** The mechanism is local (at the synapse) and immediate (blocking happens right away). Yet SSRIs take weeks to work, which means something else is happening downstream. The diagram should note this limitation: "Mechanism shown here occurs immediately, but symptom improvement takes weeks — suggesting additional neurobiological changes."

**Caption:** SSRI mechanism at the synapse. The SSRI blocks reuptake of serotonin (shown by the X over the reuptake transporter), leaving serotonin in the synapse longer. This increases serotonin signaling but does not fully explain why SSRIs take weeks to reduce depressive symptoms.

### Concept 3: Meta-analytic evidence

**Image 3.1 — Efficacy comparison (forest plot style)**
A horizontal bar chart showing efficacy rates for different therapies treating depression:
- Psychodynamic therapy: 40% remission (with confidence interval bar)
- CBT: 43% remission (with confidence interval bar)
- Behavioral activation: 41% remission (with confidence interval bar)
- Medication (SSRI): 50% remission (with confidence interval bar)
- Placebo: 25% remission (with confidence interval bar)

The bars should overlap substantially, showing that different therapies have similar efficacy. The comparison should be visually obvious that the differences, while statistically measurable, are not large.

**What the student should notice:** All active treatments (psychodynamic, CBT, behavioral, medication) beat placebo, but they beat placebo by similar amounts. The bars overlap, meaning the difference between therapies is small relative to the uncertainty (the confidence interval). This visual embodies the Dodo bird verdict: no one therapy is clearly superior.

**Caption:** Efficacy of different treatments for depression. Meta-analysis shows all evidence-based therapies outperform placebo, but no single therapy significantly outperforms others. The overlapping confidence intervals illustrate the Dodo bird verdict: different approaches produce comparable outcomes.

## Notes on image sourcing and production

- **Freud consulting room:** A historical photograph of Freud's actual consulting room (which is preserved in photographs and now housed in the Freud Museum in London) would be ideal. If an original is unavailable, a carefully researched historical recreation that matches documented details of the room (furniture style, window, bookshelf, couch placement) is acceptable. The image should feel archival and authentic, not rendered or stylized.

- **Systematic desensitization hierarchy:** This is best produced as a clean infographic or diagram. Hand-drawn might work for emphasis; computational rendering also works. The key is clarity — the student should immediately grasp the graduated progression.

- **SSRI synapse diagram:** This is a standard neuroscience illustration. Many variations exist in textbooks and educational materials. Ensure accuracy: presynaptic neuron on left, synaptic cleft, postsynaptic neuron on right, reuptake transporters clearly labeled, serotonin molecules as distinct from other entities. Add a note about the weeks-to-work gap.

- **Meta-analytic forest plot:** This can be generated from data or created as a designed infographic. The visual should be clear and accurate. Do not exaggerate differences or minimize them. The overlapping confidence intervals are the pedagogical point.
