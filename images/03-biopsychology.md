## Figure 1: Phineas Gage's Skull — The Iron Rod's Path

**Description**: Historical image of Gage's skull with overlay showing the path of the iron rod from entry point (below left cheekbone) through the brain to exit point (top of skull).

**Key labels**:
- Entry point (below left zygomatic bone)
- Exit point (superior to coronal suture)
- Probable damage to prefrontal cortex
- Probable damage to connections to limbic system

**Source**: The original skull is at Harvard Medical School. Modern reconstructions by Damasio et al. (1994) are the most anatomically accurate.

**Caption**: Phineas Gage's skull, showing the path of the iron rod. The damage was not limited to the prefrontal cortex itself but also severed connections between the prefrontal cortex and the limbic system, explaining his difficulty with impulse control. (Based on Damasio et al., 1994)

## Figure 2: The Neuron — Structure and Parts

**Description**: Longitudinal section of a neuron showing:

- **Soma** (cell body with nucleus) — labeled in light blue
- **Dendrites** — tree-like branches extending from soma, in darker blue
- **Axon** — single long extension, with myelin sheath shown as segmented covering
- **Nodes of Ranvier** — small gaps in myelin sheath, clearly marked
- **Terminal buttons** — bulbous endings at axon terminus, in red
- **Synaptic vesicles** — small circles inside terminal buttons (magnified view)

**Scale**: Should include a scale bar showing that the soma is roughly 20 micrometers in diameter, while axons can be meters in length.

**Inset**: Magnified view of a single synaptic vesicle showing neurotransmitter molecules inside.

**Caption**: The neuron receives signals through dendrites, integrates them in the soma, and sends an electrical pulse down the axon. At the terminal buttons, the electrical signal becomes chemical—neurotransmitters are released into the synaptic cleft. The myelin sheath, produced by glial cells, insulates the axon and speeds transmission. (Diagram)

## Figure 3: The Action Potential — Changes in Membrane Potential Over Time

**Description**: Graph showing voltage (in millivolts) on the y-axis, time (in milliseconds) on the x-axis.

- Resting potential shown as a flat line at approximately -70 mV
- Threshold of excitation marked as a horizontal dashed line at approximately -55 mV
- Action potential shown as a spike, rising to approximately +30 mV at the peak, then repolarizing
- Hyperpolarization shown as a dip below resting potential before returning to baseline
- All-or-none principle illustrated: either the threshold is crossed (spike occurs) or it isn't

**Below the graph**: Simplified cross-sections of the cell membrane at key points:
- At resting potential: Na+ concentrated outside, K+ concentrated inside
- During depolarization: Na+ gates open, Na+ floods in
- At peak: Na+ gates close, K+ gates open
- During repolarization: K+ leaves the cell
- After hyperpolarization: Return to resting potential

**Caption**: The action potential is an all-or-none event. When a neuron receives signals sufficient to reach the threshold of excitation (approximately -55 mV), voltage-gated sodium and potassium channels open in sequence, creating a stereotyped spike in membrane potential. This electrical change travels down the axon at speeds up to 120 meters per second. (Graph)

## Figure 4: The Synapse and Neurotransmitter Binding

**Description**: Highly magnified cross-section of a synaptic cleft showing:

**Presynaptic terminal**:
- Synaptic vesicles clustered near the presynaptic membrane
- Some vesicles fused with membrane, releasing neurotransmitter molecules
- Neurotransmitter molecules shown as distinct shapes (e.g., colored geometric forms) floating in the synaptic space
- Mitochondria providing energy

**Synaptic cleft**: 20-nanometer space labeled clearly

**Postsynaptic membrane**:
- Receptors shown as protein structures protruding from membrane
- Only receptors matching the neurotransmitter shape shown binding the molecules
- Neighboring receptors not being activated (emphasizing specificity)
- Ion channels associated with some receptors

**Additional detail**: 
- Reuptake pumps shown in the presynaptic membrane, pulling neurotransmitter molecules back
- Enzymes breaking down excess neurotransmitter molecules

**Caption**: Neurotransmitters are released from the presynaptic terminal into the synaptic cleft. They diffuse across and bind to specific receptors on the postsynaptic membrane. This binding opens ion channels, making the postsynaptic neuron more or less likely to fire. Excess neurotransmitter is removed via reuptake or enzymatic breakdown, clearing the synapse for the next signal. (Diagram)

## Figure 5: Brain Regions and Lobes — Lateral View

**Description**: Side view of the human brain showing:

**Lobes**:
- **Frontal lobe** (green): anterior to central sulcus, including motor cortex and prefrontal cortex
- **Parietal lobe** (blue): posterior to central sulcus, including somatosensory cortex
- **Temporal lobe** (orange): lateral surface near temples, including auditory cortex
- **Occipital lobe** (purple): at the back, containing primary visual cortex

**Key structures labeled**:
- Central sulcus (boundary between frontal and parietal)
- Lateral sulcus (boundary between temporal lobe and others)
- Sylvian fissure clearly marked
- Broca's area (inferior frontal gyrus)
- Wernicke's area (superior temporal gyrus)

**Include callouts**: Small magnified views showing:
- Motor cortex homunculus (grotesque figure showing distorted body proportions reflecting cortical space allocation)
- Somatosensory cortex homunculus (similar distortion, emphasizing fingers and face)

**Caption**: The cerebral cortex is divided into four lobes, each associated with different functions. The frontal lobe handles motor control and impulse control. The parietal lobe processes sensation. The temporal lobe processes sound and contributes to memory. The occipital lobe processes vision. The motor and somatosensory homunculi show that cortical space is allocated not evenly but according to functional importance—fingers and lips get disproportionate representation. (Diagram)

## Figure 6: The Two Hemispheres and Corpus Callosum

**Description**: Superior (top-down) view of the brain showing:

- Left and right hemispheres clearly separated by the longitudinal fissure
- Corpus callosum visible as a thick white band connecting the two hemispheres
- Color-coding to show approximate specializations (language on left in red, spatial reasoning on right in blue) with a note that this is a simplification
- Indication of the 200 million axons in the corpus callosum

**Include cross-section**: Sagittal view showing corpus callosum as a thick band of white matter beneath the cortex

**Include labeled structures**: Anterior, posterior, genu, splenium of corpus callosum

**Caption**: The corpus callosum, containing roughly 200 million axons, connects the two hemispheres and allows information processed on one side to be shared with the other. While the hemispheres specialize (language on the left, spatial reasoning on the right), they are integrated rather than independent. Severing the corpus callosum (in split-brain patients) reveals the extent of this integration—the disconnected hemispheres show distinct capabilities and limitations. (Diagram)

## Figure 7: The Hippocampus and Limbic System

**Description**: Sagittal (side-view) section of brain showing:

- **Thalamus**: Sensory relay, centrally located
- **Hypothalamus**: Below thalamus, tiny but crucial
- **Pituitary gland**: Dangling from hypothalamus, interface with endocrine system
- **Hippocampus**: Curled structure in temporal lobe, labeled clearly
- **Amygdala**: Small structure near hippocampus
- **Cingulate gyrus**: Large curved structure following brain's contour

**Color-coding**: Limbic structures in warm colors (orange/red) to distinguish from cortex

**Include callout**: Magnified view of hippocampus showing its distinctive curved shape and internal structure (cornu ammonis, dentate gyrus, subiculum)

**Caption**: The limbic system, including the hippocampus, amygdala, and cingulate gyrus, processes emotion and memory. The hippocampus is crucial for consolidating new memories—damage to it (as in H.M.'s case) prevents the formation of new long-term memories while leaving older memories and procedural learning intact. The amygdala adds emotional weight to memories, making emotionally charged events highly memorable. The hypothalamus, though tiny, regulates hunger, thirst, body temperature, and sexual motivation. (Diagram)

## Figure 8: Brain Imaging Techniques — Comparison Chart

**Description**: Visual comparison of four imaging techniques showing the same anatomical region (e.g., a tumor or stroke damage) imaged with each method.

**Row 1 — CT Scan**:
- Black and white image
- Good structural detail
- Rapid acquisition
- Uses X-rays (radiation exposure)

**Row 2 — MRI**:
- High-contrast gray-scale image
- Excellent structural detail
- No radiation
- Takes longer than CT

**Row 3 — fMRI**:
- Color overlay on anatomical image showing regions of activation
- Shows brain activity during a task
- Good spatial and temporal resolution
- Functional information

**Row 4 — EEG**:
- Topographic map of electrical activity (heat map style)
- Temporal resolution in milliseconds
- Poor spatial resolution
- Good for rapid cognitive processes

**Include legend**: Explain what each color/shade represents in each imaging type

**Caption**: Different brain imaging techniques provide different types of information. CT is good for detecting gross structural abnormalities. MRI provides detailed structural images without radiation. fMRI shows which regions are active during a task. EEG records rapid electrical activity with poor localization but excellent temporal precision. Researchers often use multiple techniques to gain a complete picture. (Diagram)

## Figure 9: Neurotransmitters and Their Effects — Summary Table

**Description**: Visual representation (not a text table, but a diagram) showing neurotransmitters as distinct molecular shapes, each with associated functions and effects.

**Layout**: Neurotransmitter molecules arranged radially, each connected to:
- Brain regions where it's active (in icons)
- Behavioral effects (in descriptive labels)
- Example drugs that affect it (in side labels)

**Include**:
- Dopamine (motivation, reward, movement) — shows substantia nigra and ventral tegmental area
- Serotonin (mood, sleep) — shows raphe nuclei
- Acetylcholine (attention, memory) — shows basal forebrain
- GABA (inhibition) — shows widespread throughout brain
- Glutamate (learning, memory) — shows excitatory throughout
- Endogenous opioids (pain relief) — shows hypothalamus and brainstem

**Color coding**: Each neurotransmitter in distinct color to make learning easier

**Caption**: Different neurotransmitter systems regulate different functions. Dopamine loss in Parkinson's disease causes movement difficulties. Serotonin changes underlie mood alterations in depression. Acetylcholine loss in Alzheimer's impairs memory. Understanding these systems allows targeted drug treatment when neurotransmitter balance is disrupted. (Diagram)

## Figure 10: Gene Expression and the Environment — A Reaction Norm

**Description**: Graph showing phenotypic outcome on y-axis, environmental enrichment on x-axis.

Three reaction norms (one for each genotype):
- High genetic potential: steep upward slope
- Medium genetic potential: moderate slope
- Low genetic potential: shallow slope

All lines intersect or cross, showing that environment can change outcomes and that genetic effects depend on environmental context.

**Annotated examples**:
- Same genotype in poor environment vs. rich environment shows different outcomes
- Different genotypes in same environment show different outcomes
- Crossover showing that genetic rank can reverse with environmental change (rare but possible)

**Caption**: Genes set a range of possible outcomes, and environment determines where within that range an organism falls. This is the concept of range of reaction. The same genes can produce different phenotypes in different environments. Conversely, different genotypes in the same environment can produce different outcomes. Intelligence, height, personality traits all show this interactive pattern. (Graph)
