# Image Briefs — Chapter 5: Sensation and Perception

## 1. Concept 1: The Sensory Receptor and Transduction

**Brief:** Diagram showing the cross-section of a sensory receptor cell (e.g., rod cell from retina). Label the stimulus energy source (e.g., photon arrow), the receptor's membrane with ion channels, the intracellular response, and the output (action potential traveling down the axon to the nervous system). Annotate the three stages: (1) stimulus energy reaches receptor, (2) receptor activation (e.g., conformational change), (3) neural impulse (action potential). Arrow should show the transition from physical energy to electrical signal.

**What the student should notice:** The sensory receptor is a neuron, but specialized. The energy is transformed (transduced) from one form to another. The process is not magical—it is a chain of physical and chemical events.

**Caption:** Transduction: how stimulus energy becomes a neural signal. A photon strikes a rod cell; the cell's ion channels open; an electrical impulse travels to the brain.

## 2. Concept 1: Absolute Threshold Curve

**Brief:** Graph with stimulus intensity (dB, lux, concentration) on the x-axis and "percent detected" on the y-axis (0–100%). Draw a sigmoid curve (S-shaped) showing the transition from below-threshold (low detection %) to above-threshold (high detection %). Mark the absolute threshold at the 50% point. Shade the region below threshold in light gray, the region above in light color. Label specific points (e.g., at 5 dB: 10% detected; at 10 dB: 50% detected; at 15 dB: 90% detected).

**What the student should notice:** Threshold is not a crisp boundary. It is a probabilistic transition. You cannot detect something below threshold (except rarely), but there is no hard wall—detection probability increases gradually. The curve is smooth, not stepped.

**Caption:** Absolute threshold as the 50% detection point. Below threshold, signals are unreliable; above, they are nearly certain. The boundary is fuzzy.

## 3. Concept 2: Weber's Law — Relative JND

**Brief:** Two side-by-side scenes or diagrams. 

**Left panel:** A hand holding a 1-pound weight. A second hand adds a small weight (1 ounce). The observer's face shows "I notice!" With a small baseline, the JND is small in absolute terms.

**Right panel:** A person (or illustration) holding a 100-pound barbell. The same 1 ounce is added. The observer's face shows "No difference." With a large baseline, the same absolute addition falls below the JND.

**Text overlay:** Equation: ΔI = k·I. Show that k is constant, but ΔI scales with I.

**What the student should notice:** The JND is not a fixed amount. It is a *proportion* of the baseline. Larger baselines require larger absolute changes to be noticed. This is why a concert-goer with a loud band notices a 5 dB change but does not notice it in a quiet room with the same absolute decibel increase.

**Caption:** Weber's Law: the just-noticeable difference is a constant fraction of the baseline. Double the baseline, double the JND.

## 4. Concept 3: Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Attention

**Brief:** Split-screen or two-panel diagram.

**Top panel (Bottom-Up):** A loud alarm clock or bright flash. Arrows from the stimulus pointing to an illustration of a brain. The brain is shown as "capturing" or "pulled toward" the stimulus. Label: "Stimulus-Driven. Fast. Automatic. Not under control."

**Bottom panel (Top-Down):** An illustration of a person searching for something (e.g., looking for keys in a room). The brain is shown with a "spotlight" of attention focused on a specific location. Label: "Goal-Driven. Slow. Controlled. Under attention."

**What the student should notice:** Bottom-up is reactive; top-down is proactive. They interact (in the party example, your name breaks through your conversation because it is both unexpected—bottom-up—and personally relevant—interacting with your top-down goals).

**Caption:** Bottom-up processing is driven by the stimulus; top-down is driven by your goals. Both are at work in every moment of perception.

## 5. Concept 3: Inattentional Blindness (Gorilla Study)

**Brief:** Three panels or a video frame sequence.

**Panel 1:** Basketball players passing a ball (simplified line drawing). Label: "Task: Count the passes. Focus your attention here."

**Panel 2:** A gorilla walks through the center of the scene. It is large and obvious. Label: "A gorilla walks across for 9 seconds. Do you see it?"

**Panel 3:** Most observers watching the task focus on the players and ball. The gorilla is in the visual field but not attended. A text label says: "Nearly 50% of observers miss the gorilla. Not because it is invisible. Because attention is engaged elsewhere."

**What the student should notice:** Clear visibility ≠ conscious perception. You can look straight at something and not see it if your attention is elsewhere. This is not inattention in the colloquial sense; it is a real limit on the system's processing capacity.

**Caption:** Inattentional blindness: the failure to notice something clearly visible because attention is directed elsewhere. The gorilla is real; the blindness is real.

## 6. Integration: Perception as Interpretation

**Brief:** A single image interpreted two ways. Classical example: the faces-and-vase ambiguous image. Or: A profile of a woman looking over her shoulder / a young woman looking away. Or: A duck / a rabbit (from the chapter notes).

Show both interpretations as clear, different, and equally valid.

**What the student should notice:** The same sensory input—the same light hitting the retina—produces two completely different perceptions. This is the key insight: perception is not a passive reflection of the world. It is an interpretation. The brain *makes* a choice about how to organize the sensory data. Context, expectation, and prior experience guide that choice.

**Caption:** Same stimulus, different perceptions. This is how the brain constructs meaning from sensation. Perception is not what is out there; it is what your brain makes of it.

## Production Notes

- All diagrams should use clear, educational style: simple line drawings, labeled components, color coding where it illuminates (e.g., stimulus in red, neural response in blue).
- Avoid cartoonish or overly artistic styles that distract from the concept.
- Every image should be legible at a single-page width (readable labels, not tiny text).
- Where human faces or expressions appear, use inclusive, neutral illustrations rather than photos.
- The gorilla image (Concept 3, #5) could be a simplified illustration of a gorilla suit (as in the original study) or a stylized gorilla; clarity matters more than realism.
