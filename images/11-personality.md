# Images and Figures: Chapter 11 — Personality

## Figure list with descriptions

### Figure 1: Galen's Four Temperaments (ancient Greek model)

**Description:** A 2×2 grid showing the four temperaments plotted on two axes: Emotional (top) vs. Calm (bottom), and Changeable (left) vs. Unchangeable (right). At each intersection:
- Top-left (Emotional + Changeable): CHOLERIC — passionate, ambitious, bold (yellow bile)
- Top-right (Emotional + Unchangeable): MELANCHOLIC — reserved, anxious, unhappy (black bile)
- Bottom-left (Calm + Changeable): SANGUINE — joyful, eager, optimistic (red blood)
- Bottom-right (Calm + Unchangeable): PHLEGMATIC — calm, reliable, thoughtful (white phlegm)

**Purpose:** Shows the two-dimensional structure underlying the ancient Greek model that Galen formalized. Demonstrates that even two thousand years ago, personality was being organized along systematic dimensions.

**Source:** Based on Wundt (1874/1886) and Eysenck & Eysenck (1985).

---

### Figure 2: Freud's Structural Model (Id, Ego, Superego)

**Description:** An iceberg diagram with three sections:
- **Conscious (tip above water):** The Ego — the rational part, visible to others, mediates between id and superego
- **Preconscious (just below water):** Accessible memories and thoughts that are not currently conscious but can be brought to mind
- **Unconscious (deep water):** The Id (primitive drives, pleasure principle) and the Superego (moral conscience, rules, guilt). Freud wrote that only about 1/10 of the mind is conscious.

**Purpose:** Illustrates Freud's theory of levels of consciousness and the three personality components. Visual metaphor (iceberg) was Freud's own and remains pedagogically useful.

**Note:** The theory is not supported by modern neuroscience, but the diagram effectively teaches the conceptual structure.

---

### Figure 3: Eysenck's Two-Dimensional Model

**Description:** A 2×2 grid with two axes:
- Horizontal axis: Introversion ← → Extraversion
- Vertical axis: Stability ← → Neuroticism (emotional instability)

Four quadrants labeled with Galen's temperaments:
- Extraversion + Stability: SANGUINE (sociable, optimistic)
- Extraversion + Neuroticism: CHOLERIC (impulsive, irritable)
- Introversion + Neuroticism: MELANCHOLIC (anxious, moody)
- Introversion + Stability: PHLEGMATIC (calm, reserved)

**Purpose:** Shows how Eysenck's two dimensions map to the ancient four temperaments. Demonstrates that modern factor analysis recovered the ancient typology. Also shows why two dimensions were considered too narrow (lost nuance) and why the Big Five were needed.

**Source:** Eysenck & Eysenck (1985).

---

### Figure 4: The Big Five (OCEAN) Dimensions

**Description:** Five horizontal sliding scales (0–100 or Low–High), one for each factor:

1. **Openness to Experience:** Low (Practical, conventional) ← → High (Creative, curious, experimental)
2. **Conscientiousness:** Low (Spontaneous, disorganized) ← → High (Responsible, organized, disciplined)
3. **Extraversion:** Low (Reserved, quiet) ← → High (Outgoing, talkative, sociable)
4. **Agreeableness:** Low (Critical, competitive) ← → High (Cooperative, compassionate, trusting)
5. **Neuroticism:** Low (Calm, confident) ← → High (Anxious, moody, vulnerable to stress)

Sample profiles for three people (e.g., a professor, an athlete, an artist) showing how different profiles produce different personalities.

**Purpose:** The foundational framework of modern personality psychology. Emphasizes that these are spectra, not types. Shows how profiles can be visualized and compared.

**Source:** Costa & McCrae (1992, 1997).

---

### Figure 5: Big Five Trait Stability Across the Lifespan

**Description:** Five line graphs (one for each Big Five factor) plotting trait score (y-axis) against age in years (x-axis), typically from age 18 to 80+.

Expected patterns:
- **Openness:** Slight decline with age
- **Conscientiousness:** Increase from late teens through middle age, then slight plateau or decline
- **Extraversion:** Slight decline with age
- **Agreeableness:** Steady increase, peaking 50–70, then plateau
- **Neuroticism:** Decline with age

**Purpose:** Shows that Big Five factors are relatively stable but not fixed. Most stable in middle adulthood; more variability in late adolescence and early adulthood. Demonstrates personality change across the lifespan.

**Source:** Donnellan & Lucas (2008); Terracciano et al. (2005).

---

### Figure 6: Heritability of Big Five Traits (Minnesota Twin Study)

**Description:** A bar chart showing heritability estimates (0.0–1.0 on y-axis) for the Big Five factors:
- Leadership (highest, ~0.60)
- Extraversion (~0.50)
- Openness (~0.48)
- Conscientiousness (~0.47)
- Agreeableness (~0.42)
- Neuroticism (~0.41)

**Purpose:** Illustrates that genetics accounts for 40–60% of the variance in Big Five traits, with remaining variance from environment. Highest for leadership, more modest for neuroticism. Shows that personality is neither all genetic nor all environmental.

**Source:** Bouchard et al. (1990); Jang et al. (1996).

---

### Figure 7: Mischel's Marshmallow Test

**Description:** A simple two-panel illustration:
- Left panel: A preschooler sitting at a table with one marshmallow and a bell, with an experimenter saying, "You can eat this now, or wait and get two."
- Right panel: The same child 20 years later, representing the longitudinal follow-up showing outcomes (SAT scores, peer relationships, marital stability).

**Purpose:** Illustrates Mischel's classic test of self-regulation and delay of gratification. Shows the longitudinal design. Caption should note that the original study showed strong associations, but a 2018 replication found much weaker, context-dependent associations.

**Source:** Mischel et al. (1972, 1989); Watts et al. (2018).

---

### Figure 8: The Person-Situation Interaction

**Description:** A 2×2 diagram showing trait × situation interaction:
- Horizontal axis: Trait level (Low vs. High Conscientiousness)
- Vertical axis: Situational demand (Chaotic environment vs. Structured environment)

Shows that a highly conscientious person in a chaotic environment may appear less organized than a moderately conscientious person in a structured environment. The behavior emerges from both person and situation.

**Purpose:** Illustrates Mischel's key insight that personality is not solely in the person; it emerges from the interaction between person and situation.

**Source:** Mischel & Shoda (1995).

---

### Figure 9: U.S. Regional Personality Clusters

**Description:** A map of the United States colored in three zones:
- **Cluster 1 (Upper Midwest and Deep South):** "Friendly and Conventional" — high Agreeableness and Conscientiousness
- **Cluster 2 (West):** "Relaxed and Creative" — high Openness, high Emotional Stability
- **Cluster 3 (Northeast):** "Stressed and Neurotic" — high Neuroticism, lower Agreeableness

**Purpose:** Shows that personality traits are not randomly distributed geographically. Selective migration (people moving to places that fit their personalities) creates regional personality profiles.

**Source:** Rentfrow et al. (2013).

---

### Figure 10: MMPI-2 Clinical Profile Example

**Description:** A line graph showing a hypothetical MMPI-2 profile with 10 clinical scales on the x-axis (Hypochondriasis, Depression, Hysteria, Psychopathic Deviance, Masculinity-Femininity, Paranoia, Psychasthenia, Schizophrenia, Hypomania, Social Introversion) and T-scores (30–120) on the y-axis.

An example profile (e.g., showing elevation on Depression, Anxiety, Psychasthenia) with interpretation.

**Purpose:** Shows what a personality assessment profile looks like in clinical practice. Illustrates that personality tests produce quantitative scores that can be plotted and interpreted.

**Source:** Ben-Porath & Tellegen (2008).

---

### Figure 11: Bandura's Reciprocal Determinism Triangle

**Description:** An equilateral triangle with three points labeled:
- **Person** (top): Thoughts, beliefs, expectations, self-efficacy
- **Behavior** (bottom left): Actions, responses, habits
- **Environment** (bottom right): Context, situation, rewards, punishments

Bidirectional arrows between all three, showing that each influences the others simultaneously.

**Purpose:** Illustrates the social-cognitive perspective that personality emerges from ongoing interaction between person, behavior, and environment, not from any one in isolation.

**Source:** Bandura (1986, 1995).

---

### Figure 12: Rogers's Congruence Model

**Description:** Two diagrams side by side:

**Left (Congruence):** Two overlapping circles labeled "Real Self" and "Ideal Self," with substantial overlap. Label: "Congruence → Healthy personality, self-worth, authentic living"

**Right (Incongruence):** Two circles labeled "Real Self" and "Ideal Self," with minimal overlap. Label: "Incongruence → Anxiety, defensiveness, maladjustment"

**Purpose:** Illustrates Rogers's theory that personality health depends on the match between who you are and who you want to be. Simple, visual, memorable.

**Source:** Rogers (1980).

## Image source notes

Figures 1–3 are classical diagrams that appear in numerous textbooks; original sources cited in the chapter text.

Figures 4–9 are based on primary empirical research (Costa & McCrae, Rentfrow et al., etc.) and can be drawn from published data or created as conceptual illustrations.

Figures 10–12 are standard pedagogical diagrams used in personality psychology teaching.

All figures should use clear labeling, high contrast, and color-coding consistent with the book's visual style. Avoid clipart or overly decorative elements; the goal is clarity and teaching, not aesthetics.

## Caption guidelines

Each figure should have:
1. A title describing what is shown
2. A 1–2 sentence explanation of why this figure matters for understanding personality
3. A parenthetical source or note

Example:

**[FIGURE 4: The Big Five (OCEAN) Dimensions]**

The five dimensions of personality as measured by modern trait psychology. Each factor is a spectrum; most people fall somewhere in the middle, with variation. These five factors replicate across cultures and predict academic success, job performance, and mental health outcomes. (Costa & McCrae, 1997.)
